Below is a complete model Macbeth paragraph. Read each sentence, then choose which part of TTECEA it is — the bracket after each label shows the Assessment Objective (AO) it targets. Press Check answers, fix any red ones, and check again until they are all green.

Just after Duncan's murder, Macbeth's overwhelming guilt shows the first signs of the regret that will destroy him.

Shakespeare uses hyperbole when Macbeth cries that not even 'all great Neptune's ocean' could 'wash this blood clean' from his hand.

This exaggeration suggests his guilt is so huge that nothing could ever make him feel clean again.

The word 'blood' stands for his guilt, showing that the murder has stained him forever.

This makes the audience feel both pity for Macbeth and horror at what he has done.

It also makes the audience think about how a good man can be ruined by his own choices.

Shakespeare does this to warn his audience that terrible guilt always follows a crime committed for power.

A Jacobean audience would be especially shocked, because killing a king was seen as the worst possible crime against God's order.

Below is a model An Inspector Calls essay in skeleton form. Label each part of its structure, then press Check answers.

Introduction

What we do to others always finds its way back to us.

In An Inspector Calls, Priestley puts an entire family on trial for how they treated one powerless young woman.

Priestley presents the Inspector as a voice of conscience to argue that society survives only when we accept responsibility for one another.

Body paragraph (TTECEA+C)

Through the Inspector, Priestley confronts the Birlings with the consequences of their selfishness.

Priestley ends the Inspector's final speech with a foreboding tricolon of suffering…

…warning that the selfish will be taught their lesson “in fire and blood and anguish”.

The mounting triple image makes the punishment feel total and inescapable, like a prophecy.

This leaves the audience uneasy — implicated in the Birlings' guilt and urged to change.

Priestley's purpose is to push his 1945 audience to reject the self-interest he blamed for two world wars.

Setting the play in 1912 but writing in 1945, Priestley lets the audience see every catastrophe the Birlings cannot.

Conclusion

Ultimately, Priestley uses the Inspector to indict a society that values profit over people.

The play's controlling idea is that we are responsible for each other, or we are responsible for nothing.

Priestley's central purpose is to convince his audience that real change must come from collective conscience.

The lasting message is that no one can live as if their choices touch no one else.

Once upon a time, a kind girl named Cinderella lived with her cruel stepmother and stepsisters.

Every day, she cooked and cleaned while they treated her as a servant.

Until one day, an invitation arrived — the prince was holding a royal ball.

Because of this, her fairy godmother appeared and conjured a gown and carriage.

Because of this, the prince fell in love with her, but she fled at midnight, leaving a glass slipper.

Below is a persuasive piece arguing that schools should ban single-use plastic, told in six IUMVCC sections. Label each one, then press Check answers.

Every year our school throws away thousands of plastic bottles — it is time we stopped.

Right now that plastic is choking our oceans and poisoning the wildlife we claim to protect; we cannot wait.

We can act today: install water fountains, switch to refillable bottles, and reward every plastic-free class.

Imagine a school with empty bins, cleaner corridors, and students leading the community.

Some say plastic is cheaper and easier — but convenience today means catastrophe tomorrow.

The choice is ours: act now, and we hand the next generation a cleaner world.
