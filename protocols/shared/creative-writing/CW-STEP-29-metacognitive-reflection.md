### Creative Writing Workbook: Step 29 — Metacognitive Reflection

#### Overview

- **Type:** Workbook (self-paced, no AI)
- **Prerequisite:** Step 28 (Final Draft)
- **Feeds into:** Course completion
- **Hero's Journey Stage:** The Return with the Elixir requires reflection — understanding HOW you've been transformed.

---

#### Task: Reflect on Your Transformation

Write a short reflection (150-250 words, 3 paragraphs). This should be quick but meaningful.

---

**Paragraph 1: Growth (75-100 words)**

Look back at where you started. Compare your understanding THEN to NOW:

- Which concept grew most? (Protagonist definition? Character psychology? Structure? Empathy? Theme?)
- Which skill improved most dramatically?
- What did you think "revision" meant then vs. now?
- What surprised you most about the creative process?

_Example: "When I started, I thought a protagonist was just 'the main character' and rated my structure confidence at 2/5. Now I understand protagonist = greatest transformation, I know the Hero's Journey and archetypal structures, and I can outline a complete 6-stage story with all layers mapped (goals/needs, archetypes, empathy, theme, genre). I'd rate myself 5/5 on structure now. I thought revision meant fixing spelling — now I know it's layering meaning through 7 strategic drafts."_

**Paragraph 2: Transfer to Analysis (50-75 words)**

The "Inside-Out" Technique: How will understanding story CREATION help you ANALYSE literature?

- Give ONE specific example from a text you've studied (_Macbeth_, _An Inspector Calls_, _A Christmas Carol_, etc.)
- Which framework will you now use? (Goals vs. needs? Archetypes? Empathy techniques? Theme emergence from transformation?)

_Example: "When analysing Macbeth, I can now identify his GOAL (power/kingship) vs. his NEED (inner peace, honour). I can see his archetypal transformation: Hero to Shadow to Tragic Figure. I understand Shakespeare's empathy techniques — making us pity Macbeth despite evil acts by showing him as victim of ambition and prophecy."_
