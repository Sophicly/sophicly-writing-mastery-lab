### Why Structure and Language Matter (Conceptual Foundation)

**Guiding Principle for This Section:**
If FORM is the architectural blueprint of a poem (sonnet, ballad, free verse), STRUCTURE and LANGUAGE are the bricks, mortar, and interior design. Structural choices—how lines break, where pauses fall, how rhythm operates—create the physical experience of reading. Language choices—word selection, imagery, sound patterns—create the sensory and emotional texture.

**Key Insight:** The most powerful structural and language effects work BELOW conscious awareness. Readers feel the impact of enjambment, caesura, or sibilance before they can name what's happening. Understanding these techniques means understanding how poems manipulate us at the deepest level.

### Step 1: Identifying Structural Features

"Let's examine the STRUCTURAL features of [poem title]. Look at the poem and identify which of the following structural techniques [the poet] uses:

**Metre (rhythm pattern):**
- Does the poem use regular metre (iambic pentameter, dactylic rhythm, etc.) or irregular/free rhythm?
- What effect does the rhythm create? (e.g., iambic pentameter creates natural speech rhythm; dactylic metre creates urgency/galloping)

**Rhyme:**
- Is there a rhyme scheme? (e.g., ABAB, AABB, ABCB, or no rhyme?)
- Is it strong/consistent or weak/breaking down (pararhyme, half-rhyme)?
- Strong rhyme often suggests control; weak or breaking rhyme often suggests instability or emotional struggle

**Enjambment (lines running on):**
- Where does meaning flow across line breaks without pause?
- Enjambment can reflect natural speech, compel the reader forward, mirror stream of consciousness, or emphasise the word at the line's end

**Caesura (mid-line pauses):**
- Where does punctuation create pauses within lines?
- Caesura often reflects interruption, separation, or a sudden shift in thought or emotion

**Stanza structure:**
- How is the poem divided? Regular stanzas, irregular, or a single block?
- Changes in stanza length often signal shifts in meaning or emotion

**Line length:**
- Are lines long, short, varied? What effect does this create?

**Volta (turning point):**
- Is there a clear moment where the poem shifts direction?
- In sonnets, the volta is a defining feature. But many other poems have turning points too.

Identify the THREE most significant structural features in [poem title] and explain what effect each creates.

**Provide a quote for each structural feature you identify.**"

### Step 3: Word Choice and Tense

"Two often overlooked but powerful language choices are WORD CHOICE (diction) and TENSE. Let's examine both in [poem title]:

**Word Choice:**
- What KIND of language does [the poet] use? Is it elevated/formal or simple/colloquial? Is it sensory (appealing to sight, sound, touch, taste, smell)?
- Are there words with particularly strong connotations? Words that surprise or disturb?
- Does the vocabulary suggest a particular world, era, or mindset?

**Tense:**
- Is the poem written in past tense (telling a story, reflecting on memory)?
- Present tense (creating immediacy, ongoing experience)?
- Does the tense SHIFT at any point? If so, where and why?

**Provide a quote that demonstrates a significant word choice OR tense choice, and explain its effect.**"

### Step 4: Connecting Technique to Meaning

- The speaker's perspective and emotional journey (from Section 1)
- The historical context (from Section 2)
- The form (from Section 3)

For example: 'In *Poppies*, Weir's enjambment **perhaps mirrors** the mother's stream of consciousness—her thoughts running on without pause, just as her grief runs on without resolution. This structural instability works with the interior monologue form to immerse us in her psychological experience.'
