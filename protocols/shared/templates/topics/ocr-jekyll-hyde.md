# Topic 1: Jekyll and Hyde / Duality
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Stevenson's novella is built on the concept of duality — the idea that every human contains both good and evil. Jekyll's experiment literalises this Victorian anxiety, and students should analyse how Stevenson uses the double narrative to explore the impossibility of separating these two selves.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4*
**Extract Location:** Chapter 10: Henry Jekyll's Full Statement of the Case
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Jekyll describes the theory behind his experiment and his belief that man is fundamentally dual in nature.

### Extract
```
 1    With every day, and from both sides of my intelligence, the
 2    moral and the intellectual, I thus drew steadily nearer to
 3    that truth, by whose partial discovery I have been doomed to
 4    such a dreadful shipwreck: that man is not truly one, but
 5    truly two. I say two, because the state of my own knowledge
 6    does not pass beyond that point. Others will follow, others
 7    will outstrip me on the same lines; and I hazard the guess
 8    that man will be ultimately known for a mere polity of
 9    multifarious, incongruous and independent denizens. I, for
10    my part, from the nature of my life, advanced infallibly in
11    one direction and in one direction only. It was on the moral
12    side, and in my own person, that I learned to recognise the
13    thorough and primitive duality of man; I saw that, of the
14    two natures that contended in the field of my consciousness,
15    even if I could rightly be said to be either, it was only
16    because I was radically both; and from an early date, even
17    before the course of my scientific discoveries had begun to
18    suggest the most naked possibility of such a miracle, I had
19    learned to dwell with pleasure, as a beloved daydream, on
20    the thought of the separation of these elements. If each, I
21    told myself, could but be housed in separate identities,
22    life would be relieved of all that was unbearable; the
23    unjust might go his way, delivered from the aspirations and
24    remorse of his more upright twin; and the just could walk
25    steadfastly and securely on his upward path.
```

## Or

### Question
'Stevenson suggests that the real horror of Jekyll and Hyde is not that man has an evil side, but that he cannot control it.' How far do you agree with this view? Explore at least two moments from the novel to support your ideas. [40]

# Topic 2: Conceptual Notes
**Type:** conceptual
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde was published in 1886, during a period of intense Victorian anxiety about respectability, scientific progress, and the nature of evil. Understanding Darwin's theory of evolution, the rise of psychology, and the strict moral codes of Victorian gentlemen is essential for interpreting Stevenson's purposes.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4*
**Extract Location:** Chapter 10: Henry Jekyll's Full Statement of the Case
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Jekyll reflects on his public reputation and the secret desires he felt compelled to hide.

### Extract
```
 1    I was born in the year 18— to a large fortune, endowed
 2    besides with excellent parts, inclined by nature to
 3    industry, fond of the respect of the wise and good among my
 4    fellow-men, and thus, as might have been supposed, with
 5    every guarantee of an honourable and distinguished future.
 6    And indeed the worst of my faults was a certain impatient
 7    gaiety of disposition, such as has made the happiness of
 8    many, but such as I found it hard to reconcile with my
 9    imperious desire to carry my head high, and wear a more
10    than commonly grave countenance before the public. Hence it
11    came about that I concealed my pleasures; and that when I
12    reached years of reflection, and began to look round me and
13    take stock of my progress and position in the world, I stood
14    already committed to a profound duplicity of life. Many a
15    man would have even blazoned such irregularities as I was
16    guilty of; but from the high views that I had set before me,
17    I regarded and hid them with an almost morbid sense of
18    shame. It was thus rather the exacting nature of my
19    aspirations than any particular degradation in my faults,
20    that made me what I was.
```

# Topic 3: Hyde (Evil and Violence)
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Hyde embodies pure, undiluted evil — described in animalistic and degenerate terms that would have resonated with Victorian fears about Darwinian regression. Stevenson presents Hyde's violence as escalating and uncontrollable, suggesting that evil, once unleashed, cannot be contained. Students should analyse the language of deformity and repulsion that surrounds Hyde.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4*
**Extract Location:** Chapter 4: The Carew Murder Case
**Extract Context:** In this extract, a maidservant witnesses Hyde's unprovoked attack on Sir Danvers Carew in a London street.

### Extract
```
 1    And then all of a sudden he broke out in a great flame of
 2    anger, stamping with his foot, brandishing the cane, and
 3    carrying on (as the maid described it) like a madman. The
 4    old gentleman took a step back, with the air of one very
 5    much surprised and a trifle hurt; and at that Mr Hyde broke
 6    out of all bounds and clubbed him to the earth. And next
 7    moment, with ape-like fury, he was trampling his victim

12
13    It was two o'clock when she came to herself and called for
```

# Topic 4: Jekyll (Repression and Respectability)
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Jekyll is not simply a good man corrupted — he is a man whose obsession with appearing respectable drives him to dangerous extremes. Stevenson suggests that Victorian society's demand for moral perfection creates the very conditions that produce monsters. Students should analyse how Jekyll's language reveals guilt, self-deception, and the pressure of maintaining a public facade.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4*
**Extract Location:** Chapter 10: Henry Jekyll's Full Statement of the Case
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Jekyll describes the pleasure and freedom he felt upon first transforming into Hyde.

### Extract
```
 1    I felt younger, lighter, happier in body; within I was
 2    conscious of a heady recklessness, a current of disordered
 3    sensual images running like a mill race in my fancy, a
 4    solution of the bonds of obligation, an unknown but not an
 5    innocent freedom of the soul. I knew myself, at the first
 6    breath of this new life, to be more wicked, tenfold more
 7    wicked, sold a slave to my original evil; and the thought,
 8    in that moment, braced and delighted me like wine. I
 9    stretched out my hands, exulting in the freshness of these
10    sensations; and in the act, I was suddenly aware that I had
11    lost in stature.
12
13    There was no mirror, at that date, in my room; that which
14    stands beside me as I write, was brought there later on and
15    for the very purpose of these transformations. The night,
16    however, was far gone into the morning — the morning, black
17    as it was, was nearly ripe for the conception of the day —
18    the inmates of my house were locked in the most rigorous
19    hours of slumber, and I determined, flushed as I was with
20    hope and triumph, to venture in my new shape as far as to
21    my bedroom. I crossed the yard, wherein the constellations
22    looked down upon me, I could have thought, with wonder, the
23    first creature of that sort that their unsleeping vigilance
24    had yet disclosed to them.
```

# Topic 5: Utterson (Rationality and the Victorian Gentleman)
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Utterson is the reader's guide through the mystery, and his rational, restrained character embodies the Victorian ideal of the gentleman. Yet his reluctance to investigate, his loyalty to reputation over truth, and his refusal to acknowledge what he suspects all reveal the limitations of this ideal. Stevenson uses Utterson to show how Victorian propriety enables evil to flourish unchecked.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4*
**Extract Location:** Chapter 1: Story of the Door
**Extract Context:** In this extract, the narrator introduces Mr Utterson the lawyer and describes his character and habits.

### Extract
```
 1    Mr Utterson the lawyer was a man of a rugged countenance,
 2    that was never lighted by a smile; cold, scanty and
 3    embarrassed in discourse; backward in sentiment; lean, long,
 4    dusty, dreary and yet somehow lovable. At friendly meetings,
 5    and when the wine was to his taste, something eminently
 6    human beaconed from his eye; something indeed which never
 7    found its way into his talk, but which spoke not only in
 8    these silent symbols of the after-dinner face, but more
 9    often and loudly in the acts of his life. He was austere
10    with himself; drank gin when he was alone, to mortify a
11    taste for vintages; and though he enjoyed the theatre, had
12    not crossed the doors of one for twenty years. But he had
13    an approved tolerance for others; sometimes wondering,
14    almost with envy, at the high pressure of spirits involved
15    in their misdeeds; and in any extremity inclined to help
16    rather than to reprove. "I incline to Cain's heresy," he
17    used to say quaintly: "I let my brother go to the devil in
18    his own way." In this character, it was frequently his
19    fortune to be the last reputable acquaintance and the last
20    good influence in the lives of down-going men. And to such
21    as these, so long as they came to his chambers, he never
22    marked a shade of change in his demeanour.
```

### Question
How does Stevenson present Utterson as a typical Victorian gentleman, in this extract and elsewhere in the novel? [40]

# Topic 6: Secrecy and Reputation
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Secrecy pervades every level of the novella — from Jekyll's hidden laboratory to the sealed letters that are not to be opened until after death. Stevenson presents a society where reputation matters more than truth, and where the desire to protect respectability enables dangerous secrets to fester. Students should analyse how the narrative structure itself mirrors this culture of concealment.

# Topic 7: Science and Religion
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Stevenson wrote during a period of intense conflict between scientific progress and religious morality. Jekyll's experiment represents the danger of science without ethical limits — a Faustian bargain that Victorian readers would have recognised. The novella asks whether there are boundaries that science should not cross, and what happens when human ambition overrides moral caution.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4*
**Extract Location:** Chapter 10: Henry Jekyll's Full Statement of the Case
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Jekyll describes the moment of his first transformation after drinking the potion he has created.

### Extract
```
 1    The most racking pangs succeeded: a grinding in the bones,
 2    horrible nausea, and a horror of the spirit that cannot be
 3    exceeded at the hour of birth or death. Then these agonies
 4    began swiftly to subside, and I came to myself as if out of
 5    a great sickness. There was something strange in my
 6    sensations, something indescribably new and, from its very
 7    novelty, incredibly sweet. I felt younger, lighter, happier
 8    in body; within I was conscious of a heady recklessness, a
 9    current of disordered sensual images running like a mill
10    race in my fancy, a solution of the bonds of obligation, an
11    unknown but not an innocent freedom of the soul. I knew

13    wicked, tenfold more wicked, sold a slave to my original
```

# Topic 8: Setting (London, Fog, and Doors)
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Stevenson uses London's physical landscape — its fog, dark streets, and contrasting facades — to mirror the novella's themes of duality and concealment. The famous door to Jekyll's laboratory, featureless and sinister, becomes a symbol of the hidden life behind respectable appearances. Students should analyse how Stevenson uses pathetic fallacy and urban imagery to create atmosphere and reinforce meaning.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4*
**Extract Location:** Chapter 4: The Carew Murder Case
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Utterson travels with a police inspector to Hyde's lodgings in Soho after the murder of Sir Danvers Carew.

### Extract
```
 1    It was by this time about nine in the morning, and the
 2    first fog of the season. A great chocolate-coloured pall
 3    lowered over heaven, but the wind was continually charging
 4    and routing these embattled vapours; so that as the cab
 5    crawled from street to street, Mr Utterson beheld a
 6    marvellous number of degrees and hues of twilight; for here
 7    it would be dark like the back-end of evening; and there
 8    would be a glow of a rich, lurid brown, like the light of

17    nightmare. The thoughts of his mind, besides, were of the
18    gloomiest dye; and when he glanced at the companion of his
19    drive, he was conscious of some touch of that terror of the
20    law and the law's officers, which may at times assail the
21    most honest.
```

# Topic 9: Lanyon (Horror of Knowledge)
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Dr Lanyon represents the rational, orthodox scientist who cannot survive the knowledge of what Jekyll has done. His rapid physical decline and death after witnessing Hyde's transformation dramatise the idea that some truths are too terrible to bear. Stevenson uses Lanyon to explore the limits of Victorian rationalism and the psychological cost of confronting the irrational.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4*
**Extract Location:** Chapter 9: Dr Lanyon's Narrative
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Lanyon describes the moment when Hyde drinks the potion and transforms back into Jekyll before his eyes.

### Extract
```
 1    He put the glass to his lips and drank at one gulp. A cry
 2    followed; he reeled, staggered, clutched at the table and
 3    held on, staring with injected eyes, gasping with open
 4    mouth; and as I looked there came, I thought, a change — he
 5    seemed to swell — his face became suddenly black and the
 6    features seemed to melt and alter — and the next moment, I
 7    had sprung to my feet and leaped back against the wall, my
 8    arm raised to shield me from that prodigy, my mind submerged
 9    in terror.
10
11    "O God!" I screamed, and "O God!" again and again; for
12    there before my eyes — pale and shaken, and half fainting,
13    and groping before him with his hands, like a man restored
14    from death — there stood Henry Jekyll!
15
16    What he told me in the next hour, I cannot bring my mind to
17    set on paper. I saw what I saw, I heard what I heard, and
18    my soul sickened at it; and yet now when that sight has
19    faded from my eyes, I ask myself if I believe it, and I
20    cannot answer. My life is shaken to its roots; sleep has
21    left me; the deadliest terror sits by me at all hours of
22    the day and night; I feel that my days are numbered, and
23    that I must die; and yet I shall die incredulous. As for
24    the moral turpitude that man unveiled to me, even with tears
25    of penitence, I cannot, even in memory, dwell on it without
26    a start of horror.
```

# Topic 10: Victorian Society and Hypocrisy
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Stevenson presents Victorian society as one built on appearances, where gentlemen maintain respectable facades while concealing their true natures. The novella's all-male world of lawyers, doctors, and politicians reveals a culture where reputation is valued above honesty. Students should analyse how the social world of the novella enables Jekyll's deception and consider what Stevenson suggests about the relationship between society and individual morality.

## Either

**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4*
**Extract Location:** Chapter 1: Story of the Door
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Mr Enfield tells Utterson about the night he witnessed Hyde trampling a young girl, and how the crowd forced Hyde to pay compensation to avoid a public scandal.
