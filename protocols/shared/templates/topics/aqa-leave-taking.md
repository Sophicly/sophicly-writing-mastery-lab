# Topic 1: Enid
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Enid is the protagonist whose deteriorating mental state drives the play's central conflict. Students should analyse how Pinnock uses Enid's confusion, her longing for Jamaica, and her increasing dependence on obeah to explore the psychological toll of displacement on Caribbean immigrants in Britain, showing how the loss of homeland, community, and cultural identity manifests as profound emotional and mental suffering.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

## Question
How does Pinnock use the character of Enid to explore ideas about displacement and mental health in *Leave Taking*?

Write about:
- what Enid says and does
- how Pinnock presents Enid.

# Topic 5: Immigration and Cultural Identity
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Immigration and cultural identity form the structural backbone of the play. Students should analyse how Pinnock uses contrasting attitudes to Jamaica and England, the characters' shifting language registers, and the tension between assimilation and preservation to build a sustained exploration of what it means to belong to two cultures and feel at home in neither.

# Topic 7: Mental Health and Obeah
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Pinnock intertwines mental health and Caribbean spiritual tradition to expose the failures of a society that offers no cultural space for immigrant suffering. Students should analyse how Enid's deterioration is understood differently by different characters — as illness by her daughters, as a spiritual crisis by Mai — and how Pinnock uses obeah not as superstition but as a legitimate form of healing and cultural connection, contrasting it with the inadequacy of British institutional responses to Black mental health.

# Topic 9: Belonging and Displacement
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** The tension between belonging and displacement runs through every relationship in the play. Students should analyse how Pinnock presents characters who are physically in Britain but emotionally tethered to Jamaica, and others who are culturally British but denied full belonging by racism. Pinnock uses setting, memory, and the contrasting experiences of the two generations to argue that displacement is not a single event but an ongoing condition that reshapes itself with each generation.

# Topic 10: Mother-Daughter Relationships
