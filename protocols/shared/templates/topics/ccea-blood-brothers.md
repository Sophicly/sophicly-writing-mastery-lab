# Topic 1: Mickey
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Russell presents Mickey as the tragic centre of *Blood Brothers* — a character whose warmth, humour, and humanity in childhood are systematically eroded by the economic deprivation and social exclusion that the class system imposes on him. The contrast between Mickey and Eddie in childhood scenes is comic and affectionate; the same contrast in adulthood becomes devastating, as Mickey's lack of education and employment prospects turns the gap between them into an unbridgeable social chasm. Mickey's descent into depression and dependency on prescription drugs after his prison sentence is presented by Russell as the product of structural unemployment rather than personal weakness, making the audience complicit in the wider social failure. The dramatic irony of the audience knowing what Mickey does not — the blood brotherhood — charges his final confrontation with Eddie with a tragic recognition that arrives too late.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 3: Eddie
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Eddie is presented not as a villain but as the beneficiary of a class system he did not choose — his advantages (education, confidence, opportunity) are structural rather than earned, and Russell ensures the audience understands this by showing Eddie's genuine affection for Mickey and Linda. His education at a private school and his easy passage into university and professional life are presented in deliberate contrast to Mickey's closed doors, making the same talent and energy produce entirely different trajectories depending on social class. Eddie's tragedy is that his privilege renders him incapable of fully understanding what it has cost Mickey — his attempts to help in adulthood are well-intentioned but loaded with the patronage that wealth makes inevitable. Russell uses Eddie to show that the class system damages its beneficiaries too, though not equally: Eddie's damage is existential (rootlessness, the secret of his birth), Mickey's is material and lethal.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 4: Mrs Johnstone
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Mrs Johnstone is presented as a woman crushed by poverty yet possessed of a warmth and vitality that Russell embeds in her musical voice — her opening and recurring song establishes her as narrator, participant, and tragic centre of the play simultaneously. Her decision to give away one twin is not presented as a moral failure but as the impossible choice of a woman without resources, contextualised by Russell within a welfare state that fails the poorest families. The superstition that drives the play's action — Mrs Johnstone's fear of the consequences of separated twins learning of each other — is both her own belief and a theatrical device Russell uses to frame the real determinants of the tragedy: class and economic inequality. Her working-class directness, her capacity for love, and her inability to protect her children from the consequences of her poverty make her the play's most humanly complex figure.
**Marks:** 40

# Topic 5: Mrs Lyons
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Mrs Lyons is presented as a character defined by what she lacks — a child of her own — and whose acquisition of Edward through manipulation reveals how the middle classes use wealth and social power to take what they cannot otherwise have. Russell is careful not to make her simply villainous: her genuine love for Eddie is evident, and her paranoia about Mrs Johnstone is in part the understandable anxiety of a woman who has done something she knows was wrong. She weaponises the very superstition she plants in Mrs Johnstone's mind, turning her lie into a self-fulfilling threat — a darkly comic example of how the powerful create the conditions that justify their own anxieties. Her increasingly unstable pursuit of Mrs Johnstone in the play's second act shows how guilt and fear corrode a person who lacks the working-class resilience that Mrs Johnstone, for all her poverty, possesses.

# Topic 6: Nature vs Nurture
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The twin brothers' contrasting lives provide Russell with his central dramatic question: is character the product of birth or environment? By giving Mickey and Eddie identical genetic origins but radically different social contexts, Russell creates a controlled experiment whose conclusion is unambiguous — environment, not nature, is decisive. The boys' childhood similarities (the same curiosity, the same laughter, the same games) make their adult divergence all the more pointed: it is not who they are but where they were placed that determines their fates. Russell refuses to romanticise this conclusion: the suggestion that they were "blood brothers" throughout — and that this shared nature was not enough to overcome social difference — makes the play's argument about class all the more stark. The nature/nurture question is ultimately a question about justice: the play asks whether a just society would allow birth circumstances to determine life outcomes so completely.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 7: Social Class
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Class is the engine of *Blood Brothers*' tragedy — not the accidental difference between two brothers but the structural force that determines their education, employment, housing, mental health provision, and ultimately their deaths. Russell embeds class in every dimension of the play's theatrical language: in the characters' accents and idiom, in the physical spaces they inhabit, in the music that accompanies their different worlds. The play is set in Liverpool during the Thatcher era, when deindustrialisation produced mass unemployment in working-class communities, and Russell uses this historical context to give Mickey's fate the weight of a generation rather than an individual tragedy. The final tableau — two brothers dead on stage while the Narrator asks the audience "how much does it cost?" — is Russell's most direct challenge: the audience's comfort depends on the same class system that has just destroyed two men on stage.

# Topic 8: Superstition and Fate
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Superstition functions in *Blood Brothers* as both theatrical device and ideological critique — the "fact" that separated twins who learn of each other must die provides the play with its dramatic logic while Russell simultaneously exposes superstition as a consolation that obscures real causes. Mrs Johnstone's belief in the superstition, and Mrs Lyons's cynical exploitation of it, positions the working-class character as the one whose acceptance of fate disarms her against the middle-class character's deliberate manipulation. The Narrator's recurring presence — embodying fate, guilt, or social determinism, depending on interpretation — sustains the theatrical atmosphere of doom while allowing a rational audience to recognise that the twins' deaths are produced by unemployment, inequality, and despair rather than supernatural inevitability. Russell uses the tension between fate and social cause to ask: is tragedy the result of destiny, or of decisions made by those with the power to make different ones?

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 9: Friendship and Brotherhood
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Mickey and Eddie's friendship — spontaneous, instinctive, and governed by a childhood blood pact — is the play's emotional core and the measure of everything the class system destroys. Russell presents their childhood bond with comedic warmth (the forbidden words, the shared sweets, the games) to make its erosion in adulthood all the more painful: the same two people, separated by the social consequences of birth, find themselves unable to inhabit the same emotional space. Their adult reunion — Eddie confident and successful, Mickey imprisoned and medicated — is presented by Russell as a scene of mutual, helpless love unable to bridge the social gulf between them. The "blood brothers" pact is the play's central irony: a ritual bond that feels absolute but is no match for the structural forces that class imposes on human relationships.

# Topic 10: Violence and Tragedy
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Russell builds towards the play's violent conclusion with careful theatrical management of dramatic irony — the audience knows the twins' relationship before the characters do, and this knowledge transforms every scene into a countdown. The final confrontation in the council chamber is presented not as a crime of passion but as the inevitable product of systemic failure: Mickey's gun is in his hand because there was no other help available for his depression, unemployment, or despair. Russell frames the deaths as a social tragedy, not a personal one: the Narrator's closing question — "did you think that the cost would be yours?" — addresses the audience directly as participants in the class system that produced this outcome. The theatrical choice to have both brothers die simultaneously reinforces the play's argument: the class system does not merely harm the poor, it destroys the human connection that might otherwise cross class lines.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
