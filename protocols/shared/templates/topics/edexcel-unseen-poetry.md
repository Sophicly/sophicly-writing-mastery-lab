# Topic 1: Analysing Imagery and Metaphor
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** Imagery and metaphor are the primary means by which poetry compresses meaning, and analytical responses must move beyond identifying the device to explaining the specific conceptual work it performs in its context. Extended metaphors create structural coherence across a poem; tracking their development — and any point where they break or shift — reveals how the poet's argument evolves. When analysing a metaphor, ask what two domains are being linked, what qualities transfer from the vehicle to the tenor, and whether the comparison illuminates, distorts, or ironises its subject. The most sophisticated responses notice the gap between what a metaphor claims and what the surrounding poem complicates — this tension is where meaning is most richly concentrated.

# Topic 2: Conceptual Notes
**Type:** conceptual-notes
**Teaching Point:** Edexcel IGCSE Unseen Poetry asks students to analyse a single unseen poem for 20 marks, assessing AO1 (personal, informed response with supporting evidence) and AO2 (analysis of language, structure, and form). The Edexcel mark scheme rewards responses that develop a sustained interpretive argument and use precise technical vocabulary in the service of that argument, rather than responses that catalogue devices without evaluating their effect. A strong response constructs a reading of the poem's central concern early and then deepens and complicates that reading through close analysis of specific moments. Students should be aware that Edexcel values the word "explore" in its questions: this invites a multi-directional response that tests ideas against the poem rather than delivering a pre-formed answer.

# Topic 3: Analysing Structure and Form
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** Structure and form operate at multiple simultaneous levels: the macro-structure of how the poem moves from opening to close, the meso-structure of stanza organisation and line grouping, and the micro-structure of individual line breaks and enjambment. Each level makes meaning, and the most revealing analyses attend to all three, showing how they reinforce or work against each other. Line breaks are the poet's most powerful structural tool: a break mid-phrase creates suspension, expectation, or irony depending on what follows, and students should be taught to read the line ending and the line opening as a pair.

# Topic 4: Analysing Tone and Mood
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** Tone is the speaker's attitude toward the subject; mood is the emotional atmosphere created for the reader — the two are related but analytically distinct, and conflating them reduces the precision of a response. Tone is constructed through diction, syntax, and address. Mood is built cumulatively through image patterns, sound textures, and pacing, and may shift significantly across a poem as the speaker's stance evolves. A strong analytical approach tracks tonal shifts and asks what motivates each shift and what the movement between tones reveals about the poem's central argument.

# Topic 5: Exploring Attitudes and Perspectives
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** Every poem is spoken from a position — a perspective shaped by the speaker's relationship to the subject, their social location, their emotional state, and the gap (if any) between what they say and what the poem implies they mean. Students should distinguish carefully between the poet, the speaker, and any persona the speaker adopts, since the distance between these positions is itself a source of meaning. Attitudes are often most revealing when they are contradictory or unstable — when the speaker claims one thing but the imagery implies another.

# Topic 6: Analysing Sound and Rhythm
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** Sound devices — rhyme, half-rhyme, assonance, consonance, alliteration, onomatopoeia — are not decorative but semantic: they create aural patterns that direct the reader's attention, generate expectation, and produce effects of harmony or dissonance that reinforce or undercut the poem's stated meaning. The key analytical move is always to link the sonic texture to the poem's emotional or intellectual content: not just "this is sibilant" but "the sustained sibilance creates a hissing undertone that ironises the speaker's claims of tenderness."

**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** Comparison is an analytical skill distinct from description, and the central requirement is to move beyond alternating commentary to integrated analysis that articulates the significance of similarity and difference. The most productive comparisons identify a point of genuine tension between the two poems — where their approaches diverge in ways that illuminate something about the subject each is treating — rather than listing superficial parallels.

# Topic 8: Language and Word Choice
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** Diction analysis at its most precise attends to the specific register from which a word is drawn — latinate or Anglo-Saxon, formal or colloquial, archaic or contemporary — because register carries social and historical meaning that inflects the poem's argument. Semantic fields — clusters of words from the same conceptual domain — reveal the poem's implicit preoccupations, and tracking how a semantic field develops, fractures, or is invaded by words from a competing field is one of the most reliable routes into the poem's deeper logic.

# Topic 9: Context and Inference
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** Inference is the active process by which readers construct meaning from what is implied rather than stated, and poetry depends on this process more intensively than any other literary form. Students should be taught to distinguish between what the poem states, what it implies, and what it withholds — and to recognise that withholding is itself a technique, creating spaces the reader must fill.

# Topic 10: Personal Response and Interpretation
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** A personal response in a formal analytical context is not a record of emotional reaction but a reasoned interpretation: a claim about what the poem means or does, supported by evidence and expressed with sufficient specificity to be debatable. The Edexcel mark scheme rewards responses that are both personal and informed. The most sophisticated personal responses acknowledge the poem's resistance to single readings and present their interpretation as one of several possible, arguing for its credibility rather than asserting it as the only conclusion.
