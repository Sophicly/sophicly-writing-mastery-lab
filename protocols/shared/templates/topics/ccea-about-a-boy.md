# Topic 1: Growing Up
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Marcus's journey from isolated misfit to confident young person is the emotional spine of the novel. His growth is catalysed not by conventional family support but by building an unconventional surrogate family around himself. Hornby presents growing up as the process of learning to navigate a world that does not conform to childhood expectations — Marcus must learn to read social codes, manage his mother's depression, and accept that adulthood means tolerating uncertainty. Students should analyse how Hornby uses Marcus's internal monologue, his shifting relationship with Will, and the contrast between his old and new selves to present maturity as an active, painful, and ultimately self-directed process.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does Hornby present ideas about growing up in 'About a Boy'?

You should consider:
- how Marcus changes throughout the novel
- the methods Hornby uses to present Marcus's development
- the significance of Marcus's relationships in his journey towards maturity.

# Topic 3: Loneliness and Belonging
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Both Marcus and Will experience profound loneliness, though from opposite positions — Marcus is lonely because he cannot fit in, while Will is lonely because he has deliberately avoided connection. Hornby structures the novel so that these two forms of isolation gradually converge and resolve through their friendship. The dual narrative perspective allows the reader to see how each character's loneliness is both self-imposed and circumsturally enforced. Students should analyse how Hornby uses the alternating chapters, the metaphor of the "island" lifestyle, and the contrasting domestic environments to explore the difference between solitude and isolation, and how belonging requires vulnerability.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does Hornby present the theme of loneliness and belonging in 'About a Boy'?

You should consider:
- how both Marcus and Will experience loneliness
- the methods Hornby uses to show how characters find belonging
- the significance of the ending in relation to this theme.

# Topic 4: The Relationship Between Marcus and Will
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The Marcus-Will relationship is the structural and thematic centre of the novel. It begins as mutual exploitation — Marcus wants a father figure to stabilise his home life, Will wants Marcus to help him meet single mothers — and evolves into genuine affection. Hornby subverts the mentor-mentee dynamic: Will teaches Marcus about social survival (music, clothes, confidence), while Marcus teaches Will about emotional responsibility. The relationship resists sentimentality because both characters retain their flaws. Students should analyse how Hornby uses the dual narration, the progression of shared experiences (the shoe shopping, the Ellie subplot, the Kurt Cobain crisis), and the careful avoidance of a neat father-son resolution to present an unconventional bond built on mutual need rather than obligation.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

How does Hornby present the relationship between Marcus and Will in 'About a Boy'?

You should consider:

# Topic 5: Fiona and Mental Health
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Fiona's depression is the novel's most serious subject, handled with a deliberate tonal tension between Marcus's childlike incomprehension and the adult reader's awareness of its severity. Her suicide attempt is the inciting incident that drives Marcus to seek stability elsewhere, and her gradual recovery runs parallel to Marcus's growing independence. Hornby avoids reducing Fiona to her illness — she is also politically passionate, musically earnest, and fiercely loving — but her vulnerability forces Marcus into a premature emotional maturity. Students should analyse how Hornby uses Marcus's perspective to filter a complex adult crisis through a child's logic, how the duck-feeding scene functions as both crisis point and turning point, and how Fiona's recovery is presented as fragile and non-linear rather than triumphant.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 6: Masculinity and Identity
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Hornby interrogates what it means to be male through two contrasting models: Will's performative cool (designer clothes, pop culture knowledge, emotional detachment) and Marcus's unselfconscious sincerity (singing Joni Mitchell, wearing unfashionable clothes, expressing emotion openly). Neither model is presented as adequate — Will's surface-level masculinity is hollow, while Marcus's authenticity makes him socially vulnerable. The novel suggests that real masculine identity requires a synthesis: the social awareness to navigate the world and the emotional honesty to connect with others. Students should analyse how Hornby uses the contrasting characterisation of Will and Marcus, the role of popular culture as a social currency, and the secondary male characters (Clive, Marcus's father) to present masculinity as performance, and how both protagonists must revise their understanding of what being a man means.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does Hornby present ideas about masculinity and identity in 'About a Boy'?

You should consider:
- how Will and Marcus represent different ideas about masculinity
- the methods Hornby uses to explore how characters construct their identities
- the significance of how these ideas develop through the novel.

# Topic 7: Bullying and Social Pressure
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Marcus's experience of bullying is presented not as dramatic violence but as the slow, grinding humiliation of social exclusion — being laughed at for singing, wearing the wrong shoes, having a mother who is visibly different. Hornby captures the particular cruelty of early-1990s secondary school through the precision of social codes that Marcus cannot decode. The introduction of Ellie — herself an outsider, but one who has chosen rebellion rather than victimhood — offers Marcus an alternative model for surviving social hostility. Students should analyse how Hornby uses the accumulation of small humiliations, Marcus's internal rationalisation of his treatment, and the contrast between passive endurance and active resistance to present bullying as a systemic social force rather than an individual failing.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 8: Will's Character Development
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Will begins the novel as a self-satisfied bachelor whose entire life is structured around avoiding commitment. His "island" philosophy — "every man is an island" — is a deliberate inversion of Donne's famous line and represents his belief that independence equals freedom. The novel systematically dismantles this philosophy through his encounters with Marcus, Rachel, and the SPAT mothers. Will's development is slower and more resistant than Marcus's; he frequently retreats into cynicism even as he is being changed. Students should analyse how Hornby uses Will's first-person chapters to show the gap between his self-image and his actual behaviour, how his relationship with Marcus forces him to confront his emotional immaturity, and how the novel presents his growth not as a dramatic transformation but as a gradual, reluctant surrender of self-protection.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

How does Hornby present the character of Will and his development in 'About a Boy'?

# Topic 9: Family and Unconventional Relationships
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Hornby challenges the conventional nuclear family model throughout the novel. Marcus lives with a depressed single mother, visits a largely absent father, and ultimately constructs a patchwork family from Will, Ellie, and later Rachel and her son Ali. Will's lack of family is initially presented as freedom but gradually revealed as deprivation. The SPAT group represents a community of imperfect families trying to support each other. Students should analyse how Hornby uses the multiplicity of family structures in the novel, the recurring motif of parents who are themselves struggling to grow up, and the ending — where Marcus's expanded network of adults provides the security his biological family cannot — to present family as a chosen network of mutual support rather than a fixed biological unit.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does Hornby present ideas about family and unconventional relationships in 'About a Boy'?

You should consider:
- the different family structures presented in the novel
- the methods Hornby uses to show how characters build relationships
- the significance of the idea that family can be chosen rather than given.

# Topic 10: Humour and Tone
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Hornby's comic tone is one of the novel's defining features, but it serves a serious purpose: it makes painful subjects — depression, bullying, emotional neglect — accessible without trivialising them. The humour arises primarily from the dual narration, where the gap between Marcus's earnest misreadings of social situations and Will's cynical self-awareness creates dramatic irony. Hornby also uses bathos (deflating serious moments with mundane observations) and cultural references to create a distinctive voice that balances warmth with detachment. Students should analyse how Hornby uses comedic timing, the contrast between the two narrators' tonal registers, and the strategic placement of humour alongside the novel's darkest moments (the suicide attempt, the Dead Duck Day) to engage the reader emotionally without resorting to melodrama.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does Hornby use humour and tone to explore serious themes in 'About a Boy'?

You should consider:
- examples of how Hornby creates humour in the novel
- the methods Hornby uses to balance comedy with serious subject matter
- the significance of the novel's tone in engaging the reader.

[40 marks]
