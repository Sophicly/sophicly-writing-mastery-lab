# Topic 5: Lady Macbeth

# Topic 6: Kingship and Tyranny
**Type:** whole-text
**Teaching Point:** Shakespeare contrasts legitimate kingship (Duncan, Edward, Malcolm) with Macbeth's tyrannical rule to explore what makes a good ruler. Students should connect this to Jacobean ideas about the divine right of kings and the consequences of regicide, recognising that the play was written for a monarch who survived an assassination attempt.

# Topic 7: Loyalty and Betrayal
**Type:** whole-text
**Teaching Point:** The play opens with the punishment of a traitor and ends with the defeat of another. Students must examine how Shakespeare presents loyalty and betrayal through parallel structures — Macbeth's betrayal of Duncan mirrors the Thane of Cawdor's treachery — and consider how the motif of false trust reflects Jacobean anxieties following the Gunpowder Plot.

# Topic 8: Violence
**Type:** whole-text

# Topic 9: Masculinity
**Type:** whole-text
**Teaching Point:** Masculinity is repeatedly weaponised in the play. Lady Macbeth questions her husband's manhood to goad him into murder, Macbeth uses the same tactic on the murderers he hires, and Macduff is told to take his grief "like a man." Students must explore how Shakespeare interrogates what it means to be a man and how toxic constructions of masculinity drive the tragedy forward.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

# Topic 10: Appearance versus Reality
**Type:** whole-text
**Teaching Point:** "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" establishes deception as a structural principle of the play. Students should explore how Shakespeare uses dramatic irony, equivocation, and the motif of false faces to show that nothing in Macbeth's Scotland is what it seems — connecting to the Jacobean fear of Catholic equivocation and hidden treason.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
