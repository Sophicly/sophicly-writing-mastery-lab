# Topic 1: Jane Eyre (protagonist)
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Jane is a radical protagonist for the Victorian period -- a woman who insists on spiritual and intellectual equality with men despite her poverty, plain appearance, and lack of social standing. Bronte uses first-person narration to give the reader unmediated access to Jane's passionate inner world, making her refusal to compromise her principles the moral centre of the novel.

# Topic 3: Rochester
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Rochester is a complex Byronic hero -- passionate, brooding, morally ambiguous, and haunted by his secret marriage to Bertha Mason. Bronte uses him to explore the tension between desire and morality. Students should analyse how Rochester's deception complicates the reader's sympathy, and how his eventual maiming and blinding can be read as symbolic punishment or as the levelling that enables true equality with Jane.

# Topic 4: Love and Passion
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Love in Jane Eyre is presented as a force that must be balanced with moral integrity and self-respect. Bronte challenges the Victorian ideal of passive female devotion by showing Jane as an active agent in her own love story -- one who leaves Rochester at the height of her passion because her principles demand it. The contrast between Rochester's passionate love and St. John's cold, dutiful proposal defines what genuine partnership looks like.

# Topic 5: Independence and Self-Respect
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Jane's independence is the novel's defining moral principle. From her childhood rebellion against the Reeds to her refusal to become Rochester's mistress, Jane consistently chooses self-respect over comfort or security. Bronte uses Jane's inheritance and her voluntary return to Rochester to show that true independence is not about isolation but about entering relationships as an equal.

**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Bronte exposes the cruelty of the Victorian class system through Jane's experiences as a dependent, a charity pupil, and a governess -- a figure who occupied an uncomfortable middle ground between servant and family. Jane's insistence on spiritual equality regardless of social rank is Bronte's most direct challenge to the rigid hierarchies of her time. Students should analyse how different characters embody class prejudice.

# Topic 9: Bertha Mason
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Bertha Mason is one of the novel's most complex and debated figures. She functions as both a Gothic device -- the madwoman in the attic -- and a symbol of everything Jane fears becoming: a woman consumed by passion, stripped of agency, and locked away by patriarchal power. Bronte's portrayal raises difficult questions about race, empire, and the treatment of mentally ill women in the Victorian period. Students should analyse how Bertha's presence haunts the novel and how she can be read as Jane's dark double.

# Topic 10: Gender and Patriarchy
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Bronte challenges Victorian gender expectations throughout the novel. Jane resists male authority at every stage -- from John Reed's bullying to Brocklehurst's humiliation to Rochester's attempted bigamy to St. John's cold command. Her famous declaration that women feel just as men feel is one of the most overtly feminist passages in Victorian fiction. Students should analyse how Bronte uses Jane's narrative voice to expose and critique patriarchal power.
