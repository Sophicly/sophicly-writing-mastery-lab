# Topic 1: Diagnostic — Disabled (Wilfred Owen)
**Type:** single
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Owen's war poem contrasts a young soldier's vibrant pre-war life with his bleak, dependent existence after losing his limbs. The shifting tenses and sensory imagery expose society's indifference to those it once celebrated, establishing your baseline for analysing how poets use voice, structure, and imagery to convey loss and disillusionment.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1, AO2

## Source A
**Title:** Disabled
**Author:** Wilfred Owen
**Context:** First World War poetry. Written in 1917 while Owen was recovering at Craiglockhart War Hospital, the poem presents a young soldier who has lost his limbs in battle and now sits in a wheelchair, ignored by society.

# Topic 3: "Out, Out—" (Robert Frost)
**Type:** single
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Frost's narrative poem recounts a boy's fatal accident with a buzz saw, using deceptively simple language and an abrupt ending to expose the fragility of life and the cold pragmatism of those who survive. Students should analyse how Frost's conversational tone, personification of the saw, and the final dismissive couplet create a disturbing tension between tragedy and indifference.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1, AO2

## Source A
**Title:** "Out, Out—"
**Author:** Robert Frost
**Context:** Early 20th century American poetry. The poem, published in 1916, was inspired by a real incident in which a young boy died after a saw accident in New England. The title alludes to Macbeth's soliloquy on the brevity and meaninglessness of life.

**Teaching Point:** Alvi's poem captures a moment of cultural connection and displacement as the speaker has her hand hennaed by an unknown girl at an Indian bazaar. # Topic 5: The Bright Lights of Sarajevo (Tony Harrison)
**Type:** single
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Harrison's poem juxtaposes the everyday rituals of young people in war-torn Sarajevo — queuing for bread, meeting for dates — against the constant threat of mortar fire. The contrast between normality and destruction, and the poet's careful observation of small human acts of resilience, reveals how life persists amid conflict. Students should analyse how Harrison uses imagery, structure, and contrast to convey both devastation and defiance.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1, AO2

## Source A
**Title:** The Bright Lights of Sarajevo
**Author:** Tony Harrison
**Context:** Contemporary poetry responding to the Siege of Sarajevo (1992–1996), the longest siege of a capital city in modern warfare. Harrison, a poet and dramatist known for exploring class and conflict, visited Sarajevo during the war and wrote from direct observation.

# Topic 6: Still I Rise (Maya Angelou)
**Type:** single
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Angelou's defiant poem uses a powerful, rhythmic voice to confront historical oppression and assert personal and collective resilience. The repeated refrain "I rise" builds cumulative force, while the imagery shifts from natural phenomena to bodily confidence, transforming pain into triumph. Students should analyse how Angelou's use of direct address, rhetorical questions, and rhythmic patterning creates an unshakeable tone of empowerment.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1, AO2

## Source A
**Title:** Still I Rise
**Author:** Maya Angelou
**Context:** African-American poetry. Published in 1978 in the collection *And Still I Rise*, the poem draws on Angelou's experience of racial and gender discrimination in the United States. It is both a personal declaration and a collective anthem of Black resilience.
