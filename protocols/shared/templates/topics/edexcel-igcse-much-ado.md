# Topic 1: Beatrice
**Type:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Beatrice is Shakespeare's most outspoken female character in the play. Students must explore how her wit masks deeper feelings, how her independence challenges Elizabethan gender norms, and how her fierce loyalty to Hero drives her most powerful moment in the play — the command to "Kill Claudio."
**Marks:** 25
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4

# Topic 3: Benedick
**Type:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Benedick begins as a self-proclaimed bachelor who mocks love and marriage. Students must trace his transformation from witty misogynist to devoted lover, exploring how the gulling scene punctures his pride and how his willingness to challenge Claudio reveals a deeper sense of honour than his early bravado suggests.
**Marks:** 25
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4

## Either — Extract Question

**Extract Location:** Act 2 Scene 3, lines 209-233
**Extract Context:** Benedick has just overheard Don Pedro, Leonato and Claudio discussing (as a trick) how deeply Beatrice loves him. Left alone, he reflects on what he has heard.

### Extract

> **BENEDICK**
> 1 &ensp; This can be no trick. The conference was sadly
> 2 &ensp; borne; they have the truth of this from Hero; they
> 3 &ensp; seem to pity the lady. It seems her affections have
> 4 &ensp; their full bent. Love me? Why, it must be requited.
> 5 &ensp; I hear how I am censured: they say I will bear
> 6 &ensp; myself proudly if I perceive the love come from her;
> 7 &ensp; they say too that she will rather die than give any
> 8 &ensp; sign of affection. I did never think to marry. I
> 9 &ensp; must not seem proud; happy are they that hear their
> 10 &ensp; detractions and can put them to mending. They say
> 11 &ensp; the lady is fair; 'tis a truth, I can bear them
> 12 &ensp; witness. And virtuous; 'tis so, I cannot reprove it.
> 13 &ensp; And wise, but for loving me; by my troth, it is no
> 14 &ensp; addition to her wit, nor no great argument of her
> 15 &ensp; folly, for I will be horribly in love with her. I
> 16 &ensp; may chance have some odd quirks and remnants of wit
> 17 &ensp; broken on me because I have railed so long against

> 22 &ensp; his humour? No! The world must be peopled. When I
> 23 &ensp; said I would die a bachelor, I did not think I
> 24 &ensp; should live till I were married.

# Topic 4: Love and Courtship
**Type:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Shakespeare presents two contrasting models of love: the conventional, idealised romance of Claudio and Hero, and the combative, intellectual courtship of Benedick and Beatrice. Students should explore how the play tests both models, showing that superficial love based on appearance is fragile while love grounded in honest exchange proves more durable.
**Marks:** 25
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4

## Either — Extract Question

**Extract Location:** Act 1 Scene 1, lines 156-179
**Extract Context:** The soldiers have returned from the wars. Claudio confides in Benedick that he has fallen in love with Hero.

# Topic 5: Deception and Manipulation
**Type:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Deception operates on every level of the play, from the benevolent gulling of Benedick and Beatrice to Don John's malicious plot against Hero. Students must distinguish between deception that leads to truth (the overhearing scenes) and deception that destroys it (the slander of Hero), exploring how Shakespeare uses parallel structures to examine whether the ends justify the means.
**Marks:** 25
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4

## Either — Extract Question

**Extract Location:** Act 3 Scene 1, lines 37-60
**Extract Context:** Hero and Ursula are performing a staged conversation in the orchard, knowing that Beatrice is hidden and listening. They discuss how deeply Benedick loves Beatrice.

### Extract

> **HERO**
> 1 &ensp; But Nature never framed a woman's heart
> 2 &ensp; Of prouder stuff than that of Beatrice.
> 3 &ensp; Disdain and scorn ride sparkling in her eyes,
> 4 &ensp; Misprising what they look on, and her wit
> 5 &ensp; Values itself so highly that to her
> 6 &ensp; All matter else seems weak. She cannot love,
> 7 &ensp; Nor take no shape nor project of affection,
> 8 &ensp; She is so self-endeared.
>
> **URSULA**
> 9 &ensp; Sure, I think so,
> 10 &ensp; And therefore certainly it were not good
> 11 &ensp; She knew his love, lest she make sport at it.
>
> **HERO**
> 12 &ensp; Why, you speak truth. I never yet saw man,
> 13 &ensp; How wise, how noble, young, how rarely featured,

> 15 &ensp; She would swear the gentleman should be her sister;
> 16 &ensp; If black, why, Nature, drawing of an antic,

> 19 &ensp; If speaking, why, a vane blown with all winds;
> 20 &ensp; If silent, why, a block moved with none.
> 21 &ensp; So turns she every man the wrong side out
> 22 &ensp; And never gives to truth and virtue that

# Topic 6: Honour and Reputation
**Type:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Honour in Messina is fragile and gendered: a woman's honour depends entirely on her sexual reputation, while a man's depends on his public standing and willingness to fight. Students must explore how the accusation against Hero exposes the destructive power of this code, and how Shakespeare critiques a society that would condemn an innocent woman on the word of a villain.
**Marks:** 25
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4

# Topic 7: Hero
**Type:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Hero is the play's most silenced character. She speaks little, obeys her father, and is publicly shamed for a crime she did not commit. Students should explore how Shakespeare uses Hero's passivity to expose the cruelty of a patriarchal system that treats women as property, and consider whether her silence is a sign of weakness or a different kind of strength.

# Topic 8: Don John
**Type:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Don John is the play's antagonist, a self-proclaimed villain who delights in causing misery. As a bastard son, he occupies a marginal social position that breeds resentment. Students should explore how Shakespeare uses Don John as a catalyst for the plot's darker turn, and consider how his villainy exposes the fragility of the social bonds in Messina that are so easily destroyed.
**Marks:** 25
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4

# Topic 9: Gender and Patriarchy
**Type:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Much Ado takes place in a world where men control women's destinies. Fathers choose husbands, a woman's value rests on her chastity, and even Beatrice must ask a man to act on her behalf when she demands justice for Hero. Students should explore how Shakespeare both reflects and questions Elizabethan patriarchal norms, particularly through the contrast between Beatrice's defiance and Hero's obedience.

# Topic 10: Appearance vs Reality
**Type:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Almost nothing in Messina is as it seems. Benevolent deceptions trick Benedick and Beatrice into love, while malicious deception destroys Hero's reputation. The masked ball, the staged window scene, and Hero's faked death all blur the line between appearance and reality. Students should explore how Shakespeare uses these layered deceptions to question whether truth can ever be reliably known.
**Marks:** 25
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
