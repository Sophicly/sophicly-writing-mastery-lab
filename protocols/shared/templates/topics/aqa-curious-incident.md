# Topic 1: Christopher
**Type:** A-MT
**Teaching Point:** Christopher Boone is the first-person narrator whose neurodivergent perspective shapes every aspect of the novel's form and content. Students should analyse how Haddon uses Christopher's literal thinking, his reliance on logic and mathematics, and his difficulty with emotions and social cues to explore what it means to see the world differently -- and how the reader is forced to reinterpret familiar human experiences through a narrator who finds them bewildering, frightening, or simply illogical.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

## Question
How does Haddon use the character of Christopher to explore ideas about neurodivergence, logic, and independence in *The Curious Incident of the Dog in the Night-Time*?

Write about:
- what Christopher says and does in the novel
- how Haddon presents Christopher.

[30 marks + 4 AO4]

# Topic 3: Ed Boone

# Topic 4: Truth and Lies
**Type:** A-MT
**Teaching Point:** The novel is structured around the uncovering of lies -- Christopher's investigation into Wellington's death leads him to the far more devastating truth about his mother. Students should analyse how Haddon uses Christopher's absolute commitment to truth (he cannot tell lies) as a contrast to the adult world's reliance on deception, and how the discovery of each lie propels the narrative forward while dismantling Christopher's sense of safety and trust.

# Topic 6: Isolation and Difference

# Topic 7: Judy Boone
**Type:** A-MT
**Teaching Point:** Judy Boone is the absent mother whose letters reveal a woman overwhelmed by the demands of caring for Christopher and trapped in a marriage that left her feeling inadequate. Students should analyse how Haddon uses the letters as a delayed narrative device -- Christopher and the reader discover them simultaneously -- to complicate simple judgements about abandonment, and how Judy's guilt, her genuine love, and her limitations as a parent explore the tension between what a family needs and what an individual can endure.

# Topic 8: Bravery and Independence
**Type:** A-MT
**Teaching Point:** Christopher's journey to London is the novel's climactic test of bravery -- a sequence in which every element of the ordinary world (crowds, noise, strangers, the Underground) becomes a genuine ordeal. Students should analyse how Haddon redefines bravery through Christopher's perspective, presenting it not as the absence of fear but as the determination to act despite being terrified, and how Christopher's growing independence -- solving the mystery, travelling alone, passing his A-level -- represents a profound personal achievement that the neurotypical world might undervalue.

# Topic 9: Perspective and Understanding
**Type:** A-MT
**Teaching Point:** The novel's entire narrative method is an exercise in perspective -- the reader must interpret the emotional world that Christopher describes but does not fully understand. Students should analyse how Haddon uses Christopher's literal, logical narration to create dramatic irony (the reader grasps truths that Christopher misses), and how the novel explores the limits of empathy and understanding between people who process the world in fundamentally different ways.

# Topic 10: Maths and Logic
**Type:** A-MT
**Teaching Point:** Mathematics is Christopher's safe space -- the domain where everything is provable, ordered, and free from the ambiguity of human emotion. Students should analyse how Haddon uses Christopher's mathematical digressions (prime number chapters, the Monty Hall problem, Conway's Soldiers) not as decorative quirks but as structural and thematic devices that reveal his character, provide him with a sense of control, and offer an alternative way of finding meaning and beauty in a world he often finds chaotic and frightening.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4
