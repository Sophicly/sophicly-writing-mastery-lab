# Topic 1: Digging
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Digging by Seamus Heaney explores the relationship between physical labour and creative work across three generations. Students should analyse how Heaney uses the sensory imagery of turf-cutting, the pen-as-spade metaphor, and the shift from admiration of his father and grandfather to the assertion of his own craft to explore identity, heritage, and the tension between continuity and change.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Digging
**Focus Poet:** Seamus Heaney

## Question
Compare how the poets present ideas about identity and heritage in 'Digging' and one other poem from the Identity collection.

# Topic 3: Follower
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Follower by Seamus Heaney explores the shifting dynamic between father and son as the child who once idolised and stumbled after his ploughman father now finds the roles reversed in adulthood. Students should analyse how Heaney uses the precise agricultural vocabulary, the regular quatrain form that mirrors the discipline of ploughing, and the ambiguous final stanza to explore admiration, inadequacy, and the painful reversal that ageing brings to family relationships.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Follower
**Focus Poet:** Seamus Heaney

## Question
Compare how the poets present changing relationships between generations in 'Follower' and one other poem from the Identity collection.

# Topic 4: Mid-Term Break

Students should analyse how Heaney uses the restrained, matter-of-fact tone, the reversal of expected roles where adults weep while the child remains composed, and the devastating final line to explore how grief and sudden loss reshape a young person's sense of self and family.

# Topic 6: Storm on the Island
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Storm on the Island by Seamus Heaney describes an island community bracing for a violent storm, but the poem operates on multiple levels as a meditation on fear, community resilience, and the political tensions of Northern Ireland concealed in the title. Students should analyse how Heaney uses the conversational first-person plural, the military vocabulary of bombardment, and the shift from collective confidence to vulnerability to explore how identity is tested when familiar certainties are stripped away.

# Topic 10: Dulce et Decorum Est
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Dulce et Decorum Est by Wilfred Owen dismantles the patriotic lie that it is sweet and fitting to die for one's country by confronting the reader with the horrifying reality of a gas attack in World War One. Students should analyse how Owen uses the exhausted, stumbling imagery of the opening, the nightmarish present-tense drowning, and the bitter direct address to the reader to challenge the ideals of duty and honour that shape young soldiers' identities.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Dulce et Decorum Est
**Focus Poet:** Wilfred Owen

## Question
Compare how the poets present the reality of conflict and its impact on individuals in 'Dulce et Decorum Est' and one other poem from the Identity collection.

# Topic 11: Death of a Naturalist
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Death of a Naturalist by Seamus Heaney charts the loss of childhood innocence through the speaker's changing perception of a flax-dam and its frogs. Students should analyse how Heaney uses the lush, celebratory sensory language of the first stanza, the sudden shift to threatening imagery in the second, and the contrast between the teacher's sanitised explanation and the visceral reality to explore how growing up involves disillusionment and a more complex understanding of the world.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
