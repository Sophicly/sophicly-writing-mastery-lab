# Topic 1: Napoleon
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Orwell presents Napoleon as the embodiment of totalitarian corruption, a figure whose seizure of power is all the more chilling for its quiet, methodical nature — expelling Snowball through trained dogs rather than winning any public argument. His progressive adoption of human habits, culminating in walking on two legs and sleeping in the farmhouse, enacts the novel's central irony: the revolutionary becomes the tyrant he displaced. Orwell's choice to model Napoleon on Stalin invites readers to see the novel as a precise historical allegory, but the character transcends the specific parallel to indict any leader who subordinates collective good to personal power. The manipulation of Squealer as propaganda mouthpiece reveals that Napoleon's authority depends not on force alone but on the systematic falsification of memory and truth.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question

- the methods and techniques Orwell uses to reveal Napoleon's corruption, including symbolism, irony, and allegory
- the significance of Napoleon in conveying Orwell's ideas about totalitarianism, leadership, and political betrayal.

# Topic 3: Snowball
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Snowball represents the idealistic, intellectually driven revolutionary who is ultimately destroyed not by his own failures but by the political cunning of those who fear his competence and popular appeal. His Windmill plan, which Napoleon first opposes then steals and rebrands, is Orwell's sharpest illustration of how authoritarian regimes appropriate and corrupt the visions of their opponents. After his expulsion, Snowball's transformation into the regime's scapegoat — blamed for every setback on the farm — demonstrates how totalitarian power requires a permanent enemy to deflect accountability. Orwell modelled Snowball on Trotsky, but his significance extends beyond the historical parallel to explore the vulnerability of genuine idealism against organised ruthlessness.

# Topic 4: Boxer
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Boxer's tragedy is Orwell's most emotionally potent argument against blind loyalty to authority: his two maxims — "I will work harder" and "Napoleon is always right" — embody a selfless dedication that is systematically exploited and finally rewarded with slaughter. His physical strength, the very quality that makes him the farm's most valuable worker, cannot protect him from the political system that reduces all value to utility. Orwell structures Boxer's story as an inversion of the fairy tale reward narrative: his years of superhuman labour earn him not the promised retirement pasture but a knacker's cart. The animals' inability to read the word "knacker" on the van is a devastating comment on the relationship between education, ignorance, and political vulnerability.

# Topic 5: Power and Corruption
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The novel's central thesis — that power corrupts, and that revolutionary ideals are among its earliest casualties — is enacted through the systematic revision of the Seven Commandments, each alteration made at night to erase the evidence of deviation. Orwell traces a precise arc from the exhilaration of Old Major's vision to the indistinguishable faces of pigs and men in the final scene, suggesting that the corruption of power is not accidental but structurally inevitable. The changing of "All animals are equal" to "but some animals are more equal than others" is the novel's most famous irony, compressing the entire critique of totalitarian doublespeak into a single grammatical contradiction. Orwell presents power not simply as corrupting individuals but as corrupting systems, language, and collective memory.

# Topic 6: Propaganda and Language
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Orwell makes language itself a site of political struggle: Squealer's rhetorical techniques — selective statistics, appeals to fear, the rewriting of history — demonstrate how propaganda functions not by inventing falsehoods wholesale but by manipulating memory and framing to make the false seem self-evident. The progressive simplification of the Commandments into "Four legs good, two legs bad" — and its eventual reversal — illustrates how slogans replace thought, leaving no cognitive space for resistance. Orwell's choice of the pig as the propaganda mouthpiece is precise: pigs are associated with intelligence in the novel, suggesting that it is the misuse of intellectual capacity, not ignorance, that makes propaganda possible. The animals' inability to remember Old Major's original speech mirrors the reader's own vulnerability to the slow accumulation of "official" versions of reality.

# Topic 7: The Commandments
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The Seven Commandments function as the novel's most explicit structural symbol, establishing the revolutionary contract and then serving as the document through which its betrayal is tracked — each alteration a precise marker of the pigs' moral regression. Orwell's decision to have the Commandments altered secretly, rather than publicly repealed, is central to his argument: the power of authoritarian revision lies in its invisibility, leaving the animals doubting their own memory rather than the text before them. The collapse of all seven into "All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others" is a formally brilliant act of compression, encoding the entire critique of Soviet communism in a single paradox. Benjamin's literacy — his ability to read but unwillingness to act — introduces a further layer of moral complexity about the relationship between knowledge and resistance.

# Topic 8: Class and Inequality
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Orwell presents class inequality as the revolution's most persistent failure: despite Old Major's vision of universal animal equality, hierarchies re-emerge almost immediately, with pigs claiming intellectual superiority to justify material privilege. The allocation of resources — milk, apples, farmhouse bedrooms — follows a class logic that mirrors the human society the animals overthrew, exposing the contradiction between revolutionary rhetoric and structural self-interest. Orwell depicts the working animals as complicit in their own oppression through a combination of limited education, short memory, and the psychological comfort of simple loyalty. The final image of pigs and men indistinguishable at the card table literalises the novel's central irony: the oppressed have become their oppressors, and class inequality has simply changed its costume.

# Topic 9: Revolution and Betrayal
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The novel's structure enacts the classic revolutionary arc of liberation, consolidation, and betrayal, compressed into a barnyard allegory that makes the political pattern newly visible to readers who might overlook it in historical or journalistic form. Old Major's speech establishes a utopian vision whose internal contradictions — who enforces equality? who decides who leads? — are never addressed, and it is into these gaps that Napoleon's authoritarianism flows. Orwell presents the betrayal of the revolution not as a sudden dramatic reversal but as a series of small accommodations, each individually defensible, that cumulatively constitute a complete transformation. The song "Beasts of England" is suppressed once the revolution is complete, removing the very language of aspiration that made the rebellion possible — a detail Orwell uses to argue that authoritarian regimes fear imagination as much as opposition.

# Topic 10: Education and Ignorance
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Orwell presents the animals' varying degrees of literacy and education as a direct determinant of their political vulnerability: the pigs' monopoly on reading and writing is the practical mechanism through which they maintain ideological control. The sheep, who can only bleat slogans, and Boxer, who cannot read past the letter D, are the novel's most politically helpless characters — not because they lack moral quality but because they lack the intellectual tools to interrogate what they are told. Benjamin, the donkey who can read but refuses to engage, represents a different and perhaps more damning form of political failure: the cynicism that enables injustice through disengagement. Orwell's allegorical argument is systemic as much as individual: when the powerful control education, ignorance becomes not a personal failing but a political weapon.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
