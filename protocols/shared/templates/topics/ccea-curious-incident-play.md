# Topic 1: Christopher
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Stephens's adaptation presents Christopher through a theatrical language that enacts his experience rather than merely representing it — the ensemble cast who physically embody the world he navigates, the precise lighting and sound design that externalises sensory experience, and his direct address to the audience all create a dramatic form shaped by Christopher's way of perceiving. Christopher's reliability as a narrator is both his strength and the play's central dramatic irony: he observes facts with extraordinary precision while misreading emotional registers that other characters take for granted, creating a gap between what he reports and what the audience understands that Stephens uses to generate simultaneous comedy and pathos. His journey to London — a journey of terrifying complexity for him, trivially routine for most of the other characters — is staged with physical theatre that makes the audience inhabit the experience of sensory overwhelm rather than observe it from outside. The play ultimately argues that Christopher's "difference" is a difference of perception, not of moral worth.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 3: Ed Boone
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Ed Boone is presented as one of theatre's most complex and empathetic portraits of a parent overwhelmed by circumstances beyond his preparation or capacity — his love for Christopher is evident and genuine, yet it produces the lie that drives the plot's crisis. Stephens (following Haddon) presents Ed's decision to conceal Judy's letters not as cruelty but as a desperate attempt to manage an impossible situation, giving the audience enough access to Ed's emotional world to understand his choices while maintaining Christopher's betrayal as real and significant. The physical staging of their relationship — the challenge of touch for Christopher, the careful negotiations of contact — creates theatrical moments of great tenderness, particularly the "holding hands" gesture (touching fingers rather than clasping) that becomes their non-verbal language of connection. Ed's emotional breakdown in Christopher's presence is staged in ways that make his grief simultaneously visible and incomprehensible to the character most affected by it.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 4: Judy Boone
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Judy Boone's role in the play is mediated through absence — she exists primarily as the subject of Ed's lie, as the author of the sequentially revealed letters, and eventually as the person Christopher travels to find — which makes Stephens's theatrical challenge one of creating a fully realised character out of this structural deferral. Her letters, which Christopher reads aloud on stage, are given their own theatrical rhythm and emotional register, functioning as a kind of direct address that creates intimacy despite physical distance. Judy's acknowledgement that she found Christopher "too difficult" is presented without moral condemnation — Stephens follows Haddon in treating this as a painful honesty rather than an indictment — and her ongoing commitment to Christopher after his arrival in London demonstrates that her departure was not abandonment but self-preservation. Her character asks the audience to sit with the uncomfortable reality that love and capacity are not always commensurate.

# Topic 5: Truth and Lies
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Truth is Christopher's foundational value — his hatred of lying is not merely a preference but a logical position rooted in his need for a reliable, predictable world, and the play explores what happens when the world is structured around the comfortable lies that most people accept without examining. Ed's central lie destroys Christopher's sense of safe ground, triggering the play's crisis, but Stephens embeds it within a wider examination of the social function of untruth: the "white lies" characters exchange throughout the play are shown to be protective rather than manipulative, yet they operate on exactly the same mechanism as Ed's deception. Stephens uses the theatrical device of Christopher's book-within-a-play to frame the whole action as a quest for truth — Christopher is writing the story the audience is watching, making his investigation of what happened to Wellington an act of epistemological commitment as much as detective curiosity. The play asks whether a world structured entirely around Christopher's demand for truth would be better or just differently limited.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 6: Difference and Neurodiversity
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Stephens's adaptation is distinctive in its refusal to present Christopher's neurodiversity as tragedy or as obstacle to be overcome — the theatrical form of the production gives Christopher's perceptions the status of reality, so that his experience of the world is not rendered as deficiency but as a different but equally valid mode of being. The stage is empty, the ensemble embody multiple characters, and information appears through light, sound, and physical theatre rather than realistic scenery — all of which creates a theatrical world modelled on Christopher's perceptions rather than on neurotypical theatrical convention. Stephens and the National Theatre production (directed by Marianne Elliott) made the radical decision that accessibility into Christopher's world should come through theatrical form rather than narrative condescension, and this choice transforms the audience's relationship from sympathetic observer to experiential participant. The play argues implicitly that the challenge is not Christopher's difference but the world's inflexibility in accommodating it.

# Topic 7: Independence and Growth
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Christopher's journey to London is staged as an act of genuinely extraordinary bravery, with the ensemble cast physically embodying the chaos and sensory overwhelm of Paddington Station in a theatrical sequence that makes the mundane terrifying and Christopher's navigation of it genuinely heroic. Stephens presents Christopher's independence not as a linear developmental arc but as a series of hard-won, specific achievements — each tube line managed, each stranger asked for help, each system decoded — that accumulate into a journey of real magnitude. The play is careful to show independence not as liberation from difference but as competence developed within it: Christopher doesn't stop being overwhelmed by complexity, he develops strategies for functioning despite it. His achievement of an A grade in his A-level — announced at the play's end with his characteristic mathematical confidence — is Stephens's statement about what extraordinary achievement looks like when it is measured against the actual challenge, not the conventional benchmark.

# Topic 8: Staging and Theatrical Techniques
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Stephens's adaptation is inseparable from its theatrical form — the decision to adapt Haddon's novel for the stage required solving the problem of how to represent a first-person, interiority-heavy narrative in a public, three-dimensional medium, and Stephens's solution is to use the theatre's own machinery to embody Christopher's perceptions. The black box stage with its illuminated grid suggests the logical, mathematical ordering system through which Christopher processes experience; the ensemble's fluid transitions between characters, objects, and environment externalise Christopher's capacity to perceive and process multiple streams of information simultaneously. Direct address to the audience collapses the conventional theatrical separation between narrator and witness, making the audience Christopher's collaborators in the investigation rather than observers of it. Specific staging choices — the map of the London Underground projected above the stage, the choreographed chaos of Paddington — are not decorative but functional, doing work that a novel can do through prose interiority and that a play must accomplish through visible, physical means.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 9: Trust and Relationships
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Trust in the play is presented as Christopher's most precious and most fragile resource — his need for predictability and honesty makes trust much more demanding to establish and much more catastrophically damaged when broken than it is for most people. The relationship with Ed is the play's central study in trust's fragility: built through years of careful accommodation, destroyed by a single discovered lie, and left in a state of uncertain repair at the play's end. Stephens presents the repair process without false optimism — Ed's promise to rebuild Christopher's trust is accepted but unproven, and the play ends with achievement rather than restored security. The other relationships in Christopher's life — with Siobhan, with neighbours, with strangers on the journey — map different registers of trust: professional, neighbourly, transactional, and each is rendered with specific theatrical detail that shows Christopher's calibration of safety within every interaction. Trust, in the play's terms, is not a feeling but a calculated assessment.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 10: Bravery and the Unknown
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The play redefines bravery in terms specific to Christopher's experience — the courage required to navigate Paddington Station is of a different order from conventional physical heroism, but Stephens's staging makes its demands viscerally legible to an audience who would otherwise have no access to that experience. The theatrical rendering of sensory overwhelm — overlapping voices, strobing lights, the ensemble closing in — physically enacts what Christopher must face and manage every time he encounters an unfamiliar, complex environment, transforming the audience into temporary inhabitants of his challenge. Stephens presents bravery as action in the face of one's specific fear, not in the face of a universally recognised danger, and Christopher's particular fears (strangers, disorder, the unexpected) are given their full theatrical weight. The final image — Christopher's confidence in the mathematical truth that 2 to the power of any number grows without limit — frames his future as an expansion into the unknown, grounded not in fearlessness but in the discovery that he can function despite fear.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
