# Topic 5: Mrs Birling — Class Prejudice
**Type:** A-MT
**Teaching Point:** Mrs Birling represents the entrenched class prejudice of the upper middle class. Her refusal to help Eva at the Brumley Women's Charity Organisation — purely because Eva used the Birling name — demonstrates how class snobbery can have fatal consequences, and her inability to recognise her own guilt makes her Priestley's most damning portrait of wilful ignorance.

# Topic 9: Eva Smith — Exploitation
**Type:** A-MT
**Teaching Point:** Eva Smith is never seen on stage, yet she is the moral centre of the play. Priestley constructs her as a representative of the entire exploited working class — sacked for asking for fair wages, dismissed for a jealous whim, used and abandoned by men with power. Her absence forces the audience to see how easily the vulnerable are erased by those in authority.

# Topic 10: Class and Social Hierarchy
**Type:** A-MT
**Teaching Point:** Class division is the structural backbone of the play. Priestley sets the action in 1912 — a period of rigid social hierarchy — but writes for a 1945 audience who had lived through two wars that temporarily dissolved class barriers. Every character's treatment of Eva maps directly onto their position in the class system, making the play an argument that hierarchy itself enables cruelty.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4
