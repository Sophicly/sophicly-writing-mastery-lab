# Topic 1: Analysing Imagery and Metaphor
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 24
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** Imagery and metaphor are the primary means by which poetry compresses meaning, and analytical responses must move beyond identifying the device to explaining the specific conceptual work it performs in its context. Extended metaphors create structural coherence across a poem; tracking their development — and any point where they break or shift — reveals how the poet's argument evolves. When analysing a metaphor, ask what two domains are being linked, what qualities transfer from the vehicle to the tenor, and whether the comparison illuminates, distorts, or ironises its subject. The most sophisticated responses notice the gap between what a metaphor claims and what the surrounding poem complicates — this tension is where meaning is most richly concentrated.

# Topic 2: Conceptual Notes
**Type:** conceptual-notes
**Teaching Point:** AQA Unseen Poetry (Paper 2, Section C) asks students to analyse a single unseen poem for up to 24 marks (AO1 and AO2), then compare it with a second unseen poem for up to 8 marks. The skills being assessed are transferable analytical capacities, not knowledge of specific texts: close reading of language, structure, and form; the ability to sustain a coherent personal interpretation; and precise use of technical vocabulary in the service of argument rather than as an end in itself. A strong response constructs a thesis about the poem's central effect or concern and then tests that thesis against specific textual evidence, remaining willing to modify the argument in response to the poem's complexity. Students should be taught to begin with the poem's overall impression before drilling into specific lines, so that local analysis is always anchored to a coherent reading of the whole.

# Topic 3: Analysing Structure and Form
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 24
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** Structure and form operate at multiple simultaneous levels: the macro-structure of how the poem moves from opening to close, the meso-structure of stanza organisation and line grouping, and the micro-structure of individual line breaks and enjambment. Each level makes meaning, and the most revealing analyses attend to all three, showing how they reinforce or work against each other. Line breaks are the poet's most powerful structural tool: a break mid-phrase creates suspension, expectation, or irony depending on what follows, and students should be taught to read the line ending and the line opening as a pair. Form choices — sonnet, free verse, dramatic monologue — signal a conversation with tradition that is itself part of the poem's meaning; a sonnet that refuses to resolve its volta, or free verse that suddenly rhymes, is making an argument through formal deviation.

# Topic 4: Analysing Tone and Mood
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 24
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** Tone is the speaker's attitude toward the subject; mood is the emotional atmosphere created for the reader — the two are related but analytically distinct, and conflating them reduces the precision of a response. Tone is constructed through diction, syntax, and address: the specific word choices the poet makes, the grammatical structures they prefer, and who or what the speaker appears to be talking to. Mood is built cumulatively through image patterns, sound textures, and pacing, and may shift significantly across a poem as the speaker's stance evolves. A strong analytical approach tracks tonal shifts — from reverence to irony, from intimacy to distance — and asks what motivates each shift and what the movement between tones reveals about the poem's central argument.

# Topic 5: Exploring Attitudes and Perspectives
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 24
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** Every poem is spoken from a position — a perspective shaped by the speaker's relationship to the subject, their social location, their emotional state, and the gap (if any) between what they say and what the poem implies they mean. Identifying that position precisely is the foundation of attitude analysis: a poem about loss spoken by someone in denial produces very different effects from the same subject spoken by someone in acceptance. Students should distinguish carefully between the poet, the speaker, and any persona the speaker adopts, since the distance between these positions is itself a source of meaning. Attitudes are often most revealing when they are contradictory or unstable — when the speaker claims one thing but the imagery implies another — and the most sophisticated analyses locate these tensions rather than resolving them prematurely.

# Topic 6: Analysing Sound and Rhythm
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 24
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** Sound devices — rhyme, half-rhyme, assonance, consonance, alliteration, onomatopoeia — are not decorative but semantic: they create aural patterns that direct the reader's attention, generate expectation, and produce effects of harmony or dissonance that reinforce or undercut the poem's stated meaning. Rhythm works similarly, with metrical regularity suggesting control, predictability, or constraint, while metrical variation — a sudden feminine ending, an extra unstressed syllable — signals disturbance or release. Students should be taught to read poems aloud in analysis, because the physical experience of speaking the sounds often reveals effects invisible to the eye. The key analytical move is always to link the sonic texture to the poem's emotional or intellectual content: not just "this is sibilant" but "the sustained sibilance creates a hissing undertone that ironises the speaker's claims of tenderness."

# Topic 7: Comparing Two Unseen Poems
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 24
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** Comparison is an analytical skill distinct from description, and the central requirement is to move beyond alternating commentary ("Poem A does X, Poem B does Y") to integrated analysis that articulates the significance of similarity and difference. The most productive comparisons identify a point of genuine tension between the two poems — where their approaches diverge in ways that illuminate something about the subject each is treating — rather than listing superficial parallels. Structuring a comparison around shared concerns (both poems address loss; both use extended metaphor) but divergent methods allows students to use the contrast to make claims about what each method achieves or forecloses.

# Topic 8: Language and Word Choice
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 24
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** Diction analysis at its most precise attends to the specific register from which a word is drawn — latinate or Anglo-Saxon, formal or colloquial, archaic or contemporary — because register carries social and historical meaning that inflects the poem's argument. A single unexpected word choice in an otherwise consistent register constitutes a micro-deviation that is never accidental: it is where the poet's argument is most concentrated. Students should be trained to ask not just what a word denotes but what it connotes, and which of its connotations the poetic context activates, suppresses, or ironises.

# Topic 9: Context and Inference
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 24
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** Inference is the active process by which readers construct meaning from what is implied rather than stated, and poetry depends on this process more intensively than any other literary form. A poem's meaning is rarely fully explicit; it is built in the gaps between what is said and what the surrounding context implies, between the speaker's apparent confidence and the poem's structural hesitations. Students should be taught to distinguish between what the poem states, what it implies, and what it withholds — and to recognise that withholding is itself a technique, creating spaces the reader must fill.

# Topic 10: Personal Response and Interpretation
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Marks:** 24
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Teaching Point:** A personal response in a formal analytical context is not a record of emotional reaction but a reasoned interpretation: a claim about what the poem means or does, supported by evidence and expressed with sufficient specificity to be debatable. The examiner is assessing whether the student can construct and sustain an interpretation, not whether they have been moved by the poem, so the analytical discipline of the response must frame and control the personal engagement. The most sophisticated personal responses acknowledge the poem's resistance to single readings and present their interpretation as one of several possible, arguing for its credibility rather than asserting it as the only conclusion.
