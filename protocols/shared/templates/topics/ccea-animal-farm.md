# Topic 1: Napoleon
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Orwell presents Napoleon as a study in how revolutionary idealism is hollowed out by the pursuit of personal power — a figure whose methods (silence, violence, the manipulation of information) mirror those of Stalin with satirical precision. Napoleon says almost nothing in the early chapters, and Orwell uses this silence strategically: while Snowball argues and persuades, Napoleon accumulates power through the education of the dogs, the institution of fear, and the corruption of the pigs. His gradual appropriation of Snowball's ideas — including the windmill he originally opposed — shows a politician who understands that what is done matters less than who is seen to have initiated it. By the novel's end, Napoleon's transformation into a two-legged, whisky-drinking figure indistinguishable from a human farmer completes Orwell's central satirical argument: the revolution has been entirely betrayed.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does George Orwell present Napoleon in *Animal Farm?*

You should consider:
- how Napoleon acquires and maintains his power on the farm
- how Napoleon changes through the course of the novel
- how Orwell uses Napoleon to satirise political tyranny and the corruption of revolution.

# Topic 3: Snowball
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Snowball is presented as the revolutionary idealist — energetic, articulate, and genuinely committed to the principles of Animalism — whose elimination is a necessary precondition for Napoleon's dictatorship. Orwell gives Snowball the novel's most persuasive rhetoric and most beneficial plans (the windmill, the defence committees) to make clear what is lost when he is driven out. After his expulsion, Snowball is progressively transformed by Napoleon's propaganda into a scapegoat for all failures and a justification for all repression — a process that enacts Orwell's argument about how totalitarian regimes manufacture enemies. The uncertainty about whether Snowball was a traitor from the beginning — the revision of history that Squealer performs so successfully — is one of the novel's most disturbing insights into how memory and truth are controlled.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does George Orwell present Snowball in *Animal Farm?*

You should consider:
- how Orwell establishes Snowball's character and his role in the early revolution
- how Snowball's treatment by Napoleon and the pigs develops the novel's themes
- how Snowball's absence continues to shape events after his expulsion.

# Topic 4: Boxer
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Boxer is presented with genuine affection and equal pathos — his loyalty, his physical strength, and his two maxims ("I will work harder" and "Napoleon is always right") making him simultaneously the revolution's greatest asset and its most exploited victim. Orwell uses Boxer to embody the working-class virtues of diligence and solidarity that the revolution claims to serve, before showing exactly how those virtues are cynically exploited by a ruling class that treats labour as a commodity. The scene of Boxer's removal in the knacker's van — and Squealer's brazen lie about a hospital — is the novel's emotional climax, the moment at which the pigs' betrayal is most nakedly exposed. Boxer's inability to read or to question — "Napoleon is always right" — is both his tragedy and Orwell's warning about the political dangers of deferred authority.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 5: Power and Corruption
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** *Animal Farm* is fundamentally a study in the mechanics of power's corruption — how the principles of equality that motivate revolution are gradually replaced by the privileges of a new ruling class that reproduces the injustices it claims to have overthrown. Orwell structures this corruption as a series of small, each individually deniable, revisions to the Commandments, so that the animals' inability to remember the originals becomes the instrument of their own oppression. The famous final revision — "All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others" — captures in a single logical absurdity the full extent of the ideological betrayal. By ending with the pigs and humans indistinguishable at the card table, Orwell argues that the problem is not individual corruption but the structural tendency of concentrated power to reproduce exploitation regardless of the ideology that initially justified it.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question

- how Orwell shows power being used to exploit and deceive the other animals
- how Orwell uses language and structure to trace the stages of the revolution's corruption.

# Topic 6: Propaganda and Language
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Orwell, himself a writer acutely conscious of language's political uses, makes the control of language and narrative the pigs' primary instrument of domination. Squealer is the novel's embodiment of propaganda: his rhetorical techniques — false statistics, appeals to fear, the rewriting of history — parody the language of political apology with devastating precision. The revision of the Commandments, performed silently and denied when challenged, dramatises Orwell's argument in *Politics and the English Language* that the corruption of language enables the corruption of thought. By making the sheep's bleat ("Four legs good, two legs bad" → "Four legs good, two legs better") the novel's emblem of mass political manipulation, Orwell shows how simple slogans overwhelm complex thought — and how easily the powerless participate in their own subjugation.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 7: The Commandments
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The Seven Commandments function in *Animal Farm* as both the revolution's founding charter and the primary instrument through which its betrayal is enacted — their progressive alteration marking the stages of the pigs' ideological corruption with ironic precision. Orwell uses the physical act of revision — painting over old letters in the barn at night — to suggest that the rewriting of principle is furtive yet deniable, relying on the community's imperfect memory and the pigs' monopoly on literacy. Each alteration is logically consistent with the pigs' self-serving argument — "surely, comrades, you do not want Jones to come back?" — showing how ideological accommodation is always rationalised. The compression of all seven commandments into one final, self-contradictory maxim is Orwell's most economical satirical move, reducing the entire arc of the revolution to a single paradox.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 8: Class and Inequality
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** While *Animal Farm* is primarily a satire on Soviet Communism, its class critique has broader application — Orwell shows how inequality is reproduced even by systems explicitly designed to abolish it, because those who control the means of production (and in the novel, the means of communication) inevitably use that control to advantage themselves. The early utopian equality of the revolution — equal food, equal labour, equal rest — is progressively dismantled as the pigs claim special entitlements on the grounds of mental work, and the other animals lack both the literacy and the organised power to challenge them. Moses the raven's return with his tales of Sugarcandy Mountain shows how religious consolation is tolerated by rulers when it pacifies the workforce and makes present inequality bearable. Orwell's animal hierarchy — pigs at the top, horses in the middle, hens and sheep at the base — deliberately echoes human class structures, making his satirical argument universally applicable.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 9: Revolution and Betrayal
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Orwell structures *Animal Farm* as a parable of revolution's self-betrayal — the ideals of equality and liberation that motivate the uprising against Jones are not merely abandoned but actively inverted, so that the animals' lives end worse than they began. The revolution itself is presented with genuine excitement and idealism: Old Major's speech, the expulsion of Jones, the early harvest conducted without human supervision all carry a utopian energy that makes the subsequent corruption all the more devastating. Orwell is careful to show the betrayal as gradual and incremental rather than a single decisive act, suggesting that revolution is not betrayed by a sudden coup but by the accumulation of small compromises and the exploitation of the revolutionary majority's trust. The ending — the animals unable to distinguish pig from man — literalises the complete cycle: a revolution that has reproduced exactly what it overthrew.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question

You should consider:

# Topic 10: Education and Ignorance
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Orwell presents education — specifically literacy and the ability to think critically — as the precondition for political freedom, making the animals' varying degrees of literacy a direct map of their vulnerability to exploitation. The pigs' monopoly on reading and writing is the foundation of their power: they can revise the Commandments, produce statistics, and rewrite history precisely because the other animals cannot verify their claims. Boxer's inability to learn the alphabet beyond the letter D is presented not as comic but as tragic — the intellectual limitation that makes his loyalty fatal. Orwell's critique is aimed not only at deliberately withheld education but at the self-imposed intellectual submission of animals like the sheep, who choose the simplicity of a slogan over the difficulty of understanding — a warning about the political cost of intellectual passivity.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
