# Topic 1: Enid
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Pinnock presents Enid as a woman caught between the cultural world she has tried to assimilate into and the Caribbean heritage she has suppressed in the attempt — her brittleness, her rigidity about English propriety, and her fraught relationship with her daughters are all products of the survival strategies she adopted as an immigrant. Her dismissal of Mai's spirituality and her insistence on her daughters' educational conformity reveal the internalised values of a host culture that has never fully accepted her, making Enid a figure who has paid the costs of assimilation without receiving its promised rewards. Pinnock's dialogue for Enid is often clipped and evasive, making her emotional life legible through what she cannot say rather than what she does — a technique that positions the audience to read her silences as the damage of a life spent managing other people's expectations of who she should be. Her eventual emotional opening is all the more powerful for being so long deferred.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 3: Del
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Del is presented as the daughter who has most fully absorbed English cultural norms — aspirational, academically focused, and uncomfortable with the Caribbean elements of her heritage that she associates with backwardness or embarrassment. Her relationship with her Jamaican background is primarily one of distance and denial, and Pinnock uses her trajectory to explore the psychological cost of second-generation assimilation: Del has adopted the dominant culture's values so thoroughly that she risks losing the capacity to understand her own family. Her visit to Mai — initially reluctant — becomes a pivotal encounter in which she begins to engage with the spiritual and cultural inheritance she has been taught to dismiss. Del's arc suggests that the recovery of cultural identity is possible, but requires the willingness to value what the dominant culture has systematically devalued.

# Topic 4: Viv
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Viv is presented as the more emotionally volatile and culturally conflicted of the two sisters — her difficulties at school and her resistance to Enid's plans for her reflect a deeper alienation from both the English world her mother is trying to enter and the Caribbean world she has been disconnected from. Where Del's response to cultural displacement is achievement and conformity, Viv's is rebellion and emotional intensity, and Pinnock uses the contrast between the sisters to explore how the same circumstances produce different survival strategies. Viv's connection to Mai is more instinctive than Del's, suggesting an openness to cultural inheritance that her mother's assimilationist agenda has not entirely closed off. Her storyline engages directly with the emotional costs of being caught between cultures in a society that offers no framework for holding both simultaneously.

# Topic 5: Mai
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Mai is the play's most theatrically distinctive figure — a Cuban-born woman who practices obeah (Caribbean spiritual healing) in contemporary London, and whose presence insists on the validity of cultural and spiritual systems that the dominant culture dismisses as superstition. Pinnock uses Mai to represent the persistence of Caribbean cultural identity in the face of assimilationist pressure: she has not abandoned her practices to fit in, and her authority over the characters who seek her help suggests that what she offers meets a real need that English society cannot. Her scenes have a ritual, heightened quality that distinguishes them from the domestic realism of the play's other settings, and Pinnock uses this theatrical difference to ask the audience to consider what frameworks of meaning are being lost when immigrant communities are pressured to abandon their cultural inheritance. Mai also functions as a link to the ancestral past that Enid has tried to sever.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 6: Cultural Identity
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** *Leave Taking* is fundamentally about the crisis of cultural identity experienced by a Caribbean immigrant family in Britain — the pressure to abandon cultural heritage as the price of social acceptance, and the psychological and relational damage this pressure inflicts. Pinnock presents cultural identity not as a fixed inheritance but as something that must be actively claimed or chosen, particularly by the second generation for whom it is mediated by a mother's own conflicted relationship with it. The play suggests that cultural displacement creates a kind of homelessness — not belonging fully to the country of origin (Del and Viv have never lived there) or to the country of settlement (Britain has not accepted the family as belonging) — that requires a conscious act of cultural recovery. Pinnock's theatrical use of Caribbean ritual, language, and music alongside British domestic realism performs this dual inheritance on stage, suggesting that both can be held simultaneously.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 7: Mother-Daughter Relationships
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The mother-daughter relationships at the heart of *Leave Taking* are presented by Pinnock as sites of both deep connection and painful miscommunication — Enid loves her daughters fiercely, but her love is expressed through a programme of assimilation that her daughters experience as the suppression of who they are. The different relationships Enid has with Del (who fulfils her ambitions) and Viv (who resists them) allow Pinnock to explore how a parent's own unresolved relationship with cultural identity is transmitted and transformed across generations. Mai's relationship with her own absent daughter adds a counterpoint: a woman who has maintained her cultural identity at the cost of separation from her child, raising the question of whether cultural authenticity and maternal connection can be fully reconciled. Pinnock presents the repair of these relationships as dependent on honesty about the immigrant experience rather than continued performance of assimilation.

# Topic 8: The Immigrant Experience
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Pinnock's portrayal of the immigrant experience in *Leave Taking* is notable for its refusal of both victimhood and simple celebration — Enid's life in Britain has been one of sustained effort, strategic self-reinvention, and survival in a society that has accepted her labour without accepting her person. The play is set against the backdrop of the Windrush generation's experiences of racism, limited housing, and restricted opportunity, and Pinnock embeds these social realities in the characters' material circumstances and emotional lives rather than foregrounding them as explicit political statement. The "leave taking" of the title has multiple resonances — leaving the Caribbean homeland, leaving the old self behind, and eventually the possibility of leaving the false self that assimilation has required — all of which map the immigrant journey as a process of repeated loss and difficult renegotiation. Pinnock gives Enid's story a specifically gendered dimension: as a Black woman, she navigates both racial and gender hierarchies simultaneously.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 9: Spirituality and Tradition
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Spirituality in *Leave Taking* is presented through Mai's practice of obeah as a living cultural system with real efficacy rather than superstition to be condescended to — and Pinnock's theatrical treatment of these scenes gives them a ritual authority that challenges the dominant culture's rationalist framework. The conflict between Mai's spiritual worldview and Enid's adopted English secular values is one of the play's central tensions, representing a broader conflict between inherited Caribbean tradition and the cultural norms that define respectability in Britain. Pinnock uses the younger generation's reluctant engagement with Mai to suggest that tradition persists even when actively suppressed — that it exerts a claim on people who have been taught to dismiss it. The play ultimately presents the recovery of spiritual and cultural tradition not as regression but as a necessary component of psychological wholeness for characters who have been fragmented by the experience of displacement.

# Topic 10: Generational Conflict
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The generational conflict in *Leave Taking* is inseparable from the cultural displacement that generates it — Enid's daughters rebel not simply against parental authority but against a cultural programme of assimilation that they are expected to continue without understanding its cost to Enid. Pinnock presents the conflict between Enid and her daughters as a communication failure rooted in the things Enid cannot tell them: the sacrifices she has made, the shame and loss she has internalised, the version of herself she has suppressed. The daughters' connection to Mai represents a generational renegotiation — a reaching back past the first generation's compromise to a cultural heritage that was never meant to be abandoned. Pinnock suggests that generational conflict in immigrant families is distinctive in its stakes: what is at issue is not merely household rules but the terms on which a cultural identity survives into the future.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
