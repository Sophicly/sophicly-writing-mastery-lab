# Topic 1: Atticus Finch
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Atticus Finch is Lee's most sustained portrait of principled moral courage, a man who defends Tom Robinson not because he expects to win but because the alternative — allowing a known injustice to proceed unchallenged — would make him complicit in it. Lee presents his calm, methodical courtroom demeanour as itself a form of moral argument: he speaks to the jury as rational adults capable of overriding prejudice, an appeal that fails legally but succeeds in exposing the gap between American ideals and American practice. His parenting of Scout and Jem is inseparable from his wider ethical philosophy — "you never really understand a person until you consider things from his point of view" — making empathy both a private and a civic value in the novel. The tension between Atticus's faith in the law and the law's failure to protect Tom Robinson is Lee's central structural irony, unresolved at the novel's end.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 3: Scout
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Scout's first-person narrative voice is central to the novel's moral architecture: her childhood perspective allows Lee to present the injustices of Maycomb with a fresh, unfiltered clarity that exposes adult society's rationalisations for what they are. The gap between what Scout observes and what she fully understands generates much of the novel's irony, as she reports conversations and events whose full significance is visible to the reader before it is to her. Her journey from naive child to empathetic young adult is the novel's bildungsroman arc, and Lee marks its key stages through Scout's changing relationship with figures she initially misunderstands — Boo Radley, Calpurnia, and even the Cunninghams. Lee uses Scout's gender, as well as her age, to complicate the novel's social picture: Scout resists the femininity Maycomb's women prescribe for her, making her development a challenge to gender norms as well as racial ones.

# Topic 4: Boo Radley
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Boo Radley functions as the novel's most complex symbol of the gap between rumour and reality, of the way fear and ignorance construct narratives around those who are different or hidden. Lee's gradual revelation of Boo's acts of silent kindness — the gifts in the knothole, the blanket around Scout's shoulders, the mending of Jem's trousers — works structurally against the children's Gothic imaginings, replacing each superstition with a quiet, generous reality. His emergence at the novel's climax to save Scout and Jem is Lee's most dramatic structural reversal, as the figure who embodied childhood fear becomes the most powerful protector in the novel. Heck Tate's decision to cover up Boo's killing of Bob Ewell is presented as an act of social mercy — protecting a fragile, reclusive man from unwanted public exposure — raising complex questions about when law should yield to compassion.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 5: Tom Robinson
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Tom Robinson's trial and death constitute Lee's most direct engagement with the racial injustice of the American South, and his characterisation is carefully constructed to make the injustice undeniable: he is courteous, hardworking, and his physical disability — his left arm was caught in a cotton gin — makes Mayella's accusation logically impossible. Yet Lee also acknowledges the limitations of her own narrative: Tom is seen almost entirely through white eyes, his interiority filtered through Scout's limited perspective, making him a symbol of injustice as much as a fully realised individual. His conviction despite Atticus's clear demolition of the prosecution's case is the novel's most shattering legal moment, demonstrating that in Maycomb's court a black man's truth cannot outweigh a white man's lie. Tom's death — shot seventeen times "trying to escape" — completes the injustice and forces the reader to confront the lethal consequences of structural racism.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 6: Racial Injustice
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Lee embeds racial injustice in every layer of Maycomb's social fabric, from the physical segregation of the courthouse gallery to the unspoken rule that a black man's word can never stand against a white woman's — a convention so powerful it overrides physical evidence and legal argument. The trial of Tom Robinson is the novel's formal centre, but Lee ensures that racial injustice is also visible in the smaller textures of daily life: Calpurnia's code-switching between Black and white communities, the condescension of the missionary circle women, Scout's questions about why Walter Cunningham looks "the way he does." Lee's historical setting — 1930s Alabama — is chosen to make the racial hierarchy feel both historically specific and, through Scout's child perspective, newly scandalous to a reader who might have grown accustomed to accepting it as normal. The novel was published in 1960 during the Civil Rights Movement, making its arguments about racial justice simultaneously historical and urgently contemporary.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 7: Moral Courage
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Lee distinguishes consistently between physical bravery and moral courage, presenting the latter as the higher and more difficult virtue through characters who act rightly in full knowledge of the social cost. Atticus's definition of courage — "when you know you're licked before you begin but you begin anyway and see it through no matter what" — is embodied not only in his own courtroom conduct but in Mrs Dubose's fight against morphine addiction, a detail he uses explicitly to teach Jem that courage has many forms. The novel presents moral courage as requiring imagination — specifically, the empathetic capacity to see from another's perspective — rather than simply resolution or strength. Lee structures the novel so that moral courage is consistently unrewarded by social outcome: Atticus loses, Tom dies, Bob Ewell's malice goes unchecked — yet the novel argues through Scout's growth that the exercise of moral courage transforms the person who practises it, regardless of the result.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 8: Growing Up and Innocence
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The novel's bildungsroman structure traces Scout and Jem's loss of the uncomplicated moral clarity of childhood as they absorb the realities of Maycomb's racial and social hierarchies — a process Lee presents as painful but necessary. Jem's response to Tom Robinson's conviction — turning his back, refusing to talk about it — is a more dramatically rendered loss of innocence than Scout's, because Jem is old enough to understand what has been lost and why. Lee uses the mockingbird motif to frame innocence as something that must be actively protected: to "kill a mockingbird" is to destroy something that harms no one and exists only to give pleasure, and the novel extends this to Tom Robinson and Boo Radley. The children's growing understanding that Maycomb's adult world contains injustice that good intentions alone cannot correct is the knowledge that ends their childhood, and Lee presents this loss as the foundational cost of moral understanding.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 9: Social Class and Prejudice
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Maycomb's rigid social hierarchy operates through intersecting axes of race, class, and family reputation, and Lee maps these with anthropological precision through Scout's observant narration — the Finches, the Cunninghams, the Ewells, and the Black community each occupy fixed positions whose logic Scout questions but cannot yet fully dismantle. The Cunninghams are poor but proud, refusing charity and embodying a code of reciprocal dignity; the Ewells are poor without the compensating moral capital, and Bob Ewell's accusation against Tom Robinson is in part a bid to reassert social superiority over a black man he perceives as his only inferior. Aunt Alexandra's obsession with "fine folk" and family "streaks" is Lee's sharpest satirical portrait of class prejudice, presented as its own form of irrational snobbery that Scout's democratic instincts consistently resist. Lee uses these class distinctions to argue that prejudice is not only racial but structural — a habit of categorisation that distorts human judgment across every social boundary.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 10: Justice and the Law
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Lee presents a fundamental and irresolvable tension between legal justice — the formal system of courts and verdicts — and moral justice, the intuitive sense of right that the legal system is supposed to enshrine but in Maycomb's case consistently fails to deliver. Atticus's faith in the law is simultaneously the novel's noblest position and its most poignant illusion: he believes the courtroom is the great equaliser, yet the evidence of the narrative demonstrates that it functions instead as a site where social prejudice is given legal sanction. Heck Tate's decision to cover up Boo Radley's killing of Bob Ewell introduces a third position — discretionary justice administered outside the law by a figure of legal authority — which the novel treats sympathetically, complicating any simple equation between law and justice. Lee's refusal to resolve this tension is her most honest structural choice: the novel ends with injustice unredressed and the law's complicity unchanged, asking the reader to sit with the discomfort that Maycomb's children cannot.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
