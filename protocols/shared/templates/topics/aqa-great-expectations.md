# Topic 1: Pip (protagonist -- ambition, class, moral growth)
**Type:** protagonist
**Teaching Point:** Pip is the novel's first-person narrator and moral centre, whose journey from innocent orphan to ashamed social climber to humbled, self-aware man drives the entire plot. Dickens uses Pip's retrospective narration to create dramatic irony -- the older Pip judges his younger self's snobbery and ingratitude. Students should analyse how Dickens presents Pip's moral corruption through wealth and social ambition, and his eventual redemption through suffering, loyalty, and the recognition that true worth has nothing to do with class.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

# Topic 3: Miss Havisham (bitterness / manipulation / decay)
**Type:** central_theme
**Teaching Point:** Miss Havisham is one of Dickens's most Gothic creations -- a wealthy woman frozen in the moment of her betrayal, who uses Estella as a weapon of revenge against all men. Dickens uses her decaying bridal dress and the rotting wedding feast as powerful symbols of how bitterness consumes the self. Students should analyse how Dickens presents Miss Havisham as both a villain who manipulates others and a tragic victim of her own obsession, and how her late remorse complicates the reader's judgement.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

# Topic 4: Social class and ambition
**Type:** central_theme
**Teaching Point:** Social class is the driving force of the novel's plot. Pip's desire to become a gentleman -- and his shame at his working-class origins -- exposes the hypocrisy of Victorian class distinctions. Dickens shows that wealth does not make a gentleman: the convict Magwitch is more generous and loyal than the genteel Compeyson, and Joe's simple goodness outshines Pip's acquired manners. Students should analyse how Dickens uses the contrast between characters of different classes to challenge the reader's assumptions about worth and respectability.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

# Topic 5: Guilt and conscience
**Type:** central_theme
**Teaching Point:** Guilt haunts Pip from the opening chapter, when he steals food for the convict Magwitch, and it never fully leaves him. Dickens uses guilt as a moral compass -- Pip's conscience constantly reminds him of his failings even when he tries to suppress it. Students should analyse how Dickens presents guilt as both a destructive force (Pip's shame about Joe) and a redemptive one (his eventual recognition of what he owes to those who truly love him), and how guilt connects to the novel's wider themes of crime, punishment, and moral responsibility.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

## Extract
**Extract Location:** Chapter 6

 1  My state of mind regarding the pilfering from which I had been so
 2  unexpectedly turned back, did not impel me to frank disclosure; but
 3  I hope it had some dregs of good at the bottom of it.
 4
 5  I do not recall that I felt any tenderness of conscience in
 6  reference to Mrs. Joe, when the fear of being found out was lifted
 7  off me. But I loved Joe -- perhaps for no better reason in those
 8  early days than because the dear fellow let me love him -- and, as
 9  to him, my inner self was not so easily composed. It was much upon
10  my mind (particularly when I first saw him looking about for his
11  file) that I ought to tell Joe the whole truth. Yet I did not, and
12  for the reason that I mistrusted that if I did, he would think me
13  worse than I was. The fear of losing Joe's confidence, and of
14  thenceforth sitting in the chimney corner at night staring drearily
15  at my for ever lost companion and friend, tied up my tongue. I
16  morbidly represented to myself that if Joe knew it, I never
17  afterwards could see him at the fireside feeling his fair whisker,
18  without thinking that he was meditating on it. That, if Joe knew
19  it, I never afterwards could see him glance, however casually, at
20  yesterday's meat or pudding when it came on to-day's table, without
21  thinking that he was debating whether I had been in the pantry.
22  That, if Joe knew it, and at any subsequent period of our joint
23  domestic life remarked that his beer was flat or thick, the
24  conviction that he suspected Tar in it, would bring a rush of blood
25  to my face. In a word, I was too cowardly to do what I knew to be
26  right, as I had been too cowardly to avoid doing what I knew to be
27  wrong.

# Topic 6: Estella (coldness / love / victimhood)
**Type:** central_theme
**Teaching Point:** Estella is raised by Miss Havisham as a weapon against men -- trained to be beautiful but heartless, to attract admiration and break hearts. Dickens uses her to explore how cruelty is learned rather than innate, and how emotional abuse damages both victim and perpetrator. Students should analyse how Dickens presents Estella as simultaneously cold and pitiable, and how her warnings to Pip that she has "no heart" make her both honest and tragic. Her eventual suffering at the hands of Drummle mirrors Miss Havisham's original betrayal, completing a cycle of emotional violence.

# Topic 7: Magwitch (crime / redemption / loyalty)
**Type:** central_theme
**Teaching Point:** Magwitch is the novel's most dramatic example of Dickens's argument that the justice system punishes poverty, not wickedness. A convict who was driven to crime by starvation and exploited by the gentleman criminal Compeyson, Magwitch devotes his life in exile to making Pip a gentleman -- proving that generosity and loyalty can exist in the most unlikely places. Students should analyse how Dickens uses Pip's horror at discovering his benefactor to expose the hypocrisy of class prejudice, and how Magwitch's devotion ultimately transforms Pip's understanding of what it means to be worthy.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

# Topic 8: Joe Gargery (goodness / loyalty / class)
**Type:** central_theme
**Teaching Point:** Joe Gargery is the moral benchmark of the novel -- a man of unwavering kindness, loyalty, and humility who represents everything Pip abandons in his pursuit of gentility, and everything he must return to in order to find peace. Dickens uses Joe to challenge the Victorian equation of manners with morality: Joe is inarticulate and socially awkward, but he is the truest gentleman in the book. Students should analyse how Dickens presents Joe's quiet dignity in contrast to Pip's acquired snobbery, and how Joe's forgiveness of Pip is presented as the novel's deepest expression of love.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

# Topic 9: Justice and the law
**Type:** central_theme
**Teaching Point:** Dickens presents the Victorian justice system as deeply flawed -- punishing poverty while protecting wealth and privilege. The contrast between Magwitch and Compeyson at their trial is the novel's clearest indictment: both committed the same crime, but Compeyson's gentleman appearance earned him a lighter sentence while Magwitch's rough exterior condemned him. Students should analyse how Dickens uses Jaggers (the lawyer who washes his hands of moral responsibility), Newgate Prison, and the convict ships to present justice as a system that serves the powerful rather than the innocent.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

# Topic 10: Education and self-improvement
**Type:** central_theme
**Teaching Point:** Education in Great Expectations operates on two levels: formal learning (reading, writing, manners) and moral education (understanding what truly matters in life). Dickens shows that Pip's formal education as a gentleman ultimately teaches him nothing of real value, while his moral education -- earned through suffering, guilt, and the example of Joe and Magwitch -- is what finally makes him worthy. Students should analyse how Dickens uses Mr Wopsle's great-aunt's absurd school, Biddy's quiet intelligence, and Matthew Pocket's tutoring to present different attitudes to education, and how the novel ultimately argues that self-improvement means moral growth, not social climbing.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
