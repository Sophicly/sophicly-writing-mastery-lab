# Topic 1: Prospero
**Type:** extract-essay
**Teaching Point:** Prospero is the play's central figure — a deposed duke who wields magic to control others and engineer his restoration. His complex relationship with power, knowledge and forgiveness drives every plot strand. Students must analyse how Shakespeare uses Prospero to explore authority, paternal control and the renunciation of power.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

## Extract
**Extract Location:** Act 1, Scene 2

> 1  Hast thou, spirit,
> 2  Performed to point the tempest that I bade thee?
> 3
> 4  ARIEL
> 5  To every article.
> 6  I boarded the King's ship; now on the beak,
> 7  Now in the waist, the deck, in every cabin,
> 8  I flamed amazement. Sometime I'd divide
> 9  And burn in many places; on the topmast,
> 10 The yards and bowsprit, would I flame distinctly,
> 11 Then meet and join. Jove's lightning, the precursors
> 12 O' th' dreadful thunderclaps, more momentary
> 13 And sight-outrunning were not; the fire and cracks
> 14 Of sulphurous roaring the most mighty Neptune
> 15 Seem to besiege and make his bold waves tremble,
> 16 Yea, his dread trident shake.
> 17
> 18 PROSPERO
> 19 My brave spirit!
> 20 Who was so firm, so constant, that this coil
> 21 Would not infect his reason?
> 22
> 23 ARIEL
> 24 Not a soul
> 25 But felt a fever of the mad and played

# Topic 3: Caliban
**Type:** extract-essay
**Teaching Point:** Caliban is the island's original inhabitant, enslaved by Prospero. Shakespeare presents him as both monstrous and sympathetic, giving him some of the play's most beautiful poetry. Students should explore how his characterisation raises questions about nature versus nurture, colonialism and the legitimacy of Prospero's rule.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

## Extract
**Extract Location:** Act 1, Scene 2

> 1  CALIBAN
> 2  This island's mine, by Sycorax my mother,
> 3  Which thou tak'st from me. When thou cam'st first,
> 4  Thou strok'st me and made much of me, wouldst give me
> 5  Water with berries in 't, and teach me how
> 6  To name the bigger light and how the less,
> 7  That burn by day and night. And then I loved thee
> 8  And showed thee all the qualities o' th' isle,
> 9  The fresh springs, brine pits, barren place and fertile.
> 10 Cursed be I that did so! All the charms
> 11 Of Sycorax, toads, beetles, bats, light on you,
> 12 For I am all the subjects that you have,
> 13 Which first was mine own king; and here you sty me
> 14 In this hard rock, whiles you do keep from me
> 15 The rest o' th' island.
> 16
> 17 PROSPERO
> 18 Thou most lying slave,
> 19 Whom stripes may move, not kindness! I have used thee,
> 20 Filth as thou art, with humane care, and lodged thee
> 21 In mine own cell, till thou didst seek to violate
> 22 The honour of my child.
> 23
> 24 CALIBAN
> 25 O ho, O ho! Would 't had been done!

# Topic 4: Power and Control
**Type:** extract-essay
**Teaching Point:** Power operates on multiple levels in The Tempest: political (usurpation of Milan), magical (Prospero's art), colonial (control of the island's inhabitants) and patriarchal (Prospero over Miranda). Students should analyse how Shakespeare dramatises the acquisition, abuse and eventual relinquishment of power.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

# Topic 5: Magic and the Supernatural
**Type:** extract-essay
**Teaching Point:** Magic is the mechanism through which Prospero exercises control over every character and event. Shakespeare uses the supernatural both as dramatic spectacle and as a metaphor for theatrical art itself. Students should consider how Prospero's magic relates to Jacobean anxieties about sorcery and how the play ultimately presents the renunciation of magical power.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

## Extract
**Extract Location:** Act 5, Scene 1

> 1  PROSPERO
> 2  Ye elves of hills, brooks, standing lakes and groves,
> 3  And ye that on the sands with printless foot
> 4  Do chase the ebbing Neptune, and do fly him
> 5  When he comes back; you demi-puppets that
> 6  By moonshine do the green sour ringlets make,
> 7  Whereof the ewe not bites; and you whose pastime
> 8  Is to make midnight mushrooms, that rejoice
> 9  To hear the solemn curfew; by whose aid,
> 10 Weak masters though ye be, I have bedimmed
> 11 The noontide sun, called forth the mutinous winds,
> 12 And 'twixt the green sea and the azured vault
> 13 Set roaring war; to the dread rattling thunder
> 14 Have I given fire, and rifted Jove's stout oak
> 15 With his own bolt; the strong-based promontory
> 16 Have I made shake, and by the spurs plucked up
> 17 The pine and cedar. Graves at my command
> 18 Have waked their sleepers, oped, and let 'em forth
> 19 By my so potent art. But this rough magic
> 20 I here abjure, and when I have required
> 21 Some heavenly music, which even now I do,
> 22 To work mine end upon their senses that
> 23 This airy charm is for, I'll break my staff,
> 24 Bury it certain fathoms in the earth,
> 25 And deeper than did ever plummet sound
> 26 I'll drown my book.

# Topic 6: Colonialism and Freedom
**Type:** extract-essay
**Teaching Point:** The Tempest is widely read as an allegory of European colonialism. Prospero arrives on an inhabited island, enslaves its native (Caliban) and imposes his language and culture. Students should explore how Shakespeare dramatises the tension between coloniser and colonised, and how Caliban's desire for freedom reflects broader questions about ownership, legitimacy and oppression.

# Topic 7: Miranda
**Type:** extract-essay
**Teaching Point:** Miranda is the only female character in the play. Shakespeare presents her as both innocent and perceptive -- she has known only the island and her father, yet she shows compassion, intelligence and a willingness to defy Prospero's control. Students should consider how her characterisation reflects and challenges Jacobean attitudes to women, and how Prospero's paternal authority shapes her experience.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

# Topic 8: Ariel
**Type:** extract-essay
**Teaching Point:** Ariel is a spirit bound to serve Prospero, performing his magic in exchange for the promise of freedom. Shakespeare presents Ariel as ethereal, loyal yet yearning for liberty -- a figure who mirrors Caliban's enslavement but responds with obedience rather than rebellion. Students should analyse how Ariel's characterisation explores themes of service, freedom and the relationship between master and servant.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

**Extract Location:** Act 5, Scene 1

> 4  Would become tender.

> 6  PROSPERO
> 7  Dost thou think so, spirit?

> 9  ARIEL

# Topic 9: Forgiveness and Reconciliation
**Type:** extract-essay
**Teaching Point:** The play moves towards Prospero's decision to forgive those who wronged him rather than destroy them. Shakespeare presents forgiveness as a conscious moral choice -- Prospero must overcome genuine fury to choose virtue over vengeance. Students should explore how the ending balances justice with mercy, and whether the reconciliation Shakespeare stages is genuine or forced.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

## Extract
**Extract Location:** Act 5, Scene 1

> 1  PROSPERO
> 2  [To Alonso] Behold, sir King,
> 3  The wronged Duke of Milan, Prospero.
> 4  For more assurance that a living prince
> 5  Does now speak to thee, I embrace thy body;
> 6  And to thee and thy company I bid
> 7  A hearty welcome.
> 8
> 9  ALONSO

> 13 Beats as of flesh and blood; and, since I saw thee,

> 15 I fear, a madness held me. This must crave,

# Topic 10: Illusion and Reality
**Type:** extract-essay
**Teaching Point:** The Tempest blurs the line between what is real and what is manufactured by Prospero's art. The masque, the banquet, even the storm itself are illusions. Shakespeare uses this theme to reflect on the nature of theatre, with Prospero's famous "Our revels now are ended" speech serving as both in-play commentary and a meditation on performance. Students should analyse how Shakespeare uses illusion to explore truth, deception and the power of art.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

## Extract
**Extract Location:** Act 4, Scene 1

> 1  PROSPERO
> 2  Our revels now are ended. These our actors,
> 3  As I foretold you, were all spirits and
> 4  Are melted into air, into thin air;
> 5  And, like the baseless fabric of this vision,
> 6  The cloud-capped towers, the gorgeous palaces,
> 7  The solemn temples, the great globe itself,
> 8  Yea, all which it inherit, shall dissolve,
> 9  And, like this insubstantial pageant faded,
> 10 Leave not a rack behind. We are such stuff
> 11 As dreams are made on, and our little life
> 12 Is rounded with a sleep. Sir, I am vexed.
> 13 Bear with my weakness; my old brain is troubled.
> 14 Be not disturbed with my infirmity.
> 15 If you be pleased, retire into my cell
> 16 And there repose. A turn or two I'll walk
> 17 To still my beating mind.
> 18
> 19 FERDINAND and MIRANDA
> 20 We wish your peace.
> 21
> 22 PROSPERO
> 23 Come with a thought! I thank thee, Ariel. Come!
> 24
> 25 [Enter ARIEL]
> 26
> 27 ARIEL
> 28 Thy thoughts I cleave to. What's thy pleasure?
> 29
> 30 PROSPERO
> 31 Spirit, we must prepare to meet with Caliban.
