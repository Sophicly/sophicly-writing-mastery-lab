# Topic 1: The Inspector
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Priestley presents the Inspector as a theatrical device as much as a character — his function is not to solve a crime but to precipitate a moral reckoning, and Priestley deliberately withholds certainty about his nature to prevent the Birlings from, or the audience from, dismissing his message as the product of ordinary police procedure. His technique of showing the photograph to each character separately, his refusal to allow evasion, and his calm command of every scene suggest an authority that transcends the social hierarchies Birling tries to invoke. The Inspector's famous final speech — warning of "fire and blood and anguish" if the lesson of collective responsibility goes unlearned — aligns him with prophetic or divine judgement rather than social realism, making the play's message explicitly moral rather than merely political. Priestley times the speech to 1945, when an audience would have already witnessed the fire and blood of two world wars.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does J.B. Priestley present the Inspector in *An Inspector Calls?*

You should consider:
- how Priestley establishes the Inspector's authority and methods
- how the Inspector challenges each member of the Birling family
- how Priestley uses the Inspector to develop the play's themes about social responsibility and moral judgement.

# Topic 3: Mr Birling
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Priestley uses Mr Birling as a carefully constructed satirical target — his confident pronouncements at the dinner table (the Titanic is "unsinkable", there will be no war, "a man has to look after himself and his family") are all historically falsified by the time of the play's first performance in 1945, making him an emblem of the complacent, self-serving capitalism that Priestley holds responsible for the suffering the Inspector reveals. Birling's primary concern throughout the Inspector's visit is reputation and the threat to business, not Eva Smith's suffering — a prioritisation that Priestley presents as morally bankrupt. His relief at the possibility that the Inspector was a hoax, and his inability to see that this changes nothing morally, is his defining characteristic: he confuses the legal with the ethical, a distinction the play exists to dismantle. The dramatic irony of his speeches positions the audience as morally superior from the outset.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 4: Sheila
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Sheila is the play's most significant agent of moral development — she moves from passive participant in the Birlings' comfortable world to active advocate for the Inspector's message, making her transformation the vehicle through which Priestley argues for the possibility of change. Her initial response to her role in Eva's dismissal — genuine, undefensive horror and acceptance of responsibility — stands in direct contrast to her parents' evasions, and Priestley uses this contrast to suggest that genuine moral education is possible for those willing to face the truth. Sheila's function as the audience's representative is reinforced by her role as observer: she watches the other interrogations, processes their implications, and voices the moral conclusions the audience is intended to draw. Her final refusal to accept the possibility that the Inspector was a hoax — "it doesn't make any difference" — gives her the play's clearest articulation of collective moral responsibility.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 5: Eric
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Eric's characterisation is more complex than his initial presentation suggests — the nervous drinking, the social discomfort, and the reckless relationship with Eva Smith reveal a young man formed by exactly the class and gender expectations Priestley is critiquing. His theft from Birling's business to support Eva is morally ambiguous (a genuine gesture of care, but also an appropriation of money that is not his) and his relationship with Eva was begun through coercion — Priestley refuses to allow Eric simple redemption. His genuine distress at Eva's death and his willingness to accept responsibility put him alongside Sheila as a representative of the younger generation's capacity for moral growth, though he lacks her clarity and articulacy. Eric's confrontation with his father — "I'm ashamed of you" — is one of the play's most dramatically loaded moments, reversing the expected generational authority.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 6: Social Responsibility
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Social responsibility — the idea that individuals are responsible not just for their own families but for the wider community — is the play's explicit moral argument, stated most directly in the Inspector's final speech and reinforced by the structural device of each Birling's separate contribution to Eva's destruction. Priestley structures the revelations to show how a chain of individually rationalised actions produces a collective catastrophe, making the diffusion of blame impossible: each character's contribution was real, even if none intended the final outcome. The play was written in 1945 for an audience debating the post-war social contract, and the Inspector's argument for collective responsibility maps directly onto the Beveridge Report's vision of a welfare state. Priestley's theatrical technique — the claustrophobic single set, the real-time unfolding of events — intensifies the sense that there is no escape from moral accountability.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does J.B. Priestley present the theme of social responsibility in *An Inspector Calls?*

You should consider:

- how different characters respond to the idea of social responsibility

# Topic 7: Class and Capitalism
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The class system in *An Inspector Calls* is presented not merely as social inequality but as a structure of exploitation in which the comfort of the Birlings depends directly on the vulnerability of people like Eva Smith. Priestley's choice of an industrial capitalist family — Birling owns a factory, sits on the bench, expects a knighthood — as his subject positions class and capitalism as interconnected systems of power that the Inspector's interrogation exposes. Each character's involvement with Eva maps a different dimension of class power: Birling's economic control over her livelihood, Sheila's consumer power, Mrs Birling's charitable gatekeeping, Gerald's class-based sexual entitlement. Priestley's 1912 setting, used for a play written in 1945, allows him to position the class attitudes on stage as the cause of the "fire and blood and anguish" the audience has just lived through — a historical argument as well as a moral one.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 8: Guilt and Conscience
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Priestley distributes guilt across the Birling family unequally — some accept it, some deny it, some transfer it — and this variation in response to moral responsibility is the play's central structural device. The Inspector's method of forcing each character to confront their role in Eva's story is a dramatisation of conscience: he externalises the moral reckoning that an honest person would have performed internally. Priestley distinguishes sharply between guilt as an emotion (Eric and Sheila experience this genuinely) and guilt as a legal or reputational exposure (what Birling and Mrs Birling fear), suggesting that the latter without the former is morally worthless. The play's open ending — the real phone call suggesting a real inquiry — raises the question of whether legal consequence is needed to force moral accountability, a question Priestley deliberately leaves unresolved.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does J.B. Priestley explore guilt and conscience in *An Inspector Calls?*

You should consider:
- how different characters respond to the revelation of their role in Eva Smith's story
- how Priestley distinguishes between genuine remorse and self-interested denial
- how Priestley uses guilt and conscience to develop the play's moral argument.

# Topic 9: Generational Conflict
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The generational divide in *An Inspector Calls* — with the older Birlings refusing moral accountability and the younger generation accepting it — carries Priestley's most optimistic political argument: that the attitudes that caused the first half of the twentieth century's catastrophes need not persist into the second half. Sheila and Eric's readiness to accept responsibility is presented in contrast to their parents' defensiveness, suggesting that the class attitudes of the Edwardian generation are not simply human nature but historically specific formations that can be unlearned. Priestley, writing in 1945, was addressing an audience deciding what kind of society to build after the war, and the generational contrast performs an argument about choice: the younger characters model the possibility of choosing differently. The circular structure of the play's ending — another Inspector on his way — suggests that if the older generation's values prevail, history will simply repeat itself.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does J.B. Priestley present generational conflict in *An Inspector Calls?*

You should consider:
- how Priestley distinguishes between the older and younger characters' responses to the Inspector
- what the generational contrast suggests about the possibility of social change
- how Priestley uses the play's structure to develop his argument about generational responsibility.

# Topic 10: Gender and Power
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Gender is a significant dimension of the play's power analysis — Eva Smith is triply vulnerable as a working-class woman without family support, and the Birlings' various exercises of power over her are shaped in part by gendered assumptions about who is owed consideration. Mrs Birling's treatment of Eva at the Brumley Women's Charity Organisation is particularly rich: a woman who has set herself up as a moral arbiter of other women's behaviour, enforcing exactly the gender norms that deny women like Eva any legitimate claim to sympathy. Gerald's relationship with Eva/Daisy is framed in the language of rescue and care, but the power differential — wealth, social position, the ability to end the relationship without consequence — is never absent. Sheila's challenge to her mother's authority and her refusal to be silenced by Gerald's "You're not the type" represent Priestley's vision of a specifically feminist moral clarity.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does J.B. Priestley present gender and power in *An Inspector Calls?*

You should consider:
- how gender shapes Eva Smith's vulnerability and her treatment by different characters
- how Priestley presents Mrs Birling's use of gender norms as a form of class power
- how female characters in the play are affected by and respond to the power structures around them.
