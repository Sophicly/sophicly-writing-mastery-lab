# Topic 1: Shylock and Prejudice
**Type:** whole-text
**Teaching Point:** Students must explore how Shakespeare presents Shylock as both villain and victim, examining how anti-Semitic prejudice operates in Venice. Strong answers will consider Elizabethan attitudes to Jewish people and how Shakespeare complicates audience sympathy through key speeches like "Hath not a Jew eyes?"
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

## Question
How does Shakespeare present ideas about prejudice through the character of Shylock in *The Merchant of Venice*? You must consider language, form and structure and refer to the context of the play in your answer.

# Topic 3: Justice vs Mercy
**Type:** whole-text
**Teaching Point:** The trial scene is the play's dramatic climax and the tension between justice and mercy drives its resolution. Students should explore how Portia's "quality of mercy" speech sets Christian mercy against the strict legalism of Shylock's bond, and consider how Shakespeare uses this conflict to examine power, religion and the law in Venetian society.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

# Topic 4: Portia
**Type:** whole-text
**Teaching Point:** Portia is the play's most complex female character — intelligent, eloquent and resourceful, yet constrained by her father's will and the expectations placed on women. Students should consider how Shakespeare uses her disguise as Balthasar to explore ideas about gender, power and authority, and how her role in the trial scene shifts the balance of the play.

# Topic 5: Love and Friendship
**Type:** whole-text
**Teaching Point:** The play interweaves romantic love (Bassanio and Portia, Lorenzo and Jessica) with deep male friendship (Antonio and Bassanio). Students should examine how Shakespeare contrasts different forms of love, consider Antonio's sacrificial devotion, and explore whether love in the play is always genuine or sometimes entangled with financial self-interest.

# Topic 6: Wealth and Money
**Type:** whole-text
**Teaching Point:** Venice is a mercantile city and money drives almost every relationship in the play — from Bassanio's courtship of Portia to the bond itself. Students should explore how Shakespeare uses wealth to reveal character and expose moral contradictions, particularly the tension between Christian ideals of generosity and the commercial realities of trade and usury.

# Topic 7: Appearance vs Reality
**Type:** whole-text
**Teaching Point:** Deception and disguise run through the play at every level — the casket test rewards the suitor who sees beyond outward appearance, Portia disguises herself as a male lawyer, and Jessica escapes dressed as a boy. Students should explore how Shakespeare uses these layers of deception to examine whether characters and institutions in Venice are truly what they seem.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

# Topic 8: Antonio
**Type:** whole-text
**Teaching Point:** Antonio is the titular merchant yet remains one of the play's most enigmatic figures — melancholic, generous to a fault, and willing to risk his life for Bassanio. Students should consider how Shakespeare presents his sadness, his treatment of Shylock, and his role as both hero and instigator of the bond, exploring whether he is truly virtuous or deeply flawed.

# Topic 9: The Bond
**Type:** whole-text
**Teaching Point:** The pound-of-flesh bond is the engine of the plot and raises questions about law, revenge and human worth. Students should trace how Shakespeare builds dramatic tension around the bond from its seemingly light-hearted proposal to its deadly consequences in the trial, and consider what the bond reveals about the relationship between Shylock and the Christian characters.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

## Question
How does Shakespeare use the bond between Shylock and Antonio to create dramatic tension in *The Merchant of Venice*? You must consider language, form and structure and refer to the context of the play in your answer.

# Topic 10: Gender
**Type:** whole-text
**Teaching Point:** Shakespeare repeatedly draws attention to gender roles through cross-dressing, the casket test's control over Portia's marriage, and the ring plot's power games. Students should explore how female characters navigate a patriarchal world, how disguise temporarily disrupts gender hierarchies, and what the play ultimately suggests about women's agency in Elizabethan and Venetian society.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
