# Topic 1: Viola
**Type:** protagonist
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Viola is the play's emotional anchor — shipwrecked, grieving, and forced to navigate Illyria disguised as a man. Shakespeare uses her to expose the gap between outward performance and inner feeling. Students should analyse how her soliloquies reveal honesty and self-awareness that no other character possesses, and how her willingness to endure unrequited love contrasts with Orsino's and Olivia's self-indulgent passion.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

**Extract Location:** Act 2, Scene 2

### Extract
```
 1    VIOLA: I left no ring with her. What means this lady?
 2    Fortune forbid my outside have not charmed her!
 3    She made good view of me, indeed so much
 4    That sure methought her eyes had lost her tongue,
 5    For she did speak in starts distractedly.
 6    She loves me, sure! The cunning of her passion
 7    Invites me in this churlish messenger.
 8    None of my lord's ring? Why, he sent her none!
 9    I am the man. If it be so — as 'tis —
10    Poor lady, she were better love a dream.
11    Disguise, I see thou art a wickedness
12    Wherein the pregnant enemy does much.
13    How easy is it for the proper false
14    In women's waxen hearts to set their forms!
15    Alas, our frailty is the cause, not we,
16    For such as we are made of, such we be.
17    How will this fadge? My master loves her dearly,
18    And I, poor monster, fond as much on him,
19    And she, mistaken, seems to dote on me.
20    What will become of this? As I am man,
21    My state is desperate for my master's love.
22    As I am woman, now, alas the day,
23    What thriftless sighs shall poor Olivia breathe!
24    O Time, thou must untangle this, not I;
25    It is too hard a knot for me t'untie.
```

# Topic 3: Malvolio
**Type:** central_character
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Malvolio is the play's most controversial figure — a puritanical steward whose rigid self-importance invites ridicule, yet whose punishment arguably exceeds his offence. Shakespeare uses his humiliation to dramatise the tension between social aspiration and fixed hierarchy, and to test the audience's capacity for sympathy. Students should analyse how the forged letter scene exposes his vanity while the dark-room imprisonment raises uncomfortable questions about cruelty disguised as comedy.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

**Extract Location:** Act 3, Scene 4

### Extract
```
 1    MALVOLIO: Sweet lady, ho, ho!
 2    OLIVIA: Smil'st thou? I sent for thee upon a sad occasion.
 3    MALVOLIO: Sad, lady? I could be sad. This does make
 4    some obstruction in the blood, this cross-gartering,
 5    but what of that? If it please the eye of one, it is
 6    with me as the very true sonnet is: "Please one and
 7    please all."
 8    OLIVIA: Why, how dost thou, man? What is the matter
 9    with thee?
10    MALVOLIO: Not black in my mind, though yellow in my
11    legs. It did come to his hands, and commands shall
12    be executed. I think we do know the sweet Roman hand.
13    OLIVIA: Wilt thou go to bed, Malvolio?
14    MALVOLIO: To bed? Ay, sweetheart, and I'll come to thee.
15    OLIVIA: God comfort thee! Why dost thou smile so, and
16    kiss thy hand so oft?
17    MARIA: How do you, Malvolio?
18    MALVOLIO: At your request? Yes, nightingales answer
19    daws!
20    MARIA: Why appear you with this ridiculous boldness
21    before my lady?
22    MALVOLIO: "Be not afraid of greatness." 'Twas well writ.
23    OLIVIA: What mean'st thou by that, Malvolio?
24    MALVOLIO: "Some are born great" —
25    OLIVIA: Ha?
```

# Topic 4: Love and Desire
**Type:** central_theme
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Love in Twelfth Night is excessive, irrational, and often misdirected. Orsino wallows in romantic fantasy, Olivia falls for a disguise, and Malvolio loves himself above all. Shakespeare suggests that desire tells us more about the lover than the beloved, and that genuine love — Viola's quiet devotion — is the rarest thing in Illyria. Students should analyse how the play exposes the performative and self-serving nature of desire while still celebrating love's transformative potential.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

**Extract Location:** Act 1, Scene 1

### Extract
```
 1    ORSINO: If music be the food of love, play on,
 2    Give me excess of it that, surfeiting,
 3    The appetite may sicken and so die.
 4    That strain again, it had a dying fall.
 5    O, it came o'er my ear like the sweet sound
 6    That breathes upon a bank of violets,
 7    Stealing and giving odour. Enough, no more,
 8    'Tis not so sweet now as it was before.
 9    O spirit of love, how quick and fresh art thou,
10    That, notwithstanding thy capacity
11    Receiveth as the sea, nought enters there,
12    Of what validity and pitch soe'er,
13    But falls into abatement and low price
14    Even in a minute! So full of shapes is fancy
15    That it alone is high fantastical.
16    CURIO: Will you go hunt, my lord?
17    ORSINO:                          What, Curio?
18    CURIO: The hart.
19    ORSINO: Why, so I do, the noblest that I have.
20    O, when mine eyes did see Olivia first
21    Methought she purged the air of pestilence;
22    That instant was I turned into a hart,
23    And my desires, like fell and cruel hounds,
24    E'er since pursue me.
```

# Topic 5: Disguise and Identity
**Type:** central_theme
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Disguise in Twelfth Night goes far deeper than Viola's costume. Olivia disguises desire as grief, Orsino disguises self-love as devotion, Feste disguises wisdom as foolishness, and Malvolio is tricked into a grotesque performance of someone he is not. Shakespeare uses a world saturated with deception to ask whether identity is something fixed or something we perform — and whether self-knowledge is possible when everyone around you is playing a part.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

**Extract Location:** Act 1, Scene 5

### Extract
```
 1    VIOLA: Good madam, let me see your face.
 2    OLIVIA: Have you any commission from your lord to
 3    negotiate with my face? You are now out of your text.
 4    But we will draw the curtain and show you the
 5    picture. [She unveils] Look you, sir, such a one I was
 6    this present. Is't not well done?
 7    VIOLA: Excellently done, if God did all.
 8    OLIVIA: 'Tis in grain, sir; 'twill endure wind and weather.
 9    VIOLA: 'Tis beauty truly blent, whose red and white
10    Nature's own sweet and cunning hand laid on.
11    Lady, you are the cruell'st she alive
12    If you will lead these graces to the grave
13    And leave the world no copy.
14    OLIVIA: O sir, I will not be so hard-hearted. I will give
15    out divers schedules of my beauty. It shall be
16    inventoried and every particle and utensil labelled
17    to my will, as: item, two lips, indifferent red; item,
18    two grey eyes, with lids to them; item, one neck, one
19    chin, and so forth. Were you sent hither to praise me?
20    VIOLA: I see you what you are: you are too proud.
21    But if you were the devil, you are fair.
22    My lord and master loves you. O, such love
23    Could be but recompensed though you were crowned
24    The nonpareil of beauty.
```

# Topic 6: Olivia
**Type:** central_character
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Olivia moves from self-imposed mourning to impulsive desire in a single scene, abandoning her seven-year vow of grief the moment Cesario speaks. Shakespeare uses her to satirise the performative nature of emotion in Illyria — her mourning was a kind of disguise, and her love for Cesario is based on an illusion. Yet her directness and courage in pursuing what she wants give her a vitality that complicates simple mockery. Students should analyse how Shakespeare balances comedy and sympathy in her characterisation.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

**Extract Location:** Act 3, Scene 1

### Extract
```
 1    OLIVIA: O, what a deal of scorn looks beautiful
 2    In the contempt and anger of his lip!
 3    A murd'rous guilt shows not itself more soon
 4    Than love that would seem hid. Love's night is noon. —
 5    Cesario, by the roses of the spring,
 6    By maidhood, honour, truth, and everything,
 7    I love thee so that, maugre all thy pride,
 8    Nor wit nor reason can my passion hide.
 9    Do not extort thy reasons from this clause,
10    For that I woo, thou therefore hast no cause.
11    But rather reason thus with reason fetter:
12    Love sought is good, but given unsought is better.
13    VIOLA: By innocence I swear, and by my youth,
14    I have one heart, one bosom, and one truth,
15    And that no woman has, nor never none
16    Shall mistress be of it save I alone.
17    And so adieu, good madam. Never more
18    Will I my master's tears to you deplore.
19    OLIVIA: Yet come again, for thou perhaps mayst move
20    That heart which now abhors to like his love.
```

# Topic 7: Orsino
**Type:** central_character
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Orsino is a duke who luxuriates in romantic suffering rather than acting on his feelings. He sends messengers instead of wooing Olivia himself, and his language reveals a man more devoted to the aesthetics of passion than to any real person. Shakespeare uses him to satirise Petrarchan love conventions, but the intimate exchanges with Cesario gradually reveal a capacity for genuine connection beneath the posturing. Students should analyse how Orsino's attitudes to love and gender are both challenged and confirmed by the play's resolution.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

**Extract Location:** Act 2, Scene 4

### Extract
```
 1    ORSINO: Come hither, boy. If ever thou shalt love,
 2    In the sweet pangs of it, remember me,
 3    For such as I am, all true lovers are,
 4    Unstaid and skittish in all motions else
 5    Save in the constant image of the creature
 6    That is beloved. How dost thou like this tune?
 7    VIOLA: It gives a very echo to the seat
 8    Where love is throned.
 9    ORSINO:                 Thou dost speak masterly.
10    My life upon't, young though thou art, thine eye
11    Hath stayed upon some favour that it loves.
12    Hath it not, boy?
13    VIOLA:              A little, by your favour.
14    ORSINO: What kind of woman is't?
15    VIOLA:                           Of your complexion.
16    ORSINO: She is not worth thee then. What years, i'faith?
17    VIOLA: About your years, my lord.
18    ORSINO: Too old, by heaven! Let still the woman take
19    An elder than herself; so wears she to him;
20    So sways she level in her husband's heart.
21    For, boy, however we do praise ourselves,
22    Our fancies are more giddy and unfirm,
23    More longing, wavering, sooner lost and worn,
24    Than women's are.
25    VIOLA:              I think it well, my lord.
```

# Topic 8: Sir Toby and the Subplot
**Type:** supporting_theme
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Sir Toby Belch is the lord of misrule in Olivia's household, presiding over a world of drinking, singing, and increasingly cruel practical jokes. His clash with Malvolio dramatises the conflict between festive liberty and puritanical restraint — a tension with real political resonance in Shakespeare's England. Students should analyse how Shakespeare uses the subplot to test the boundaries of acceptable comedy, and consider whether Sir Toby's revelry represents genuine freedom or a darker form of exploitation, particularly in his manipulation of Sir Andrew Aguecheek.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

**Extract Location:** Act 2, Scene 3

### Extract
```
 1    SIR TOBY: Approach, Sir Andrew. Not to be abed after
 2    midnight is to be up betimes, and "diluculo
 3    surgere" — thou know'st.
 4    SIR ANDREW: Nay, by my troth, I know not; but I know
 5    to be up late is to be up late.
 6    SIR TOBY: A false conclusion! I hate it as an unfilled
 7    can. To be up after midnight and to go to bed then is
 8    early; so that to go to bed after midnight is to go
 9    to bed betimes. Does not our lives consist of the four
10    elements?
11    SIR ANDREW: Faith, so they say, but I think it rather
12    consists of eating and drinking.
13    SIR TOBY: Thou'rt a scholar; let us therefore eat and
14    drink. Maria, I say, a stoup of wine!
15    MALVOLIO: [entering] My masters, are you mad? Or what are
16    you? Have you no wit, manners, nor honesty, but to
17    gabble like tinkers at this time of night? Do ye
18    make an alehouse of my lady's house, that ye squeak
19    out your coziers' catches without any mitigation or
20    remorse of voice? Is there no respect of place,
21    persons, nor time in you?
22    SIR TOBY: Art any more than a steward? Dost thou think
23    because thou art virtuous there shall be no more
24    cakes and ale?
```

# Topic 9: Appearance vs Reality
**Type:** supporting_theme
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Almost nothing in Illyria is as it appears. Viola looks like a man, Olivia's grief conceals desire, Orsino's devotion conceals narcissism, and a forged letter makes Malvolio believe a fantasy. Shakespeare builds dramatic irony into almost every scene — the audience sees truths the characters cannot — and uses Feste to articulate the play's deepest insight: that the wise may be foolish and the foolish wise. Students should analyse how the gap between appearance and reality creates both comedy and pathos.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

**Extract Location:** Act 2, Scene 4

### Extract
```
 1    VIOLA: My father had a daughter loved a man —
 2    As it might be, perhaps, were I a woman,
 3    I should your lordship.
 4    ORSINO:                   And what's her history?
 5    VIOLA: A blank, my lord. She never told her love,
 6    But let concealment, like a worm i'th' bud,
 7    Feed on her damask cheek. She pined in thought,
 8    And with a green and yellow melancholy
 9    She sat like Patience on a monument,
10    Smiling at grief. Was not this love indeed?
11    We men may say more, swear more, but indeed
12    Our shows are more than will; for still we prove
13    Much in our vows, but little in our love.
14    ORSINO: But died she of her love, my boy?
15    VIOLA:                                    I am all the daughters
16    of my father's house,
17    And all the brothers too; and yet, I know not.
18    Sir, shall I to this lady?
19    ORSINO:                     Ay, that's the theme.
20    To her in haste; give her this jewel; say
21    My love can give no place, bide no denay.
```

# Topic 10: Class and Social Order
**Type:** supporting_theme
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Twelfth Night repeatedly tests the boundaries of social hierarchy. Malvolio's fantasy of marrying Olivia represents a servant's transgressive ambition, Maria engineers his downfall yet rises through her own marriage to Sir Toby, and the festive setting of Twelfth Night traditionally licensed the temporary inversion of rank. Students should analyse how Shakespeare uses these disruptions to explore Elizabethan anxieties about social mobility, and consider whether the ending restores order or permanently unsettles it — particularly given Malvolio's unresolved threat of revenge.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

**Extract Location:** Act 5, Scene 1

### Extract
```
 1    MALVOLIO: Madam, you have done me wrong,
 2    Notorious wrong.
 3    OLIVIA:              Have I, Malvolio? No.
 4    MALVOLIO: Lady, you have. Pray you peruse that letter.
 5    You must not now deny it is your hand.
 6    Write from it if you can, in hand or phrase,
 7    Or say 'tis not your seal, not your invention.
 8    You can say none of this. Well, grant it then,
 9    And tell me, in the modesty of honour,
10    Why you have given me such clear lights of favour,
11    Bade me come smiling and cross-gartered to you,
12    To put on yellow stockings, and to frown
13    Upon Sir Toby and the lighter people?
14    And acting this in an obedient hope,
15    Why have you suffered me to be imprisoned,
16    Kept in a dark house, visited by the priest,
17    And made the most notorious geck and gull
18    That e'er invention played on? Tell me why!
19    OLIVIA: Alas, Malvolio, this is not my writing,
20    Though I confess much like the character.
21    But out of question, 'tis Maria's hand.
22    And now I do bethink me, it was she
23    First told me thou wast mad; then cam'st in smiling,
24    And in such forms which here were presupposed
25    Upon thee in the letter. Prithee be content.
```
