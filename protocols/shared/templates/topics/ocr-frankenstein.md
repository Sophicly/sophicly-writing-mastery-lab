# Topic 1: Victor Frankenstein (protagonist)
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Victor is both protagonist and cautionary figure. Shelley constructs him as a man of brilliance undone by obsessive ambition, whose refusal to accept responsibility for his creation drives the novel's tragedy. Students should analyse how Shelley uses Victor's own narration to expose his self-deception and moral failings.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4*
**Extract Location:** Volume 1, Chapter 4
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Victor describes the consuming obsession that drove him during the creation of the Creature, and the toll it took on his health and relationships.

### Extract
```
 1    No one can conceive the variety of feelings which bore me
 2    onwards, like a hurricane, in the first enthusiasm of
 3    success. Life and death appeared to me ideal bounds, which
 4    I should first break through, and pour a torrent of light
 5    into our dark world. A new species would bless me as its
 6    creator and source; many happy and excellent natures would
 7    owe their being to me. No father could claim the gratitude
 8    of his child so completely as I should deserve theirs.
 9    Pursuing these reflections, I thought, that if I could
10    bestow animation upon lifeless matter, I might in process
11    of time (although I now found it impossible) renew life
12    where death had apparently devoted the body to corruption.
13
14    These thoughts supported my spirits, while I pursued my
15    undertaking with unremitting ardour. My cheek had grown
16    pale with study, and my person had become emaciated with
17    confinement. Sometimes, on the very brink of certainty, I
18    failed; yet still I clung to the hope which the next day
19    or the next hour might realise. One secret which I alone
20    possessed was the hope to which I had dedicated myself; and
21    the moon gazed on my midnight labours, while, with unrelaxed
22    and breathless eagerness, I pursued nature to her hiding-
23    places. Who shall conceive the horrors of my secret toil as
24    I dabbled among the unhallowed damps of the grave?
```

# Topic 2: Conceptual Notes
**Type:** conceptual
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Frankenstein (1818) was written during a period of rapid scientific advancement and Romantic reaction against Enlightenment rationalism. Understanding the novel's context — galvanism, the French Revolution, Milton's Paradise Lost, and the Promethean myth — is essential for interpreting Shelley's purposes and methods.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4*
**Extract Location:** Volume 1, Chapter 4
**Extract Context:** In this extract from early in Victor's narrative, he reflects on the dangerous allure of forbidden knowledge and the moment he discovered the secret of life.

### Extract
```
 1    When I found so astonishing a power placed within my hands,
 2    I hesitated a long time concerning the manner in which I
 3    should employ it. Although I possessed the capacity of
 4    bestowing animation, yet to prepare a frame for the
 5    reception of it, with all its intricacies of fibres,
 6    muscles, and veins, still remained a work of inconceivable
 7    difficulty and labour. I doubted at first whether I should
 8    attempt the creation of a being like myself, or one of
 9    simpler organisation; but my imagination was too much
10    exalted by my first success to permit me to doubt of my
11    ability to give life to an animal as complex and wonderful
12    as man.
```

# Topic 3: The Creature (monstrosity and humanity)
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The Creature is Shelley's most complex creation — at once sympathetic and terrifying. Born innocent, he is driven to violence by rejection and isolation. Students should analyse how Shelley uses the Creature's eloquent narration to challenge the reader's assumptions about who the real monster is, and consider how his treatment reflects Enlightenment debates about natural goodness versus social corruption.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4*
**Extract Location:** Volume 2, Chapter 7
**Extract Context:** In this extract, the Creature confronts Victor on the Mer de Glace and pleads for understanding, arguing that his violent actions were caused by the cruelty he suffered from humanity.

### Extract
```
 1    'I am malicious because I am miserable. Am I not shunned
 2    and hated by all mankind? You, my creator, would tear me
 3    to pieces, and triumph; remember that, and tell me why I
 4    should pity man more than he pities me? You would not call
 5    it murder if you could precipitate me into one of those
 6    ice-rifts, and destroy my frame, the work of your own
 7    hands. Shall I respect man when he condemns me? Let him
 8    live with me in the interchange of kindness; and, instead
 9    of injury, I would bestow every benefit upon him with
10    tears of gratitude at his acceptance. But that cannot be;
11    the human senses are insurmountable barriers to our union.
12    Yet mine shall not be the submission of abject slavery. I
13    will revenge my injuries: if I cannot inspire love, I will
14    cause fear, and chiefly towards you my arch-enemy, because
15    my creator, do I swear inextinguishable hatred. Have a
16    care: I will work at your destruction, nor finish until I
17    desolate your heart, so that you shall curse the hour of
18    your birth.'
19
20    A fiendish rage animated him as he said this; his face was
21    wrinkled into contortions too horrible for human eyes to
22    behold; but presently he calmed himself and proceeded —
23
24    'I intended to reason. This passion is detrimental to me;
25    for you do not reflect that you are the cause of its excess.'
```

**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Ambition in Frankenstein is presented as a destructive force that blinds its possessors to moral responsibility. Victor's Promethean overreach — his desire to conquer death itself — mirrors the Romantic critique of Enlightenment science. Students should trace how Shelley constructs ambition as a form of madness that isolates the individual and destroys those around them.

# Topic 5: Isolation and loneliness
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Isolation pervades every level of the novel — Victor isolates himself through obsession, the Creature is isolated by his appearance, and Walton is isolated by his Arctic ambition. Shelley presents solitude not as peaceful retreat but as a destructive force that corrupts both creator and creation. Students should analyse how isolation functions as both cause and consequence of the novel's central tragedy.

**Extract Context:** In this extract, the Creature describes his first experiences of rejection by humanity after leaving Victor's apartment, culminating in being driven from the village.

### Extract
```

 5    weapons, I escaped to the open country and fearfully took
 6    refuge in a low hovel, quite bare, and making a wretched

# Topic 6: Creation and responsibility
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The theme of creation and responsibility lies at the heart of the novel's moral argument. Victor creates life but immediately abandons his creation, setting in motion the chain of destruction that follows. Shelley draws on Milton's Paradise Lost to frame the Creature as an abandoned Adam, exploring what a creator owes to the beings it brings into existence. Students should consider how Shelley critiques Victor's failure of parental and moral duty.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4*
**Extract Location:** Volume 2, Chapter 7
**Extract Context:** In this extract, the Creature has finished telling Victor his story and makes his demand: that Victor create a female companion for him, arguing that his creator owes him this act of responsibility.

### Extract
```
 1    'I demand a creature of another sex, but as hideous as
 2    myself; the gratification is small, but it is all that I
 3    can receive, and it shall content me. It is true, we shall

# Topic 7: Nature vs nurture
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Shelley uses the Creature's development to explore whether evil is innate or learned. Born without language, morality, or malice, the Creature educates himself through observation and reading, developing sensitivity and compassion — until repeated rejection turns him to violence. Students should analyse how Shelley presents the Creature's moral evolution as evidence that cruelty is made, not born, connecting this to Enlightenment debates about human nature (Locke, Rousseau).

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4*
**Extract Location:** Volume 2, Chapter 5
**Extract Context:** In this extract, the Creature describes how he secretly observed the De Lacey family from his hiding place beside their cottage, and how witnessing their kindness began to shape his own moral understanding.

### Extract
```

 2    endeared them to me: when they were unhappy, I felt
 3    depressed; when they rejoiced, I sympathised in their joys.
 4    I saw few human beings besides them; and if any other
 5    happened to enter the cottage, their harsh manners and rude
 6    gait only enhanced to me the superior accomplishments of my
 7    friends. The old man, I could perceive, often endeavoured
 8    to encourage his children, as sometimes I found that he

# Topic 8: The sublime and nature
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Shelley, writing within the Romantic tradition, uses the natural world as both a source of consolation and a mirror for emotional turmoil. The Alpine landscapes, the Arctic wastes, and the thunderstorms function as expressions of the sublime — overwhelming natural power that dwarfs human ambition. Students should analyse how settings reflect character psychology and how Shelley uses the Romantic sublime to comment on Victor's transgression against natural order.

**Extract Context:** In this extract, Victor travels to Chamonix and the Alps seeking relief from his guilt and grief after the deaths of William and Justine.

# Topic 10: Knowledge and discovery
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Frankenstein presents knowledge as inherently dangerous when pursued without moral restraint. Victor, Walton, and the Creature all seek knowledge — scientific, geographical, and intellectual — and all suffer for it. Shelley frames the novel as a warning against the Enlightenment belief that progress is always beneficial, using the Promethean subtitle to signal that stealing divine knowledge carries terrible consequences.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4*
**Extract Location:** Letter 4 (Walton's letters)
**Extract Context:** In this extract from the novel's framing narrative, Victor warns Captain Walton about the dangers of pursuing knowledge and ambition, drawing on his own experience as a cautionary tale.

### Extract
```
 1    'You seek for knowledge and wisdom, as I once did; and I
 2    ardently hope that the gratification of your wishes may not

 5    yet, when I reflect that you are pursuing the same course,
 6    exposing yourself to the same dangers which have rendered
 7    me what I am, I imagine that you may deduce an apt moral

 9    your undertaking, and console you in case of failure. Prepare
