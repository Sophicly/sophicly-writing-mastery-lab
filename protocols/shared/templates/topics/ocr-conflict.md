# Topic 1: Dulce et Decorum Est
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Dulce et Decorum Est by Wilfred Owen exposes the lie that dying for one's country is noble through the visceral depiction of a gas attack in the trenches. Students should analyse how Owen uses the dramatic shift from exhausted marching to frantic panic, the grotesque imagery of the dying soldier, and the bitter direct address to the reader to dismantle the patriotic rhetoric of Horace's motto. The irregular sonnet-like structure mirrors the chaos Owen describes.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Focus Poem:** Dulce et Decorum Est
**Focus Poet:** Wilfred Owen

## Question
Compare how the poets present the reality of conflict in 'Dulce et Decorum Est' and the poem printed on the exam paper.

# Topic 3: The Man He Killed
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** The Man He Killed by Thomas Hardy uses the voice of an ordinary soldier reflecting on shooting an enemy in battle. Students should analyse how Hardy uses the conversational tone, the halting repetitions and self-corrections in the central stanza, and the hypothetical pub meeting to expose the senselessness of war -- that the man he killed was someone just like him, driven by poverty rather than ideology.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 5: The Charge of the Light Brigade
**Type:** poetry_named

Students should focus on how Tennyson uses the relentless dactylic rhythm, the repetition of "the six hundred," and the contrast between heroic obedience and catastrophic military blunder to honour the soldiers' bravery whilst implicitly questioning the competence of those who gave the order.

# Topic 8: A Wife in London
**Type:** poetry_named

Students should analyse how Hardy uses the two-part structure, the fog as a symbol of grief and obscured knowledge, and the cruel dramatic irony of the letter's hopeful tone to present war's devastation not on the battlefield but in the domestic world it destroys.

# Topic 12: Come On, Come Back
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Come On, Come Back by Stevie Smith imagines a future war in which a female soldier, Vaudevue, has lost her memory after exposure to a chemical weapon and wanders to a lake where she drowns. Students should analyse how Smith uses the dreamlike, detached narrative voice, the haunting repetition of the title phrase, and the eerie beauty of the moonlit scene to present war as something that destroys identity itself -- leaving its victims not dead but emptied of everything that made them human.
