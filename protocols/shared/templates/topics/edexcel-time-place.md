# Topic 1: Ozymandias
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Ozymandias by Percy Bysshe Shelley uses a ruined statue in the desert to explore the inevitable decline of power and human arrogance. Students should analyse how Shelley uses the framing narrative of the traveller, the ironic juxtaposition of the inscription "Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!" with the surrounding emptiness, and the vast desert imagery to argue that no ruler or civilisation can resist the erosive power of time. The Romantic context and Shelley's radical political beliefs intensify the poem's challenge to tyranny.
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Ozymandias
**Focus Poet:** Percy Bysshe Shelley

## Question
Compare how poets present the power of time over human achievement in 'Ozymandias' and one other poem from the Time and Place anthology.

# Topic 3: London
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** London by William Blake presents a nightmarish vision of the city as a place of institutional oppression and human suffering. Students should analyse how Blake uses the repetition of "every" and "marks," the semantic field of confinement through "charter'd" streets and river, and the oxymoronic "Marriage hearse" to argue that London's suffering is not natural but imposed by church, monarchy, and social systems. The poem's context within Songs of Experience and Blake's radical dissenting politics are essential.
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** London
**Focus Poet:** William Blake

## Question
Compare how poets present a place shaped by suffering or injustice in 'London' and one other poem from the Time and Place anthology.

**Type:** poetry_named

Students should analyse how Wordsworth uses the shift from confidence to awe, the personification of the mountain as a towering, pursuing presence, and the reflective blank verse to explore how a specific place — the lake — becomes the site of a profound psychological transformation. The Romantic belief in nature as a moral teacher underpins the entire episode.

# Topic 13: Kamikaze
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Kamikaze by Beatrice Garland tells the story of a Japanese pilot who turns back from his suicide mission, only to be shunned by his family and community for the rest of his life. # Topic 14: To Autumn
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** To Autumn by John Keats personifies the season as a figure resting amid the harvest, presenting autumn not as decline but as a time of rich abundance and quiet fulfilment. Students should analyse how Keats uses the sensory overload of the opening stanza, the drowsy personification in the second, and the elegiac evening sounds of the third to trace the movement of a single day and an entire season towards ending. The Romantic context, Keats's awareness of his own mortality, and the poem's composition after a walk near Winchester are essential background.

# Topic 15: As Imperceptibly as Grief
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** As Imperceptibly as Grief by Emily Dickinson uses the gradual, almost invisible transition from summer to autumn as an extended metaphor for how grief quietly recedes. Students should analyse how Dickinson uses the slow accumulation of natural imagery, her characteristic dashes and compression, and the paradox of a departure that is both longed-for and mourned to present time and seasonal change as forces that operate beyond human control or perception. The reclusive New England context and Dickinson's preoccupation with loss shape the poem's elusive, meditative quality.

# Topic 16: Storm on the Island
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Storm on the Island by Seamus Heaney describes an island community bracing for and enduring a violent storm, using the experience to explore how nature can reduce human confidence to fear. Students should analyse how Heaney uses the collective "we" voice, the paradoxical "exploding comfortably," and the progression from assurance to vulnerability to present a place whose identity is defined by its exposure to elemental forces. The poem's Northern Irish context and possible allegorical reading — "Stormont" hidden in the title — add a political dimension to the landscape.
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Storm on the Island
**Focus Poet:** Seamus Heaney

## Question
Compare how poets present the experience of enduring nature's power in a specific place in 'Storm on the Island' and one other poem from the Time and Place anthology.
