# Topic 1: When We Two Parted
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Byron's poem explores the pain of a secret love affair that has ended. Its restrained, almost numb tone masks intense bitterness, and the cyclical structure — opening and closing with cold silence — mirrors the speaker's inability to move past betrayal. Students should examine how Byron uses sensory imagery of coldness and death to convey emotional devastation.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** When We Two Parted
**Focus Poet:** Lord Byron

## Question
Compare how poets present the pain of lost love in 'When We Two Parted' and one other poem from the Love and Relationships anthology.

# Topic 3: Love's Philosophy
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Shelley uses the natural world as an extended argument for physical union, building a persuasive case through accumulating examples of things in nature that join together. The poem's driving rhythm and rhetorical questions create an almost playful urgency, but beneath the charm lies a speaker who equates natural law with romantic entitlement. Students should analyse how Shelley's use of listing and personification builds his argument.

# Topic 4: Porphyria's Lover
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Browning's dramatic monologue reveals a speaker whose obsessive love becomes murderous possession. The chilling calm of the narration — describing strangling Porphyria with her own hair as though it were reasonable — forces readers to recognise the horror through the gap between the speaker's tone and his actions. Students should explore how Browning uses the unreliable narrator to expose the dangers of treating a person as an object to be owned.

# Topic 16: Climbing My Grandfather
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Waterhouse sustains a single extended metaphor of climbing a grandfather's body as though it were a mountain, transforming physical closeness into an act of exploration and discovery. The poem's steady, unhurried pace and lack of punctuation create a sense of careful, reverent movement, and the final image of placing an ear to the chest to hear the heartbeat makes the abstract bond between generations physically tangible. Students should examine how Waterhouse uses the climbing metaphor to convey love, trust, and the desire to truly know another person.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Climbing My Grandfather
**Focus Poet:** Andrew Waterhouse
