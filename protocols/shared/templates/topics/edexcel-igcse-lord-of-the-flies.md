# Topic 1: Ralph
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Golding presents Ralph as a symbol of democratic order and rational civilisation, using his initial confidence and physical attractiveness to contrast sharply with his eventual psychological disintegration. His relationship with Piggy — the intellectual he simultaneously relies on and distances himself from — illuminates Golding's critique of leadership that prioritises popularity over principle. As the novel progresses, Ralph's grip on rule-making deteriorates, suggesting that civilisation is a fragile construct maintained only by collective belief. Golding uses Ralph's final breakdown on the beach to argue that the veneer of civilisation conceals deep, instinctual savagery in all humans.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does William Golding present Ralph in *Lord of the Flies*?

You should consider:
- how Golding presents Ralph's qualities as a leader and his changing authority over the boys
- the methods and techniques Golding uses to contrast Ralph with Jack and Piggy
- the significance of Ralph's journey in conveying Golding's wider ideas about civilisation and human nature.

# Topic 3: Jack
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Jack Merridew is Golding's most explicit embodiment of humanity's capacity for violence, introduced as a disciplined choirmaster before descending into a painted warlord who rules through terror and ritual. Golding's use of the mask motif is central: once Jack paints his face, he sheds the inhibitions of civilised identity and acts with increasing cruelty, suggesting that social norms alone restrain violent impulse. His transformation from rule-follower to dictator mirrors historical examples of fascism, inviting readers to read the novel as a post-war allegory. The rhetorical power Jack wields — offering meat and excitement in place of rescue — demonstrates how demagogues exploit immediate desire over long-term reason.

# Topic 4: Piggy
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Piggy represents the rational, scientific intellect that civilised society frequently marginalises, and Golding reinforces this through the physical symbols attached to him: his glasses enable fire and progress, yet his body is mocked and his voice is perpetually ignored. His death — struck by the boulder and falling into the sea — is Golding's most violent symbolic act, signalling the complete destruction of reason and democracy on the island. Despite his social exclusion, Piggy consistently articulates the most morally sound positions, suggesting that Golding sees a tragic gap between intelligence and power. The progressive destruction of his glasses tracks the boys' descent precisely, making Piggy a structural barometer of civilisational collapse.

# Topic 5: Simon
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Simon occupies a unique spiritual role in the novel, associated with nature, vision, and innate goodness that stands apart from both Ralph's pragmatism and Jack's savagery. His conversation with the Lord of the Flies is the novel's most philosophically dense passage, as Golding uses free indirect discourse to have the pig's head voice the truth Simon already intuits: "the beast is within you." Unlike the other boys, Simon does not project fear outward but confronts it directly, making his murder during the frenzied ritual dance a profoundly ironic tragedy. Golding's use of bioluminescence to frame Simon's body as it drifts out to sea elevates him to a Christ-like martyr figure, sacrificed by the very community he sought to enlighten.

# Topic 6: Civilisation vs Savagery
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The conflict between civilisation and savagery is the novel's central structural tension, enacted through the opposition between the conch — a democratic instrument of order — and the Lord of the Flies, a rotting symbol of primal violence. Golding structures the novel so that civilised rules erode incrementally: the signal fire is neglected, the shelters are abandoned, and the conch's authority is openly mocked before it is finally shattered. This trajectory argues that civilisation is not a natural state but a sustained collective performance that collapses under pressure. Written in the aftermath of World War Two, the novel interrogates the Enlightenment faith in human progress by showing that even innocent children, removed from adult supervision, recreate the same hierarchies and atrocities as adult societies.

# Topic 7: Power and Leadership
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Golding presents two competing models of leadership through Ralph and Jack — the democratic, reason-based authority of the conch versus the charismatic, fear-based power of the hunt — ultimately suggesting that the second model is more naturally compelling to a frightened group. Power in the novel is always contingent: Ralph's authority depends on the boys choosing to honour it, while Jack's derives from physical threat and the visceral excitement of the kill. Golding's structural choice to end with a naval officer imposing adult order from outside undercuts any easy resolution, implying that civilised authority must always be enforced externally. This critique of leadership is sharpened by the irony that the officer's warship carries the same potential for violence that the boys have just enacted on a smaller scale.

# Topic 8: Fear and the Beast
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Fear is Golding's primary mechanism for exposing the irrationality lurking beneath civilised behaviour: the beast is never an external creature but a collective psychological projection that the boys externalise to make their own violence explicable. The progression of the beast — from vague night terror, to dead parachutist, to enthroned Lord of the Flies — mirrors the boys' own psychological deterioration, as each new sighting is shaped by the violence the boys have already committed. Simon's recognition that "maybe the beast is only us" is the novel's moral centre, yet it is dismissed by the very group whose behaviour has already confirmed it. Golding's use of the pig's head as a physical manifestation of the beast blurs the boundary between external symbol and internal truth, reinforcing his thesis that evil is an intrinsic human capacity.

# Topic 9: Loss of Innocence
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The boys' progressive loss of innocence is one of Golding's most sustained structural arguments, as the island — initially presented through Edenic imagery of fruit, warmth, and freedom — is systematically transformed into a site of terror and death. Golding tracks this loss through the gradual abandonment of the rules the boys themselves established, suggesting that moral innocence requires constant vigilance rather than being a natural condition. Ralph's weeping at the novel's close — for "the end of innocence, the darkness of man's heart" — is Golding's explicit authorial intervention, naming what the narrative has demonstrated. The choice to use children rather than adults intensifies this loss, stripping away the comforting assumption that violence and corruption are adult vices.

# Topic 10: Violence and Morality
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Golding presents violence not as an aberration but as a seductive force that carries its own logic and pleasure, most clearly in the hunting chants and ritual dances where the boys' inhibitions dissolve into communal frenzy. The escalation from pig-hunting to the murders of Simon and Piggy is structurally precise: each act of violence makes the next easier, demonstrating that moral thresholds erode through incremental violation rather than sudden collapse. Golding raises uncomfortable questions about collective morality by making most of the boys complicit in both murders, denying readers the comfort of a small group of villains. The novel's moral argument is ultimately pessimistic: without external enforcement, the boys are unable to sustain the moral frameworks they themselves recognise as right.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
