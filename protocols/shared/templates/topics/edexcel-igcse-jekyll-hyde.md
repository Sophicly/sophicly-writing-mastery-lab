# Topic 1: Duality — Jekyll and Hyde
**Type:** thematic
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Duality is the central theme of the novella. Stevenson uses the physical split between Jekyll and Hyde to explore the idea that every human contains both good and evil, and that Victorian society's insistence on moral perfection forces the darker self underground rather than eliminating it.

**Marks:** 25
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**Extract Location:** Chapter 10: Henry Jekyll's Full Statement of the Case

### Extract
```
 1  I thus drew steadily nearer to that truth, by whose partial
 2  discovery I have been doomed to such a dreadful shipwreck: that
 3  man is not truly one, but truly two. I say two, because the
 4  state of my own knowledge does not pass beyond that point.
 5  Others will follow, others will outstrip me on the same lines;
 6  and I hazard the guess that man will be ultimately known for a
 7  mere polity of multifarious, incongruous and independent
 8  denizens. I, for my part, from the nature of my life, advanced
 9  infallibly in one direction and in one direction only. It was on
10  the moral side, and in my own person, that I learned to
11  recognise the thorough and primitive duality of man; I saw that,
12  of the two natures that contended in the field of my
13  consciousness, even if I could rightly be said to be either, it
14  was only because I was radically both; and from an early date,
15  even before the course of my scientific discoveries had begun to
16  suggest the most naked possibility of such a miracle, I had
17  learned to dwell with pleasure, as a beloved daydream, on the
18  thought of the separation of these elements. If each, I told
19  myself, could but be housed in separate identities, life would
20  be relieved of all that was unbearable; the unjust might go his
21  way, delivered from the aspirations and remorse of his more
22  upright twin; and the just could walk steadfastly and securely
23  on his upward path, doing the good things in which he found his
24  pleasure, and no longer exposed to disgrace and penitence by the
25  hands of this extraneous evil.
```

**Type:** character
**Format:** either_or

# Topic 4: Jekyll — Repression and Respectability
**Type:** character
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Jekyll is a respected doctor and gentleman who conceals his darker desires behind a facade of respectability. Stevenson uses his confession to show that Victorian society's demand for moral perfection does not eliminate sin but drives it underground, creating the very conditions that make Hyde's emergence inevitable.

## Either
**Marks:** 25
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**Extract Location:** Chapter 10: Henry Jekyll's Full Statement of the Case

### Extract
```
 1  I was born in the year 18— to a large fortune, endowed besides
 2  with excellent parts, inclined by nature to industry, fond of
 3  the respect of the wise and good among my fellow-men, and thus,
 4  as might have been supposed, with every guarantee of an
 5  honourable and distinguished future. And indeed the worst of my
 6  faults was a certain impatient gaiety of disposition, such as
 7  has made the happiness of many, but such as I found it hard to
 8  reconcile with my imperious desire to carry my head high, and
 9  wear a more than commonly grave countenance before the public.
10  Hence it came about that I concealed my pleasures; and that
11  when I reached years of reflection, and began to look round me
12  and take stock of my progress and position in the world, I
13  stood already committed to a profound duplicity of life. Many a
14  man would have even blazoned such irregularities as I was guilty
15  of; but from the high views that I had set before me, I
16  regarded and hid them with an almost morbid sense of shame. It
17  was thus rather the exacting nature of my aspirations than any
18  particular degradation in my faults, that made me what I was
19  and, with even a deeper trench than in the majority of men,
20  severed in me those provinces of good and ill which divide and
21  compound man's dual nature.
```

# Topic 5: Utterson — Rationality and the Victorian Gentleman
**Type:** character
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Utterson is the novella's primary narrator-figure and represents the rational, restrained Victorian gentleman. His loyalty, discretion, and reluctance to judge are presented as admirable qualities, yet Stevenson also shows how these same qualities blind him to the truth. His determination to find a logical explanation for Jekyll's behaviour mirrors the reader's own detective-like engagement with the mystery.

## Either
**Marks:** 25
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**Extract Location:** Chapter 1: Story of the Door

### Extract
```
 1  Mr. Utterson the lawyer was a man of a rugged countenance, that
 2  was never lighted by a smile; cold, scanty and embarrassed in
 3  discourse; backward in sentiment; lean, long, dusty, dreary,
 4  and yet somehow lovable. At friendly meetings, and when the
 5  wine was to his taste, something eminently human beaconed from
 6  his eye; something indeed which never found its way into his
 7  talk, but which spoke not only in these silent symbols of the
 8  after-dinner face, but more often and loudly in the acts of his
 9  life. He was austere with himself; drank gin when he was alone,
10  to mortify a taste for vintages; and though he enjoyed the
11  theatre, had not crossed the doors of one for twenty years. But
12  he had an approved tolerance for others; sometimes wondering,
13  almost with envy, at the high pressure of spirits involved in
14  their misdeeds; and in any extremity inclined to help rather
15  than to reprove. "I incline to Cain's heresy," he used to say
16  quaintly: "I let my brother go to the devil in his own way."
17  In this character, it was frequently his fortune to be the last
18  reputable acquaintance and the last good influence in the lives
19  of down-going men. And to such as these, so long as they came
20  to his chambers, he never marked a shade of change in his
21  demeanour.
```

**Type:** thematic
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Secrecy pervades the novella at every level -- characters keep secrets, the narrative structure withholds information, and locked doors and sealed letters control access to truth. # Topic 7: Science and Religion
**Type:** thematic
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Stevenson wrote during a period of intense debate between scientific progress and religious faith. Jekyll's experiment represents science transgressing moral and spiritual boundaries -- playing God by separating the soul into its component parts. The novella raises questions about whether there are limits to what science should attempt, reflecting Victorian anxieties about Darwinism, vivisection, and the erosion of religious certainty.

# Topic 8: Setting — London, Fog, and Doors
**Type:** thematic
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Stevenson uses London's geography symbolically throughout the novella. The contrast between Jekyll's grand front entrance and Hyde's blistered back door represents the dual nature of Victorian respectability. Fog, darkness, and labyrinthine streets create a Gothic atmosphere that mirrors moral confusion and concealment, while also reflecting the real conditions of 1880s London where respectability and depravity existed side by side.

# Topic 9: Lanyon — The Horror of Knowledge
**Type:** character
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Dr Lanyon represents orthodox, conservative science and the limits of rational understanding. His fatal shock at witnessing Hyde's transformation shows that some knowledge is too terrible to bear. Stevenson uses Lanyon's death to suggest that the truth about human nature -- that evil is not separate from us but part of us -- is so threatening to Victorian certainty that it can literally destroy a man.

# Topic 10: Victorian Society and Hypocrisy
**Type:** thematic
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The novella is fundamentally a critique of Victorian hypocrisy. Stevenson shows a society of outwardly respectable professional men -- lawyers, doctors, politicians -- who all harbour secrets and pass no judgement on each other's hidden lives. The world of the novella is exclusively male and upper-middle-class, and its culture of silence enables Jekyll's double life. Hyde is not an aberration but a product of a society that demands impossible standards of public virtue.
