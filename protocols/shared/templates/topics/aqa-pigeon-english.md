# Topic 1: Harri
**Type:** A-MT
**Teaching Point:** Harri is the first-person narrator whose innocent, optimistic voice contrasts sharply with the violence and deprivation of the estate. Students should analyse how Kelman uses Harri's naivety -- his misunderstanding of gang culture, his detective game, his wonder at everyday English life -- to expose the gap between childhood perception and adult reality, and how his immigrant perspective defamiliarises the reader's assumptions about Britain.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

## Question
How does Kelman use the character of Harri to explore ideas about innocence and the immigrant experience in *Pigeon English*?

Write about:
- what Harri says and does in the novel
- how Kelman presents Harri.

# Topic 3: The Gang and Dean
**Type:** A-MT
**Teaching Point:** Dean and the Dell Farm Crew represent the seductive pull of gang culture on vulnerable young people. # Topic 4: Violence and Knife Crime
**Type:** A-MT
**Teaching Point:** The novel opens with a stabbing and builds toward Harri's own death, framing the entire narrative within a cycle of violence. Students should analyse how Kelman uses Harri's innocent perspective to present knife crime not as sensationalised spectacle but as an ordinary, almost mundane feature of estate life, and how this normalisation is itself the novel's most powerful critique of a society that allows children to grow up surrounded by lethal danger.

# Topic 7: The Pigeon
**Type:** A-MT
**Teaching Point:** The pigeon is both a literal presence on the estate and a symbolic voice that narrates short, italicised sections throughout the novel. Students should analyse how Kelman uses the pigeon as a second narrator -- an observer who sees what Harri cannot -- to foreshadow danger, comment on human behaviour, and provide a detached, almost mythic perspective on the events of the story, linking the bird's freedom and survival instincts to Harri's vulnerability and entrapment.

# Topic 10: Language and Voice
**Type:** A-MT
**Teaching Point:** Kelman's use of Harri's distinctive voice -- a blend of Ghanaian English, London slang, and childish idiom -- is the novel's defining literary technique. Students should analyse how this hybrid language creates humour and pathos simultaneously, how Harri's unique expressions (his similes, his lists, his exclamations) characterise him as both culturally displaced and endlessly adaptable, and how Kelman uses voice itself to explore the relationship between language, identity, and power.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

## Question
How does Kelman use language and voice to present ideas about identity and culture in *Pigeon English*?

Write about:
- how Harri's distinctive way of speaking is used in the novel
- how Kelman uses language to explore ideas about identity and belonging.
