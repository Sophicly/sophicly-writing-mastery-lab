# Topic 1: Pip
**Type:** protagonist
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Pip is both the narrator and the protagonist whose moral development structures the entire novel. His journey from forge apprentice to London gentleman and back again is Dickens' vehicle for examining class, guilt, and self-knowledge. Students must analyse how Dickens uses first-person retrospective narration to create a dual perspective -- the experiencing child and the reflective adult -- and what Pip's errors reveal about Victorian social aspiration.

# Topic 3: Miss Havisham
**Type:** character
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Miss Havisham is one of Dickens' most powerful Gothic creations -- a woman frozen in the moment of her betrayal, who weaponises Estella to take revenge on all men. Students should analyse how Dickens uses imagery of decay, stasis, and fire to present her, and consider how her eventual remorse complicates a reading of her as simply villainous.

# Topic 4: Social Class and Ambition
**Type:** central_theme
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Social class is the engine of the novel's plot. Pip's desire to rise from the working class to become a gentleman drives his choices and his suffering. Dickens uses the contrast between the forge and London, between Joe and the Finches of the Grove, to expose how the Victorian class system corrupts genuine human values. Students should analyse how Dickens critiques the equation of wealth with moral worth.

# Topic 5: Guilt and Conscience
**Type:** theme
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Guilt pervades the novel from its opening scene in the graveyard. Pip's theft for the convict, his shame about Joe, and his horror at discovering the true source of his fortune all generate a sustained exploration of conscience. Dickens shows guilt as both destructive and ultimately redemptive, suggesting that moral awareness -- however painful -- is the foundation of genuine goodness.

# Topic 7: Magwitch
**Type:** character
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Magwitch is the novel's great revelation -- the convict from the marshes who becomes Pip's secret benefactor. His return overturns Pip's assumptions about class and merit, forcing both Pip and the reader to reconsider who is truly worthy of respect. Dickens uses Magwitch to argue that generosity and loyalty matter more than birth or social standing, and to expose the cruelty of a legal system that criminalises poverty.

# Topic 9: Justice and the Law
**Type:** theme
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Dickens presents the Victorian justice system as brutal, class-biased, and fundamentally unjust. From the prison hulks to Jaggers' handwashing rituals to Magwitch's death sentence, the law punishes poverty rather than protecting the vulnerable. Students should analyse how Dickens uses settings, characters, and plot to distinguish between legal justice and moral justice, and consider how his own experiences as a court reporter shaped his critique.

# Topic 10: Education and Self-Improvement
**Type:** theme
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Education in Great Expectations operates on two levels: formal learning (Biddy's school, Mr Pocket's tutoring) and moral education (the lessons Pip learns through suffering and experience). Dickens explores how true self-improvement is not about acquiring polish or social graces but about developing empathy, humility, and self-knowledge. Students should analyse how Dickens contrasts superficial and genuine education throughout the novel.
