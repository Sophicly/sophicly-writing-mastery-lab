# Topic 1: Ozymandias
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Shelley's sonnet uses a fragmented statue in a desert to expose the futility of tyrannical power. The layered narration — traveller to speaker to reader — distances us from Ozymandias while the ironic juxtaposition of "Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!" against "Nothing beside remains" delivers the poem's central argument. Students should analyse how Shelley subverts the sonnet form to mirror the collapse of authority.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Ozymandias
**Focus Poet:** Percy Bysshe Shelley

## Question

# Topic 3: Mametz Wood
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Sheers commemorates the 1916 Battle of Mametz Wood by imagining the unearthing of soldiers' remains decades later. The poem transforms the battlefield into a site of fragile beauty — bones become "broken bird's egg" and arms link "like a dance macabre." Students should analyse how Sheers uses the earth as a metaphor for memory, and how the final image of the open mouths singing challenges the idea that the dead are truly silent.

**Focus Poem:** Mametz Wood
**Focus Poet:** Owen Sheers

# Topic 4: Valentine
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Duffy rejects conventional romantic gifts in favour of an onion as a symbol of honest love — layered, capable of causing tears, and persistent. The poem's direct, assertive tone and short, declarative sentences challenge sentimentality while the increasingly threatening imagery ("lethal," "cling to your knife") reveals love's possessive undertow. Students should examine how Duffy uses the extended metaphor to present love as complex rather than idealised.

# Topic 5: Sonnet 43
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Barrett Browning's Petrarchan sonnet attempts to quantify infinite love through a series of measured comparisons — "to the depth and breadth and height / My soul can reach." The religious language and references to "Grace" and "childhood's faith" elevate romantic love to a spiritual plane. Students should analyse how the rigid sonnet structure both contains and intensifies the speaker's overwhelming emotion, and how the poem's Victorian context shapes its declarations.

# Topic 6: Dulce et Decorum Est
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Owen's poem dismantles the patriotic lie that dying for one's country is noble by forcing the reader to witness a gas attack in visceral, nightmarish detail. The shift from exhausted marching to frantic action to haunting memory creates a three-part structure that mirrors trauma itself. Students should analyse how Owen uses the direct address to "My friend" and the bitter Latin title to target those who glorify war from safety.

# Topic 8: As Imperceptibly as Grief
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Dickinson traces the almost invisible departure of summer as an extended metaphor for the way grief quietly recedes. Students should analyse how Dickinson uses the seasonal metaphor and her characteristic punctuation to explore loss, time, and the difficulty of pinpointing emotional change.

# Topic 9: Hawk Roosting
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Hughes gives voice to a hawk whose monologue reveals absolute, unapologetic power — "I kill where I please because it is all mine." The poem can be read as a study of nature's indifference to morality or as an allegory for dictatorship and unchecked authority. Students should analyse how Hughes uses the first-person voice, the absence of metaphor from the hawk's perspective, and the controlled, declarative syntax to present power as something that requires no justification.

# Topic 10: The Soldier
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Brooke's Petrarchan sonnet presents dying in war as a noble, almost beautiful sacrifice — the soldier's body will make a foreign field "forever England." Written before Brooke experienced real combat, the poem's idealism and patriotic fervour contrast sharply with later war poetry. Students should analyse how Brooke uses the pastoral imagery and gentle rhythm to create a romanticised vision of death, and consider how context shapes our reading of the poem's sincerity.

# Topic 11: She Walks in Beauty
**Type:** poetry_named

# Topic 14: Death of a Naturalist
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Heaney traces a boy's loss of innocence through the shift from fascinated frogspawn-collecting to revulsion at the adult frogs' aggressive return to the dam. The poem's two-stanza structure mirrors the before-and-after of childhood wonder collapsing into fear, and the language shifts from warm, sensory descriptions to threatening, militaristic imagery. Students should analyse how Heaney uses sound, texture, and the contrast between the two stanzas to present growing up as a violent rupture rather than a gentle transition.

# Topic 15: Invictus
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Henley wrote this poem while recovering from the amputation of his leg, and its defiant declaration — "I am the master of my fate, / I am the captain of my soul" — has become one of literature's most recognised statements of personal resilience. The tight quatrains, strong rhyme scheme, and martial vocabulary create an unshakeable sense of resolve. Students should analyse how Henley uses the extended metaphor of darkness and the direct, commanding voice to present the human will as unconquerable even in suffering.
**Marks:** 40

**Focus Poem:** Invictus

# Topic 16: To Autumn
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Keats personifies autumn as a figure caught in various attitudes — sitting on a granary floor, drowsing by a cider-press, watching the last gleanings. The poem moves from abundance through stillness to the dying sounds of evening, tracing the season's arc without ever mentioning death directly. Students should analyse how Keats uses rich sensory imagery, the three-stanza structure mirroring autumn's progression, and the acceptance of transience to present beauty and mortality as inseparable.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** To Autumn
**Focus Poet:** John Keats
