# Topic 1: The Narrator (Everyman, Survival, Colonial Guilt)
**Type:** character
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The unnamed Narrator functions as an everyman figure whose educated, rational perspective is systematically dismantled by the Martian invasion. Wells uses him to expose the complacency of imperial Britain: a man who once viewed colonised peoples with detachment is forced to experience the terror of being colonised himself, creating a powerful ironic reversal that underpins the entire novel.

# Topic 3: The Martians (Alien Threat and Colonialism Reversed)
**Type:** character
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The Martians are not simply alien monsters but a deliberate mirror of British imperialism. Wells inverts the colonial relationship: an advanced civilisation arrives to exploit and exterminate a less technologically developed population. Their lack of emotion and purely functional biology forces Victorian readers to see themselves through the eyes of the colonised.

# Topic 4: Science and Technology
**Type:** theme
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Wells, himself a science student of T.H. Huxley, uses the novel to explore the limits of human scientific confidence. Victorian Britain's technological supremacy — its warships, artillery, and industrial infrastructure — is rendered utterly useless against the Martians' Heat-Ray and Black Smoke. The novel warns that technological progress does not guarantee survival or moral superiority.

# Topic 5: Imperialism and Colonialism
**Type:** theme
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The War of the Worlds is fundamentally a critique of imperialism. Wells explicitly compares the Martian invasion to the European extermination of the Tasmanians, forcing his readers to recognise that what they are experiencing in fiction is what colonised peoples experienced in reality. The novel asks: if a more advanced civilisation has the right to conquer, what moral objection can Britain raise when it becomes the victim?

# Topic 6: Survival and Fear
**Type:** theme
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The invasion strips away the veneer of civilisation and exposes the primal instinct for survival. Wells shows how quickly social norms collapse under existential threat: crowds stampede, families are separated, and individuals resort to violence against each other. Fear is presented not as weakness but as the fundamental human response that both preserves and degrades humanity.

# Topic 7: The Destruction of Civilisation
**Type:** theme
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Wells systematically demolishes every pillar of Victorian civilisation — military, infrastructure, government, commerce, and social order. The ruined landscapes he describes anticipate the devastation of twentieth-century total war. By reducing London to rubble, Wells forces readers to confront the fragility of the systems they take for granted and the speed with which order can collapse into chaos.

# Topic 8: Religion and Faith (The Curate)
**Type:** character
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The curate represents the failure of organised religion to provide comfort or meaning in the face of genuine catastrophe. His descent from conventional piety into hysteria and madness contrasts sharply with the Narrator's pragmatic rationalism. Wells, a committed atheist, uses the curate to argue that religious faith crumbles when confronted with events that cannot be reconciled with a benevolent God.

# Topic 9: Nature and Evolution
**Type:** theme
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The novel is deeply informed by Darwinian evolutionary theory. The Martians represent a possible future for humanity — intellectually advanced but physically diminished, devoid of emotion or compassion. Wells reverses the concept of survival of the fittest: it is not human ingenuity but humble bacteria — the lowest organisms on Earth — that ultimately defeat the Martians, suggesting that nature operates by laws that transcend any species' technology or intelligence.

# Topic 10: Human Resilience and Adaptation
**Type:** theme
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Despite the collapse of civilisation, Wells shows humanity's capacity to endure, adapt, and rebuild. The artilleryman's underground survival plan — however delusional — represents one response to catastrophe, while the Narrator's dogged persistence represents another. The novel ultimately affirms that human resilience lies not in military power but in the stubborn will to survive and the ability to learn from devastation.
