# Topic 1: Stanhope
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Stanhope is the protagonist whose psychological disintegration under the pressure of trench warfare drives the play. Students should analyse how Sherriff presents a young man who is simultaneously an exceptional leader and a deeply damaged individual, using his alcoholism, his fear of Raleigh's hero worship exposing his vulnerability, and his fierce sense of duty to argue that war destroys even its best men from the inside out.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

## Question
How does Sherriff use the character of Stanhope to explore ideas about the psychological cost of war in *Journey's End*?

Write about:
- what Stanhope says and does
- how Sherriff presents Stanhope.

# Topic 4: The Futility of War
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Futility is the dominant theme of the play. Sherriff builds a slow, inevitable march towards a raid and a final assault that the officers know will achieve nothing meaningful, yet they carry out their orders regardless. Students should analyse how Sherriff uses dramatic irony, the confined setting, and the contrast between the generals' detached orders and the officers' lived reality to construct a sustained argument that the First World War was a catastrophic waste of human life.

# Topic 5: Duty and Heroism
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Sherriff interrogates what heroism really means in wartime. Students should analyse how the officers fulfil their duty not through dramatic battlefield courage but through the quiet, grinding endurance of daily trench life — inspecting trenches, writing letters, leading raids they know are pointless. Sherriff presents true heroism as the choice to carry on despite fear, exhaustion, and the knowledge that death is likely, complicating any simple or glorified notion of wartime bravery.

# Topic 6: Osborne
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Osborne is the moral and emotional anchor of the company. Students should analyse how Sherriff uses Osborne's calm wisdom, his fatherly care for the younger officers, and his quiet acceptance of the suicidal raid to represent the best qualities that war destroys. His death is the play's emotional turning point, and the muted, uncomfortable dinner scene that follows is Sherriff's most devastating commentary on how war forces men to suppress grief in order to survive.

# Topic 7: Fear and Coping Mechanisms
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Every character in the play copes with fear differently: Stanhope drinks, Hibbert feigns illness, Trotter eats and draws circles, Osborne reads and maintains routine. Students should analyse how Sherriff uses these varied responses to show that fear is universal and inescapable in the trenches, and that the ways men manage their terror reveal both the resilience and the fragility of the human mind under prolonged, unbearable stress.

# Topic 8: Class and the Officer System
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** The play is set entirely among public-school-educated officers, and Sherriff uses their language, behaviour, and relationships to expose the class assumptions built into the British military. Students should analyse how the officer system depends on shared codes of honour, understatement, and emotional suppression rooted in the public-school tradition, and how Sherriff reveals both the strengths and the absurdities of a military hierarchy structured around social class rather than merit.

# Topic 9: Comradeship and Loyalty
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** The bonds between the officers are the only source of warmth in the play's bleak world. Students should analyse how Sherriff presents comradeship as both a survival mechanism and a source of profound pain — the closer the men become, the more devastating each loss. The relationships between Stanhope and Osborne, Osborne and Raleigh, and ultimately Stanhope and Raleigh demonstrate that loyalty in wartime is inseparable from grief.

# Topic 10: The Dugout — Setting as Theme
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** The entire play takes place in a single dugout, and Sherriff uses this confined, claustrophobic space as a dramatic device to intensify every conflict and emotion. Students should analyse how the setting traps the characters physically and psychologically, how the sounds of war penetrate from above, and how the contrast between the mundane domestic details of dugout life and the violence outside creates a sustained tension that mirrors the unbearable pressure of trench warfare.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4
