# Topic 1: Nurse's Song (Innocence) and Nurse's Song (Experience)
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Blake's paired Nurse's Songs are the ideal entry point to the Youth and Age cluster because they present the same scenario -- a nurse watching children play -- from radically opposed perspectives. Students should analyse how the Innocence version uses reassuring dialogue, natural imagery, and a regular rhythm to convey trust and joy, while the Experience version employs bitter irony, wintry imagery, and a controlling tone to reveal adult disillusionment. Comparing the two poems teaches the core skill of contrasting presentation across a shared theme.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Focus Poem:** Nurse's Song (Innocence) / Nurse's Song (Experience)
**Focus Poet:** William Blake

## Question
**(a)** Compare how Blake presents attitudes to childhood in 'Nurse's Song' from *Songs of Innocence* and 'Nurse's Song' from *Songs of Experience*.

[20 marks]

**(b)** Explore in detail how Blake uses language and structure to convey the speaker's feelings in 'Nurse's Song' from *Songs of Experience*.

# Topic 3: Holy Thursday (Innocence)
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Holy Thursday from Songs of Innocence depicts the annual procession of charity-school children to St Paul's Cathedral. Students should analyse how Blake uses the visual imagery of colour and light, the simile of children as "flowers of London town," and the hymn-like rhythm to create a surface impression of benevolence -- while subtle details such as the "grey-headed beadles" and the children walking "two & two" hint at institutional control beneath the apparent celebration.

# Topic 5: The Song of the Old Mother
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Yeats presents the exhausting daily labour of an elderly woman through a first-person dramatic monologue. Students should analyse how Yeats uses the contrast between the old mother's relentless work (kindling fire, scrubbing, cleaning) and the idle young who "dream" and "lie long," the cyclical structure that mirrors her repetitive routine, and the final image of the fading fire to explore how ageing brings both wisdom and weariness, and how youth takes its ease for granted.

# Topic 6: Follower
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Heaney presents a child's admiration for his father's farming skill, then reverses the relationship in the final stanza as the ageing father now follows the adult son. Students should analyse how Heaney uses the precise technical vocabulary of ploughing, the regular quatrains that mirror the furrow's discipline, the physical imagery of the father's power ("His shoulders globed like a full sail"), and the poignant final reversal to explore how the passage of time transforms the dynamic between parent and child.

# Topic 7: Climbing My Grandfather
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Waterhouse uses an extended metaphor of a child climbing a mountain to represent exploring a grandfather's body and character. Students should analyse how the sustained conceit maps physical features onto landscape (the "dusty" hand, the "summit" of the head), how the free verse mirrors the unplanned, exploratory nature of the climb, and how the lack of punctuation at key moments conveys breathless intimacy. The poem celebrates the bond between generations through touch and closeness rather than words.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Focus Poem:** Climbing My Grandfather
**Focus Poet:** Andrew Waterhouse

# Topic 8: Old Man, Old Man
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Fanthorpe presents an elderly father's decline from capable independence to frailty, observed by his adult daughter. Students should analyse how Fanthorpe uses the contrast between past tense (the man who could "do anything") and present tense helplessness, the cataloguing of DIY skills now lost, the shift from third person to direct address ("Let me"), and the tentative, reaching tone of the final stanza to explore role reversal, vulnerability, and the difficulty of offering care to someone who once needed no help.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Focus Poem:** Old Man, Old Man

# Topic 11: Piano
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Lawrence presents a man overwhelmed by childhood memories triggered by hearing a woman sing. # Topic 12: On Ageing
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Angelou presents ageing as a source of pride and defiance rather than decline. Students should analyse how Angelou uses the imperative opening ("When you see me sitting quietly"), the rhythmic, speech-like cadences, the refusal to accept pity or diminishment, and the final declaration of inner vitality to challenge stereotypes about old age. The poem's directness and humour make it an effective counterpoint to poems that present ageing as loss, offering students a contrasting perspective on the theme.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Focus Poem:** On Ageing
**Focus Poet:** Maya Angelou
