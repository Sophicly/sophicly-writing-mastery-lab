# Topic 1: Social Responsibility
**Type:** thematic
**Teaching Point:** Priestley uses the Inspector as a mouthpiece to argue that society functions as an interconnected community, not as isolated individuals. Students should explore how the play's structure — the gradual revelation of each character's involvement — reinforces the message that responsibility cannot be evaded.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does Priestley present ideas about social responsibility in *An Inspector Calls*? You must consider language, form and structure in your answer.

# Topic 3: Sheila Birling
**Type:** character
**Teaching Point:** Sheila's journey from a naive, self-absorbed young woman to the moral conscience of the family is the play's clearest example of generational hope. Students should examine how Priestley uses her language shifts — from light slang to direct, challenging statements — to dramatise her transformation.

# Topic 4: Mr Birling
**Type:** character
**Teaching Point:** Mr Birling is Priestley's vehicle for satirising capitalist self-interest and wilful ignorance. His dramatic irony — confidently wrong predictions about the Titanic and war — is placed deliberately at the start of the play so the audience distrusts him from the outset. Students should explore how his language reveals pride, bluster, and an inability to accept responsibility.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 5: Mrs Birling
**Type:** character
**Teaching Point:** Mrs Birling represents the entrenched prejudice of the upper class. Her refusal to accept any blame — even when her own actions are shown to have fatal consequences — makes her the most resistant character in the play. Students should analyse how Priestley uses her cold, formal language and the dramatic structure of Act Two to expose her hypocrisy.

# Topic 6: Eric Birling
**Type:** character
**Teaching Point:** Eric's storyline — drinking, the forced relationship with Eva, and theft from his father's business — reveals how privilege without guidance leads to destructive behaviour. Unlike his parents, Eric does accept responsibility, aligning him with Sheila as part of the younger generation's capacity for change. Students should consider how Priestley withholds Eric's full involvement until Act Three for maximum dramatic impact.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 7: The Inspector
**Type:** character
**Teaching Point:** The Inspector operates outside the realistic conventions of the play — he knows things before being told, controls the pace of revelations, and delivers the final moral message directly to the audience. Students should explore how Priestley uses his ambiguous identity and commanding language to create a figure who functions as judge, prophet, and dramatic device simultaneously.

# Topic 8: Gerald Croft
**Type:** character
**Teaching Point:** Gerald occupies a complex middle ground — he genuinely helped Eva/Daisy for a time, yet ultimately abandoned her when it became inconvenient. His willingness to investigate whether the Inspector was real aligns him with the older generation's desire to escape consequences. Students should analyse how Priestley uses Gerald to show that charm and apparent kindness can still serve self-interest.

# Topic 9: Class
**Type:** thematic
**Teaching Point:** Class divisions underpin every interaction in the play — from Mr Birling's sacking of Eva for requesting higher wages to Mrs Birling's refusal to help a pregnant working-class woman. Priestley structures the play so that each act exposes a different dimension of class exploitation. Students should explore how language registers, stage directions, and the Birlings' dining room setting all reinforce the class hierarchy the Inspector challenges.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 10: Generational Divide
**Type:** thematic
**Teaching Point:** The split between the older Birlings (who reject responsibility) and the younger generation (who accept it) is central to Priestley's socialist message. Writing in 1945 for a post-war audience, Priestley uses this divide to argue that meaningful social change depends on the young. Students should examine how the play's cyclical ending — the second phone call — punishes the older generation's complacency while vindicating Sheila and Eric's moral growth.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does Priestley use the differences between the older and younger generations to present his ideas in *An Inspector Calls*? You must consider language, form and structure in your answer.
