# Topic 1: Sherlock Holmes — Intellect, Deduction, Isolation
**Type:** protagonist
**Teaching Point:** Holmes is defined by his extraordinary intellect and powers of deduction, but Conan Doyle also presents him as emotionally detached and dependent on stimulation. Students should explore how Holmes's brilliance isolates him from ordinary human experience, and how Conan Doyle uses him to examine whether pure reason comes at a personal cost.

# Topic 3: Watson — Loyalty, Narrator, Everyman
**Type:** character
**Teaching Point:** Watson serves as both narrator and emotional anchor of the novel. As an everyman figure, he provides a contrast to Holmes's cold rationalism, and his loyalty to Holmes drives much of the action. Students should explore how Conan Doyle uses Watson's first-person narration to control the reader's access to information, and how his romantic subplot with Mary Morstan humanises the detective story.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

**Teaching Point:** Conan Doyle presents a complex picture of justice in the novel, where legal justice and moral justice do not always align. # Topic 5: The British Empire and Colonialism
**Type:** theme
**Teaching Point:** The Sign of the Four is deeply embedded in the context of the British Empire. The Agra treasure originates in colonial India, and the entire plot is driven by imperial violence and exploitation. Students should explore how Conan Doyle presents empire as both a source of adventure and a corrupting force, and how characters like Small and Sholto are shaped by their colonial experiences.

**Teaching Point:** Holmes represents the triumph of scientific method in an age of rapid advancement. # Topic 7: Wealth and Greed
**Type:** theme
**Teaching Point:** The Agra treasure drives every major event in the novel: betrayal, murder, obsession, and pursuit. Conan Doyle presents wealth as a corrupting force that destroys those who seek it — Sholto dies in fear, Small is consumed by vengeance, and the treasure itself is ultimately lost. Students should analyse how Conan Doyle uses the treasure as a symbol and consider what the novel suggests about the moral cost of greed.

# Topic 10: Loyalty and Friendship

**Teaching Point:** Loyalty operates on multiple levels in the novel: Watson's devotion to Holmes, Small's bond with Tonga, and the pact of "the sign of the four" itself. Conan Doyle presents loyalty as both noble and dangerous — it can inspire selfless courage but also bind people to destructive courses of action. Students should explore how different forms of loyalty are presented and what Conan Doyle suggests about the limits of faithful devotion.
