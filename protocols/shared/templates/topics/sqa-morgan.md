# Topic 1: In the Snack-bar
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** In the Snack-bar follows the speaker helping a blind, elderly man navigate a Glasgow cafe to the basement toilet. Morgan uses the slow, halting pace of the journey, the visceral physical detail of the man's struggle, and the contrast between the indifferent crowd and the speaker's quiet compassion to explore dignity, isolation, and society's failure to care for its most vulnerable. Students should analyse how the poem's structure mirrors the painful journey and how Morgan withholds sentimentality to make the emotional impact more powerful.
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** none
**Focus Poem:** In the Snack-bar
**Focus Poet:** Edwin Morgan

## Question
Choose a poem by Edwin Morgan which explores the theme of human suffering or compassion. By referring to poetic techniques, show how the poet explores this theme effectively.

# Topic 3: Trio
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Trio captures a fleeting moment of joy as three young people walk down Buchanan Street on Christmas Eve carrying a chihuahua, a baby, and a guitar. Morgan uses the snapshot-like observation, the accumulation of warm detail, and the sudden shift into a triumphant declaration that their happiness defeats "the vale of tears" to celebrate spontaneous human connection. Students should analyse how the poem's structure moves from observation to transcendence and how Morgan uses religious imagery to elevate an ordinary moment into something sacred.

# Topic 4: Good Friday
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Good Friday presents a bus journey through Glasgow on a religious holiday where three drunken men share a fish supper, creating an unintentional parallel with Christ's disciples and the Last Supper. Morgan uses the contrast between the sacred and the mundane, the careful observation of the men's camaraderie, and the ambiguous ending to explore how meaning and fellowship can emerge in unexpected places. Students should analyse how Morgan layers religious allusion beneath a realist Glasgow scene and how the poem challenges assumptions about where holiness resides.

# Topic 5: Winter
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Winter presents the breakdown of a relationship through the metaphor of a harsh, frozen landscape. Morgan uses the bitter cold, the imagery of separation and silence, and the sparse, clipped language to convey emotional distance and loss. The poem's setting externalises the speaker's inner desolation, making the season itself an expression of grief. Students should analyse how Morgan uses the pathetic fallacy of winter, the structure of the poem, and the contrast between warmth and cold to explore the pain of a relationship's end.

# Topic 6: Hyena
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Hyena is a dramatic monologue in which the scavenging animal speaks directly to the reader, justifying its role in the natural world with chilling logic and dark humour. Morgan uses the hyena's unapologetic voice, the unsettling intimacy of its address, and the blurring of boundaries between animal instinct and human cruelty to force readers to confront uncomfortable truths about nature, death, and survival. Students should analyse how Morgan's choice of persona, the conversational tone, and the disturbing imagery create a poem that challenges readers' moral assumptions.

# Topic 7: Slate
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Slate uses the image of a school slate to explore memory, education, and the passage of time. Morgan traces how the slate — once a tool for childhood learning — becomes a symbol for what is written, erased, and lost as life progresses. The poem's layered imagery connects the physical surface of the slate to the fragility of human memory and experience. Students should analyse how Morgan uses the extended metaphor, the interplay between permanence and erasure, and the poem's reflective tone to explore how the past shapes identity.

# Topic 8: The Starlings in George Square
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** The Starlings in George Square describes the authorities' failed attempts to drive thousands of starlings from a Glasgow square, transforming a real urban event into a playful meditation on nature's refusal to be controlled. Morgan uses the escalating absurdity of the council's methods, the vivid sound imagery of the birds, and the celebratory tone to present the starlings as a force of anarchic vitality. Students should analyse how Morgan uses humour, rhythm, and the contrast between bureaucratic order and natural chaos to explore freedom and resistance.

# Topic 9: Glasgow Sonnet (i)
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Glasgow Sonnet (i) uses the strict Petrarchan sonnet form to describe the squalid reality of a high-rise flat in Glasgow — the broken lift, the vandalised surroundings, the family trapped in poverty. Morgan's deliberate choice of a form associated with love and beauty to contain images of deprivation creates a powerful irony that forces the reader to confront social inequality. Students should analyse how Morgan exploits the tension between the sonnet's elegant structure and its bleak content, and how the accumulation of concrete detail builds a damning portrait of urban neglect.

**AOs:** none
**Focus Poem:** Glasgow Sonnet (i)
