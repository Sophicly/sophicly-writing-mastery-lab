## Text One (Unseen)
**Title:** Riots Reveal London's Two Disparate Worlds
**Author:** Martin Fletcher (c. 2011)
**Context:** 21st Century non-fiction. An extract from an article by NBC News correspondent Martin Fletcher, published around 2011. Fletcher reports on the London riots and the sharp socio-economic divides they exposed.

1  LONDON - You really don't have to look very far for the reasons for London's rioting, looting
2  and generally thuggish frenzy of violence, located mostly in poorer, peripheral communities.
3  The lives of most rioters are so far removed from those of the country's leaders it's like they
4  occupy different planets.
5  Think about this: To cope with the violence, London's Mayor Boris Johnson interrupted his
6  family vacation in North America.
7  Prime Minister David Cameron returned from his vacation in a (shared) ten thousand pounds a
8  week villa in Italy where he was reportedly taking tennis lessons with a coach flown out from
9  Britain.
10 Home Secretary Theresa May cut short her vacation in Switzerland.
11 Deputy Prime Minister Nick Clegg returned from his vacation in France.
12 I was wondering as I watched, from a safe distance, a mob of young men, running, looting and
13 throwing bottles, when the last time was that one of them had a vacation. Probably never. I
14 thought, this is their vacation. It's a break from their routine of hanging around street-corners.
15 It's fun, for them. They were laughing as police chased them.
16 Don't get me wrong; they should arrest every last person who broke the law and terrified
17 innocent residents. But is it really surprising that under-educated, jobless youths go on the
18 rampage, faced with the inconceivable gap between the rich and the poor and their sense of
19 utter hopelessness and disenfranchisement?
20 They feel they deserve a slice of the cake too. Their sense of entitlement is this: We are
21 entitled to steal what we can.
22 Violence and lawlessness spread across London as the capital's most senior police officer
23 urged the public to clear the streets in affected communities. Property and vehicles have
24 been set on fire in several areas, some burning out of control.
25 As the young and poor loot, burn and destroy, how about this: It was reported this week that
26 106 Bond Street, one of London's most prestigious shopping addresses, was sold for 28.5
27 million pounds (around $47 million). Cash. To the son of an entrepreneur. Mind-bogglingly,
28 there were 22 other cash buyers bidding for the property.
29 It was also reported this week that an average-wage earner who wants to buy a London
30 house would have to save his/her entire salary for 31 years.
31 A jobless youth in Peckham or Hackney may as well live on Mars for all his chances of legally
32 obtaining a flatscreen TV or a laptop computer. So egged on by their buddies, jazzed up by
33 the screaming mob, they're having a ball. Carpe diem. Seize the moment, and grab what you
34 can.
35 Anger. Hopelessness. A complete lack of social values. All this is true. But the word on the
36 street does not mention any of the above. The word that spread among youth and children as
37 young as eight is much simpler: Go to the shops, grab what you can, while you can.
38 But where are the parents? As youngsters aged 8 to 18 and older go home with their stolen
39 booty, what are their parents saying? So far there isn't one report of goods being returned to
40 their rightful owners.
41 A final thought. One reporter pointed out that in Clapham where the shopping area had been
42 picked clean, the only shop left unlooted and untouched was the book shop.

## Text Two (Anthology)
**Title:** The Danger of a Single Story
**Author:** Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie (2009)
**Context:** 21st Century non-fiction. Adapted from a TED Talk delivered by Nigerian novelist Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie. She speaks about the power of storytelling and the danger of allowing a single narrative to define a person, a people, or a place.

1  I'm a storyteller. And I would like to tell you a few personal stories about what I like to call
2  "the danger of the single story." I grew up on a university campus in eastern Nigeria. My
3  mother says that I started reading at the age of two, although I think four is probably close
4  to the truth. So I was an early reader, and what I read were British and American children's
5  books.
6  I was also an early writer, and when I began to write, at about the age of seven, stories in
7  pencil with crayon illustrations that my poor mother was obligated to read, I wrote exactly
8  the kinds of stories I was reading: all my characters were white and blue-eyed, they played
9  in the snow, they ate apples, and they talked a lot about the weather, how lovely it was that
10 the sun had come out.
11 Now, this despite the fact that I lived in Nigeria. I had never been outside Nigeria. We didn't
12 have snow, we ate mangoes, and we never talked about the weather, because there was no
13 need to.
14 What this demonstrates, I think, is how impressionable and vulnerable we are in the face
15 of a story, particularly as children. Because all I had read were books in which characters
16 were foreign, I had become convinced that books by their very nature had to have foreigners
17 in them and had to be about things with which I could not personally identify. Now, things
18 changed when I discovered African books. There weren't many of them available, and they
19 weren't quite as easy to find as the foreign books.
20 But because of writers like Chinua Achebe and Camara Laye, I went through a mental shift
21 in my perception of literature. I realized that people like me, girls with skin the colour of
22 chocolate, whose kinky hair could not form ponytails, could also exist in literature. I started
23 to write about things I recognized.
24 Now, I loved those American and British books I read. They stirred my imagination. They
25 opened up new worlds for me. But the unintended consequence was that I did not know that
26 people like me could exist in literature. So what the discovery of African writers did for me
27 was this: it saved me from having a single story of what books are.
28 I come from a conventional, middle-class Nigerian family. My father was a professor. My
29 mother was an administrator. And so we had, as was the norm, live-in domestic help, who
30 would often come from nearby rural villages. So, the year I turned eight, we got a new house
31 boy. His name was Fide. The only thing my mother told us about him was that his family was
32 very poor. My mother sent yams and rice, and our old clothes, to his family. And when I
33 didn't finish my dinner, my mother would say, "Finish your food! Don't you know? People
34 like Fide's family have nothing." So I felt enormous pity for Fide's family.
35 Then one Saturday, we went to his village to visit, and his mother showed us a beautifully
36 patterned basket made of dyed raffia that his brother had made. I was startled. It had not
37 occurred to me that anybody in his family could actually make something. All I had heard
38 about them was how poor they were, so that it had become impossible for me to see them
39 as anything else but poor. Their poverty was my single story of them.
40 Years later, I thought about this when I left Nigeria to go to university in the United States.
41 I was 19. My American roommate was shocked by me. She asked where I had learned to
42 speak English so well, and was confused when I said that Nigeria happened to have English
43 as its official language. She asked if she could listen to what she called my "tribal music",
44 and was consequently very disappointed when I produced my tape of Mariah Carey.
45 She assumed that I did not know how to use a stove.
46 What struck me was this: she had felt sorry for me even before she saw me. Her default
47 position toward me, as an African, was a kind of patronizing, well-meaning pity. My
48 roommate had a single story of Africa: a single story of catastrophe. In this single story,
49 there was no possibility of Africans being similar to her in any way, no possibility of feelings
50 more complex than pity, no possibility of a connection as human equals.
51 So, after I had spent some years in the U.S. as an African, I began to understand my
52 roommate's response to me. If I had not grown up in Nigeria, and if all I knew about Africa
53 were from popular images, I too would think that Africa was a place of beautiful landscapes,
54 beautiful animals, and incomprehensible people, fighting senseless wars, dying of poverty
55 and AIDS, unable to speak for themselves and waiting to be saved by a kind, white
56 foreigner. I would see Africans in the same way that I, as a child, had seen Fide's family.
57 But I must quickly add that I too am just as guilty in the question of the single story. A few
58 years ago, I visited Mexico from the U.S. The political climate in the U.S. at the time was
59 tense, and there were debates going on about immigration. And, as often happens in
60 America, immigration became synonymous with Mexicans. There were endless stories of
61 Mexicans as people who were fleecing the healthcare system, sneaking across the border,
62 being arrested at the border, that sort of thing.
63 I remember walking around on my first day in Guadalajara, watching the people going to
64 work, rolling up tortillas in the marketplace, smoking, laughing. I remember first feeling
65 slight surprise. And then, I was overwhelmed with shame. I realized that I had been so
66 immersed in the media coverage of Mexicans that they had become one thing in my mind,
67 the abject immigrant. I had bought into the single story of Mexicans and I could not have
68 been more ashamed of myself.
69 So that is how to create a single story, show a people as one thing, as only one thing, over
70 and over again, and that is what they become.
71 Stories matter. Many stories matter. Stories have been used to dispossess and to malign,
72 but stories can also be used to empower and to humanize. Stories can break the dignity of
73 a people, but stories can also repair that broken dignity.
74 The American writer Alice Walker wrote this about her Southern relatives who had moved to
75 the North. She introduced them to a book about the Southern life that they had left behind.
76 "They sat around, reading the book themselves, listening to me read the book, and a kind
77 of paradise was regained."
78 I would like to end with this thought: that when we reject the single story, when we realize
79 that there is never a single story about any place, we regain a kind of paradise.

## Text One (Unseen)
**Title:** The Weight of Witness
**Author:** Christina Lamb (2014)
**Context:** 21st Century non-fiction. An extract from the memoir of award-winning war correspondent Christina Lamb, reflecting on a visit to a refugee camp during a humanitarian crisis in the Middle East.

1  The dust hit us first, before the smell, before the noise, before anything. A fine, orange
2  powder that coated your teeth and settled in the creases of your skin like ochre face paint.
3  Our driver pulled the jeep to a halt at the crest of the ridge and there it was below us:
4  the camp. From this distance it looked almost peaceful — a grid of white tents stretching
5  to the horizon, fluttering gently like a field of paper flowers. It was only as we descended
6  that the scale of it became real. Thirty thousand people were living here in what had been,
7  six months earlier, an empty stretch of desert.
8  The camp had its own geography, its own weather systems. Close to the main gate, where
9  the aid trucks arrived, things were relatively ordered: distribution points, a medical tent
10 with a red crescent painted on its roof, a registration queue that snaked for what seemed
11 like half a mile. But further in, away from the eyes of the agencies and the cameras, the
12 order dissolved. Tents sagged into the sand. Makeshift shelters of cardboard and tarpaulin
13 leaned against one another like drunken companions. Children with bare feet and matted
14 hair played in the grey water that pooled between the rows.
15 I had reported from camps before — in Pakistan, in Sudan, in the Democratic Republic of
16 Congo — and each time I told myself that the next one would not affect me the way the
17 last one had. Journalists develop their armour. You learn to see through the viewfinder,
18 to frame suffering as composition: foreground, middle distance, background. You learn to
19 ask the questions that will produce the quotes your editors need, the kind that fit neatly
20 into a two-minute package or eight hundred words of copy. You learn, above all, to
21 separate yourself from what you are seeing.
22 But the armour is never as thick as you think.
23 I found her in the section they called the overflow, which was really just the far edge of the
24 camp where new arrivals were deposited when there was no more room. She was sitting
25 on a plastic sheet, her back against a half-collapsed tent pole, cradling something in her
26 arms. I thought at first it was a bundle of rags. It was her daughter, perhaps two years old,
27 perhaps less. It was difficult to tell. Malnutrition does that — it robs children of their age,
28 makes them ancient and infant at the same time.
29 The woman looked up at me and I expected the expression I had seen a thousand times:
30 the blank stare, the look that says I am past feeling anything. But her face was animated.
31 She was talking rapidly in Arabic, gesturing at the child, at the tent, at the sky, as if
32 explaining something terribly important that I absolutely needed to understand. My
33 translator caught fragments. She was saying that her daughter had been walking two weeks
34 ago. That she had been laughing. That she used to pull the cat's tail and shriek when it
35 hissed.
36 She was telling me that her daughter was a person.
37 That is the thing they never train you for. Not the bodies, not the rubble, not the sound of
38 shelling at three in the morning. It is the moment when someone looks at you and demands
39 that you see them as they were, not as they are. When a mother holds up the ghost of her
40 child's laughter and says: remember this. This is who she is.
41 I wrote my report that evening in the back of the jeep, my laptop balanced on a cardboard
42 box, the generator humming outside. Eight hundred words. Lead paragraph, context
43 paragraph, human interest, statistics, quote from an aid worker, quote from a government
44 spokesperson, and a closing line that tried to capture something of what I had felt. My
45 editor called it "powerful." He said it might make the front page. I hung up the satellite
46 phone and sat in the darkness for a long time, listening to the wind move through thirty
47 thousand tents.

## Text Two (Anthology)
**Title:** A Passage to Africa
**Author:** George Alagiah (1999)
**Context:** 20th Century non-fiction. Alagiah writes about his experiences as a television reporter during the war in Somalia, Africa in the 1990s. He won a special award for his report on the incidents described in this passage.

1  I saw a thousand hungry, lean, scared and betrayed faces as I criss-crossed Somalia
2  between the end of 1991 and December 1992, but there is one I will never forget.
3  I was in a little hamlet just outside Gufgaduud, a village in the back of beyond, a place the
4  aid agencies had yet to reach. In my notebook I had jotted down instructions on how to get
5  there. 'Take the Badale Road for a few kilometres till the end of the tarmac, turn right on
6  to a dirt track, stay on it for about forty-five minutes — Gufgaduud. Go another fifteen
7  minutes approx. — like a ghost village.'
8  In the ghoulish manner of journalists on the hunt for the most striking pictures, my
9  cameraman and I tramped from one hut to another. What might have appalled us when
10 we'd started our trip just a few days before no longer impressed us much. The search for
11 the shocking is like the craving for a drug: you require heavier and more frequent doses
12 the longer you're at it. Pictures that stun the editors one day are written off as the same
13 old stuff the next. This sounds callous, but it is just a fact of life. It's how we collect and
14 compile the images that so move people in the comfort of their sitting rooms back home.
15 There was Amina Abdirahman, who had gone out that morning in search of wild, edible
16 roots, leaving her two young girls lying on the dirt floor of their hut. They had been sick for
17 days, and were reaching the final, enervating stages of terminal hunger. Habiba was ten
18 years old and her sister, Ayaan, was nine. By the time Amina returned, she had only one
19 daughter. Habiba had died. No rage, no whimpering, just a passing away — that simple,
20 frictionless, motionless deliverance from a state of half-life to death itself. It was, as I said
21 at the time in my dispatch, a vision of 'famine away from the headlines, a famine of quiet
22 suffering and lonely death'.
23 There was the old woman who lay in her hut, abandoned by relations who were too weak
24 to carry her on their journey to find food. It was the smell that drew me to her doorway:
25 the smell of decaying flesh. Where her shinbone should have been there was a festering
26 wound the size of my hand. She'd been shot in the leg as the retreating army of the
27 deposed dictator took revenge on whoever it found in its way. The shattered leg had fused
28 into the gentle V-shape of a boomerang. It was rotting; she was rotting. You could see it
29 in her sick, yellow eyes and smell it in the putrid air she recycled with every struggling
30 breath she took.
31 And then there was the face I will never forget.
32 My reaction to everyone else I met that day was a mixture of pity and revulsion. Yes,
33 revulsion. The degeneration of the human body, sucked of its natural vitality by the twin
34 evils of hunger and disease, is a disgusting thing. We never say so in our TV reports. It's
35 a taboo that has yet to be breached. To be in a feeding centre is to hear and smell the
36 excretion of fluids by people who are beyond controlling their bodily functions. To be in a
37 feeding centre is surreptitiously to wipe your hands on the back of your trousers after
38 you've held the clammy palm of a mother who has just cleaned vomit from her child's
39 mouth.
40 There's pity, too, because even in this state of utter despair they aspire to a dignity that
41 is almost impossible to achieve. An old woman will cover her shrivelled body with a soiled
42 cloth as your gaze turns towards her. Or the old and dying man who keeps his hoe next to
43 the mat with which, one day soon, they will shroud his corpse, as if he means to go out
44 and till the soil once all this is over.
45 I saw that face for only a few seconds, a fleeting meeting of eyes before the face turned
46 away, as its owner retreated into the darkness of another hut. In those brief moments
47 there had been a smile, not from me, but from the face. It was not a smile of greeting, it
48 was not a smile of joy — how could it be? — but it was a smile nonetheless. It touched me
49 in a way I could not explain. It moved me in a way that went beyond pity or revulsion.
50 What was it about that smile? I had to find out. I urged my translator to ask the man why
51 he had smiled. He came back with an answer. 'It's just that he was embarrassed to be
52 found in this condition,' the translator explained. And then it clicked. That's what the smile
53 had been about. It was the feeble smile that goes with apology, the kind of smile you might
54 give if you felt you had done something wrong.
55 Normally inured to stories of suffering, accustomed to the evidence of deprivation, I was
56 unsettled by this one smile in a way I had never been before. There is an unwritten code
57 between the journalist and his subjects in these situations. The journalist observes, the
58 subject is observed. The journalist is active, the subject is passive. But this smile had
59 turned the tables on that tacit agreement. Without uttering a single word, the man had
60 posed a question that cut to the heart of the relationship between me and him, between
61 us and them, between the rich world and the poor world. If he was embarrassed to be
62 found weakened by hunger and ground down by conflict, how should I feel to be standing
63 there so strong and confident?
64 I resolved there and then that I would write the story of Gufgaduud with all the power and
65 purpose I could muster. It seemed at the time, and still does, the only adequate answer a
66 reporter can give to the man's question.
67 I have one regret about that brief encounter in Gufgaduud. Having searched through my
68 notes and studied the dispatch that the BBC broadcast, I see that I never found out what
69 the man's name was. Yet meeting him was a seminal moment in the gradual collection of
70 experiences we call context. Facts and figures are the easy part of journalism. Knowing
71 where they sit in the great scheme of things is much harder. So, my nameless friend, if
72 you are still alive, I owe you one.

## Text One (Unseen)
**Title:** The Elephant's Last Stand
**Author:** Daphne Sheldrick (2012)
**Context:** 21st Century non-fiction. An extract from the memoirs of Daphne Sheldrick, the Kenyan conservationist who founded the David Sheldrick Wildlife Trust. She describes an encounter with elephants threatened by poaching and the dilemma of human intervention.

1  We found the herd at dusk, gathered around a waterhole that had shrunk to a muddy disc
2  the colour of old blood. There were nine of them: three adult females, two adolescents still
3  awkward in their growing bodies, and four calves who pressed close to their mothers' legs,
4  trunks curling and uncurling nervously. They knew we were there. Their ears — vast grey
5  sails veined with pink — fanned slowly forward and back, reading the air for information
6  about us.
7  I had been working with elephants for over thirty years by then, but the sight of them still
8  knocked the breath from me. There is nothing in nature quite like the presence of an
9  elephant. They carry a gravity about them that has nothing to do with their size, though
10 their size is astonishing — the lead female was perhaps four tons, her tusks sweeping
11 forward in long curves like the prows of ancient ships. It is something else entirely. A
12 stillness. An intelligence behind the eyes that watches you with a patience that feels older
13 than any human wisdom.
14 The matriarch turned her head slowly and looked at me. I say "looked" but the word is
15 inadequate. She regarded me. She considered me. And in that long, steady gaze I felt the
16 accumulated weight of everything her species has endured at the hands of mine: the
17 centuries of ivory trade, the shrinking forests, the fences that carve up migration routes
18 older than any human boundary. She did not charge or trumpet. She simply looked, and it
19 was worse.
20 The rangers with me were young men from the local village. They had grown up alongside
21 elephants in the way that many rural Kenyans do — with a mixture of respect and
22 resentment. Elephants destroyed crops. They flattened fences. They could kill a man with a
23 single swing of the trunk. The rangers understood this. Their fathers' fields bore the scars.
24 But they were here now, in green uniforms with radios clipped to their belts, employed to
25 protect the very creatures that had once been their enemies. This contradiction did not
26 trouble them as much as it troubled visiting journalists, who wanted simple stories of
27 heroes and villains.
28 The truth is more complicated. Conservation in Africa does not happen in a vacuum. It
29 happens in villages where children go hungry, where clinics have no medicine, where a
30 single tusk is worth more than a farmer earns in a decade. To stand in a boardroom in
31 London or New York and declare that elephants must be saved is easy. To stand in a field
32 of trampled maize and say the same thing requires a different kind of courage.
33 The youngest calf separated from the group and waded into the waterhole, sinking up to
34 its belly in the warm mud. It rolled onto its side and waved its trunk in the air, a gesture so
35 absurdly joyful that one of the rangers laughed out loud. The matriarch swung her head
36 toward him — a warning — and he fell silent. But he was still smiling. We all were.
37 That is the paradox at the heart of every conservationist's life. You love a creature whose
38 survival depends on changing the minds of people who have every reason not to care. You
39 ask communities to sacrifice for a cause that may never benefit them directly. And all the
40 while, the poachers circle closer, the ivory price climbs, and the herds grow smaller.
41 I watched the calf haul itself out of the mud, shake itself in a spray of brown droplets, and
42 trot back to its mother on unsteady legs. The matriarch rumbled — a sound so deep it
43 vibrated in my chest like a second heartbeat — and the herd moved off together into the
44 darkening bush, single file, the calves wedged safely between the adults.
45 I have been asked many times whether there is hope for the elephant. I always say yes.
46 But the truth that I hold privately is this: hope is not a plan. And the elephant, for all its
47 ancient wisdom, cannot save itself.

## Text Two (Anthology)
**Title:** The Explorer's Daughter
**Author:** Kari Herbert (2004)
**Context:** 21st Century non-fiction. As a small child, Herbert lived among the Inughuit people in the Arctic. In 2002 she revisited the area, staying near Thule in North Greenland. ## Text One (Unseen)
**Title:** Scientific Proof is a Myth
**Author:** Ethan Siegel (21st Century)
**Context:** 21st Century non-fiction. An analysis challenging the widely-held perception of scientific "proofs" in the context of key scientific theories. Siegel emphasises the constraints of human observation and the distinction between strong evidence and definitive proof.

1  You've heard of our greatest scientific theories: the theory of evolution, the Big Bang theory,
2  the theory of gravity. You've also heard of the concept of a proof, and the claims that certain
3  pieces of evidence prove the validities of these theories. Fossils, genetic inheritance, and
4  DNA prove the theory of evolution. The Hubble expansion of the Universe, the evolution of
5  stars, galaxies, and heavy elements, and the existence of the cosmic microwave background
6  prove the Big Bang theory. And falling objects, GPS clocks, planetary motion, and the
7  deflection of starlight prove the theory of gravity.
8  Except that's a complete lie. While they provide very strong evidence for those theories, they
9  aren't proof. In fact, when it comes to science, proving anything is an impossibility.
10 Reality is a complicated place. All we have to guide us, from an empirical point of view, are
11 the quantities we can measure and observe. Even at that, those quantities are only as good
12 as the tools and equipment we use to make those observations and measurements.
13 Distances and sizes are only as good as the measuring sticks you have access to; brightness
14 measurements are only as good as your ability to count and quantify photons; even time
15 itself is only known as well as the clock you have to measure its passage. No matter how
16 good our measurements and observations are, there's a limit to how good they are.
17 We also can't observe or measure everything. Even if the Universe weren't subject to the
18 fundamental quantum rules that govern it, along with all its inherent uncertainty, it wouldn't
19 be possible to measure every state of every particle under every condition all the time. At
20 some point, we have to extrapolate. This is incredibly powerful and incredibly useful, but it's
21 also incredibly limiting.
22 In order to come up with a model capable of predicting what will happen under a variety of
23 conditions, we need to understand a few things.
24 1. What we're capable of measuring, and to what precision.
25 2. What's been measured thus far, under specific initial conditions.
26 3. What laws hold for these phenomena, i.e., what observed relationships exist between
27 specific quantities.
28 4. And what the limits are for the things we presently know.
29 If you understand these things, you have the right ingredients to formulate a scientific theory:
30 a framework for explaining what we already know happens as well as predicting what will
31 happen under new, untested circumstances.
32 Our best theories, like the aforementioned theory of evolution, the Big Bang theory, and
33 Einstein's General Relativity, cover all of these bases. They have an underlying quantitative
34 framework, enabling us to predict what will happen under a variety of situations, and to then
35 go out and test those predictions empirically. So far, these theories have demonstrated
36 themselves to be eminently valid. Where their predictions can be described by mathematical
37 expressions, we can tell not only what should happen, but by how much. For these theories
38 in particular, among many others, measurements and observations that have been performed
39 to test these theories have been supremely successful.
40 But as validating as that is — and as powerful as it is to falsify alternatives — it's completely
41 impossible to prove anything in science.

## Text Two (Anthology)
**Title:** Young and dyslexic? You've got it going on
**Author:** Benjamin Zephaniah (2015)
**Context:** 21st Century non-fiction. This article was published in The Guardian, adapted from Zephaniah's contribution to Creative, Successful, Dyslexic. He writes about his experiences of growing up with undiagnosed dyslexia and how he turned it to his advantage.

1  As a child I suffered, but learned to turn dyslexia to my advantage, to see the world more
2  creatively. We are the architects, we are the designers.
3  I'm of the generation where teachers didn't know what dyslexia was. The big problem with
4  the education system then was that there was no compassion, no understanding and no
5  humanity. I don't look back and feel angry with the teachers. The ones who wanted to have
6  an individual approach weren't allowed to. The idea of being kind and thoughtful and
7  listening to problems just wasn't done: the past is a different kind of country.
8  At school my ideas always contradicted the teachers'. I remember one teacher saying that
9  human beings sleep for one-third of their life and I put my hand up and said, "If there's a
10 God isn't that a design fault? If you've built something, you want efficiency. If I was God I
11 would have designed sleep so we could stay awake. Then good people could do one-third
12 more good in the world."
13 The teacher said, "Shut up, stupid boy. Bad people would do one-third more bad." I thought
14 I'd put in a good idea. I was just being creative. She also had a point, but the thing was, she
15 called me stupid for even thinking about it.
16 I remember a teacher talking about Africa and the 'local savages' and I would say, "Who are
17 you to talk about savages?" She would say, "How dare you challenge me?" — and that would
18 get me into trouble.
19 Once, when I was finding it difficult to engage with writing and had asked for some help, a
20 teacher said, "It's all right. We can't all be intelligent, but you'll end up being a good
21 sportsperson, so why don't you go outside and play some football?" I thought, "Oh great",
22 but now I realise he was stereotyping me.
23 I had poems in my head even then, and when I was 10 or 11 my sister wrote some of them
24 down for me. When I was 13 I could read very basically but it would be such hard work that
25 I would give up. I thought that so long as you could read how much the banknote was worth,
26 you knew enough or you could ask a mate.
27 I got thrown out of a lot of schools, the last one at 13. I was expelled partly because of
28 arguing with teachers on an intellectual level and partly for being a rude boy and fighting. I
29 didn't stab anybody, but I did take revenge on a teacher once. I stole his car and drove it
30 into his front garden. I remember him telling us the Nazis weren't that bad. He could say
31 that in the classroom. When I was in borstal I used to do this thing of looking at people I
32 didn't want to be like. I saw a guy who spent all his time sitting stooped over and I thought,
33 "I don't want to be like that," so I learned to sit with a straight back. Being observant helped
34 me make the right choices.
35 A high percentage of the prison population are dyslexic, and a high percentage of the
36 architect population. If you look at the statistics, I should be in prison: a black man brought
37 up on the wrong side of town whose family fell apart, in trouble with the police when I was
38 a kid, unable to read and write, with no qualifications and, on top of that, dyslexic. But I
39 think staying out of prison is about conquering your fears and finding your path in life.
40 When I go into prisons to talk to people I see men and women who, in intelligence and other
41 qualities, are the same as me. But opportunities opened for me and they missed theirs,
42 didn't notice them or didn't take them.
43 I never thought I was stupid. I didn't have that struggle. If I have someone in front of me
44 who doesn't have a problem reading and writing telling me that black people are savages I
45 just think, "I'm not stupid — you're the one who's stupid." I just had self-belief.
46 For my first book I told my poems to my girlfriend, who wrote them down for me. It really
47 took off, especially within the black community. I wrote 'wid luv' for 'with love'. People
48 didn't think they were dyslexic poems, they just thought I wrote phonetically.
49 At 21 I went to an adult education class in London to learn to read and write. The teacher
50 told me, "You are dyslexic," and I was like, "Do I need an operation?" She explained to me
51 what it meant and I suddenly thought, "Ah, I get it. I thought I was going crazy."
52 I wrote more poetry, novels for teenagers, plays, other books and recorded music. I take
53 poetry to people who do not read poetry. Still now, when I'm writing the word 'knot', I have
54 to stop and think, "How do I write that?" I have to draw something to let me know what the
55 word is to come back to it later. If I can't spell 'question' I just put a question mark and come
56 back to it later.
57 When I look at a book, the first thing I see is the size of it, and I know that's what it's like
58 for a lot of young people who find reading tough. When Brunel University offered me the job
59 of professor of poetry and creative writing, I knew my students would be officially more
60 educated than me. I tell them, "You can do this course and get the right grade because you
61 have a good memory — but if you don't have passion, creativity, individuality, there's no
62 point." In my life now, I find that people accommodate my dyslexia. I can perform my poetry
63 because it doesn't have to be word perfect, but I never read one of my novels in public.
64 When I go to literary festivals I always get an actor to read it out for me. Otherwise all my
65 energy goes into reading the book and the mood is lost.
66 If someone can't understand dyslexia it's their problem. In the same way, if someone
67 oppresses me because of my race I don't sit down and think, "How can I become white?"
68 It's not my problem, it's theirs and they are the ones who have to come to terms with it.
69 If you're dyslexic and you feel there's something holding you back, just remember: it's not
70 you. In many ways being dyslexic is a natural way to be.
71 What's unnatural is the way we read and write. If you look at a pictorial language like
72 Chinese, you can see the word for a woman because the character looks like a woman. The
73 word for a house looks like a house. It is a strange step to go from that to a squiggle that
74 represents a sound.
75 So don't be heavy on yourself. And if you are a parent of someone with dyslexia don't think
76 of it as a defect. Dyslexia is not a measure of intelligence: you may have a genius on your
77 hands. Having dyslexia can make you creative. If you want to construct a sentence and
78 can't find the word you are searching for, you have to think of a way to write round it. This
79 requires being creative and so your 'creativity muscle' gets bigger.
80 Kids come up to me and say, "I'm dyslexic too," and I say to them, "Use it to your
81 advantage, see the world differently. Us dyslexic people, we've got it going on — we are the
82 architects. We are the designers." It's like these kids are proud to be like me and if that
83 helps them, that is great. I didn't have that as a child. I say to them, "Bloody non-dyslexics
84 ... who do they think they are?"

## Text One (Unseen)
**Title:** Into the Crowd
**Author:** Michael Palin (2004)
**Context:** 21st Century non-fiction. An extract from the travel writings of Michael Palin, in which he describes his experience of witnessing the Tomatina festival in Bunol, Spain — a chaotic annual event in which thousands of people throw tomatoes at each other in the streets.

1  We arrived in Bunol at seven in the morning, which seemed absurdly early for what was
2  essentially a food fight. But the streets were already full. Makeshift stalls sold cheap
3  T-shirts and swimming goggles — the goggles, I would later discover, were not a joke but
4  a genuine necessity — and the air smelled of coffee and cheap churros and the particular
5  electric excitement that gathers when thousands of people are about to do something
6  magnificently pointless together.
7  The town itself is tiny. A cluster of whitewashed houses piled up a hillside, narrow streets
8  barely wide enough for a single car, balconies draped with plastic sheeting to protect the
9  stonework from what was to come. The residents had the weary, affectionate look of people
10 whose hometown has been hijacked by lunatics, which in a sense it had. Every year, on
11 the last Wednesday of August, thirty thousand visitors descend on a town of nine thousand
12 to hurl one hundred and fifty tons of overripe tomatoes at one another. There is no prize.
13 There is no winner. There is no point whatsoever, and that is entirely the point.
14 At eleven o'clock, the first truck rumbled into the main street. It was piled high with
15 tomatoes — an absurd, wobbling mountain of red — and as it inched through the crowd,
16 hands reached up and began grabbing fistfuls. Within seconds the air was thick with them.
17 The sound was extraordinary: a wet, continuous percussion, like standing inside a drum
18 during a rainstorm, punctuated by shrieks and laughter and the occasional cry of someone
19 who had taken a particularly accurate shot to the ear.
20 I stood at the edge of the crowd, my camera raised, trying to maintain the professional
21 detachment of the observer. This lasted approximately four seconds, at which point a
22 tomato the size of a cricket ball struck me squarely on the chest and exploded in a spray
23 of seeds and juice. A young Spanish woman nearby erupted in laughter, pointed at me, and
24 then threw another one. I had ceased to be a journalist. I was a target.
25 The transformation was instant and total. Within minutes I was ankle-deep in pulp, my
26 shoes making a grotesque sucking noise with every step. The gutters ran red, as if the
27 entire town were bleeding. People slipped and fell and were hauled up by strangers who
28 then pelted them at close range. A man in swimming trunks and nothing else slid down the
29 street on his stomach, surfing the tomato slick like a human toboggan. An elderly woman
30 on a balcony above tipped a bucket of water over the crowd below and received a standing
31 ovation.
32 It occurred to me, somewhere in the middle of the chaos, that I had never seen so many
33 people so completely, unselfconsciously happy. There was no aggression in it, no edge. The
34 tomatoes were a shared joke, a communal act of beautiful absurdity. For one hour, in a
35 tiny town in Valencia, every rule of adult behaviour was suspended, and the result was a
36 kind of liberation that no amount of rational explanation can quite capture.
37 At noon, a second rocket was fired and the throwing stopped, as if a switch had been
38 flipped. Within minutes, fire engines were hosing down the streets and the crowd was
39 dispersing, drenched and grinning and stinking magnificently of tomato. Shop shutters
40 were raised. The plastic sheeting came down from the balconies. The town put itself back
41 together with the practised efficiency of people who have done this many times before.
42 As we drove away, I looked back at Bunol, gleaming and clean in the midday sun, and
43 thought that there was something quietly wonderful about a place that allows itself to go
44 completely mad once a year, and then tidies up afterwards as if nothing had happened.

## Text Two (Anthology)
**Title:** A Game of Polo with a Headless Goat
**Author:** Emma Levine (2003)
**Context:** 21st Century non-fiction. Levine travelled throughout Asia researching and filming unusual sports. In this passage she writes about a donkey race in Karachi, Pakistan.

1  We drove off to find the best viewing spot, which turned out to be the crest of the hill so we
2  could see the approaching race. I asked the lads if we could join in the 'Wacky Races' and
3  follow the donkeys, and they loved the idea. 'We'll open the car boot, you climb inside and
4  point your camera towards the race. As the donkeys overtake us, we'll join the cars.' 'But
5  will you try and get to the front?' 'Oh yes, that's no problem.'
6  The two lads who had never been interested in this Karachi sport were suddenly fired up
7  with enthusiasm. We waited for eternity on the brow of the hill, me perched in the boot with
8  a zoom lens pointing out. Nearly one hour later I was beginning to feel rather silly when the
9  only action was a villager on a wobbly bicycle, who nearly fell off as he cycled past and
10 gazed around at us.
11 Several vehicles went past, and some donkey-carts carrying spectators. 'Are they coming?'
12 we called out to them. 'Coming, coming,' came the reply. I was beginning to lose faith in
13 its happening, but the lads remained confident.
14 Just as I was assuming that the race had been cancelled, we spotted two approaching
15 donkey-carts in front of a cloud of fumes and dust created by some fifty vehicles roaring up
16 in their wake. As they drew nearer, Yaqoob revved up the engine and began to inch the car
17 out of the lay-by. The two donkeys were almost dwarfed by their entourage; but there was
18 no denying their speed — the Kibla donkey is said to achieve speeds of up to 40 kph, and
19 this looked close. The two were neck-and-neck, their jockeys perched on top of the tiny
20 carts using their whips energetically, although not cruelly.
21 The noise of the approaching vehicles grew; horns tooting, bells ringing, and the special
22 rattles used just for this purpose (like maracas, a metal container filled with dried beans).
23 Men standing on top of their cars and vans, hanging out of taxis and perched on lorries, all
24 cheered and shouted, while the vehicles jostled to get to the front of the convoy.
25 Yaqoob chose exactly the right moment to edge out of the road and swerve in front of the
26 nearest car, finding the perfect place to see the two donkeys and at the front of the
27 vehicles. This was Formula One without rules, or a city-centre rush hour gone anarchic; a
28 complete flouting of every type of traffic rule and common sense.
29 Our young driver relished this unusual test of driving skills. It was survival of the fittest, and
30 depended upon the ability to cut in front of a vehicle with a sharp flick of the steering wheel
31 (no lane discipline here); quick reflexes to spot a gap in the traffic for a couple of seconds;
32 nerves of steel, and an effective horn. There were two races — the motorized spectators at
33 the back; in front, the two donkeys, still running close and amazingly not put off by the
34 uproar just behind them. Ahead of the donkeys, oncoming traffic — for it was a main road
35 — had to dive into the ditch and wait there until we had passed. Yaqoob loved it. We stayed
36 near to the front, his hand permanently on the horn and his language growing more
37 colourful with every vehicle that tried to cut in front.
38 The road straightened and levelled, and everyone picked up speed as we neared the end of
39 the race. But just as they were reaching the finishing line, the hospital gate, there was a
40 near pile-up as the leading donkey swerved, lost his footing and he and the cart tumbled
41 over. The race was over.
42 And then the trouble began. I assumed the winner was the one who completed the race but
43 it was not seen that way by everyone. Apart from the two jockeys and 'officials' (who, it
44 turned out, were actually monitoring the race) there were over a hundred punters who had
45 all staked money on the race, and therefore had strong opinions. Some were claiming that
46 the donkey had fallen because the other one had been ridden too close to him. Voices were
47 raised, fists were out and tempers rising. Everyone gathered around one jockey and official,
48 while the bookmakers were trying to insist that the race should be re-run.
49 Yaqoob and Iqbal were nervous of hanging around a volatile situation. They agreed to find
50 out for me what was happening, ordering me to stay inside the car as they were swallowed
51 up by the crowd. They emerged some time later. 'It's still not resolved,' said Iqbal, 'but
52 it's starting to get nasty. I think we should leave.' As we drove away, Yaqoob reflected on
53 his driving skills. 'I really enjoyed that,' he said as we drove off at a more sedate pace. 'But
54 I don't even have my licence yet because I'm underage!'
55 They both found this hilarious, but I was glad he hadn't told me before; an inexperienced,
56 underage driver causing a massive pile-up in the middle of the high-stakes donkey race
57 could have caused problems.

## Text One (Unseen)
**Title:** The Year of Living Differently
**Author:** Alex Kerr (2009)
**Context:** 21st Century non-fiction. An extract from the memoirs of Alex Kerr, an American writer and Japanologist, describing his first experience of arriving in a remote mountain village in central Japan as a young man. He was there to study traditional crafts.

1  The bus dropped me at the end of the road, which was also the end of the world as I
2  understood it. Beyond the last stop there was nothing but forest — a wall of cedar and
3  cypress so dense it seemed to absorb light rather than reflect it. The driver pointed up a
4  narrow path that disappeared into the trees, said something I didn't understand, and
5  closed the doors. The bus reversed laboriously in the tiny turning circle and grumbled back
6  the way it had come. I stood there with my rucksack and my dictionary and the growing
7  suspicion that I had made a serious mistake.
8  The path climbed steeply for twenty minutes before the trees parted and I saw the village.
9  It sat in a bowl of terraced fields, perhaps thirty houses with thatched roofs the colour of
10 dark honey, smoke rising from some of them in slow vertical lines that suggested an
11 absolute absence of wind. A river ran through the lower edge of the settlement, spanned by
12 a wooden bridge that looked as if it had been there since the beginning of time. Everything
13 was extraordinarily still. No cars, no machinery, no music. Just the river, and somewhere
14 above me, a bird I couldn't identify repeating the same two notes with mechanical
15 precision.
16 My host family lived in the largest house, which I later learned had been built over two
17 hundred years ago by a master carpenter who had walked from Kyoto to design it. The
18 entrance was a wooden platform, worn smooth by generations of stockinged feet. I removed
19 my shoes, as I had been taught, but the old woman who greeted me — my host's mother,
20 as it turned out — gestured that I should also remove my socks. Bare feet on old wood:
21 that was my first lesson. The floor was alive in a way that carpeted surfaces are not. It was
22 warm where the sun had reached it, cool in the shadows, and it creaked in a way that felt
23 less like age than like conversation.
24 She led me through a series of rooms divided by paper screens. Each room was almost
25 empty — a low table, a cushion, a scroll painting on the wall. I had come from an American
26 suburb where every room was full of furniture and every surface was covered with objects.
27 The emptiness here felt deliberate, curated, as if the space itself was the point. Later I would
28 learn the word 'ma', which the Japanese use for the gap between things, the pause in
29 music, the silence between words. It was my second lesson: that absence could be a form
30 of beauty.
31 Dinner was served on the floor, around a sunken hearth called an irori. A fish I didn't
32 recognise hung on a stick over the embers, its skin blistering slowly. There were bowls of
33 rice, pickled vegetables in colours I had never associated with food — vivid purple, a green
34 so bright it looked artificial — and a soup that tasted of the sea and the forest simultaneously.
35 My host, a quiet man in his sixties named Nakamura, spoke no English. His mother spoke
36 no English. His daughter, who had come from the city for the weekend, spoke a little, and
37 through her I pieced together fragments of understanding. Nakamura made paper. His
38 family had made paper here for four hundred years. The mulberry trees that provided the
39 fibre grew on the hillside above the village. The water from the river was cold enough and
40 clean enough to produce the particular translucency for which their paper was known.
41 I ate, and listened, and looked at the fire, and felt something shift inside me that I could
42 not yet name. It was not happiness, exactly, or belonging. It was the sensation of having
43 arrived somewhere that had been waiting for me without my knowing it, the way a key
44 arrives at a lock — not with force, but with the quiet precision of a thing finding its intended
45 place.

## Text Two (Anthology)
**Title:** Beyond the Sky and the Earth: A Journey into Bhutan
**Author:** Jamie Zeppa (1999)
**Context:** 20th Century non-fiction. When Zeppa was 24 years old she left Canada to teach in Bhutan. This memoir grew out of an essay she wrote about her early days in the country.

1  Mountains all around, climbing up to peaks, rolling into valleys, again and again. Bhutan is
2  all and only mountains. I know the technical explanation for the landscape, landmass meeting
3  landmass, the Indian subcontinent colliding into Asia thirty or forty million years ago, but I
4  cannot imagine it. It is easier to picture a giant child gathering earth in great armfuls, piling
5  up rock, pinching mud into ridges and sharp peaks, knuckling out little valleys and gorges,
6  poking holes for water to fall through.
7  It is my first night in Thimphu, the capital, a ninety-minute drive from the airport in Paro. It
8  took five different flights over four days to get here, from Toronto to Montreal to Amsterdam
9  to New Delhi to Calcutta to Paro. I am exhausted, but I cannot sleep. From my simple, pine-
10 paneled room at the Druk Sherig hotel, I watch mountains rise to meet the moon. I used to
11 wonder what was on the other side of mountains, how the landscape resolved itself beyond
12 the immediate wall in front of you. Flying in from the baked-brown plains of India this
13 morning, I found out: on the other side of mountains are mountains, more mountains and

3  had placed a tuning fork against my ribs. The clicks of a sperm whale are not sounds in the

12 heavier than mine, folded into ridges and valleys that scientists are only beginning to map.
13 She slowed. I slowed. We hung there in the water column, perhaps ten feet apart, and the
14 ocean seemed to hold its breath. The light came from above in shafts that moved across
15 her skin like searchlights, illuminating the scars that cross-hatched her flank — battle marks
16 from giant squid encounters in the deep canyons a thousand metres below us, a world I
17 would never see and she navigated every day.
18 Then she turned her eye toward me.

20 side of the head. It is a dark, liquid eye, ringed with a pale border that makes it look
21 startlingly human. And it is an eye that sees you. Not through you, not past you, but into
22 you, in a way that no photograph can capture and no description can quite convey. I have
23 looked into the eyes of many animals — dogs, horses, the great apes at close quarters in
24 Borneo — and each time there is a flicker of mutual recognition, a bridge. But the whale's
25 gaze was different. It was not curiosity. It was assessment. She was reading me with a sense
26 I don't possess, her clicks washing over my body, building a three-dimensional sonar
27 portrait of my skeleton, my lungs, the air in my sinuses, the rhythm of my heart. She knew
28 things about me that I didn't know about myself.

36 flukes spreading above me like a canopy, blocking the sun, and she descended. The clicks
