# Topic 1: Redemption
**Type:** central_theme
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The opening characterisation of Scrooge is the spiritual and moral baseline from which his redemption will be measured. Students should analyse how Dickens uses cold imagery, listing, and metaphor to establish Scrooge as frozen, isolated, and morally dead — then trace the cumulative process of redemption across the five staves, arguing that even the hardest heart can be renewed through confrontation with past, present, and future.

## Either
**Marks:** 25
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**Extract Location:** Stave 1

### Extract
```
 1    Oh! But he was a tight-fisted hand at the grindstone, Scrooge!
 2    a squeezing, wrenching, grasping, scraping, clutching,
 3    covetous, old sinner! Hard and sharp as flint, from which
 4    no steel had ever struck out generous fire; secret, and
 5    self-contained, and solitary as an oyster. The cold within
 6    him froze his old features, nipped his pointed nose, shrivelled
 7    his cheek, stiffened his gait; made his eyes red, his thin
 8    lips blue; and spoke out shrewdly in his grating voice. A
 9    frosty rime was on his head, and on his eyebrows, and his
10    wiry chin. He carried his own low temperature always about
11    with him; he iced his office in the dog-days; and didn't
12    thaw it one degree at Christmas.
13
14    External heat and cold had little influence on Scrooge.
15    No warmth could warm, no wintry weather chill him. No wind
16    that blew was bitterer than he, no falling snow was more
17    intent upon its purpose, no pelting rain less open to
18    entreaty. Foul weather didn't know where to have him. The
19    heaviest rain, and snow, and hail, and sleet, could boast
20    of the advantage over him in only one respect. They often
21    "came down" handsomely, and Scrooge never did.
22
23    Nobody ever stopped him in the street to say, with gladsome
24    looks, "My dear Scrooge, how are you? When will you come
25    to see me?" No beggars implored him to bestow a trifle,
26    no children asked him what it was o'clock, no man or woman
27    ever once in all his life inquired the way to such and such
28    a place, of Scrooge. Even the blind men's dogs appeared to
29    know him; and when they saw him coming on, would tug their
30    owners into doorways and up courts; and then would wag their
31    tails as though they said, "No eye at all is better than
32    an evil eye, dark master!"
```

# Topic 5: The Cratchits and Family
**Type:** supporting_character
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The Cratchit family serves as Dickens' moral counterpoint to Scrooge, demonstrating that warmth, love, and generosity can flourish despite material deprivation. Their domestic happiness contrasts sharply with Scrooge's solitary wealth, while Tiny Tim's vulnerability makes the consequences of social neglect personal and urgent.

# Topic 6: Poverty
**Type:** central_theme
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Dickens wrote A Christmas Carol partly in response to the 1843 parliamentary report on child labour, and poverty is central to his social message. He presents it not as a moral failing of the poor but as a systemic injustice perpetuated by the indifference of the wealthy. Students should analyse how Dickens uses Scrooge's callous language about prisons and workhouses to critique Malthusian economics and the Poor Law.

# Topic 8: Generosity (Fezziwig)
**Type:** theme_and_character
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Fezziwig represents the benevolent employer Dickens wished more Victorian businessmen would emulate. His generosity costs relatively little in financial terms but creates enormous happiness, standing in direct contrast to Scrooge's miserly philosophy. Students should analyse how Dickens uses the Fezziwig episode to argue that employers have a moral obligation to their workers and that generosity of spirit matters more than material wealth.

# Topic 9: Isolation
**Type:** supporting_theme
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Dickens presents isolation as both a cause and consequence of Scrooge's miserliness. His physical solitude — dark chambers, empty rooms, silence — mirrors his emotional disconnection from humanity. The Ghost of Christmas Past reveals that this isolation has deep roots in Scrooge's childhood abandonment, suggesting it is a learned defence rather than a natural state. Students should analyse how Dickens uses setting and contrast to show that isolation is the enemy of moral life.

# Topic 10: Guilt (Marley)
**Type:** theme_and_character
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Marley's Ghost functions as a dramatic warning and a mirror for Scrooge's future. His chains — forged in life through greed and indifference — are Dickens' most powerful symbol of moral consequence. Students should analyse how Dickens uses Marley to establish the stakes of the novella and to argue that failure to help others in life brings eternal punishment. Marley's guilt is both personal regret and a broader indictment of a society that values profit over people.

# Topic 11: Christmas Spirit
**Type:** supporting_theme
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** For Dickens, Christmas is not merely a religious festival but a symbol of everything good in human nature — charity, fellowship, compassion, and joy. The Ghost of Christmas Present embodies this abundance and warmth, while Fred's defence of Christmas in Stave 1 provides Dickens' most direct statement of what the season should represent. Students should analyse how Dickens uses the idea of Christmas to argue for a more compassionate and socially responsible society.
