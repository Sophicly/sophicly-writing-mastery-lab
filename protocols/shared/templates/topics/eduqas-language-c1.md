## Source A
**Title:** City of the Beasts
**Author:** Isabel Allende (2004)
**Context:** 21st Century prose-fiction. It is an extract from the novel City of the Beasts by Isabel Allende published in 2004. It tells the story of fifteen-year-old Alex Cold and his family.

Alex Cold lives with his parents and two younger sisters, Andrea and Nicole, in a small American town, but when his mother becomes ill, family life changes beyond recognition.

1  Alexander Cold awakened at dawn, startled by a nightmare. He had been dreaming that an
2  enormous black bird had crashed against the window with a clatter of shattered glass, flown into
3  the house, and carried off his mother. In the dream, he had watched helplessly as it clasped her
4  clothing in its yellow claws, flew out the same broken window, and disappeared into a sky heavy
5  with dark clouds.
6  What had awakened him was the noise from the storm: wind lashing the trees, rain on the
7  rooftop, and thunder. He turned on the light with a sensation of being adrift in a boat, and pushed
8  closer to the bulk of the large dog sleeping beside him. He pictured the roaring Pacific Ocean a
9  few blocks from his house, spilling in furious waves against the rocks. He lay listening to the
10 storm and thinking about the black bird and about his mother, waiting for the pounding in his
11 chest to die down. He was still tangled in the images of his bad dream.
12 Alexander looked at the clock: 6.30, time to get up. Outside, it was beginning to get light. He
13 decided that this was going to be a terrible day, one of those days when it's best to stay in bed
14 because everything is going to turn out bad. There had been a lot of days like that since his
15 mother got sick; sometimes the air in the house felt heavy, like being at the bottom of the sea.
16 At breakfast Alex was not in the mood to applaud his father's efforts at making pancakes. His
17 father was not exactly a good cook; the only thing he knew how to do was pancakes, and they
18 always turned out like rubber-tyre tortillas. His children didn't want to hurt his feelings, so they
19 pretended to eat them, but any time he wasn't looking, they spit them out.
20 "When's Momma going to get better?" Nicole asked, trying to spear a rubbery pancake with her
21 fork.
22 "Shut up, Nicole," Alex replied.
23 "Momma's going to die," Andrea added.
24 "Liar! She's not going to die!" shrieked Nicole.
25 "You two are just kids. You don't know what you're talking about!" Alex exclaimed.
26 "Here, girls. Quiet now. Momma is going to get better," his father interrupted, without much
27 conviction.
28 Alex was angry with his father, his sisters, life in general — even with his mother for getting sick.
29 He rushed out of the kitchen, ready to leave without breakfast.
30 Except for his father's pancakes and an occasional tuna-and-mayonnaise sandwich, no one in
31 the family had cooked for months. There was nothing in the refrigerator but orange juice, milk
32 and ice cream; at night they ordered in pizza or Chinese food. At first it was almost like a party,
33 because each of them ate whenever and whatever they pleased, mainly sweets, but by now
34 everyone missed the balanced diet of normal times.
35 Alex had realised during those months how enormous their mother's presence had been and
36 how painful her absence was now. He missed her easy laughter and her affection, even her
37 discipline. She was stricter than his father, and sharper. It was impossible to fool her; she could
38 see the unseeable. He missed her music, her flowers, the once-familiar fragrance of fresh-baked
39 cookies, and the smell of paint. It used to be that his mother could work several hours in her
40 studio, keep the house immaculate, and still welcome her children after school with cookies.
41 Now she barely got out of bed to walk through the rooms with a confused air, as if she didn't
42 recognise anything; she was too thin, and her sunken eyes were circled with shadows. Her
43 canvases, which once were explosions of colour, sat forgotten on their easels, and her oil paints
44 dried in their tubes. His mother seemed to have shrunk; she was little more than a silent ghost.

**Title:** The Hunger Games
**Author:** Suzanne Collins (2008)
**Context:** 21st Century prose-fiction. It is an extract from the novel The Hunger Games by Suzanne Collins published in 2008. It tells the story of sixteen-year-old Katniss Everdeen and her family.

Katniss lives with her mother, sister, Primrose, and Primrose's cat, Buttercup, in an area called Seam, part of District 12, where citizens live according to strict laws enforced by the Capitol.

1  When I wake up, the other side of the bed is cold. My fingers stretch out, seeking Prim's
2  warmth but finding only the rough canvas cover of the mattress. She must have had bad
3  dreams and climbed in with our mother. Of course, she did. This is the day of the reaping.
4  I prop myself up on one elbow. There's enough light in the bedroom to see them. My little
5  sister, Prim, curled up on her side, cocooned in my mother's body, their cheeks pressed to-
6  gether. In sleep, my mother looks younger, still worn but not so beaten-down. Prim's face is as
7  fresh as a raindrop, as lovely as the primrose for which she was named. My mother was very
8  beautiful once, too. Or so they tell me.
9  Sitting at Prim's knees, guarding her, is the world's ugliest cat. Mashed-in nose, half of one ear
10 missing, eyes the color of rotting squash. Prim named him Buttercup, insisting that his muddy
11 yellow coat matched the bright flower. He hates me. Or at least distrusts me. Even though it
12 was years ago, I think he still remembers how I tried to drown him in a bucket when Prim
13 brought him home. Scrawny kitten, belly swollen with worms, crawling with fleas. The last thing
14 I needed was another mouth to feed. But Prim begged so hard, cried even, I had to let him
15 stay. It turned out okay. My mother got rid of the vermin and he's a born mouser. Even catches
16 the occasional rat. Sometimes, when I clean a kill, I feed Buttercup the entrails. He has
17 stopped hissing at me.
18 Entrails. No hissing. This is the closest we will ever come to love.
19 I swing my legs off the bed and slide into my hunting boots. Supple leather that has
20 molded to my feet. I pull on trousers, a shirt, tuck my long dark braid up into a cap, and grab
21 my forage bag. On the table, under a wooden bowl to protect it from hungry rats and cats
22 alike, sits a perfect little goat cheese wrapped in basil leaves. Prim's gift to me on reaping day.
23 I put the cheese carefully in my pocket as I slip outside.
24 Our part of District 12, nicknamed the Seam, is usually crawling with coal miners heading
25 out to the morning shift at this hour. Men and women with hunched shoulders, swollen
26 knuckles, many who have long since stopped trying to scrub the coal dust out of their broken
27 nails, the lines of their sunken faces. But today the black cinder streets are empty. Shutters on
28 the squat gray houses are closed. The reaping isn't until two. May as well sleep in. If you can.
29 Our house is almost at the edge of the Seam. I only have to pass a few gates to reach the
30 scruffy field called the Meadow. Separating the Meadow from the woods, in fact enclosing all of
31 District 12, is a high chain-link fence topped with barbed-wire loops. In theory, it's supposed to
32 be electrified twenty-four hours a day as a deterrent to the predators that live in the woods —
33 packs of wild dogs, lone cougars, bears — that used to threaten our streets. But since we're
34 lucky to get two or three hours of electricity in the evenings, it's usually safe to touch. Even so, I
35 always take a moment to listen carefully for the hum that means the fence is live. Right now, it's
36 silent as a stone. Concealed by a clump of bushes, I flatten out on my belly and slide under a
37 two-foot stretch that's been loose for years. There are several other weak spots in the fence, but
38 this one is so close to home I almost always enter the woods here.
39 As soon as I'm in the trees, I retrieve a bow and sheath of arrows from a hollow log.
40 Electrified or not, the fence has been successful at keeping the flesh-eaters out of District 12.
41 Inside the woods they roam freely, and there are added concerns like venomous snakes, rabid
42 animals, and no real paths to follow. But there's also food if you know how to find it. My father
43 knew, and he taught me some before he was blown to bits in a mine explosion. There was
44 nothing even to bury. I was eleven then. Five years later, I still wake up screaming for him to run.

## Source A
**Title:** Life of Pi
**Author:** Yann Martel (2001)
**Context:** This extract is from a novel by Yann Martel. In this section the central character, Pi, is on a sinking ship. The ship is carrying the animals belonging to Pi's father, who owns a zoo.

1  Inside the ship, there were noises. Deep structural groans. I stumbled and fell. No harm done.
2  I got up. With the help of the handrails I went down the stairwell four steps at a time. I had
3  gone down just one level when I saw water. Lots of water. It was blocking my way. It was
4  surging from below like a riotous crowd, raging, frothing and boiling. Stairs vanished into
5  watery darkness. I couldn't believe my eyes. What was this water doing here? Where had it
6  come from? I stood nailed to the spot, frightened and incredible and ignorant of what I should
7  do next. Down there was where my family was.
8  I ran up the stairs. I got to the main deck. The weather wasn't entertaining any more. I was
9  very afraid. Now it was plain and obvious: the ship was listing badly. And it wasn't level the
10 other way either. There was a noticeable incline going from bow to stern. I looked overboard.
11 The water didn't look to be eighty feet away. The ship was sinking. My mind could hardly
12 conceive it. It was as unbelievable as the moon catching fire.
13 Where were the officers and the crew? What were they doing? Towards the bow I saw some
14 men running in the gloom. I thought I saw some animals too, but I dismissed the sight as
15 illusion crafted by rain and shadow. We had the hatch covers over their bay pulled open when
16 the weather was good, but at all times the animals were kept confined to their cages. These
17 were dangerous wild animals we were transporting, not farm livestock. Above me, on the
18 bridge, I thought I heard some men shouting.
19 The ship shook and there was that sound, the monstrous metallic burp. What was it? Was it
20 the collective scream of humans and animals protesting their oncoming death? Was it the ship
21 itself giving up the ghost? I fell over. I got to my feet. I looked overboard again. The sea was
22 rising. The waves were getting closer. We were sinking fast.
23 I clearly heard monkeys shrieking. Something was shaking the deck. A gaur — an Indian wild
24 ox — exploded out of the rain and thundered by me, terrified, out of control, berserk. I looked
25 at it, dumbstruck and amazed. Who in God's name had let it out?
26 I ran for the stairs to the bridge. Up there was where the officers were, the only people on the
27 ship who spoke English, the masters of our destiny here, the ones who would right this wrong.
28 They would explain everything. They would take care of my family and me. I climbed to the
29 middle bridge. There was no one on the starboard side. I ran to the port side. I saw three men,
30 crew members. I fell. I got up. They were looking overboard. I shouted. They turned. They
31 looked at me and at each other. They spoke a few words. They came towards me quickly. I
32 felt gratitude and relief welling up in me. I said, "Thank God I've found you. What is happening?
33 I am very scared. There is water at the bottom of the ship. I am worried about my family. I
34 can't get to the level where our cabins are. Is this normal? Do you think—"
35 One of the men interrupted me by thrusting a life jacket into my arms and shouting something
36 in Chinese. I noticed an orange whistle dangling from the life jacket. The men were nodding
37 vigorously at me. When they took hold of me and lifted me in their strong arms, I thought
38 nothing of it. I thought they were helping me. I was so full of trust in them that I felt grateful as
39 they carried me in the air. Only when they threw me overboard did I begin to have doubts.
