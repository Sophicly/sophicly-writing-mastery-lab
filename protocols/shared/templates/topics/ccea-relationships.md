# Topic 1: Sonnet 116
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Shakespeare's Sonnet 116 defines love by what it is not — it does not alter, bend, or fade. The poem's legal register ("impediments," "admit") frames love as a binding contract, while the navigation metaphor ("star to every wandering bark") presents it as a fixed point in an unstable world. The confident, almost defiant tone builds toward the audacious final couplet, which stakes the speaker's entire literary identity on the argument. Students should analyse how Shakespeare uses the sonnet form, rhetorical structure, and metaphor to present an idealised, absolute vision of love.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Sonnet 116
**Focus Poet:** William Shakespeare

## Question
Compare how the poets present ideas about the permanence of love in 'Sonnet 116' and one other poem from the Relationships collection.

# Topic 3: When We Two Parted
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Byron's poem explores the aftermath of a secret love affair, using the language of death and mourning to describe the end of a relationship. The poem's restraint — its controlled stanzas and measured rhythm — masks intense bitterness, while the cyclical structure of opening and closing with cold silence traps the speaker in unresolved grief. The biographical context of Byron's rumoured affair and the poem's concern with public reputation versus private feeling are key. Students should analyse how Byron uses sensory imagery of coldness and silence, and the motif of secrecy, to present the lasting pain of betrayal.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** When We Two Parted
**Focus Poet:** Lord Byron

# Topic 4: Porphyria's Lover
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Browning's dramatic monologue reveals a speaker whose desire to possess his lover culminates in murder. The chilling calm of the narration — describing the act of strangling Porphyria with her own hair as though it were perfectly reasonable — forces readers to recognise horror through the gap between the speaker's tone and his actions. The Victorian context of anxieties about female independence and male control, and Browning's interest in abnormal psychology, are essential. Students should analyse how Browning uses the unreliable narrator, pathetic fallacy, and the disturbingly domestic final tableau to explore obsessive love and power.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Porphyria's Lover
**Focus Poet:** Robert Browning

# Topic 5: Sonnet 43
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Barrett Browning's sonnet is a passionate declaration of love that attempts to measure an emotion beyond measurement. The anaphoric repetition of "I love thee" builds cumulative intensity, while the poem's movement from physical depth and breadth to spiritual transcendence and finally childhood innocence traces love through every dimension of human experience. The biographical context of Elizabeth's secret courtship with Robert Browning and her defiance of her controlling father add layers of personal courage. Students should analyse how Barrett Browning uses the Petrarchan sonnet form, abstract imagery, and the poem's place within the Sonnets from the Portuguese to present love as all-encompassing and eternal.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Sonnet 43
**Focus Poet:** Elizabeth Barrett Browning

# Topic 6: The Farmer's Bride
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Mew's dramatic monologue presents a marriage defined by mutual isolation — a young wife who fears physical intimacy and a husband whose sympathy gradually curdles into frustrated desire. The poem's power lies in its dual perspective: the farmer's voice is the only one we hear, yet his language increasingly reduces his wife to a trapped animal ("like a mouse," "like a leveret"). The shift to the desperate, possessive final stanza reveals how loneliness and social pressure distort even genuine feeling. Students should analyse how Mew uses natural imagery, shifting pronouns, and the dramatic monologue form to expose the power imbalance within the marriage.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** The Farmer's Bride
**Focus Poet:** Charlotte Mew

# Topic 7: Walking Away
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Day Lewis captures the universal parental experience of watching a child grow independent, using the specific memory of his son walking to a school football match as a lens for deeper separation. The poem's hesitant rhythm and self-correcting similes — "like a satellite / Wrenched from its orbit" — reveal a father still processing pain eighteen years later. The final assertion that "selfhood begins with a walking away" reframes loss as a necessary act of love. Students should analyse how Day Lewis uses extended metaphor, the gap between the event and its retelling, and the controlled four-stanza structure to explore why letting go is both painful and essential.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Walking Away
**Focus Poet:** Cecil Day Lewis

# Topic 8: Eden Rock
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Causley reimagines his dead parents in an idealised, sunlit landscape that hovers between memory and afterlife. The poem's quiet, precise domestic detail — the Thermos, the HP Sauce, the "sprigged" dress — grounds the supernatural moment in tender realism, making the impossible scene feel achingly tangible. The final lines, "I had not thought that it would be like this," leave the reader uncertain whether the speaker is approaching death, dreaming, or simply reaching toward something he can never recover. Students should analyse how Causley uses specific sensory detail, the present tense, and deliberate ambiguity to blur the boundary between memory and vision.
**Marks:** 30

# Topic 9: Follower
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Heaney traces the reversal of a father-son relationship from childhood hero-worship to adult role-reversal. The poem's agricultural language — "mapping the furrow exactly" — celebrates the father's physical skill with an almost reverent precision, while the stumbling child who once tripped and fell in the furrow becomes the capable adult. The final stanza's shift to present tense delivers the devastating reversal: the father now follows the son, "stumbling behind." Students should analyse how Heaney uses technical farming vocabulary, the regular quatrain form that mirrors the discipline of ploughing, and the ambiguous final image to explore admiration, guilt, and the painful shifting of power between generations.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Follower
**Focus Poet:** Seamus Heaney

## Question

# Topic 10: Mother Any Distance
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Armitage uses the extended metaphor of a tape measure to capture a son's move towards independence while remaining tethered to his mother. The poem's spatial movement — from ground floor to loft — mirrors the emotional arc of growing up, and the final image of the tape reaching "to breaking point" dramatises the tension between needing to separate and fearing the snap. The near-sonnet form (fifteen lines instead of fourteen) itself enacts the idea of stretching beyond a fixed boundary. Students should analyse how Armitage uses measurement imagery, spatial structure, and the modified sonnet form to explore the boundaries of familial love.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Mother Any Distance
**Focus Poet:** Simon Armitage

# Topic 11: Before You Were Mine
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Duffy reimagines her mother's glamorous youth before motherhood, addressing her in second person as though reclaiming a version of her that existed before the speaker was born. The possessive title sets up the poem's central tension — the speaker's love is tangled with guilt about what her mother sacrificed. The vivid cultural references to Marilyn Monroe and George Square root the mother's lost freedom in a specific time, while the final image of the mother's "high-heeled red shoes" becomes a symbol of everything motherhood required her to surrender. Students should analyse how Duffy uses tense shifts, possessive language, and specific cultural detail to explore how a child's existence inevitably reshapes a parent's identity.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Before You Were Mine
**Focus Poet:** Carol Ann Duffy
