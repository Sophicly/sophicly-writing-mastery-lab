# Topic 1: Analysing Imagery and Figurative Language
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Imagery and figurative language are the primary means by which poets construct meaning beyond the literal, and the ability to identify and analyse specific images — metaphors, similes, personification, symbolism — with precision and with reference to their effect is the foundation of all strong unseen poetry response. Students must move beyond labelling devices to examine why a particular image has been chosen: what it reveals about the speaker's attitude, what emotional response it generates, and how it contributes to the poem's central ideas. Comparing how two poets deploy figurative language requires tracking both similarity — shared imagery fields can suggest shared ideas — and difference, since the same vehicle (e.g. water, fire, light) can carry entirely opposite tenors depending on context. Analytical precision here means quoting the exact phrase, naming the technique, and articulating the specific effect in relation to meaning, not simply emotional impression.

# Topic 3: Structure and Form
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Structure and form are not decorative features but meaning-making choices: the number of stanzas, their regularity or irregularity, line length, enjambment, caesura, and the poem's overall shape all contribute to the way meaning is created and the emotional experience of reading. Students should approach structural analysis by asking what the form does — does regularity suggest control or constraint? does enjambment create momentum or unease? does a single-stanza poem suggest a single sustained thought or an overwhelming rush of feeling? Form choices including sonnet, dramatic monologue, lyric, elegy, or free verse each carry their own conventional associations that poets exploit, subvert, or combine. In comparative work, structural similarities between two poems often reveal shared attitudes or concerns, while structural contrast can illuminate how two poets reach different conclusions about the same subject.

# Topic 4: Tone and Attitude
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Identifying a poem's tone — the speaker's attitude towards their subject — is the first step in reading any unseen poem accurately, since misreading tone leads to misreading the entire text. Tone is constructed through diction (formal or colloquial, elevated or plain), imagery (celebratory or ominous), rhythm (controlled or fragmented), and the relationship established with the reader (intimate, confrontational, elegiac). Students must distinguish between the poet, the poem's speaker, and any persona the speaker adopts — an important distinction in dramatic monologues and persona poems. Tracking shifts in tone within a single poem is an advanced skill that often yields the most insightful analysis, since tonal change frequently signals the poem's central argument or emotional turning point. Comparing attitude in two poems requires identifying whether speakers share a perspective, adopt opposing ones, or begin from the same position and arrive at different conclusions.

# Topic 5: Sound and Rhythm
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Sound and rhythm are among the most under-analysed elements in unseen poetry response, yet they are often where a poem's emotional energy is most precisely encoded — rhythm creates expectation and its disruption creates surprise, while sound patterns reinforce or undercut semantic meaning. Students should develop sensitivity to metrical regularity (iambic patterns suggesting order or inevitability), disruption (a spondee or extra syllable creating emphasis or discomfort), and the varied effects of rhyme (full rhyme for resolution, slant rhyme for ambiguity, no rhyme for conversational realism or fragmentation). Alliteration, assonance, and onomatopoeia should always be linked to semantic meaning rather than described in isolation: it is not enough to note that sibilance is present, but to explain what the hissing effect contributes to the poem's ideas. In comparison work, two poems on the same subject may use contrasting sound strategies — one formal and controlled, one fragmented and discordant — to argue quite different positions.

# Topic 6: Comparison Techniques
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Effective comparative writing is not alternating analysis of two poems but the sustained examination of a shared question, with both poems always in view — the critical skill is identifying what exactly is being compared, whether attitude, technique, structure, or effect, and maintaining that focus throughout. The most common structural error in Part B responses is writing two separate analyses joined by a concluding paragraph; effective comparison weaves between the poems at the level of the idea, using connectives such as "similarly", "in contrast", "whereas", "both poets", and "unlike" to signal the relationship between the two texts at every stage. Students should aim to identify one or two central points of comparison and pursue them with depth — one well-developed comparison with precise textual support from both poems outweighs five surface observations. Comparative analysis should always evaluate significance: it is not merely what is similar or different that matters, but what that similarity or difference reveals about how each poet thinks about their subject.

# Topic 7: Language and Word Choice
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Word choice in poetry is always a selection from alternatives, and close analysis of diction should always ask what the chosen word achieves that its alternatives could not — why "shattered" rather than "broken", why "whisper" rather than "speak", why an unexpected register or semantic field. Register — formal, colloquial, archaic, scientific, religious — carries associations that contribute to meaning before the reader is consciously aware of them, and shifts in register within a poem are often the key to understanding its central argument. Connotation is the critical concept here: a single word can carry an enormous weight of cultural, emotional, or political association, and poets choose with precision to exploit or disrupt these associations. In comparative work, analysing lexical choices across two poems on the same subject can reveal how each poet positions themselves in relation to that subject — the same subject treated with clinical detachment in one poem and visceral intimacy in another tells us as much about attitude as any explicit statement.

# Topic 8: Symbolism and Extended Metaphor
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Symbolism operates when an object, image, or figure in a poem accumulates meaning beyond its literal function, carrying emotional or ideological weight that enriches and complicates the poem's surface narrative. Extended metaphor — a metaphorical comparison sustained across multiple lines or an entire poem — requires students to track how the metaphor develops, where it is strained or subverted, and what its breaking point, if it has one, reveals about the poem's ideas. The analysis of symbolism and extended metaphor must always be grounded in specific textual evidence: it is not sufficient to assert that water "symbolises" something, but to show how the specific deployment of water imagery in this poem creates that symbolic meaning. In comparative work, two poems may deploy the same symbol with very different intent — a journey can signify hope or entrapment, depending on how it is rendered — and noting both the shared symbol and the divergent treatment offers the most sophisticated comparative analysis.

# Topic 9: Personal Response and Evaluation
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** A personal response in unseen poetry analysis is not a record of emotional reaction but a considered evaluation of how successfully the poet has achieved their apparent intentions — it requires the student to identify what the poem is trying to do and then to assess how effectively specific techniques serve that aim. The EDUQAS mark scheme rewards students who demonstrate genuine engagement with poems as whole constructs rather than lists of features, which means that evaluation must always be grounded in analysis: "this poem is effective because the extended metaphor of the caged bird sustains a consistent critique of oppression without becoming didactic" is evaluation, while "I thought this poem was powerful" is not. Personal response is also about interpretive choice: where a poem is genuinely ambiguous, selecting a defensible reading and arguing for it with evidence is more valuable than hedging between all possible interpretations. Students should aim to use the personal response element of their answer to demonstrate that they have moved beyond what the poem says to why and how it says it in the way it does.

# Topic 10: Contextual Understanding
**Type:** unseen-poetry
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Contextual understanding in unseen poetry requires students to draw on what they know about the period, tradition, or cultural background that might inform a poem they have not seen before — identifying allusions to biblical narrative, classical mythology, historical event, or poetic tradition is itself a form of contextual analysis. For EDUQAS Part B, AO3 (contexts) is specifically assessed, and students must demonstrate that they understand how the context of production or reception shapes meaning — not biographical speculation, but an understanding of how the historical or cultural moment leaves its mark on the poem's language, concerns, or form. Context is always in service of interpretation: the purpose of noting that a poem was written during the First World War is not to show knowledge but to illuminate why certain images of sacrifice, duty, and futility carry the weight they do. In comparative work, the most sophisticated AO3 responses identify how both poems are shaped by their specific contexts and then ask what this reveals about each poet's relationship to the ideas they are exploring.
