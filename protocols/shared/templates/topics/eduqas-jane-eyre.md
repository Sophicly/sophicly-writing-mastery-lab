# Topic 1: Jane Eyre (protagonist)
**Type:** protagonist
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Jane is Bronte's radical reimagining of the Victorian heroine -- plain, poor, and dependent, yet fiercely assertive about her right to equality and emotional fulfilment. Bronte uses first-person retrospective narration to give Jane intellectual authority over her own story, and the EDUQAS either/or format allows students to choose between close language analysis of a key speech and a broader argument about her significance across the whole novel.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 23

### Extract
```
 1    "Do you think, because I am poor, obscure, plain, and little, I am
 2    soulless and heartless? You think wrong! -- I have as much soul as
 3    you, -- and full as much heart! And if God had gifted me with some
 4    beauty and much wealth, I should have made it as hard for you to
 5    leave me, as it is now for me to leave you. I am not talking to you
 6    now through the medium of custom, conventionalities, nor even of
 7    mortal flesh; -- it is my spirit that addresses your spirit; just as
 8    if both had passed through the grave, and we stood at God's feet,
 9    equal, -- as we are!"
10
11    "As we are!" repeated Mr. Rochester -- "so," he added, enclosing me
12    in his arms, gathering me to his breast, pressing his lips on my
13    lips: "so, Jane!"
14
15    "Yes, so, sir," I rejoined: "and yet not so; for you are a married
16    man -- or as good as a married man, and wed to one inferior to you --
17    to one with whom you have no sympathy -- whom I do not believe you
18    truly love; for I have seen and heard you sneer at her. I would
19    scorn such a union: therefore I am better than you -- let me go!"
20
21    "Where, Jane? To Ireland?"
22
23    "Yes -- to Ireland. I have spoken my mind, and can go anywhere now."
24
25    "Jane, be still; don't struggle so, like a wild frantic bird that is
26    rending its own plumage in its desperation."
```

# Topic 3: Rochester
**Type:** character
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Rochester is Bronte's Byronic hero -- passionate, worldly, morally compromised, and magnetically attractive despite his flaws. His secret marriage to Bertha and his attempted bigamy with Jane raise profound questions about whether love can justify deception. Students should analyse how Bronte generates sympathy for Rochester while simultaneously condemning his actions, and how his blinding and maiming at the end can be read as penance, equalisation, or even emasculation.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 14

### Extract
```
 1    He lifted up the sable waves of hair which lay horizontally over his
 2    brow, and showed a solid enough mass of intellectual organs, but an
 3    abrupt deficiency where the suave sign of benevolence should have
 4    risen.
 5
 6    "Now, ma'am, am I a fool?"
 7
 8    "Far from it, sir. You would, perhaps, think me rude if I inquired
 9    in return whether you are a philanthropist?"
10
11    "There again! Another stick of the penknife, when she pretended to
12    pat my head: and that is because I said I did not find it
13    impossible to be as good as you. No: I am not a philanthropist; but
14    I bear a conscience;" and he pointed to the prominences which are
15    said to indicate that faculty, and which, fortunately for him, were
16    sufficiently conspicuous; giving, indeed, a marked breadth to the
17    upper part of his head: "and, besides, I once had a kind of rude
18    tenderness of heart. When I was as old as you, I was a feeling
19    fellow enough; partial to the unfledged, unfostered, and unlucky;
20    but Fortune has knocked me about since: she has even kneaded me with
21    her knuckles, and now I flatter myself I am hard and tough as an
22    India-rubber ball; pervious, though, through a chink or two still,
23    and with one sentient point in the middle of the lump. Yes: does
24    that leave hope for me?"
25
26    "Hope of what, sir?"
27
28    "Of my final re-transformation from India-rubber back to flesh?"
```

# Topic 4: Love and Passion
**Type:** theme
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Love in Jane Eyre is never simple or unconditional -- Bronte insists that genuine love must coexist with equality, honesty, and self-respect. The novel tests this idea by placing Jane between two inadequate models of love: Rochester's consuming passion (which demands she surrender her principles) and St. John's cold, dutiful proposal (which denies her emotional needs entirely). Only when both parties are transformed can a balanced union emerge.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 37

# Topic 5: Independence and Self-Respect
**Type:** theme
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Jane's insistence on independence is what distinguishes her from the passive, submissive ideal of Victorian womanhood. Bronte presents self-respect not as selfishness but as a moral imperative -- Jane would rather starve on the moor than live in comfort as Rochester's mistress. Students should trace how Jane's independence develops from childhood rebellion (the Red Room) through economic self-sufficiency (Lowood, Thornfield, her inheritance) to the final marriage conducted entirely on her own terms.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 27

### Extract
```
 1    "Jane, I ever like your tone of voice: it still renews hope, it
 2    sounds so truthful. When I hear it, it carries me back a year. I
 3    forget that you have formed a new tie. But you said you would not
 4    leave me -- you said you would be with me still."
 5
 6    "I will be with you, if you will -- only not as your mistress; that
 7    I shall never be."
 8
 9    He clasped his arms round me, and drew me towards him. In his face
10    I saw a change: it was becoming dark and threatening. I felt afraid
11    -- but I would not flinch. I saw that something bitter was coming.
12
13    "Oh, Jane, this is bitter! This -- this is wicked. Would you not be
14    happy, Jane?"
15
16    I steeled myself, and said --
17
18    "No, sir; I should not be happy. I should feel degraded. I should
19    not be myself. Do you think I can stay to become nothing to you? Do
20    you think, because I am poor and plain, I have no feelings? I tell
21    you I have -- and if you were to treat me so, you would kill the
22    best part of both of us."

# Topic 6: Social Class and Inequality
**Type:** theme
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Jane occupies an uneasy position in the Victorian class system -- too educated to be a servant, too poor to be a gentleman's equal. Bronte uses this liminal status to expose the cruelty and hypocrisy of a society that judges people by birth and wealth rather than character. The novel's treatment of class intersects with gender: as a penniless, unconnected woman, Jane experiences the double disadvantage of being both female and poor, making her eventual self-assertion all the more radical.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 17

# Topic 7: Religion and Morality
**Type:** theme
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Bronte presents three distinct models of Christianity through Brocklehurst (hypocritical punishment), Helen Burns (passive endurance and heavenly reward), and St. John Rivers (cold, self-denying duty) -- and rejects all three. Jane forges her own moral code that balances spiritual conviction with personal feeling. Students should analyse how Bronte critiques institutional religion while affirming a deeply personal faith, and how Jane's moral decisions (leaving Rochester, rejecting St. John) are framed as spiritual acts rather than merely practical ones.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 7

### Extract

 1    "You have a wicked heart; and you must pray to God to change it: to
 2    give you a new and clean one: to take away your heart of stone and
 3    give you a heart of flesh."
 4
 5    I was about to propound a question, touching the manner in which
 6    that operation of changing my heart was to be performed, when Mrs.
 7    Reed preceded me by her rapid transit to the subject.
 8
 9    "Mr. Brocklehurst, I believe I intimated in the letter which I
10    wrote to you three weeks ago, that this little girl has not quite
11    the character and disposition I could wish: should you admit her
12    into Lowood school, I should be glad if the superintendent and
13    teachers were requested to keep a strict eye on her, and, above
14    all, to guard against her worst fault, a tendency to deceit. I
15    mention this in your hearing, Jane, that you may not attempt to

# Topic 8: The Red Room / Childhood Suffering
**Type:** theme
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** The Red Room is one of the most symbolically rich episodes in the novel -- a Gothic space of confinement, injustice, and psychological terror that shapes Jane's character for the rest of the narrative. Bronte uses the child Jane's suffering at Gateshead and Lowood to establish the novel's central argument: that cruelty breeds not submission but defiance, and that the impulse to resist injustice is the foundation of moral strength. The Red Room also introduces the novel's supernatural atmosphere and foreshadows Jane's later imprisonments (Thornfield's secret, the moors).

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 2

### Extract
```
 1    The red-room was a square chamber, very seldom slept in, I might
 2    say never, indeed, unless when a chance influx of visitors at
 3    Gateshead Hall rendered it necessary to turn to account all the
 4    accommodation it contained: yet it was one of the largest and
 5    stateliest chambers in the mansion. A bed supported on massive
 6    pillars of mahogany, hung with curtains of deep red damask, stood
 7    out like a tabernacle in the centre; the two large windows, with
 8    their blinds always drawn down, were half shrouded in festoons and
 9    falls of similar drapery; the carpet was red; the table at the
10    foot of the bed was covered with a crimson cloth; the walls were a
11    soft fawn colour with a blush of pink in it; the wardrobe, the
12    toilet-table, the chairs were of darkly polished old mahogany. Out
13    of these deep surrounding shades rose high, and glared white, the
14    piled-up mattresses and pillows of the bed, spread with a snowy
15    Marseilles counterpane. Scarcely less prominent was an ample
16    cushioned easy-chair near the head of the bed, also white, with a
17    footstool before it; and looking, as I thought, like a pale
18    throne.
19
20    This room was chill, because it seldom had a fire; it was silent,
21    because remote from the nursery and kitchen; solemn, because it
22    was known to be so seldom entered. The house-maid alone came here
23    on Saturdays, to wipe from the mirrors and the furniture a week's
24    quiet dust: and Mrs. Reed herself, at far intervals, visited it to
25    review the contents of a certain secret drawer in the wardrobe,
26    where were stored divers parchments, her jewel-casket, and a
27    miniature of her dead husband.
```

# Topic 9: Bertha Mason
**Type:** character
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Bertha Mason is one of the most debated figures in English literature. On one level she is a Gothic device -- the madwoman in the attic whose existence creates the novel's central crisis. But she is also a woman imprisoned, silenced, and dehumanised by a husband who married her for money and then locked her away when she became inconvenient. Post-colonial and feminist critics have read Bertha as a symbol of everything the Victorian marriage plot suppresses: female rage, colonial exploitation, and the violence done to women who do not conform. Students should consider whose perspective we see Bertha through, and what Bronte gains (and loses) by never giving her a voice.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 26

# Topic 10: Gender and Patriarchy
**Type:** theme
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Jane Eyre is a sustained challenge to Victorian patriarchy. Bronte places her heroine in a series of relationships with powerful men -- John Reed, Brocklehurst, Rochester, St. John Rivers -- each of whom attempts to control, define, or diminish her. Jane resists them all, not through physical power but through moral and intellectual assertion. Students should analyse how Bronte exposes the mechanisms of patriarchal control (economic dependence, religious authority, emotional manipulation) and how Jane's refusal to be silenced anticipates feminist arguments that would not be fully articulated for decades.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 12

### Extract
```
 1    It is in vain to say human beings ought to be satisfied with
 2    tranquillity: they must have action; and they will make it if they
 3    cannot find it. Millions are condemned to a stiller doom than mine,
 4    and millions are in silent revolt against their lot. Nobody knows
 5    how many rebellions besides political rebellions ferment in the
 6    masses of life which people earth. Women are supposed to be very
 7    calm generally: but women feel just as men feel; they need exercise
 8    for their faculties, and a field for their efforts, as much as
 9    their brothers do; they suffer from too rigid a restraint, too
10    absolute a stagnation, precisely as men would suffer; and it is
11    narrow-minded in their more privileged fellow-creatures to say that
12    they ought to confine themselves to making puddings and knitting
13    stockings, to playing on the piano and embroidering bags. It is
14    thoughtless to condemn them, or laugh at them, if they seek to do
15    more or learn more than custom has pronounced necessary for their
16    sex.
17
18    When thus alone, I not unfrequently heard Grace Poole's laugh: the
19    same peal, the same low, slow ha! ha! which, when first heard, had
20    thrilled me: I heard, too, her eccentric murmurs; stranger than her

22    others when I could not account for the sounds she made. Sometimes
23    I saw her: she would come out of her room with a basin, or a plate,
24    or a tray, in her hand, go down to the kitchen and shortly return,
25    generally (oh, romantic reader, forgive me for telling the plain
26    truth!) bearing a pot of porter.
