# Topic 1: Prospero
**Type:** protagonist
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Prospero's "rough magic" speech is the pivot of the entire play -- the moment absolute power is voluntarily surrendered. Students should explore how Shakespeare uses Prospero to examine whether controlling others can ever be justified, and how the decision to "drown my book" transforms him from tyrannical magician to flawed but merciful human.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 5, Scene 1

### Extract
1  PROSPERO: Ye elves of hills, brooks, standing lakes and groves,
2  And ye that on the sands with printless foot
3  Do chase the ebbing Neptune, and do fly him
4  When he comes back; you demi-puppets that
5  By moonshine do the green sour ringlets make
6  Whereof the ewe not bites; and you whose pastime
7  Is to make midnight mushrooms, that rejoice
8  To hear the solemn curfew; by whose aid,
9  Weak masters though ye be, I have bedimmed
10 The noontide sun, called forth the mutinous winds,
11 And 'twixt the green sea and the azured vault
12 Set roaring war; to the dread rattling thunder
13 Have I given fire, and rifted Jove's stout oak
14 With his own bolt; graves at my command
15 Have waked their sleepers, oped, and let 'em forth
16 By my so potent art. But this rough magic
17 I here abjure, and when I have required
18 Some heavenly music, which even now I do,
19 To work mine end upon their senses that
20 This airy charm is for, I'll break my staff,
21 Bury it certain fathoms in the earth,
22 And deeper than did ever plummet sound
23 I'll drown my book.

### Question
Look at how Shakespeare presents Prospero's power in this extract. You should consider:
- what Prospero says about his magical abilities
- Shakespeare's use of language and structure
- the context of the extract within the play.

[20]

## Part B
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

### Question
To what extent does Shakespeare present Prospero as a just and fair ruler in *The Tempest*? [20]

# Topic 3: Caliban
**Type:** character
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Caliban's confrontation with Prospero in Act 1 is one of Shakespeare's most politically charged exchanges. The competing claims to the island -- Caliban's inheritance versus Prospero's "civilising" mission -- invite postcolonial readings that connect the play to Jacobean encounters with the Americas. Students should explore how Shakespeare complicates any simple reading of Caliban as "monster" or victim.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 1, Scene 2

### Extract
1  CALIBAN: This island's mine, by Sycorax my mother,
2  Which thou tak'st from me. When thou cam'st first,
3  Thou strok'st me and made much of me, wouldst give me
4  Water with berries in't, and teach me how
5  To name the bigger light and how the less,
6  That burn by day and night. And then I loved thee,
7  And showed thee all the qualities o'th' isle:
8  The fresh springs, brine pits, barren place and fertile.
9  Cursed be I that did so! All the charms
10 Of Sycorax, toads, beetles, bats, light on you!
11 For I am all the subjects that you have,
12 Which first was mine own king; and here you sty me
13 In this hard rock, whiles you do keep from me
14 The rest o'th' island.
15 PROSPERO:                   Thou most lying slave,
16 Whom stripes may move, not kindness! I have used thee,
17 Filth as thou art, with humane care, and lodged thee
18 In mine own cell, till thou didst seek to violate
19 The honour of my child.
20 CALIBAN: O ho, O ho! Would't had been done!
21 Thou didst prevent me; I had peopled else
22 This isle with Calibans.
23 MIRANDA: Abhorred slave,
24 Which any print of goodness wilt not take,
25 Being capable of all ill! I pitied thee,
26 Took pains to make thee speak, taught thee each hour
27 One thing or other. When thou didst not, savage,
28 Know thine own meaning, but wouldst gabble like
29 A thing most brutish, I endowed thy purposes
30 With words that made them known.

### Question
Look at how Shakespeare presents the conflict between Caliban and Prospero in this extract. You should consider:
- what Caliban and Prospero say and do
- Shakespeare's use of language and structure
- the context of the extract within the play.

[20]

## Part B
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

### Question
To what extent does Shakespeare present Caliban as a sympathetic character in *The Tempest*? [20]

# Topic 4: Power and Control
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Prospero's first interaction with Ariel establishes the master-servant dynamic that underpins the entire play. His reminder of Ariel's past imprisonment and his threats of further punishment reveal how even apparently benevolent authority relies on coercion. Students should analyse how Shakespeare uses this exchange to question whether power can ever be exercised without exploitation.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 1, Scene 2

### Extract
1  ARIEL: Is there more toil? Since thou dost give me pains,
2  Let me remember thee what thou hast promised,
3  Which is not yet performed me.
4  PROSPERO:                        How now? Moody?
5  What is't thou canst demand?
6  ARIEL:                          My liberty.
7  PROSPERO: Before the time be out? No more!
8  ARIEL:                                      I prithee,
9  Remember I have done thee worthy service,
10 Told thee no lies, made no mistakings, served
11 Without or grudge or grumblings. Thou did promise
12 To bate me a full year.
13 PROSPERO:                  Dost thou forget
14 From what a torment I did free thee?
15 ARIEL:                                  No.
16 PROSPERO: Thou dost, and think'st it much to tread the ooze
17 Of the salt deep,
18 To run upon the sharp wind of the north,
19 To do me business in the veins o'th' earth
20 When it is baked with frost.
21 ARIEL:                        I do not, sir.
22 PROSPERO: Thou liest, malignant thing! Hast thou forgot
23 The foul witch Sycorax, who with age and envy
24 Was grown into a hoop? Hast thou forgot her?
25 ARIEL: No, sir.
26 PROSPERO:        Thou hast. Where was she born?
27 Speak! Tell me!
28 ARIEL: Sir, in Algier.
29 PROSPERO:               O, was she so? I must
30 Once in a month recount what thou hast been,
31 Which thou forget'st.

### Question
Look at how Shakespeare presents the theme of power in this extract. You should consider:
- what Prospero and Ariel say and do
- Shakespeare's use of language and structure
- the context of the extract within the play.

## Part B
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

### Question

# Topic 5: Magic and the Supernatural
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The masque sequence and Prospero's "Our revels now are ended" speech blur the line between theatrical spectacle and magical power. The sudden dissolution of the masque when Prospero remembers Caliban's plot reveals that even his art has limits. Students should explore how Shakespeare uses magic as both a creative force and a metaphor for the power of theatre itself.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 4, Scene 1

### Extract
1  PROSPERO: You do look, my son, in a moved sort,
2  As if you were dismayed. Be cheerful, sir.
3  Our revels now are ended. These our actors,
4  As I foretold you, were all spirits, and
5  Are melted into air, into thin air;
6  And, like the baseless fabric of this vision,
7  The cloud-capped towers, the gorgeous palaces,
8  The solemn temples, the great globe itself,
9  Yea, all which it inherit, shall dissolve,
10 And, like this insubstantial pageant faded,
11 Leave not a rack behind. We are such stuff
12 As dreams are made on, and our little life
13 Is rounded with a sleep. Sir, I am vexed.
14 Bear with my weakness; my old brain is troubled.
15 Be not disturbed with my infirmity.
16 If you be pleased, retire into my cell
17 And there repose. A turn or two I'll walk
18 To still my beating mind.
19 FERDINAND and MIRANDA: We wish your peace.
20 PROSPERO: Come with a thought! I thank thee, Ariel. Come!
21
22                     Enter ARIEL
23
24 ARIEL: Thy thoughts I cleave to. What's thy pleasure?
25 PROSPERO:                                              Spirit,
26 We must prepare to meet with Caliban.
27 ARIEL: Ay, my commander. When I presented Ceres,
28 I thought to have told thee of it, but I feared
29 Lest I might anger thee.

### Question
Look at how Shakespeare presents magic and illusion in this extract. You should consider:

# Topic 6: Colonialism and Freedom
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Caliban's "isle is full of noises" speech is among the most beautiful verse Shakespeare ever wrote -- and he gives it to the character the other characters call a "monster." Students should explore how this complicates colonial readings of the play, and how Caliban's alliance with Stephano and Trinculo both mirrors and parodies the European power structures that have dispossessed him.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 3, Scene 2

### Extract
1  CALIBAN: Be not afeard. The isle is full of noises,
2  Sounds and sweet airs that give delight and hurt not.
3  Sometimes a thousand twangling instruments
4  Will hum about mine ears, and sometime voices
5  That, if I then had waked after long sleep,
6  Will make me sleep again; and then, in dreaming,
7  The clouds methought would open and show riches
8  Ready to drop upon me, that when I waked
9  I cried to dream again.
10 STEPHANO: This will prove a brave kingdom to me, where
11 I shall have my music for nothing.
12 CALIBAN: When Prospero is destroyed.
13 STEPHANO: That shall be by and by. I remember the story.
14 TRINCULO: The sound is nothing. There is more profit
15 in the other.
16 STEPHANO: Not I; I'll follow Caliban. Lead, monster; we'll
17 follow. I would I could see this taborer. He lays it on.
18 TRINCULO: Wilt come? I'll follow Stephano.
19 CALIBAN: I'll show thee the best springs. I'll pluck thee berries.
20 I'll fish for thee and get thee wood enough.
21 A plague upon the tyrant that I serve!
22 I'll bear him no more sticks, but follow thee,
23 Thou wondrous man.

# Topic 7: Miranda
**Type:** character
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Miranda's opening speech establishes her as the play's moral compass -- her instinctive compassion for the shipwrecked sailors contrasts sharply with her father's calculated manipulation. Students should explore how Shakespeare uses Miranda to examine the tension between innocence and experience, and how her limited knowledge of the world makes her both admirably empathetic and dangerously naive.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 1, Scene 2

### Extract
1  MIRANDA: If by your art, my dearest father, you have
2  Put the wild waters in this roar, allay them.
3  The sky, it seems, would pour down stinking pitch,
4  But that the sea, mounting to th' welkin's cheek,
5  Dashes the fire out. O, I have suffered
6  With those that I saw suffer! A brave vessel,
7  Who had, no doubt, some noble creature in her,
8  Dashed all to pieces. O, the cry did knock
9  Against my very heart! Poor souls, they perished.
10 Had I been any god of power, I would
11 Have sunk the sea within the earth, or ere
12 It should the good ship so have swallowed and
13 The fraughting souls within her.
14 PROSPERO:                          Be collected.
15 No more amazement. Tell your piteous heart
16 There's no harm done.
17 MIRANDA:                  O, woe the day!
18 PROSPERO:                                   No harm.
19 I have done nothing but in care of thee,
20 Of thee, my dear one, thee, my daughter, who
21 Art ignorant of what thou art, naught knowing
22 Of whence I am, nor that I am more better
23 Than Prospero, master of a full poor cell,
24 And thy no greater father.

# Topic 8: Ariel
**Type:** character
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The Act 5 exchange between Ariel and Prospero is the moral turning point of the play. Ariel -- a spirit, "but air" -- expresses pity for the suffering nobles, and this prompts Prospero to choose mercy over vengeance. Students should explore how Shakespeare uses Ariel's emotional intelligence to challenge Prospero's authority and to raise questions about what it means to be truly human.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 5, Scene 1

### Extract
1  ARIEL: Your charm so strongly works 'em
2  That if you now beheld them your affections
3  Would become tender.
4  PROSPERO:                 Dost thou think so, spirit?
5  ARIEL: Mine would, sir, were I human.
6  PROSPERO:                              And mine shall.
7  Hast thou, which art but air, a touch, a feeling
8  Of their afflictions, and shall not myself,
9  One of their kind, that relish all as sharply
10 Passion as they, be kindlier moved than thou art?
11 Though with their high wrongs I am struck to th' quick,
12 Yet with my nobler reason 'gainst my fury
13 Do I take part. The rarer action is
14 In virtue than in vengeance. They being penitent,
15 The sole drift of my purpose doth extend
16 Not a frown further. Go release them, Ariel.
17 My charms I'll break, their senses I'll restore,
18 And they shall be themselves.
19 ARIEL:                          I'll fetch them, sir.
20 PROSPERO: [draws a magic circle]
21 Now does my project gather to a head.
22 My charms crack not, my spirits obey, and Time
23 Goes upright with his carriage. How's the day?
24 ARIEL: On the sixth hour, at which time, my lord,
25 You said our work should cease.
26 PROSPERO:                         I did say so,
27 When first I raised the tempest.

# Topic 9: Forgiveness and Reconciliation
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The reunion between Prospero and Alonso is the emotional climax of the play, yet Shakespeare leaves the forgiveness deliberately ambiguous. Alonso's remorse is genuine, but Antonio and Sebastian remain silent -- never explicitly repenting. Students should consider whether the reconciliation is complete or merely a surface restoration of order, and what Shakespeare suggests about the limits of forgiveness.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 5, Scene 1

### Extract
1  PROSPERO: [to Alonso] Behold, sir King,
2  The wronged Duke of Milan, Prospero.
3  For more assurance that a living prince
4  Does now speak to thee, I embrace thy body;
5  And to thee and thy company I bid
6  A hearty welcome.
7  ALONSO:              Whe'er thou beest he or no,
8  Or some enchanted trifle to abuse me,
9  As late I have been, I not know. Thy pulse
10 Beats as of flesh and blood; and since I saw thee,
11 Th' affliction of my mind amends, with which
12 I fear a madness held me. This must crave,
13 An if this be at all, a most strange story.
14 Thy dukedom I resign, and do entreat
15 Thou pardon me my wrongs. But how should Prospero
16 Be living, and be here?
17 PROSPERO: [to Gonzalo] First, noble friend,
18 Let me embrace thine age, whose honour cannot
19 Be measured or confined.
20 GONZALO:                   Whether this be
21 Or be not, I'll not swear.
22 PROSPERO:                    You do yet taste
23 Some subtleties o'th' isle, that will not let you
24 Believe things certain. Welcome, my friends all!
25 [to Antonio and Sebastian] But you, my brace of lords,
26 were I so minded,
27 I here could pluck his highness' frown upon you
28 And justify you traitors. At this time
29 I will tell no tales.

# Topic 10: Illusion and Reality
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The banquet scene in Act 3 is Shakespeare's most spectacular theatrical illusion within the play. The vanishing feast, Ariel's harpy transformation, and the nobles' bewildered reactions dramatise how Prospero uses staged spectacle to manipulate perception and impose moral judgement. Students should explore how Shakespeare uses this scene to reflect on the power of theatre itself to reveal truth through illusion.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 3, Scene 3

### Extract
1  [Solemn and strange music; PROSPERO on the top, invisible.
2  Enter several strange Shapes, bringing in a banquet; and
3  dance about it with gentle actions of salutations; and,
4  inviting the King etc. to eat, they depart.]
5  ALONSO: Give us kind keepers, heavens! What were these?
6  SEBASTIAN: A living drollery! Now I will believe
7  That there are unicorns; that in Arabia
8  There is one tree, the phoenix' throne, one phoenix
9  At this hour reigning there.
10 ANTONIO:                       I'll believe both;
11 And what does else want credit, come to me
12 And I'll be sworn 'tis true. Travellers ne'er did lie,
13 Though fools at home condemn 'em.
14 GONZALO:                            If in Naples
15 I should report this now, would they believe me
16 If I should say I saw such islanders?
17 For certes these are people of the island,
18 Who though they are of monstrous shape, yet note
19 Their manners are more gentle, kind, than of
20 Our human generation you shall find
21 Many, nay, almost any.
22 PROSPERO: [aside]        Honest lord,
23 Thou hast said well, for some of you there present
24 Are worse than devils.
25 ALONSO:                  I cannot too much muse
26 Such shapes, such gesture, and such sound, expressing,
27 Although they want the use of tongue, a kind
28 Of excellent dumb discourse.
