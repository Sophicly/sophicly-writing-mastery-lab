# Topic 1: Phil
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Kelly presents Phil as one of contemporary drama's most unsettling figures — a character whose complete emotional stillness and refusal to speak most of the time makes him simultaneously the group's dominant force and its most opaque. Phil's silence is theatrical power: by saying almost nothing while the other characters perform their anxiety around him, Kelly dramatises how authority can be exercised without words, and how a leadership vacuum is filled not by the most ethical but by the most psychologically dominant. His methodical consumption of food throughout the play — crisps, cornets, waffle — is both comedically mundane and deeply disturbing in context, suggesting a person who has disengaged from the moral dimensions of the situation entirely. Phil's eventual disappearance into a new, equally controlling silence after Adam's death raises the most troubling question the play poses: is he an anomaly or an extreme point on a spectrum of human indifference?
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 3: Leah
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Leah is the play's moral conscience and its most complex voice — her rambling, philosophical monologues addressed to the silent Phil perform a kind of desperate meaning-making in the face of events that resist any comfortable interpretation. Kelly gives Leah the play's most sustained verbal energy, filling the silences that Phil creates with a stream of consciousness that moves from chimpanzee social behaviour to the nature of happiness to the structure of identity, suggesting a mind that reaches for intellectual frameworks when emotional ones fail. Her questions — "Are we the same as or different from our ancestors?", "What is it that makes us human?" — directly articulate the play's thematic concerns, and the contrast between her verbal richness and Phil's silence dramatises the gap between moral awareness and moral action. Leah's eventual withdrawal from the group, and her replacement by a version of Phil's silence (she "just doesn't talk anymore"), is the play's most disturbing moral endpoint.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 4: John Tate
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** John Tate's arc from panicked group leader to converted Christian is one of the play's sharpest observations about the psychology of collective crisis — his initial authority is entirely performed (shouting, threats, the word "love" used as menace), and it collapses the moment Phil offers a more coherent narrative. Kelly presents him as a character whose social power depends entirely on the absence of a stronger personality, making his fall from leadership as sudden and complete as his brief rise. His conversion to Christianity at the play's end reads simultaneously as genuine psychological refuge and as a commentary on how people seek frameworks of absolution when confronted by their own moral failure. John Tate shows how adolescent social hierarchies are constructed through performance rather than merit, and how completely they can be dismantled.
**Marks:** 40

# Topic 5: Cathy
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Cathy represents perhaps the play's most explicit diagnosis of how collective moral collapse can accelerate in a willing participant — she is not reluctant or conflicted in her complicity but enthusiastic, suggesting that for some people the release from conventional moral constraint is experienced as pleasure rather than crisis. Her escalating involvement — from participant in the cover-up to the person who frames an innocent man — reveals how moral thresholds shift under group pressure, and Kelly presents her as the logical endpoint of a process that begins with everyone's compromise. Her cheerful, matter-of-fact manner in reporting what she has done is more disturbing than if she were visibly violent, because it suggests she is not deviant but simply more completely adapted to the group's moral framework than the others. Cathy functions as a warning about the institutional dangers of rewarding complicity with approval.

# Topic 6: Guilt and Morality
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** *DNA* is structured as a moral pressure test — Kelly places his characters in a crisis that progressively exposes the gap between their articulated moral values and their actual behaviour, asking the audience to examine where on that spectrum they would fall. The original accident is ambiguous (the bullying was real, the fall may not have been intentional), but the subsequent cover-up involves genuinely knowing moral choices, and Kelly tracks how each character negotiates, evades, or accepts responsibility. The play refuses the comfort of punishment: the group largely escapes legal consequence, but Kelly suggests this does not resolve the moral problem — the question of what they owe Adam, and each other, persists beyond the plot. Leah's monologues are the play's most direct engagement with moral philosophy, and her eventual silence suggests that sustained moral consciousness has costs that the group dynamic makes impossible to bear.

# Topic 7: Group Dynamics and Peer Pressure
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Kelly structures *DNA* as an anatomy of group psychology — how collectives make decisions that individuals might not, how social pressure overrides individual moral judgement, and how belonging to a group creates its own imperative that is harder to resist than any external authority. The play's episodic structure (street/field alternation) formally enacts the way decisions are made, reported, and escalated within the group, with the field scenes as the leadership consultations that determine the group's next move. Phil's cover-up plan works precisely because it offers the group a narrative of shared complicity: everyone has a role, everyone is implicated, and therefore no one can break ranks without destroying themselves. Kelly draws on real social psychology research (referenced in Leah's chimp monologue) to ground the play's observations in documented human behaviour, making its diagnosis of group dynamics both specific and universally applicable.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 8: Violence and Consequences
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Violence in *DNA* is both the precipitating event (Adam's bullying and fall) and the recurring instrument of the group's subsequent decisions — Kelly traces how each act of violence or moral compromise makes the next one easier, mapping a moral escalation with unsettling precision. The violence against Adam is not presented as exceptional — it emerges from the ordinary social cruelty of school hierarchies — but its consequences are catastrophic, suggesting that the boundary between everyday social sadism and serious harm is thinner than people like to believe. The decision to allow Adam to be found rather than rescue him is the play's moral nadir, and Kelly gives it to Phil — the play's most rational, decisive character — to show that violence and abandonment are not always products of passion or weakness but can be cold, deliberate choices. The play offers no resolution of consequences: the group disperses, and the cost is distributed across multiple individual futures.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 9: Leadership and Power
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** *DNA* presents leadership not as a quality of character but as a function of context — whoever offers the most coherent narrative in a moment of crisis becomes the group's authority, regardless of the moral content of that narrative. Phil's leadership displaces John Tate's not because Phil is more ethical or more socially dominant, but because he is calmer and his plan has a certain brutal coherence. Kelly uses this transition to show how groups in crisis prioritise solutions over ethics — the question is never "is this right?" but "will this work?" Phil's plan does work, in the narrow sense, which is exactly what makes it so troubling: the play refuses to provide the comfort of failure as punishment for moral error. The final redistribution of social capital within the group — Cathy rising, Leah withdrawing — suggests that complicity, rather than integrity, is rewarded by the social economy of adolescent groups.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 10: Silence and Communication
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Kelly uses silence as one of the play's primary dramatic languages — Phil's silence is the most obvious instance, but the play is full of moments where what is not said carries more weight than what is. Phil and Leah's scenes are structured around the asymmetry between her verbosity and his near-complete non-response, and Kelly uses this asymmetry to ask whether communication requires a willing recipient, and what happens to a person whose moral concerns meet only silence. The play's episodic structure — short scenes, overlapping revelations, characters who speak only to report rather than reflect — mimics the communication patterns of a group managing a shared secret: information is distributed on a need-to-know basis, and honest feeling is suppressed in the interest of collective self-protection. Kelly's theatrical choice to give Leah the most philosophically sophisticated language while surrounding her with characters who either cannot or will not engage with it dramatises the isolation of moral consciousness in a group defined by self-interest.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
