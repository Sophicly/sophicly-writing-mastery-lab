# Topic 1: Social Responsibility
**Type:** extract_plus_whole
**Teaching Point:** Social responsibility is the central theme of An Inspector Calls. Priestley uses the Birling family's collective involvement in Eva Smith's downfall to argue that we are all responsible for one another, challenging the individualist attitudes of 1912 capitalist society.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**AO4:** 5
**Extract Location:** Act 3

## Extract
The passage near the end of Act 3 where the Inspector delivers his final speech before leaving. He warns the Birlings that there are "millions and millions and millions of Eva Smiths and John Smiths" and that if people do not learn to share responsibility for one another, they will be taught "in fire and blood and anguish." This is his climactic moral statement to the family before his departure.

# Topic 3: Sheila Birling
**Type:** extract_plus_whole
**Teaching Point:** Sheila undergoes the most significant transformation in the play, moving from a naive, privileged young woman to someone who accepts moral responsibility. Her development represents Priestley's hope that the younger generation can change.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**AO4:** 5
**Extract Location:** Act 1

## Extract
The passage in Act 1 where Sheila learns she was responsible for getting Eva Smith sacked from Milwards. She becomes distressed and admits she had the girl dismissed out of jealousy because Eva had held a dress against herself and smiled, making Sheila feel inferior. Sheila is visibly upset and says she felt "rotten" about it at the time.

# Topic 4: Mr Birling
**Type:** extract_plus_whole
**Teaching Point:** Mr Birling represents the selfish capitalist class that Priestley sought to criticise. His dramatic irony-laden predictions and refusal to accept responsibility make him a vehicle for Priestley's argument that unchecked capitalism and self-interest are socially destructive.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**AO4:** 5
**Extract Location:** Act 1

## Extract
The passage early in Act 1 where Mr Birling delivers his speech at the dinner table about the Titanic being "unsinkable" and his confident predictions that there will be no war. He lectures Gerald and Eric about how "a man has to mind his own business and look after himself and his own" and dismisses ideas of community responsibility as nonsense.

# Topic 5: Mrs Birling
**Type:** extract_plus_whole
**Teaching Point:** Mrs Birling embodies the cold, class-conscious attitudes of the upper middle class. Her refusal to help Eva and her insistence that the father of the child should bear sole responsibility creates devastating dramatic irony, as she unknowingly condemns her own son Eric.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**AO4:** 5
**Extract Location:** Act 2

## Extract
The passage in Act 2 where Mrs Birling describes her role as chair of the Brumley Women's Charity Organisation and explains why she refused to help Eva Smith. She states that the girl had used the name "Mrs Birling" which she found impertinent, and that the case was not deserving of assistance. She insists the father of the unborn child should be made entirely responsible.

# Topic 6: Eric Birling
**Type:** extract_plus_whole
**Teaching Point:** Eric represents the flawed but redeemable younger generation. His alcoholism, theft, and treatment of Eva reveal the hidden corruption beneath the respectable Birling facade, yet his genuine remorse and acceptance of guilt align him with Sheila as a figure of hope.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**AO4:** 5
**Extract Location:** Act 3

## Extract
The passage in Act 3 where Eric returns and the full truth of his involvement with Eva emerges. He confesses that he got her pregnant, stole money from his father's office to support her, and that she refused to accept any more because she knew it was stolen. He is angry and emotional, turning on his mother when he realises she turned Eva away from the charity.

# Topic 7: The Inspector
**Type:** extract_plus_whole
**Teaching Point:** Inspector Goole functions as Priestley's mouthpiece and a quasi-supernatural force for moral reckoning. His methodical interrogation, commanding presence, and mysterious nature elevate him beyond a simple police inspector into a figure representing conscience, social justice, and perhaps divine judgement.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**AO4:** 5
**Extract Location:** Act 1

## Extract
The passage in Act 1 where Inspector Goole first arrives and begins questioning Mr Birling. The stage directions describe him as creating "an impression of massiveness, solidity and purposefulness." He calmly but firmly takes control of the conversation, cutting through Mr Birling's attempts to use his social standing to intimidate, and insists on questioning each family member one at a time.

# Topic 8: Gerald Croft
**Type:** extract_plus_whole
**Teaching Point:** Gerald occupies a complex moral position. His affair with Eva/Daisy showed genuine affection, making his treatment of her more nuanced than the other Birlings', yet his readiness to dismiss responsibility after the Inspector leaves reveals that class loyalty and self-preservation ultimately outweigh his conscience.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**AO4:** 5
**Extract Location:** Act 2

## Extract
The passage in Act 2 where Gerald confesses his affair with Daisy Renton. He describes how he met her at the Palace bar, rescued her from Alderman Meggarty, installed her in rooms at a friend's flat, and how their relationship developed over the summer before he ended it. He admits she was the "prettiest" and that he was genuinely fond of her.

# Topic 9: Class and Social Status
**Type:** extract_plus_whole
**Teaching Point:** Class division is the structural foundation of the play. Priestley exposes how the privilege of the Birling family enables their exploitation of Eva Smith at every turn, and how class prejudice — from the factory floor to the charity boardroom — creates a system in which the vulnerable have no protection.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**AO4:** 5
**Extract Location:** Act 1

## Extract
The passage in Act 1 where Mr Birling explains his decision to sack Eva Smith from his factory. She had been one of the ringleaders demanding a pay rise from twenty-two and sixpence to twenty-five shillings a week. Birling refused, calling it his duty to keep costs down, and dismissed the strike leaders. When the Inspector presses him, Birling defends his actions as standard business practice and insists the girl had no right to demand higher wages.

# Topic 10: Eva Smith
**Type:** extract_plus_whole
**Teaching Point:** Eva Smith never appears on stage yet she is the moral centre of the play. Her unseen presence forces the audience to confront how easily the powerless can be destroyed by those in positions of authority. She represents an entire class of exploited workers, and her absence from the stage reinforces how the vulnerable are silenced by those who hold power over them.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**AO4:** 5
**Extract Location:** Act 3

## Extract
The passage in Act 3 after the Inspector has left, where the Birlings debate whether the girl was real and whether they were all shown the same photograph. Gerald returns with evidence suggesting no girl died at the infirmary. Mr and Mrs Birling seize on this to avoid responsibility, while Sheila and Eric maintain that it does not matter whether the Inspector was real — their actions were still wrong.
