# Topic 1: Napoleon
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Napoleon is the central antagonist who represents Stalin and the corruption of revolutionary ideals. Students should trace his gradual accumulation of power from the expulsion of Snowball through to his final transformation into a human-like tyrant, analysing how Orwell uses him to explore the inevitability of corruption in totalitarian systems.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

## Question
How does Orwell use the character of Napoleon to explore the theme of totalitarianism in *Animal Farm*?

Write about:
- what Napoleon says and does in the novel
- how Orwell presents Napoleon.

# Topic 4: Boxer
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Boxer represents the loyal, exploited working class whose dedication is rewarded with betrayal. Students should analyse how Orwell uses Boxer's mottos — "I will work harder" and "Napoleon is always right" — to explore the tragedy of blind loyalty, and how his death at the knacker's yard is the novel's most emotionally powerful indictment of the pigs' regime.

# Topic 5: Power and Corruption
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** The corrupting nature of power is the novel's central theme. Students should trace the arc from the democratic promise of Old Major's speech through to the pigs' complete adoption of human behaviour, analysing how Orwell structures the narrative to show that power corrupts incrementally — each small compromise enabling the next, larger betrayal.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

## Question
How does Orwell explore the theme of power and corruption in *Animal Farm*?

- how power is gained and misused in the novel
- how Orwell presents the corrupting effects of power.

# Topic 6: Propaganda

# Topic 7: The Commandments
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** The Seven Commandments represent the founding principles of Animalism, and their gradual alteration tracks the moral collapse of the revolution. Students should analyse the significance of each revision — from "No animal shall sleep in a bed *with sheets*" to the final reduction to a single, paradoxical commandment — as Orwell's structural device for showing how ideals are corrupted through the manipulation of language.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

How does Orwell use the Seven Commandments to explore the corruption of ideals in *Animal Farm*?

- how the commandments change throughout the novel

# Topic 9: Class and Inequality
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Orwell structures Animal Farm to show that revolution against inequality can simply create new forms of the same hierarchy. Students should analyse the emerging class system — pigs as the new ruling class, dogs as enforcers, horses and hens as exploited labour — and how Orwell uses the circular structure of the novel (Manor Farm to Animal Farm and back) to argue that class systems sustain themselves regardless of ideology.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

## Question
How does Orwell explore the theme of class and inequality in *Animal Farm*?

Write about:
- how different groups of animals are treated in the novel
- how Orwell presents ideas about class and inequality.

# Topic 10: Fear and Violence
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Napoleon's dogs represent the state apparatus of terror — the secret police, the show trials, the purges. Students should analyse how Orwell presents fear as the essential companion to propaganda in maintaining totalitarian control, from the dogs' first appearance at the expulsion of Snowball through to the public executions in the yard, and how the animals' silence in the face of violence mirrors the complicity of populations under real dictatorships.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

## Question
How does Orwell use fear and violence to explore the theme of control in *Animal Farm*?

Write about:
- how fear and violence are used in the novel
- how Orwell presents the effects of fear and violence on the animals.
