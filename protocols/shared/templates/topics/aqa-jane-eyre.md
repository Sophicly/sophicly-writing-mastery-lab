# Topic 1: Jane Eyre (protagonist)
**Type:** protagonist
**Teaching Point:** Jane is defined by her fierce independence, moral integrity, and resilience in the face of suffering. Bronte uses first-person narration to give the reader direct access to Jane's inner world, making her refusal to compromise her principles the emotional and moral centre of the novel. Students should analyse how Bronte presents Jane as a radical figure for the Victorian period -- a woman who insists on equality and self-respect above material comfort.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

## Extract
**Extract Location:** Chapter 23

 1  "Do you think, because I am poor, obscure, plain, and little, I am
 2  soulless and heartless? You think wrong! -- I have as much soul as
 3  you, -- and full as much heart! And if God had gifted me with some
 4  beauty and much wealth, I should have made it as hard for you to
 5  leave me, as it is now for me to leave you. I am not talking to you
 6  now through the medium of custom, conventionalities, nor even of
 7  mortal flesh; -- it is my spirit that addresses your spirit; just as
 8  if both had passed through the grave, and we stood at God's feet,
 9  equal, -- as we are!"

11  "As we are!" repeated Mr. Rochester -- "so," he added, enclosing me
12  in his arms, gathering me to his breast, pressing his lips on my
13  lips: "so, Jane!"

15  "Yes, so, sir," I rejoined: "and yet not so; for you are a married

# Topic 3: Rochester (Byronic hero / passion / secrets)
**Type:** central_theme
**Teaching Point:** Rochester is a complex Byronic hero -- passionate, brooding, morally ambiguous, and haunted by his secret marriage to Bertha Mason. Bronte uses him to explore the tension between desire and morality. Students should analyse how Rochester's deception of Jane complicates the reader's sympathy for him, and how his eventual maiming and blinding can be read as a symbolic punishment or a levelling that enables true equality with Jane.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

# Topic 4: Love and Passion
**Type:** central_theme
**Teaching Point:** Love in Jane Eyre is presented as a force that must be balanced with moral integrity and self-respect. Bronte challenges the Victorian ideal of passive female devotion by showing Jane as an active agent in her own love story -- one who leaves Rochester at the height of her passion because her principles demand it. Students should analyse how Bronte uses the contrast between different kinds of love (Rochester's passion, St. John's cold duty) to define what a genuine and equal partnership looks like.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

# Topic 5: Independence and Self-Respect
**Type:** central_theme
**Teaching Point:** Jane's independence is the novel's defining quality. From her childhood resistance to the Reeds, through her insistence on earning her own living, to her refusal to become Rochester's mistress, Bronte presents self-respect as inseparable from moral worth. Students should analyse how Jane's famous declaration -- "I care for myself" -- represents a radical Victorian statement about female autonomy, and how her inheritance later gives her the economic independence to choose Rochester as an equal.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

## Extract
**Extract Location:** Chapter 27

 1  "I care for myself. The more solitary, the more friendless, the more
 2  unsustained I am, the more I will respect myself. I will keep the
 3  law given by God; sanctioned by man. I will hold to the principles
 4  received by me when I was sane, and not mad -- as I am now. Laws and
 5  principles are not for the times when there is no temptation: they
 6  are for such moments as this, when body and soul rise in mutiny
 7  against their rigour; stringent are they; inviolate they shall be.
 8  If at my individual convenience I might break them, what would be
 9  their worth? They have a worth -- so I have always believed; and if
10  I cannot believe it now, it is because I am insane -- quite insane:
11  with my veins running fire, and my heart beating faster than I can
12  count its throbs. Preconceived opinions, foregone determinations,
13  are all I have at this hour to stand by: there I plant my foot."
14
15  I did. Mr. Rochester, reading my countenance, saw I had done so. His
16  fury was wrought to the highest: he must yield to it for a moment,
17  whatever followed; he crossed the floor and seized my arm and
18  grasped my waist. He seemed to devour me with his flaming glance:
19  physically, I felt, at the moment, powerless as stubble exposed to
20  the draught and glow of a furnace: mentally, I still possessed my
21  soul, and with it the certainty of ultimate safety.
22
23  "Never," said he, as he ground his teeth, "never was anything at
24  once so frail and so indomitable. A mere reed she feels in my
25  hands!" (And he shook me with the force of his hold.) "I could bend
26  her with my finger and thumb: and what good would it do if I bent,
27  if I uprooted, if I crushed her? Consider that eye: consider the
28  resolute, wild, free thing looking out of it, defying me, with more
29  than courage -- with a stern triumph. Whatever I do with its cage, I
30  cannot get at it -- the savage, beautiful creature!"

# Topic 6: Social Class and Inequality
**Type:** supporting_theme
**Teaching Point:** Jane occupies an ambiguous social position throughout the novel -- not a servant, but dependent on others; educated, but without wealth or family connections. Bronte uses Jane's liminal status to expose the cruelty and hypocrisy of the Victorian class system. Students should analyse how characters like the Reeds, Brocklehurst, and Blanche Ingram represent different forms of class prejudice, and how Jane's refusal to accept her "place" challenges the social hierarchy.

# Topic 7: Religion and Morality
**Type:** supporting_theme
**Teaching Point:** Bronte presents three contrasting models of Christianity through Brocklehurst (religious hypocrisy), Helen Burns (passive endurance and spiritual acceptance), and St. John Rivers (cold, self-denying duty). Jane rejects all three extremes in favour of a personal morality grounded in conscience and feeling. Students should analyse how Bronte uses these characters to critique Victorian religious institutions while still presenting genuine faith as a source of strength for Jane.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

# Topic 8: The Red Room / Childhood Suffering
**Type:** theme_and_character
**Teaching Point:** The Red Room is one of the novel's most powerful symbols -- a site of imprisonment, injustice, and psychological terror that shapes Jane's character for the rest of the narrative. Bronte uses Jane's childhood suffering at Gateshead and Lowood to establish the novel's central concern with justice and resistance to oppression. Students should analyse how the Gothic imagery of the Red Room (the red furnishings, the mirror, the ghost-light) creates a symbolic landscape of entrapment that recurs throughout the novel.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

## Extract
**Extract Location:** Chapter 2

# Topic 9: Bertha Mason (the madwoman / colonial other)
**Type:** supporting_character
**Teaching Point:** Bertha Mason is one of the most debated figures in English literature. On one level she functions as a Gothic device -- the madwoman in the attic whose existence prevents Jane and Rochester's marriage. On another, she raises profound questions about gender, colonialism, and the silencing of women. Bronte presents Bertha through Rochester's account, which frames her as monstrous and inhuman. Students should analyse the language used to describe Bertha, considering how far Bronte invites sympathy for her and how far she is used as a foil to Jane.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

# Topic 10: Gender and Patriarchy
**Type:** theme_and_character
**Teaching Point:** Bronte wrote Jane Eyre at a time when women had almost no legal or economic independence. The novel is a sustained challenge to patriarchal authority -- from Jane's childhood defiance of John Reed, through her rejection of Rochester's attempts to dress and adorn her as his possession, to her refusal of St. John's controlling proposal. Students should analyse how Bronte uses Jane's narrative voice to articulate a proto-feminist argument for female equality, while also examining how the novel's ending (Jane's return to a diminished Rochester) can be read as either a triumph of equality or a compromise.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

## Extract
**Extract Location:** Chapter 12

 1  It is in vain to say human beings ought to be satisfied with
 2  tranquillity: they must have action; and they will make it if they

 5  how many rebellions besides political rebellions ferment in the

 7  calm generally: but women feel just as men feel; they need exercise
 8  for their faculties, and a field for their efforts, as much as their
 9  brothers do; they suffer from too rigid a restraint, too absolute a
10  stagnation, precisely as men would suffer; and it is narrow-minded
11  in their more privileged fellow-creatures to say that they ought to
