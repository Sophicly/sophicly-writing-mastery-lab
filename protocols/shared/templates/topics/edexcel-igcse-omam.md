# Topic 1: George
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** George Milton is Steinbeck's portrait of pragmatic loyalty strained almost to breaking point by the demands of caring for someone who cannot survive alone in a brutal world, and the novel's moral weight rests largely on whether the reader can hold George's final act as both killing and an act of love. His characteristic sharp impatience with Lennie coexists with an equally characteristic protectiveness, and Steinbeck uses dialogue — particularly the shared Dream speech — to show that the fantasy sustains George as much as it does Lennie, suggesting that he is not merely a reluctant caretaker but a man who needs to believe in the possibility of belonging somewhere. George's rootlessness — he is a "bindle stiff" with no fixed identity beyond his bond with Lennie — makes him both representative of his Depression-era context and deeply sympathetic as an individual. The weight of his final decision is amplified by Slim's "You hadda, George" — an endorsement that invites the reader to judge whether social necessity and compassion can justify the taking of a life.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 3: Lennie
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Lennie Small is Steinbeck's most sustained exploration of innocence as both a source of strength and a fatal vulnerability, a man whose childlike goodness is inseparable from the uncontrolled physical power that will destroy him. His recurring comfort in the tactile pleasure of soft things — mice, the puppy, Curley's wife's hair — is a single motif Steinbeck uses to unify the novel's tragic arc, each repetition escalating the stakes until the pattern resolves in catastrophe. Steinbeck does not present Lennie's actions as malicious, which is precisely what makes his fate feel most unjust: he is destroyed not by evil intent but by the collision of his nature with a world that cannot accommodate it. The dream of the farm and the rabbits is not simply escapism for Lennie but a structure of self-regulation — "if I do something bad you won't let me tend the rabbits" — suggesting that even within his limitations he grasps the logic of conditional reward and consequence.

# Topic 4: Curley's Wife
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Curley's wife is Steinbeck's most ambiguous figure, simultaneously a threat to the men's dream and its most sympathetic victim — a woman who never receives a name, who exists in the text only through the men's fearful and resentful perception of her. Her role as a danger to George and Lennie is established early and confirmed by the plot, yet Steinbeck complicates this by revealing in her final conversation with Lennie her own truncated dream — the Hollywood career that never materialised, the girlhood she cannot recover. The tension between her presentation as femme fatale and her presentation as trapped, lonely, and despairing is not resolved but held, asking the reader to see both simultaneously and to recognise that the same social system that traps the migrant workers in poverty traps her in a marriage and a ranch where she has no voice. Her death is structurally the point at which the men's dream becomes definitively impossible, making her the novel's most loaded figure of sacrifice.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 5: Crooks
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Crooks is Steinbeck's most direct embodiment of racial isolation, a man whose physical separation — he must sleep in the harness room — externalises the social exclusion that is his permanent condition in the bunkhouse world. His initial hostility to Lennie's intrusion is protective rather than cruel, the defensive posture of a man who has learned that proximity to white workers results in humiliation rather than fellowship. Steinbeck gives Crooks the novel's most articulate statement of loneliness — "a guy goes nuts if he ain't got nobody... I tell ya a guy gets too lonely an' he gets sick" — making him the character who most explicitly names what the novel's structural isolation demonstrates. His brief, painful moment of dreaming alongside George and Lennie, before Curley's wife extinguishes it with a single racial threat, is the novel's sharpest illustration of how power operates to protect hierarchy: she wields the one social advantage she has over a man who has nothing left to lose.

# Topic 6: The American Dream
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The Dream of the small farm — "a couple of acres, a house, a cow and some pigs" — is Steinbeck's central symbol of the American myth of self-determination, presented as deeply compelling and structurally impossible in equal measure. He reveals its power through the way it spreads: Candy's contribution of his savings, Crooks's brief enlistment, even Curley's wife's Hollywood fantasy — all variants of the same underlying desire for autonomy, dignity, and belonging. Yet Steinbeck consistently frames the Dream in the past tense or the conditional, and the novel's circular structure — opening and closing at the same pool — suggests that the migrant workers' lives are loops from which escape is narratively impossible. The Dream functions in the novel both as critique of capitalist mythology and as genuine human need: its impossibility is a structural injustice, not a personal failing.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 7: Loneliness and Isolation
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Steinbeck structures *Of Mice and Men* as a catalogue of loneliness, ensuring that almost every character on the ranch suffers a distinct form of isolation — racial, physical, social, gendered — that makes George and Lennie's bond appear as an anomaly requiring constant defence rather than a norm. The bunkhouse setting reinforces this: itinerant workers arrive, sleep in the same space, and move on without forming lasting bonds, because the economic system that employs them requires mobility and therefore prohibits rootedness. Steinbeck's prose style — restrained, dramatic, often stage-direction-like — mirrors the emotional withdrawal of characters who have learned that openness leads to hurt. The final scene at the pool, where George shoots Lennie alone, is the ultimate expression of the novel's loneliness: even the most sustaining human bond in the text ends in solitary violence, leaving George to rejoin the ranks of the men who drift alone.

# Topic 8: Friendship and Loyalty
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The friendship between George and Lennie is presented as exceptional precisely because it is so rare in the novel's world: Candy's astonishment — "I never seen one guy take so much trouble for another guy" — functions as a kind of narrative annotation, alerting the reader to what makes their bond structurally significant. Steinbeck tests this friendship through escalating social pressure, having George repeatedly deny Lennie or speak harshly of him to fit into the bunkhouse culture, then having Lennie's disaster force George into the ultimate act of loyalty that is also the ultimate act of destruction. The shared Dream speech, repeated like a ritual or prayer, is the formal expression of their loyalty — it is not just a plan but an affirmation of their bond, recited more for the comfort of repetition than the content. Steinbeck's tragic argument is that in the world of the novel, loyalty of this depth cannot survive: the social system demands individual mobility and punishes those who remain responsible for others.

# Topic 9: Power and Powerlessness
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Steinbeck maps a precise hierarchy of power on the ranch — the boss, Curley, the white male workers, Crooks, Curley's wife — and shows how each level asserts dominance over the one below while remaining vulnerable to those above it, a chain of cruelty generated by the economic system that places all of them under precarious conditions. Curley's violent overcompensation for his small stature is Steinbeck's most schematic illustration: his physical insecurity drives his abuse of power, and his authority over the ranch is used exclusively to intimidate rather than to lead. The scene where Curley's wife threatens Crooks is structurally the most uncomfortable power exchange in the novel, because it shows a character whom the narrative has established as victimised deploying her one available weapon — racial privilege — to crush a man with even less social standing. Steinbeck presents powerlessness not as a personal condition but as a structural one: it is the economic and social system that determines who can be harmed without consequence.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 10: The Great Depression Setting
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Steinbeck sets the novel in the Salinas Valley of California during the Great Depression, and the historical context is not merely background but a structural determinant: the migrant labour system, the impermanence of employment, and the impossibility of saving enough money to buy land are all products of the specific economic collapse of the 1930s. The ranch is a microcosm of American capitalism under stress — wages paid, work done, and workers moved on — and Steinbeck's attention to the physical details of the bunkhouse and the countryside grounds the allegory in documentary realism. The opening and closing descriptions of the Salinas River use natural imagery — willow, teal, water-snakes — to establish a world of natural beauty and cyclical permanence against which the characters' transient, disposable lives are measured. Steinbeck's setting argues that the tragedy of George and Lennie is not exceptional bad luck but the predictable outcome of a social and economic system that renders working people interchangeable and ultimately expendable.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
