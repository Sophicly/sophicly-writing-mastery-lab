# Topic 1: Dulce et Decorum Est
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Owen's poem attacks the patriotic lie that dying for one's country is noble by forcing the reader to witness the physical horror of a gas attack in the trenches. The poem moves from exhausted marching through sudden panic to the haunting recurring nightmare of a dying soldier, and the direct address to the reader in the final stanza transforms the poem into a moral accusation. Students should analyse how Owen uses the contrast between the Latin title and the visceral imagery, the shift from third-person observation to second-person confrontation, and the disturbed sonnet-like structure to expose the gap between propaganda and the reality of war.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Dulce et Decorum Est
**Focus Poet:** Wilfred Owen

## Question
Compare how the poets present the reality of suffering in war in 'Dulce et Decorum Est' and one other poem from the Conflict collection.

# Topic 3: The Charge of the Light Brigade
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Tennyson's poem commemorates the disastrous cavalry charge at Balaclava in 1854, where soldiers rode knowingly into certain death because of a miscommunicated order. The driving dactylic rhythm and relentless repetition of "cannon to the right of them, / Cannon to the left of them" create a galloping momentum that sweeps the reader into the charge alongside the soldiers. The poem simultaneously celebrates the soldiers' courage and exposes the futility of unquestioning obedience. Students should analyse how Tennyson uses rhythm, repetition, and the tension between heroic tribute and implicit criticism of command to present conflicting attitudes to duty and sacrifice.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** The Charge of the Light Brigade
**Focus Poet:** Alfred Lord Tennyson

# Topic 4: Exposure
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Owen's second poem in the anthology shifts the focus from violent action to the agonising monotony of waiting in the trenches during winter. The true enemy is not the opposing army but the weather itself — the "merciless" wind and snow that slowly kill the soldiers. The repeated refrain "But nothing happens" creates a devastating rhythm of anticlimax that captures the psychological torment of purposeless suffering. Students should analyse how Owen uses half-rhyme, personification of the elements, and the cyclical structure of false hope and disappointment to present war as a slow, dehumanising process of attrition rather than a theatre of heroic action.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Exposure
**Focus Poet:** Wilfred Owen

## Question

# Topic 5: An Irish Airman Foresees His Death
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Yeats's speaker is a WWI fighter pilot who fights not out of patriotism, duty, or hatred but from a pure, existential impulse — "a lonely impulse of delight." The poem's perfectly balanced syntax and antithetical pairings ("Those that I fight I do not hate, / Those that I guard I do not love") create a voice of absolute clarity and detachment, as though the airman has already stepped outside his own life. The political context of Ireland's ambivalent relationship with Britain during WWI is essential. Students should analyse how Yeats uses the balanced rhetorical structure, the solitary first-person voice, and the rejection of conventional motivations to present an individual who finds meaning in the act of conflict itself rather than in any cause.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** An Irish Airman Foresees His Death
**Focus Poet:** W.B. Yeats

# Topic 6: The Man He Killed
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Hardy's poem uses the voice of an ordinary soldier reflecting on the absurdity of killing a man who, in any other circumstance, would have been a friend. The colloquial, pub-conversation tone and the stumbling, self-interrupting syntax of "I shot him dead because — / Because he was my foe" expose the inadequacy of official justifications for war. The simple ballad form and the speaker's bewildered reasoning create a powerful anti-war argument precisely because it is not eloquent or intellectual. Students should analyse how Hardy uses the dramatic monologue, the hypothetical meeting in an inn, and the speaker's hesitant logic to expose the arbitrary nature of enmity in war.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** The Man He Killed
**Focus Poet:** Thomas Hardy

## Question

# Topic 7: Vergissmeinnicht
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Douglas's poem describes soldiers returning to a battlefield to find the body of a dead enemy sniper, in whose pocket lies a photograph inscribed "Vergissmeinnicht" (forget me not) by his girlfriend Steffi. The poem's power lies in the collision between the intimate, romantic keepsake and the grotesque reality of the decaying corpse. The shift between the lover's gaze and the soldier's gaze — "the dust upon the paper eye / and the burst stomach like a cave" — forces the reader to hold tenderness and horror simultaneously. Students should analyse how Douglas uses the juxtaposition of love and death, the precise physical description, and the interplay between the German inscription and English narration to present how war destroys both the individual and the relationships that gave their life meaning.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Vergissmeinnicht
**Focus Poet:** Keith Douglas

# Topic 8: Bayonet Charge
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Hughes plunges the reader into the terrified consciousness of a soldier mid-charge across no man's land, stripping away all patriotic abstraction to reveal the raw, animal panic of a body in mortal danger. The poem's three stanzas move from physical momentum through a frozen moment of existential doubt to the abandonment of all thought in favour of survival instinct. The image of the soldier as "a man who has jumped up in the dark and runs" captures the disorientation and loss of agency. Students should analyse how Hughes uses the violent, kinetic imagery, the disrupted rhythm and enjambment, and the hare as a parallel victim to present the moment when ideals collapse under the physical reality of combat.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Bayonet Charge
**Focus Poet:** Ted Hughes

## Question

# Topic 9: Poppies
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Weir's poem presents war from the perspective of a mother releasing her son to conflict, layering the ritual of Remembrance Sunday with the private grief of a parent watching a child leave. The domestic imagery — smoothing a blazer, the "cat hairs" and "sellotape" — grounds the poem in the ordinary textures of home, making the departure feel both mundane and devastating. The disrupted chronology blurs past and present, suggesting that the mother's grief exists outside normal time. Students should analyse how Weir uses the language of textiles and touch, the fragmented timeline, and the shift from domestic interior to the war memorial to present how conflict reaches into the home and reshapes the lives of those left behind.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Poppies
**Focus Poet:** Jane Weir

## Question

# Topic 10: War Photographer
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Duffy's poem explores the moral tension of a war photographer developing images in his darkroom, caught between the horrors he has witnessed and the comfortable indifference of the British public who will glance at his photographs over Sunday supplements. The poem's controlled, methodical four-stanza structure mirrors the photographer's disciplined professional process, while the imagery — "a half-formed ghost," "running children in a nightmare heat" — reveals the trauma that lies beneath his composure. Students should analyse how Duffy uses the extended metaphor of developing photographs, the contrast between "Rural England" and the war zones, and the final image of the editor selecting photographs to present the gap between those who witness conflict and those who merely observe it from a distance.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** War Photographer
**Focus Poet:** Carol Ann Duffy

## Question

# Topic 11: Belfast Confetti
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Carson's poem recreates the experience of being caught in a bomb explosion during the Troubles in Belfast, using punctuation marks as shrapnel to blur the boundary between language and violence. The streets become a text that the speaker cannot read or navigate — "I know this labyrinth so well" — and the barrage of questions in the final lines captures the disorientation of a civilian trapped in a conflict zone where familiar surroundings have become hostile and incomprehensible. Students should analyse how Carson uses the extended metaphor of punctuation as weaponry, the fragmented syntax, and the mapping of Belfast's real streets to present how sustained political conflict transforms ordinary life into a bewildering, inescapable maze.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Belfast Confetti
**Focus Poet:** Ciaran Carson

## Question
Compare how the poets present the experience of living in a place affected by conflict in 'Belfast Confetti' and one other poem from the Conflict collection.
