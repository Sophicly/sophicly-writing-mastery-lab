# Topic 1: Napoleon
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Napoleon represents the corruption of revolutionary ideals through the consolidation of personal power. Students should analyse how Orwell uses Napoleon's actions and language to satirise Stalinist totalitarianism, exploring how the pig's gradual accumulation of privilege and elimination of dissent mirrors historical autocracy. Close attention to Orwell's use of understatement and ironic detachment is essential — Napoleon's crimes are often reported in bland, matter-of-fact prose that heightens their horror. Students should consider how Napoleon is constructed as an absence of visible leadership contrasted with an overwhelming presence of fear.

# Topic 3: Snowball
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Snowball functions as Animal Farm's idealist revolutionary — energetic, principled, and ultimately destroyed. Students should explore how Orwell presents Snowball as a genuine believer in Animalism whose virtues are weaponised against him through Napoleon's propaganda machine. The contrast between Snowball's visible, reasoned leadership and Napoleon's silent, coercive control is central to the satire. Students should also consider Snowball's posthumous role as scapegoat and how his increasingly monstrous rewriting in farm history mirrors the Stalinist erasure of Trotsky.

# Topic 4: Boxer
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Boxer is Orwell's most emotionally devastating creation — a figure of immense physical strength and absolute moral innocence who is destroyed by the system he faithfully serves. Students should examine how Boxer's two maxims ('I will work harder' and 'Napoleon is always right') represent the tragic combination of industriousness and unquestioning obedience. His fate at the knacker's yard is the novel's most explicit indictment of totalitarianism's exploitation of the working class. Students should consider how Orwell uses bathos and the reactions of other animals to amplify the horror of Boxer's betrayal.

# Topic 5: Power and Corruption
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The corrupting nature of power is Animal Farm's central thesis, dramatised through the pigs' progressive adoption of human vices and the gradual inversion of the Seven Commandments. Students should trace the structural arc from rebellion to tyranny, analysing how Orwell presents power as inherently self-perpetuating and corrosive regardless of original intention. The famous final image of pigs and men becoming indistinguishable demands close reading of Orwell's prose style — detached, fable-like, rendering horror through understatement. Students should consider whether Orwell presents corruption as inevitable or contingent on specific failures of vigilance.

# Topic 6: Propaganda and Language
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Squealer is the novel's master of propaganda, and Orwell uses him to explore how language can be systematically corrupted to serve power. Students should analyse specific rhetorical techniques Squealer employs — false statistics, the invocation of Jones's return, selective historical revision — and consider how Orwell's choice of a pig named 'Squealer' encodes meaning. The manipulation of the Seven Commandments through nocturnal additions is a masterclass in how written language can be weaponised. Students should connect Orwell's linguistic concerns here to his contemporaneous essay 'Politics and the English Language'.

# Topic 7: The Commandments
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The Seven Commandments function as Animal Farm's constitutional text, and their progressive corruption is Orwell's most precise structural device for dramatising totalitarian revisionism. Students should trace the exact sequence of commandment alterations, noting how each change is accompanied by Squealer's explanatory visits and the animals' failure to remember original wording. The final reduction to 'All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others' is one of literature's most devastating satirical sentences. Students should consider how Orwell uses the animals' poor literacy as a metaphor for the vulnerability of any population denied education and historical memory.

# Topic 8: Class and Inequality
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Orwell uses Animal Farm to argue that class stratification is not merely a product of capitalism but an inherent danger within any revolutionary movement that fails to dismantle hierarchies of intelligence and access. Students should analyse how the pigs' intellectual advantage is immediately converted into social privilege — control of milk, apples, information, and ultimately the farmhouse. The novel raises uncomfortable questions about whether Animalism ever had the potential to succeed. Students should consider how Orwell uses the sheep, hens, and working horses to represent different strata of the working class and their varying susceptibility to manipulation.

# Topic 9: Revolution and Betrayal
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Animal Farm dramatises the life cycle of revolution — from Old Major's inspiring vision through the euphoric overthrow of Jones to the gradual replication of the oppressor's methods by the new rulers. Students should trace the specific moments of betrayal: the diversion of milk, the expulsion of Snowball, the executions, the adoption of human habits, and finally the card game. The question of whether Old Major's original vision was ever achievable — or whether it contained the seeds of its own corruption — is central to any sophisticated reading. Students should consider Orwell's personal political context as a disillusioned socialist writing in 1943-44.

# Topic 10: Education and Ignorance
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Orwell presents the animals' ignorance and inability to read as the primary mechanism through which tyranny is sustained — a pointed argument about the political stakes of education and literacy. Students should analyse the different gradations of ignorance among the animals: the sheep who can only bleat slogans, Boxer who cannot progress beyond four letters, the pigs who deliberately exploit their literacy advantage. Benjamin the donkey represents a disturbing variant — he can read but chooses not to engage, suggesting that cynical withdrawal is its own form of complicity. Students should consider whether Orwell views ignorance as cause or symptom of oppression.
