# Topic 1: Elizabeth Bennet (protagonist — wit, independence, pride)
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Elizabeth is Austen's most complex heroine — intelligent, witty, and fiercely independent, yet flawed by her quickness to judge. # Topic 2: Conceptual Notes
**Type:** conceptual
**Format:** either_or

# Topic 3: Mr Darcy (pride / transformation / class)
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Darcy embodies the intersection of pride and class privilege. Austen constructs his arc as a gradual humbling — from the man who insults Elizabeth at the Meryton assembly to the one who quietly rescues her family from disgrace. Students should analyse how Austen uses Darcy to examine whether pride is a vice or a justified response to one's social position.

# Topic 4: Pride and prejudice (central theme)
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The twin flaws of the title are distributed across the novel's central pair — Darcy's pride and Elizabeth's prejudice — though Austen shows that both characters possess both qualities. The novel's moral argument is that self-knowledge requires confronting one's own failings, and that first impressions are unreliable guides to character.

**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Austen presents a spectrum of marriages — from the loveless pragmatism of Charlotte and Collins to the passion of Elizabeth and Darcy — to argue that the best marriages unite affection with mutual respect. **Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Class permeates every relationship in the novel. # Topic 7: Mr Bennet and Mrs Bennet (contrasting parents)
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The Bennets represent a failed marriage and contrasting failures of parenting. Mrs Bennet's vulgar obsession with marrying off her daughters is played for comedy, but Austen shows its real consequences — Lydia's elopement is partly her mother's doing. Mr Bennet's ironic detachment, while witty, is equally damaging. Together they illustrate Austen's argument that parental responsibility shapes children's moral development.

**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Jane and Bingley represent a gentler, more trusting form of love that contrasts with Elizabeth and Darcy's combative courtship. # Topic 10: Wickham (deception / charm)
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Wickham is the novel's most dangerous figure precisely because he is so charming. Austen uses him to demonstrate that surface manners are an unreliable guide to character — the central lesson Elizabeth must learn. His easy sociability contrasts with Darcy's stiffness, yet Wickham is morally bankrupt while Darcy is honourable. Students should analyse how Austen constructs Wickham's deception and what it reveals about the dangers of first impressions.
