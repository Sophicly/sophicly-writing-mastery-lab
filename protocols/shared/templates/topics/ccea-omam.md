# Topic 1: George
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Steinbeck presents George as a man defined by the tension between self-interest and obligation — perpetually burdened by Lennie, yet unable to abandon him because their friendship gives his own life meaning and direction. Through George's terse, economical speech and his habit of restating the dream to Lennie, Steinbeck reveals a character who uses narrative as a way of sustaining hope in a world that offers very little. George's final act — shooting Lennie — is presented as the most extreme form of care, a mercy killing that also destroys the only human connection that makes George's itinerant existence bearable. Steinbeck positions George after Lennie's death as the novel's most isolated figure: a man who has gained his freedom only by losing everything that made it worthwhile.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does John Steinbeck present George in *Of Mice and Men?*

You should consider:
- how Steinbeck establishes George's character and his relationship with Lennie
- the pressures and burdens George faces throughout the novel
- how George's final decision develops the novel's themes about friendship and the American Dream.

# Topic 3: Lennie
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Lennie is presented as a figure of paradox — physically overwhelming yet childlike in his innocence, capable of accidental destruction on a scale that his gentle intentions cannot explain or excuse. Steinbeck uses animal imagery persistently in Lennie's characterisation (a bear, a dog, a bull) to suggest both his power and his inability to navigate the social world that will ultimately destroy him. The recurring motif of soft things — the mice, the puppy, Curley's wife's hair — traces a pattern of compulsive need that Lennie cannot control, building towards tragedy with the structural inevitability of a Greek drama. Lennie's disability, rendered through dialogue and behaviour rather than explicit diagnosis, asks the reader to question the moral standards applied by a society with no framework for protecting those who cannot protect themselves.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

You should consider:

# Topic 4: Curley's Wife
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Curley's wife is one of Steinbeck's most complex creations — simultaneously a threat to the men, a product of the patriarchal world that denies her a name or identity beyond her husband, and ultimately the novel's most pitiable figure. Her dreams of Hollywood stardom, revealed in her final scene with Lennie, expose the same vulnerable aspirations for dignity and recognition that motivate George and Lennie's dream of the farm. Steinbeck uses her appearance — the red dress, the curled hair — as a sign that the other men read as danger, showing how a woman with no power is nevertheless feared precisely because her powerlessness makes her desperate. Her death triggers the novella's climax not through any agency of her own, marking her as the collateral damage of a world designed without a place for her.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 5: Crooks
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Crooks is presented as the character who most clearly articulates the structural nature of the novel's injustice — isolated by race in a world where even the marginalised marginalise each other, his bunk room becomes a physical embodiment of exclusion. Steinbeck uses his brief, painful conversation with Lennie to expose the psychological damage of chronic loneliness: Crooks attacks Lennie's dream not from cruelty but from the bitter need to share his own hopelessness. His moment of joining the dream — tentatively offering his labour for a share in the farm — and his swift retraction when Curley's wife reminds him of his place in the hierarchy traces the exact shape of how hope is offered and denied to the powerless. Crooks's bookshelves and his copy of the California Civil Code signal both his intelligence and the futility of knowing one's rights without the power to exercise them.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 6: The American Dream
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Steinbeck presents the American Dream — the farm with rabbits, total independence, and self-determination — as both a genuine emotional necessity and a structural impossibility for men like George and Lennie. The dream functions in the novella as a ritual: George's telling and Lennie's reciting establish it as a kind of prayer, a way of making the present bearable rather than a credible plan. Steinbeck's title, drawn from Burns's poem about the best-laid plans of mice and men, frames the dream's failure as inevitable and universal rather than the result of individual error, shifting the critique from the characters to the social system. The brief, touching moment when Candy joins the dream — and its sudden collapse after Lennie's death — enacts in miniature the novella's entire argument about hope and its destruction.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question

# Topic 7: Loneliness and Isolation
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Steinbeck presents loneliness not as an individual misfortune but as the defining condition of the itinerant worker's life in Depression-era America — a systemic product of economic exploitation that prevents the formation of stable communities or lasting bonds. Each major character on the ranch embodies a different mode of isolation: Crooks excluded by race, Curley's wife by gender, Candy by age and disability, Curley by the suspicion that power breeds. George and Lennie's partnership is presented as exceptional — "guys like us, that work on ranches, are the loneliest guys in the world" — which makes its destruction all the more devastating. Steinbeck's sparse, dramatic prose style — short sentences, minimal interiority — formally enacts the emotional barriers these characters maintain as protection against further loss.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 8: Friendship and Loyalty
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** George and Lennie's friendship is presented as both the novella's greatest value and its tragic core — a bond strong enough to survive poverty, displacement, and danger, but unable to survive the impossible contradiction between Lennie's nature and the world's intolerance. Steinbeck emphasises the rarity of their relationship through other characters' reactions: Slim's surprised acknowledgement that such loyalty is unusual among ranch hands is a quiet commentary on what isolation does to human bonds. Their friendship is essentially asymmetrical — George gives protection and direction, Lennie gives the sense of purpose and companionship that makes George's life meaningful — and this asymmetry makes George's final act simultaneously the most loyal and the most destructive thing he could do. The novella suggests that true friendship cannot save people from structural forces larger than themselves.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 9: Power and Powerlessness
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Steinbeck maps the ranch as a microcosm of Depression-era American society in which power is distributed along lines of race, gender, economic status, and physical strength, and the powerless compensate by exercising whatever small authority they hold over those below them. Curley's bullying is driven by insecurity; Curley's wife weaponises her husband's name against Crooks; even the dream of the farm is partly a fantasy of being able to tell someone else what to do. Steinbeck uses the boss, Curley, and the implied machinery of the ranch economy to represent power's institutional form, showing how workers like George and Lennie are never truly free even when nominally employed. The novella's resolution — in which the most powerful act available to George is killing Lennie to spare him from others' violence — captures the narrowness of agency available to the powerless.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 10: The Great Depression Setting
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Steinbeck grounds *Of Mice and Men* in the specific historical reality of 1930s California — the Salinas Valley, the bindle-stiff culture of migrant agricultural labour, the desperate competition for scarce work — giving the novella's universal themes about dreams and loneliness a precise social and economic cause. The opening description of the Salinas River, with its beauty and its isolation, establishes a pastoral note that the human world immediately complicates, suggesting a gap between natural abundance and social privation. The bunkhouse — sparse, transient, communal — is the architectural expression of the itinerant economy: a space that discourages attachment because attachment is a luxury the men cannot afford. Steinbeck's use of dramatic form (minimal narration, extensive dialogue, detailed stage directions) gives the setting an objectivity that makes his social critique harder to dismiss as sentiment.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does John Steinbeck use the setting of *Of Mice and Men* to explore its themes?

You should consider:
