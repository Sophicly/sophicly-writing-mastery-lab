# Topic 1: The Emigrée
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** The Emigrée by Carol Rumens presents a speaker who clings to an idealised memory of a homeland she left as a child. Students should analyse how Rumens uses the recurring light imagery, the personification of the city as a living presence that "takes me dancing," and the defiant final stanza to explore how memory and nostalgia construct a version of belonging that resists political oppression. The Cold War context and the deliberate ambiguity of whether the speaker is a refugee or exile are essential to understanding the poem's emotional complexity.
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** The Emigrée
**Focus Poet:** Carol Rumens

## Question
Compare how poets present the power of memory in shaping a sense of belonging in 'The Emigrée' and one other poem from the Belonging anthology.

# Topic 3: Peckham Rye Lane
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Peckham Rye Lane by Adam Thorpe captures the vibrant, multicultural energy of a South London street through a rapid accumulation of sensory details. Students should analyse how Thorpe uses listing, the breathless pace created by minimal punctuation, and the blending of languages, foods, and cultural markers to present belonging as something found in shared public spaces rather than ethnic homogeneity. The contemporary urban context and the poem's celebration of diversity as ordinary life are central to its meaning.
**Marks:** 20

**Focus Poem:** Peckham Rye Lane
**Focus Poet:** Adam Thorpe

# Topic 6: Half-caste
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Half-caste by John Agard challenges the offensive term through an extended series of absurd analogies — half a Picasso, half a Tchaikovsky symphony, half a weather forecast. Students should analyse how Agard uses Caribbean dialect, the imperative "Explain yuself," and the building satirical logic to expose the ignorance behind racial labelling and to demand that identity be recognised as whole rather than fractured. The poem's postcolonial context and Agard's Guyanese-British identity are essential to understanding its assertion of belonging on one's own terms.
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Half-caste
**Focus Poet:** John Agard

# Topic 9: Search for My Tongue
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Search for My Tongue by Sujata Bhatt uses the extended metaphor of a tongue — both the physical organ and language itself — to explore the fear of losing one's mother tongue. Students should analyse how Bhatt uses the trilingual presentation (English, Gujarati script, phonetic Gujarati), the imagery of the tongue rotting and then regenerating overnight like a plant, and the triumphant tone of the ending to present the idea that cultural identity, once rooted, cannot be permanently suppressed even by immersion in another language.

# Topic 10: Checking Out Me History
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Checking Out Me History by John Agard contrasts the Eurocentric history taught in British schools with the Caribbean and wider postcolonial figures the speaker was never told about. Students should analyse how Agard uses the two contrasting voices — the mocking, nursery-rhyme tone for British history and the reverent, lyrical tone for figures like Toussaint L'Ouverture and Mary Seacole — along with the repeated refrain "Dem tell me" to argue that belonging requires knowing one's own heritage, not just the dominant culture's version of it.
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Checking Out Me History
**Focus Poet:** John Agard

# Topic 11: What Were They Like?
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** What Were They Like? by Denise Levertov imagines a future in which Vietnamese culture has been entirely destroyed by war. Students should analyse how Levertov uses the question-and-answer structure, the shift from detached inquiry to anguished response, and the repeated negation of cultural details to present the destruction of belonging at its most extreme — the total erasure of a people's customs, language, and identity. The Vietnam War context and Levertov's anti-war activism give the poem its political urgency.

# Topic 12: The Class Game

# Topic 14: Unrelated Incidents
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Unrelated Incidents by Tom Leonard is written entirely in phonetic Glaswegian dialect and challenges the assumption that Received Pronunciation is the only trustworthy voice of authority. Students should analyse how Leonard uses the narrow, autocue-shaped layout, the phonetic spelling that forces the reader to "hear" the accent, and the satirical argument that "thi trooth" is denied to regional speakers to expose how language is used as a tool of exclusion and how belonging in public life is gatekept by accent and class.
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Unrelated Incidents
**Focus Poet:** Tom Leonard

## Question

# Topic 16: Blessing
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Blessing by Imtiaz Dharker describes a burst water pipe in a Mumbai slum and the community's joyful, almost religious response to the sudden abundance of water. Students should analyse how Dharker uses the shift from drought imagery to the ecstatic rush of water, the religious connotations of "blessing" and "congregation," and the sensory richness of the scene to present belonging as something rooted in shared experience and communal need. The postcolonial context of poverty and inequality gives the poem its moral weight.
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Blessing
**Focus Poet:** Imtiaz Dharker

## Question
Compare how poets present how shared experiences create a sense of community and belonging in 'Blessing' and one other poem from the Belonging anthology.
