# Topic 1: Jo
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Delaney presents Jo as a young woman of remarkable vitality and intelligence, trapped by circumstances she did not choose — poverty, an absent father, a negligent mother — yet capable of a candour and emotional directness that cuts through the social conventions designed to keep women like her invisible. Jo's voice is the play's most distinctive achievement: comic, sharp, vulnerable, and fiercely self-aware in a way that refuses the passive victimhood her circumstances might seem to demand. Her relationships with Jimmie and with Geof are both tender and pragmatic — Jo seeks connection without illusion, understanding from early in the play that romance as conventionally practised is not available to her. Delaney uses Jo to challenge the moral frameworks of 1950s Britain that would condemn her pregnancy while ignoring the social conditions that produce her situation, positioning the audience's sympathy as a form of social critique.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 3: Helen
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Helen is one of British drama's most complex and unsentimentalised mothers — charming, selfish, funny, and intermittently loving in ways that make her impossible to simply condemn. Delaney presents Helen's negligence not as pathology but as the product of a life spent managing poverty and men, so that her abandonment of Jo to pursue Peter is the behaviour of a woman who has never been taught to put herself last. The comedy that Delaney gives Helen — her frank materialism, her banter, her unashamed self-interest — prevents the play from becoming a simple moral complaint against bad motherhood, and positions the audience to understand a woman who is doing exactly what the world has taught her to do. Helen's return at the end of the play — displacing Geof, resuming her management of Jo, reasserting the destructive bond — is presented by Delaney with deliberate ambivalence: her reappearance is both a failure and a recognition of the inescapability of the mother-daughter tie.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 4: Jimmie
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Jimmie's presence in the play is brief but structurally significant — his relationship with Jo raises questions of race, belonging, and desire in the context of late 1950s Britain that the play handles with a directness unusual for its time. Delaney presents Jimmie with warmth and dignity, refusing the degrading stereotypes that surrounded Black characters in contemporary popular culture, and she gives his relationship with Jo a quality of genuine mutual tenderness that challenges the racist assumptions about interracial relationships that would have been shared by many in her original audience. His departure for the navy removes any question of a conventional romantic resolution — Delaney uses his absence to redirect the play's interest from romance to Jo's navigation of pregnancy and social expectation entirely on her own. Jimmie's characterisation reflects Delaney's broader commitment to presenting marginalised figures as full human beings rather than social problems.

# Topic 5: Geof
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Geof is presented by Delaney as the play's most quietly radical figure — a gay man in 1950s Britain who finds in Jo's non-judgemental company a space where he can simply exist, and who reciprocates by offering her the practical, nurturing care that her mother never provided. Their domestic arrangement — sharing a flat, preparing for the baby together — presents an alternative model of family that is based on affection and mutual support rather than convention or obligation, and Delaney frames it as both comic and genuinely moving. Geof's homosexuality is handled with remarkable directness for 1958: Delaney neither pathologises it nor makes it a source of drama, simply presenting it as one aspect of a person who is, in practical terms, more capable of care than most of the ostensibly respectable characters in the play. His displacement by Helen at the end — despite being clearly the more functional carer — exposes the play's central irony: social convention protects the wrong relationships.

# Topic 6: Class and Poverty
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Class and poverty are not background conditions in *A Taste of Honey* but the structural determinants of virtually every significant event — the Salford tenement flats Jo and Helen inhabit, the precarious employment that makes Helen dependent on men with money, and the complete absence of any safety net that would allow Jo to face her pregnancy without crisis. Delaney presents poverty with the specificity of someone who knows it from the inside: the details of the flat, the management of small amounts of money, the practical logistics of survival give the play its texture. Jo and Helen's poverty is not romanticised — the play does not sentimentalise working-class life — but it is presented as a social fact that explains and partly justifies behaviours that a more comfortable world would simply condemn. Delaney's intervention in 1958 was to insist that working-class women's lives were worth dramatising on their own terms, not as cautionary tales for middle-class audiences.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 7: Race and Prejudice
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Delaney's treatment of race in *A Taste of Honey* is notable for its refusal to treat Jimmie's Blackness as a problem to be managed — the play simply presents him as a person, allowing the prejudice of other characters (and the anticipated prejudice of the original audience) to be exposed by its own contrast with his dignity and decency. Jo's pregnant anxiety about the baby's skin colour is handled with characteristic Delaney directness — the question is real, the anxiety understandable, but Delaney refuses to allow it to become the play's moral centre. The social context of 1950s Britain — racially hostile, governed by informal colour bars, beginning to see Windrush-era immigration as a "problem" — gives Jimmie's presence and Jo's relationship with him a political dimension that Delaney addresses through comedy and candour rather than didactic argument. The baby's mixed-race heritage becomes one of several ways in which Jo refuses the script her society has written for her.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 8: Gender and Independence
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** *A Taste of Honey* is one of the first British plays to centre a working-class woman's experience without either moralising about her behaviour or positioning her as an object of male concern — Delaney gives Jo a selfhood and a voice that insists on its own validity regardless of the judgements of the society around her. Jo's independence is hard-won and partial: she navigates a world structured against her at every turn (class, gender, age, unmarried pregnancy) without the resources that would make genuine independence possible. Delaney uses the contrast between Jo and Helen to map two different responses to being a woman without power: Helen uses charm and sexuality as currency, Jo refuses this transaction and pays the price. The play's refusal of a conventional ending — no rescue, no redemption, just Jo alone and Helen returned — is a formal statement of the limits of women's options in 1950s Britain and a challenge to audiences to consider what those limits reveal.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 9: Motherhood and Responsibility
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Delaney examines motherhood not as a natural vocation but as a social role with specific material requirements — requirements that neither Helen nor Jo is in a position to meet — and in doing so challenges the sentimental idealisations of motherhood that dominated 1950s culture. Helen's failure as a mother is contextualised throughout the play as the product of her own poverty and isolation rather than lack of love, making Delaney's critique structural rather than individual. Jo's ambivalence about her pregnancy — oscillating between tenderness and fear — is presented with unusual honesty for the period, acknowledging that impending motherhood under Jo's circumstances produces dread as much as joy. Geof's quasi-maternal nurturing of Jo is the play's implicit suggestion that care can be provided by whoever is willing and able, regardless of conventional role — a radical proposition in 1958 and a quietly persistent argument for rethinking what responsibility for children requires from society.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 10: Love and Loneliness
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Love in *A Taste of Honey* is consistently presented as genuine but insufficient — every relationship in the play is marked by the gap between affection and the ability to sustain commitment, a gap that Delaney traces to the social conditions that make stability impossible rather than to individual failure. Jo loves Jimmie, but the world offers them no framework for that love to persist. Jo and Geof achieve something genuinely tender, but social convention has no category for what they are to each other. Helen loves Jo in her way, but returns to reclaim her only when Peter has left her, making her love inseparable from her own need. The "taste of honey" of the title suggests exactly this quality: love and pleasure are real but fleeting, and the characters' loneliness is not the result of love's absence but of social structures that prevent it from lasting. Delaney presents loneliness as the defining condition of working-class life, produced not by emotional failure but by economic and social instability.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
