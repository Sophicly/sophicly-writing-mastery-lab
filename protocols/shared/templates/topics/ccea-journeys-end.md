# Topic 1: Stanhope
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Sherriff presents Stanhope as the play's most complex and psychologically damaged character — a man who was once a model of public school virtue and who has been fundamentally altered by three years on the Western Front, sustained now only by whisky and the force of will that refuses to let him break down in front of his men. His drinking is presented not as weakness but as the only coping mechanism available to a man of extreme sensitivity who has been required to maintain authority in conditions of systematic horror, and Sherriff ensures the audience understands this through Osborne's quiet, steady advocacy. Stanhope's relationship with Raleigh reveals the central dramatic irony: the hero Raleigh has idolised at school is unrecognisable, and yet the qualities Raleigh admired — courage, loyalty, the refusal to abandon responsibility — are all still present, simply no longer visible in their original form. Stanhope's final vigil over Raleigh's dying body is the play's most direct statement about the cost of leadership in impossible circumstances.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does R.C. Sherriff present Stanhope in *Journey's End?*

You should consider:
- how Sherriff establishes Stanhope's character and how he has been affected by the war
- how Stanhope's relationships with Osborne and Raleigh develop the play's themes
- how Sherriff uses Stanhope to explore ideas about leadership, courage, and the psychological cost of war.

# Topic 3: Raleigh
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Raleigh is presented as the play's emblem of uncorrupted idealism — his arrival in the trenches carrying the values of school patriotism and hero-worship into a reality that has long since invalidated them creates the play's central dramatic irony. Sherriff uses Raleigh's shock at Stanhope's condition to register what the war has done, positioning the audience alongside Raleigh's dawning comprehension as he moves from enthusiasm through confusion to a death that arrives before he has fully understood what he has walked into. Raleigh's letter home — which Stanhope intercepts and then, crucially, cannot bring himself to destroy after reading — is the play's most precise measure of the war's moral distortions: Stanhope fears condemnation from a school friend, but the letter contains only admiration. His death, modest and quiet in the dugout as Stanhope holds him, presents the play's most direct indictment: the youngest, most innocent figure dies first.

# Topic 4: Osborne
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Osborne functions as the play's emotional centre and its most fully rounded figure of decency — older than the others, calmer, capable of the small gestures of care (the Lewis Carroll passage before the raid) that humanise an environment defined by destruction. His role as mediator between Stanhope's volatile authority and Raleigh's naïve enthusiasm is also his dramatic function: Osborne translates the war's reality for Raleigh without destroying him, and manages Stanhope's extremes without condescension. His death in the raid — matter-of-fact, off-stage, reported — is Sherriff's most devastating structural choice: the character best fitted to survive emotionally is killed, and his death is followed by the grotesque dinner party in which Trotter and the Sergeant-Major celebrate with food and wine, dramatising the dissociation necessary to continue functioning. The chapter of *Alice in Wonderland* he reads before the raid — logic suspended, absurdity normal — is a precise metaphor for the trenches.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 5: Trotter
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Trotter's function in the play is partly comic relief — his preoccupation with food, his hand-drawn chart of hours until relief, his uncomplicated soldierly practicality — but Sherriff uses this surface cheerfulness to make a specific point about the different psychological strategies available to men in the trenches. Where Stanhope drinks and Osborne maintains gentle intellectual engagement, Trotter manages through a determined refusal to think abstractly about his situation — an intellectual self-limitation that is also a survival strategy. His class background — he is the one officer who has risen from the ranks — differentiates him from the others and is handled by Sherriff without condescension, suggesting that the public school ethos of heroic sacrifice and the working-class pragmatism of getting through are equally valid responses to the same impossible circumstances. Trotter's survival at the play's end is not presented as triumph but as continuation.

# Topic 6: The Futility of War
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Sherriff presents the futility of war through the structure of the play as much as through its content — the raid that costs Osborne his life achieves nothing tactically meaningful, and the German offensive that ends the play arrives regardless of anything the officers have done or refused to do, making all their suffering retrospectively purposeless. The confined setting of the dugout, with its constant awareness of the front just beyond, creates a theatrical sense of entrapment: the characters cannot leave, cannot change their situation, and can only wait for the random violence of an industrial war to reach them. Sherriff, himself a veteran, resists both the glorification of sacrifice and the simple anti-war polemic, presenting instead a scrupulously honest account of how men live when their world has been reduced to waiting for death. The play's title — suggesting not a destination but an ending — frames the whole action as a journey towards a conclusion that was always inevitable.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 7: Duty and Heroism
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Sherriff redefines heroism in *Journey's End* by relocating it from battlefield spectacle to the daily act of continued functioning in circumstances that make continued functioning almost impossible. Every character in the play performs a different version of duty — Stanhope's savage self-discipline, Osborne's quiet care, Trotter's pragmatic cheerfulness, Raleigh's naïve commitment — and Sherriff presents all as valid responses to the same demand. The play's most distinctive contribution to the literature of heroism is its insistence that courage is not the absence of fear but its management: Stanhope's drinking, Trotter's food charts, and Hibbert's attempted breakdown are all different points on the same spectrum of men trying to bear what cannot be borne. Sherriff was writing from experience, and this experiential honesty — the refusal to allow his characters the dignity of public heroism — is the play's most radical and enduring quality.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 8: Fear and Coping
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Fear is the play's most pervasive atmosphere — present in Stanhope's drinking, in Hibbert's breakdown, in Trotter's determined focus on the mundane, in the silences between conversations about the impending German offensive. Sherriff presents fear not as cowardice but as the entirely rational response to a situation of genuine, unmanageable danger, and the play's central ethical question is not whether to be afraid but how to continue in the face of it. The Hibbert scene — in which Stanhope confronts a man trying to use illness to leave the front — is the play's most explicit engagement with this question, and Sherriff gives Stanhope's response a psychological complexity that both compels Hibbert to stay and reveals Stanhope's own fragility: "I know how you feel, Hibbert. I feel the same." Different coping strategies are presented without hierarchy: Sherriff refuses to award moral points to those who conceal their fear over those, like Hibbert, who cannot.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 9: Friendship and Comradeship
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Sherriff presents the bonds between the men in the dugout as the war's only redemptive feature — not a compensation for its horror but the human fabric that makes survival possible at all. The friendship between Osborne and Stanhope is the play's most tender relationship, a bond of mutual understanding and care that the play shows to be more sustaining than any of the official rationales for the war. The pre-raid scene between Osborne and Raleigh — *Alice in Wonderland*, garden talk, the deliberate avoidance of what is about to happen — is Sherriff's finest dramatisation of how comradeship operates under pressure: not by confronting fear together but by creating a shared space of normality that temporarily holds it at bay. The dinner party after Osborne's death, in which Stanhope insists on drinking and toasting, is comradeship's more painful face — the maintenance of collective solidarity even when individual feeling is unbearable.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 10: Class and Leadership
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** *Journey's End* is set entirely among the officer class of the British Army, and Sherriff uses this social homogeneity to examine how the values of the Edwardian public school — duty, self-restraint, the suppression of feeling, the maintenance of authority — function as both survival tools and psychological traps in the trenches. Stanhope, Osborne, Raleigh, and Trotter all embody different inflections of the officer ethos, and Sherriff gives each enough specificity to prevent the class analysis from becoming schematic. Trotter's class difference — he earned his commission rather than being given it by birth — is handled without condescension but marks him as an outsider to the emotional codes that govern the others, codes that Sherriff presents as both admirable and cruelly limiting. The play is a memorial to a class that the war disproportionately destroyed, and Sherriff's combination of affection and clear-eyed critique is one of the most honest assessments of the officer class that the First World War produced.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
