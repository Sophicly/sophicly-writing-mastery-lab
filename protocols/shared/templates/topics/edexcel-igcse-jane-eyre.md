# Topic 1: Jane Eyre — The Protagonist
**Type:** character
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Jane is a complex, psychologically rich protagonist who defies Victorian expectations of women. Bronte uses first-person narration to give the reader unmediated access to Jane's inner life, presenting a heroine who values moral integrity and emotional honesty above wealth, beauty, or social standing. Her development from a rebellious child to a self-possessed woman structures the entire novel.

# Topic 3: Rochester
**Type:** character
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Rochester is a Byronic hero -- brooding, morally ambiguous, and passionate. Bronte uses him to explore Victorian masculinity, the corrupting influence of wealth and social privilege, and the tension between desire and morality. His secret marriage to Bertha and his attempted bigamy raise complex questions about honesty, love, and the limits of sympathy.

# Topic 4: Love and Passion
**Type:** thematic
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Love in *Jane Eyre* is presented as a powerful, potentially dangerous force that must be balanced with moral integrity. Bronte challenges the conventional Victorian romance plot by insisting that love without equality is degrading. Jane's passion for Rochester is intense and physical, but she refuses to compromise her self-respect -- choosing to leave him rather than become his mistress.

# Topic 5: Independence and Self-Respect
**Type:** thematic
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Jane's fierce independence is the moral backbone of the novel. Bronte presents a heroine who would rather suffer poverty and isolation than sacrifice her principles. This was radical in the Victorian period, when women were expected to be dependent on men. Jane's refusal to become Rochester's mistress and her rejection of St John's loveless proposal both demonstrate that independence and self-respect are non-negotiable values.

# Topic 6: Social Class and Inequality
**Type:** thematic
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Bronte uses Jane's ambiguous social position -- a gentleman's daughter raised as a dependent, then a governess -- to expose the cruelty and hypocrisy of Victorian class hierarchies. The novel critiques how wealth determines worth, showing characters like Blanche Ingram using class privilege as a weapon while lacking the moral depth of the socially inferior Jane. Bronte challenges the assumption that birth and money reflect inner value.

# Topic 7: Religion and Morality
**Type:** thematic
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Bronte presents several contrasting models of Christianity through characters like Helen Burns (patient endurance), Mr Brocklehurst (hypocritical Evangelicalism), and St John Rivers (cold, self-denying Calvinism). Jane forges her own moral code that balances Christian duty with personal feeling. The novel critiques religious hypocrisy while affirming that genuine faith and moral integrity are essential to a fulfilled life.

# Topic 8: The Red Room and Childhood Suffering
**Type:** thematic
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The red room episode is one of the most psychologically intense passages in Victorian fiction. Bronte uses Gothic imagery -- the red furnishings, the locked door, the ghostly light -- to externalise Jane's terror and entrapment. The scene establishes the novel's central concern with injustice and rebellion, and prefigures later imprisonments in the novel, including Bertha's confinement in the attic at Thornfield.

# Topic 9: Bertha Mason
**Type:** character
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Bertha Mason is one of the most debated characters in English literature. She functions as Jane's dark double -- representing the passion and rage that Jane must suppress to survive in Victorian society. Bronte's presentation raises troubling questions about empire, race, and the treatment of women deemed "mad." Bertha's confinement in the attic mirrors Jane's imprisonment in the red room, connecting themes of entrapment, patriarchal control, and female fury.

# Topic 10: Gender and Patriarchy
**Type:** thematic
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** *Jane Eyre* is a sustained critique of Victorian patriarchy. Bronte shows how men -- from John Reed to Brocklehurst to Rochester to St John Rivers -- attempt to control, define, or possess Jane. The novel insists that women have the same emotional and intellectual capacities as men, and that a relationship built on inequality is morally unacceptable. Jane's famous declaration of spiritual equality is one of the earliest feminist statements in English fiction.
