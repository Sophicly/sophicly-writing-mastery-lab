# Topic 1: Viola
**Type:** character
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Viola is the emotional centre of Twelfth Night, navigating Illyria in disguise while suppressing her own desires. Shakespeare uses her dual identity as Cesario to explore the tension between performed identity and authentic feeling, making her the character through whom the audience most directly experiences the comedy's deeper emotional currents.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 2, Scene 4

### Extract
1  VIOLA
2  Ay, but I know —
3
4  DUKE ORSINO
5  What dost thou know?
6
7  VIOLA
8  Too well what love women to men may owe.
9  In faith, they are as true of heart as we.
10 My father had a daughter loved a man
11 As it might be, perhaps, were I a woman,
12 I should your lordship.
13
14 DUKE ORSINO
15 And what's her history?
16
17 VIOLA
18 A blank, my lord. She never told her love,
19 But let concealment, like a worm i'th'bud,
20 Feed on her damask cheek. She pined in thought,
21 And with a green and yellow melancholy
22 She sat like Patience on a monument,
23 Smiling at grief. Was not this love indeed?
24 We men may say more, swear more, but indeed
25 Our shows are more than will; for still we prove
26 Much in our vows, but little in our love.

# Topic 3: Malvolio
**Type:** character
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Malvolio is both comic victim and social climber, and Shakespeare invites a complex audience response to his humiliation. His Puritanical self-importance makes him a target, but the cruelty of his imprisonment in darkness pushes the comedy toward something disturbing — forcing the audience to question where entertainment ends and persecution begins.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 2, Scene 5

# Topic 4: Love and Desire
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Twelfth Night presents love as unstable, irrational, and frequently self-indulgent. Orsino wallows in the performance of loving rather than loving itself, Olivia falls instantly for a disguised woman, and Viola loves silently without hope. Shakespeare satirises romantic excess while simultaneously showing that genuine feeling can exist beneath the absurdity.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 1, Scene 1

### Extract
1  DUKE ORSINO
2  If music be the food of love, play on,
3  Give me excess of it that, surfeiting,
4  The appetite may sicken and so die.
5  That strain again, it had a dying fall.
6  O, it came o'er my ear like the sweet sound
7  That breathes upon a bank of violets,
8  Stealing and giving odour. Enough, no more;
9  'Tis not so sweet now as it was before.
10 O spirit of love, how quick and fresh art thou,
11 That, notwithstanding thy capacity
12 Receiveth as the sea, naught enters there,
13 Of what validity and pitch soe'er,
14 But falls into abatement and low price
15 Even in a minute! So full of shapes is fancy
16 That it alone is high fantastical.
17
18 CURIO
19 Will you go hunt, my lord?
20
21 DUKE ORSINO
22 What, Curio?
23
24 CURIO
25 The hart.
26
27 DUKE ORSINO
28 Why, so I do, the noblest that I have.
29 O, when mine eyes did see Olivia first
30 Methought she purged the air of pestilence.
31 That instant was I turned into a hart,
32 And my desires, like fell and cruel hounds,
33 E'er since pursue me.

# Topic 5: Disguise and Identity
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Disguise in Twelfth Night is not simply a plot device but a lens through which Shakespeare examines the instability of identity itself. Viola's male disguise destabilises gender, social rank, and desire simultaneously, creating a chain of misrecognition that suggests identity is always partly a performance — and that the truth of the self may only emerge when the mask is removed.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 1, Scene 5

### Extract
1  VIOLA
2  I am not what I am.
3
4  OLIVIA
5  I would you were as I would have you be.
6
7  VIOLA
8  Would it be better, madam, than I am?
9  I wish it might, for now I am your fool.
10
11 OLIVIA
12 [aside] O, what a deal of scorn looks beautiful
13 In the contempt and anger of his lip!
14 A murderous guilt shows not itself more soon
15 Than love that would seem hid: love's night is noon. —
16 Cesario, by the roses of the spring,
17 By maidhood, honour, truth, and everything,
18 I love thee so that, maugre all thy pride,
19 Nor wit nor reason can my passion hide.
20 Do not extort thy reasons from this clause,
21 For that I woo, thou therefore hast no cause.
22 But rather reason thus with reason fetter:
23 Love sought is good, but given unsought is better.
24
25 VIOLA
26 By innocence I swear, and by my youth,
27 I have one heart, one bosom, and one truth,
28 And that no woman has, nor never none
29 Shall mistress be of it save I alone.
30 And so adieu, good madam. Never more
31 Will I my master's tears to you deplore.
32
33 OLIVIA
34 Yet come again, for thou perhaps mayst move
35 That heart which now abhors to like his love.

# Topic 6: Olivia
**Type:** character
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Olivia begins the play defined by mourning and withdrawal, yet Shakespeare swiftly transforms her into one of the comedy's most active and assertive figures. Her pursuit of Cesario overturns gender conventions and reveals that her prolonged grief was itself a kind of performance — one that collapses the moment genuine desire arrives.

# Topic 7: Orsino
**Type:** character
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Orsino is Shakespeare's study of a man more in love with the idea of love than with any real person. His extravagant rhetoric and shifting moods reveal a self-absorbed romanticism that borders on narcissism, yet his genuine warmth toward Cesario suggests a capacity for real connection that his performative lovesickness has buried.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 2, Scene 4

### Extract
1  DUKE ORSINO
2  Come hither, boy. If ever thou shalt love,
3  In the sweet pangs of it remember me;
4  For such as I am, all true lovers are,
5  Unstaid and skittish in all motions else
6  Save in the constant image of the creature
7  That is beloved. How dost thou like this tune?
8
9  VIOLA
10 It gives a very echo to the seat
11 Where love is throned.
12
13 DUKE ORSINO
14 Thou dost speak masterly.
15 My life upon't, young though thou art, thine eye
16 Hath stayed upon some favour that it loves.
17 Hath it not, boy?
18
19 VIOLA
20 A little, by your favour.
21
22 DUKE ORSINO
23 What kind of woman is't?
24
25 VIOLA
26 Of your complexion.

28 DUKE ORSINO

# Topic 8: Sir Toby and the Subplot
**Type:** character
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Sir Toby Belch drives the comic subplot with his appetite for disorder, drinking, and mischief. Shakespeare uses him to embody the festive, carnivalesque spirit of Twelfth Night itself, but also to expose the darker side of revelry -- his exploitation of Sir Andrew Aguecheek's money and his orchestration of Malvolio's humiliation reveal a selfishness beneath the merriment.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 2, Scene 3

# Topic 9: Appearance vs Reality
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Twelfth Night is a play in which almost nothing is what it seems. Viola appears male, Olivia's mourning masks restlessness, Orsino's love is performance, Malvolio's dignity is vanity, and Feste's foolishness conceals wisdom. Shakespeare uses this pervasive gap between appearance and reality to question whether any social identity is truly authentic.

# Topic 10: Class and Social Order
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Twelfth Night repeatedly tests the boundaries of social hierarchy. Malvolio's fantasy of becoming Count Malvolio exposes both his presumption and the rigid class structure that makes such ambition laughable. Maria, a gentlewoman servant, orchestrates the gulling plot and ultimately marries Sir Toby, suggesting that wit and resourcefulness can transcend rank -- even as the play punishes Malvolio for the same aspiration.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 5, Scene 1

### Extract
1  MALVOLIO
2  Lady, you have. Pray you, peruse that letter.
3  You must not now deny it is your hand;
4  Write from it if you can, in hand or phrase,
5  Or say 'tis not your seal, not your invention.
6  You can say none of this. Well, grant it then,
7  And tell me, in the modesty of honour,
8  Why you have given me such clear lights of favour,
9  Bade me come smiling and cross-gartered to you,
10 To put on yellow stockings, and to frown
11 Upon Sir Toby and the lighter people;
12 And acting this in an obedient hope,
13 Why have you suffered me to be imprisoned,
14 Kept in a dark house, visited by the priest,
15 And made the most notorious geck and gull
16 That e'er invention played on? Tell me why!
17
18 OLIVIA
19 Alas, Malvolio, this is not my writing,
20 Though I confess much like the character.
21 But out of question, 'tis Maria's hand.
22 And now I do bethink me, it was she
23 First told me thou wast mad; then cam'st in smiling,
24 And in such forms which here were presupposed
25 Upon thee in the letter. Prithee be content;
26 This practice hath most shrewdly passed upon thee,
27 But when we know the grounds and authors of it
28 Thou shalt be both the plaintiff and the judge
29 Of thine own cause.
30
31 FABIAN
32 Good madam, hear me speak,
33 And let no quarrel nor no brawl to come
34 Taint the condition of this present hour,
35 Which I have wondered at. In hope it shall not,
36 Most freely I confess myself and Toby
37 Set this device against Malvolio here
38 Upon some stubborn and uncourteous parts
39 We had conceived against him.
