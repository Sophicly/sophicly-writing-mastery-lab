# Topic 1: Alec
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Johnston presents Alec as a character trapped between worlds — the Anglo-Irish Protestant gentry and the ordinary Irish community represented by Jerry. Through Alec's first-person retrospective narration, Johnston foregrounds his passivity and self-awareness, creating a protagonist who understands his own complicity in the class system yet feels powerless to resist it. The epistolary frame — Alec writing from his prison cell before execution — charges every memory with dramatic irony, as the reader understands that friendship and integrity will not save him. Johnston's measured, restrained prose style mirrors Alec's emotional repression, a product of his upbringing, making moments of genuine feeling all the more striking.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does Jennifer Johnston present Alec in *How Many Miles to Babylon?*

You should consider:
- how Alec's narration shapes the reader's sympathy and understanding
- the tension between Alec's class background and his personal values
- how Johnston uses language and structure to reveal Alec's inner conflict.

# Topic 3: Jerry
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Jerry Crowe is presented as the vital, instinctive counterpart to Alec's restraint — grounded in the Irish landscape and his community in ways that Alec can admire but never fully access. Johnston uses Jerry's physicality and directness to challenge the romanticised ideals of the officer class, showing courage and loyalty as qualities not confined to rank or breeding. Jerry's decision to desert in search of his father, and his acceptance of the consequences, gives him a tragic dignity that the military hierarchy cannot recognise. Through Jerry, Johnston critiques the waste of war: a man of genuine human worth destroyed by a system that cannot see his value.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 4: Alec's Mother
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Alec's mother is presented as a cold, controlling force who embodies the rigid values of the Anglo-Irish Ascendancy — she views social class as an absolute moral category and Alec's friendship with Jerry as a fundamental transgression. Johnston uses her clipped, imperious dialogue and Alec's barely suppressed resentment to expose how class operates as an emotional weapon within families as much as across social divisions. Her insistence that Alec join the war effort reveals the performative patriotism of the gentry class, more concerned with reputation than with her son's survival. Johnston positions her, alongside the faceless military command, as one of the novel's true antagonists.

# Topic 5: War and Futility
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Johnston presents the First World War as a system of organised futility in which class-bound command structures send men to meaningless deaths while protecting the reputations of institutions. The trenches in the novel are rendered with deliberate sparseness — Johnston avoids spectacle in favour of the grinding dullness and random violence of attrition warfare, undercutting any possible heroism. Alec's execution of Jerry at Jerry's own request becomes the novel's central moral act: a gesture of personal loyalty made in direct defiance of military law, reframing loyalty and courage as private rather than national virtues. The novel's retrospective frame ensures that the reader experiences the war entirely as loss — there is no victory narrative, only a record of waste.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question

You should consider:

# Topic 6: Friendship Across Class
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The friendship between Alec and Jerry is the moral and emotional centre of the novel, presented by Johnston as a genuine bond that exposes the arbitrariness and cruelty of class division. Through shared childhood memories of riding and the Irish landscape, Johnston suggests that natural affinity transcends social conditioning — only constant external pressure from Alec's mother and the rigid military hierarchy can suppress it. The war simultaneously enables their friendship (class distinctions are partially eroded in the trenches) and destroys it, revealing the contradictions at the heart of the social order the war is supposed to defend. Johnston presents their friendship as a kind of counter-history: what might exist if society permitted it.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 7: Class and Social Division
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Class is not merely a backdrop in the novel but the engine of its tragedy — Johnston shows how it structures every relationship, determines who gives orders and who obeys, and ultimately decides who lives and who dies. Alec's inability to save Jerry is inseparable from his class position: he has the connections that might intervene, yet the system of which he is a part will not permit mercy. Johnston uses the contrast between Alec's comfortable, if unhappy, Anglo-Irish home and Jerry's tightly bound village community to show class as something people inhabit in their bodies and voices, not merely their bank accounts. The Irish context deepens this: class is entangled with colonial history, making Johnston's critique both social and political.

# Topic 8: Duty and Loyalty
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Johnston sets duty to institutions — family, nation, military command — against personal loyalty, and finds the former hollow and destructive. Alec's execution of Jerry is the novel's defining act of loyalty: illegal, compassionate, and morally superior to the military justice that condemns both men. Johnston uses the contrast between Alec's principled defiance and the impersonal bureaucratic language of the court martial to suggest that institutional duty has been corrupted into mere obedience. Jerry's desertion to find his father, itself an act of filial loyalty, is punishable by death — Johnston frames this to expose how the war machine criminalises the most human impulses.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question

You should consider:

# Topic 9: Isolation and Loneliness
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Isolation is presented as Alec's defining condition before the war, during it, and in his final hours — Johnston structures the novel so that even friendship cannot fully dissolve his essential aloneness. His childhood is marked by emotional coldness from his parents, his marriage to the war is arranged rather than chosen, and in the trenches even camaraderie cannot fill the void created by a life of emotional suppression. Johnston uses the confined, first-person retrospective voice to embody this isolation formally: the reader is locked inside Alec's perspective, as trapped as he is. The prison cell from which he narrates is the logical endpoint of a life lived on the margins of genuine belonging.

# Topic 10: The Irish Context
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Johnston situates her war narrative within a specifically Irish colonial context that complicates any simple reading of patriotism or sacrifice. The Irish characters fighting in a British war raises questions about loyalty, identity, and whose interests are served by the conflict that the English-set war novels of the period tend to elide. The Irish landscape — described with lyrical precision in Alec's memories — functions as an emblem of a life and culture that the war destroys, giving the novel's losses a specifically national as well as personal dimension. Johnston's concern with the Anglo-Irish Protestant class also engages with a community already in historical decline, adding a further layer of elegiac complexity to the novel's tone.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
