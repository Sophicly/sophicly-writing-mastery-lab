# Topic 1: Macbeth as Tragic Hero
**Type:** protagonist
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** We start with the protagonist because Macbeth's transformation from loyal thane to tyrannical king is the backbone of the entire play. Understanding how Shakespeare constructs his downfall through language, soliloquy, and dramatic structure is essential for every other topic.

## Part A — Extract
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 1 Scene 7, lines 1 to 28
**Extract Context:** *Macbeth has left the banquet and is alone, debating whether to murder King Duncan.*

### Extract
    If it were done when 'tis done, then 'twere well
    It were done quickly. If th'assassination
    Could trammel up the consequence, and catch
    With his surcease, success; that but this blow
 5  Might be the be-all and the end-all here,
    But here, upon this bank and shoal of time,
    We'd jump the life to come. But in these cases
    We still have judgement here, that we but teach
    Bloody instructions, which, being taught, return
10  To plague th'inventor. This even-handed justice
    Commends th'ingredience of our poisoned chalice
    To our own lips. He's here in double trust:
    First, as I am his kinsman and his subject,
    Strong both against the deed; then, as his host,
15  Who should against his murderer shut the door,
    Not bear the knife myself. Besides, this Duncan
    Hath borne his faculties so meek, hath been
    So clear in his great office, that his virtues
    Will plead like angels, trumpet-tongued, against
20  The deep damnation of his taking-off;
    And pity, like a naked new-born babe,
    Striding the blast, or heaven's cherubin, horsed
    Upon the sightless couriers of the air,
    Shall blow the horrid deed in every eye,
25  That tears shall drown the wind. I have no spur
    To prick the sides of my intent, but only
    Vaulting ambition, which o'erleaps itself
    And falls on th'other.

# Topic 3: Ambition
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Ambition is the driving force of the play. Shakespeare presents it as both a natural human quality and a destructive force that, unchecked by moral conscience, leads to tyranny and self-destruction. Students must trace how ambition corrupts both Macbeth and Lady Macbeth.

## Part A — Extract
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 1 Scene 5, lines 15 to 48
**Extract Context:** *Lady Macbeth reads Macbeth's letter describing his meeting with the witches and his new title, then reflects on his character.*

### Extract
    Glamis thou art, and Cawdor, and shalt be
    What thou art promised. Yet do I fear thy nature:
    It is too full o'th'milk of human kindness
    To catch the nearest way. Thou wouldst be great,
 5  Art not without ambition, but without
    The illness should attend it. What thou wouldst highly,
    That wouldst thou holily; wouldst not play false,
    And yet wouldst wrongly win. Thou'dst have, great Glamis,
    That which cries, 'Thus thou must do,' if thou have it;
10  And that which rather thou dost fear to do,
    Than wishest should be undone. Hie thee hither,
    That I may pour my spirits in thine ear
    And chastise with the valour of my tongue
    All that impedes thee from the golden round
15  Which fate and metaphysical aid doth seem
    To have thee crowned withal.

                        Enter MESSENGER

    What is your tidings?

    MESSENGER
    The King comes here tonight.

    LADY MACBETH
                        Thou'rt mad to say it!
20  Is not thy master with him, who, were't so,
    Would have informed for preparation?

    MESSENGER
    So please you, it is true. Our thane is coming.

    LADY MACBETH
    Give him tending: he brings great news.

                        Exit MESSENGER

25  That croaks the fatal entrance of Duncan

30  Stop up th'access and passage to remorse,

# Topic 4: The Supernatural
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The supernatural pervades the play through the witches, apparitions, and visions. Shakespeare uses it to externalise inner corruption, reflect Jacobean anxieties about witchcraft, and raise questions about fate versus free will. Students need to analyse both its dramatic function and its contextual significance.

## Part A — Extract
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 1 Scene 3, lines 38 to 69
**Extract Context:** *Macbeth and Banquo encounter the three witches on the heath after the battle.*

### Extract
    MACBETH
    Speak, if you can: what are you?

    FIRST WITCH
    All hail, Macbeth! Hail to thee, Thane of Glamis!

    SECOND WITCH
    All hail, Macbeth! Hail to thee, Thane of Cawdor!

    THIRD WITCH
 5  All hail, Macbeth, that shalt be king hereafter!

    BANQUO
    Good sir, why do you start and seem to fear
    Things that do sound so fair? — I'th'name of truth,
    Are ye fantastical, or that indeed
    Which outwardly ye show? My noble partner
10  You greet with present grace and great prediction
    Of noble having and of royal hope,
    That he seems rapt withal. To me you speak not.
    If you can look into the seeds of time
    And say which grain will grow and which will not,
15  Speak then to me, who neither beg nor fear
    Your favours nor your hate.

    FIRST WITCH
    Hail!

    SECOND WITCH
    Hail!

    THIRD WITCH
    Hail!

    FIRST WITCH
20  Lesser than Macbeth, and greater.

    SECOND WITCH
    Not so happy, yet much happier.

    THIRD WITCH
    Thou shalt get kings, though thou be none.
    So all hail, Macbeth and Banquo!

    FIRST WITCH
25  Banquo and Macbeth, all hail!

    MACBETH
    Stay, you imperfect speakers, tell me more.
    By Sinel's death I know I am Thane of Glamis,
    But how of Cawdor? The Thane of Cawdor lives,
    A prosperous gentleman; and to be king
30  Stands not within the prospect of belief,
    No more than to be Cawdor. Say from whence
    You owe this strange intelligence, or why
    Upon this blasted heath you stop our way
    With such a prophetic greeting. Speak, I charge you.

# Topic 5: Lady Macbeth
**Type:** character
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Lady Macbeth is Shakespeare's study of the cost of suppressing conscience. Her trajectory from ruthless determination to psychological collapse mirrors and inverts Macbeth's own arc. Students must analyse how Shakespeare uses her to explore gender, power, and guilt.

## Part A — Extract
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 2 Scene 2, lines 1 to 34
**Extract Context:** *Lady Macbeth waits alone while Macbeth carries out the murder of Duncan.*

### Extract
    LADY MACBETH
    That which hath made them drunk hath made me bold.
    What hath quenched them hath given me fire. Hark! Peace!
    It was the owl that shrieked, the fatal bellman,
    Which gives the stern'st good-night. He is about it.
 5  The doors are open, and the surfeited grooms
    Do mock their charge with snores. I have drugged their possets,
    That death and nature do contend about them
    Whether they live or die.

    MACBETH [within]
    Who's there? What, ho!

    LADY MACBETH
10  Alack, I am afraid they have awaked,
    And 'tis not done. Th'attempt and not the deed
    Confounds us. Hark! — I laid their daggers ready;
    He could not miss 'em. Had he not resembled
    My father as he slept, I had done't.

                        Enter MACBETH

15  My husband!

    MACBETH
    I have done the deed. Didst thou not hear a noise?

    LADY MACBETH
    I heard the owl scream and the crickets cry.
    Did not you speak?

    MACBETH
    When?

    LADY MACBETH
    Now.

    MACBETH
    As I descended?

    LADY MACBETH
20  Ay.

    MACBETH
    Hark! Who lies i'th'second chamber?

    LADY MACBETH
    Donalbain.

    MACBETH
    This is a sorry sight.

    LADY MACBETH
    A foolish thing, to say a sorry sight.

    MACBETH
25  There's one did laugh in's sleep, and one cried 'Murder!'

30  One cried 'God bless us!' and 'Amen' the other,

    Consider it not so deeply.

# Topic 6: Guilt and Conscience
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Guilt is Shakespeare's primary mechanism of retribution in the play. It manifests physically and psychologically — through hallucinations, sleeplessness, and madness. Students must connect guilt to the play's moral framework and Jacobean ideas about divine justice.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 5 Scene 1, lines 26 to 61
**Extract Context:** *A doctor and gentlewoman observe Lady Macbeth sleepwalking and speaking in her sleep.*

### Extract

    GENTLEWOMAN

 5  LADY MACBETH

10  time to do't. Hell is murky. Fie, my lord, fie! A soldier,

    DOCTOR
    Do you mark that?

15  LADY MACBETH

# Topic 7: Kingship and Tyranny
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Shakespeare contrasts good and bad kingship throughout Macbeth. Duncan and Edward represent divinely ordained, benevolent rule; Macbeth represents the tyranny that follows when the natural order is violated. Students must connect this to the Jacobean belief in the Divine Right of Kings and the Great Chain of Being.

## Part A — Extract
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 4 Scene 3, lines 91 to 123
**Extract Context:** *Malcolm tests Macduff's loyalty by pretending to be even more wicked than Macbeth, listing the virtues a true king should possess.*

### Extract
    MALCOLM
    But I have none. The king-becoming graces,
    As justice, verity, temp'rance, stableness,
    Bounty, perseverance, mercy, lowliness,
    Devotion, patience, courage, fortitude,
 5  I have no relish of them, but abound
    In the division of each several crime,
    Acting it many ways. Nay, had I power, I should
    Pour the sweet milk of concord into hell,
    Uproar the universal peace, confound
10  All unity on earth.

    MACDUFF
    O Scotland, Scotland!

    MALCOLM
    If such a one be fit to govern, speak.
    I am as I have spoken.

    MACDUFF
                        Fit to govern?
15  No, not to live. — O nation miserable,
    With an untitled tyrant bloody-sceptred,
    When shalt thou see thy wholesome days again,
    Since that the truest issue of thy throne
    By his own interdiction stands accursed
20  And does blaspheme his breed? Thy royal father
    Was a most sainted king. The queen that bore thee,
    Oft'ner upon her knees than on her feet,
    Died every day she lived. Fare thee well!
    These evils thou repeat'st upon thyself
25  Hath banished me from Scotland. O my breast,
    Thy hope ends here!

    MALCOLM
    Macduff, this noble passion,
    Child of integrity, hath from my soul
    Wiped the black scruples, reconciled my thoughts
30  To thy good truth and honour. Devilish Macbeth
    By many of these trains hath sought to win me
    Into his power, and modest wisdom plucks me
    From over-credulous haste.

# Topic 8: Banquo
**Type:** character
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Banquo serves as a moral foil to Macbeth. Both hear the witches' prophecies, but where Macbeth acts on them, Banquo resists temptation. His murder and ghostly return reinforce the play's themes of guilt, loyalty, and the inescapability of conscience. Students should analyse how Shakespeare uses contrast to deepen characterisation.

# Topic 9: Appearance versus Reality
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Deception runs through the play at every level: the witches' equivocal prophecies, Lady Macbeth's instruction to "look like the innocent flower / But be the serpent under't," and Macbeth's public performance of grief. Students should analyse how Shakespeare uses dramatic irony and duplicity to build tension and explore moral corruption.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

### Extract

 5  As 'twere a careless trifle.

    There's no art

15  That the proportion both of thanks and payment

# Topic 10: Violence and Its Consequences
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Violence in Macbeth escalates from honourable battlefield killing to political assassination to the slaughter of innocents. Shakespeare shows how each act of violence breeds more violence and further isolates Macbeth. Students must trace this escalation and connect it to the play's moral framework about the consequences of unchecked power.

## Part A — Extract
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 5 Scene 5, lines 1 to 33
**Extract Context:** *Macbeth is in Dunsinane Castle, preparing for the final battle. He receives news that Lady Macbeth is dead.*

### Extract

 5  Were they not forced with those that should be ours,

    MACBETH
10  I have almost forgot the taste of fears.

    To hear a night-shriek, and my fell of hair

15  Direness, familiar to my slaughterous thoughts,
