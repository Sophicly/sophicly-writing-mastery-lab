# Topic 1: Beatrice — The Protagonist
**Type:** character
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Beatrice is Shakespeare's most linguistically aggressive heroine. In this extract, her wit serves as both weapon and shield — she uses humour to maintain control over social interactions and to resist the expectation that women should be passive and compliant. Yet beneath the wordplay lies genuine feeling that she works hard to conceal.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 2, Scene 1

### Extract
1  BEATRICE  Just, if he send me no husband; for the
2  which blessing I am at him upon my knees every
3  morning and evening. Lord, I could not endure a
4  husband with a beard on his face! I had rather lie
5  in the woollen.
6  LEONATO  You may light on a husband that hath no
7  beard.
8  BEATRICE  What should I do with him? Dress him in
9  my apparel and make him my waiting-gentlewoman?
10  He that hath a beard is more than a youth, and he
11  that hath no beard is less than a man; and he that
12  is more than a youth is not for me, and he that is
13  less than a man, I am not for him. Therefore I will
14  even take sixpence in earnest of the berrord and
15  lead his apes into hell.
16  LEONATO  Well then, go you into hell?
17  BEATRICE  No, but to the gate, and there will the
18  devil meet me like an old cuckold with horns on
19  his head, and say, 'Get you to heaven, Beatrice,
20  get you to heaven; here's no place for you maids.'
21  So deliver I up my apes and away to Saint Peter;
22  for the heavens, he shows me where the bachelors
23  sit, and there live we as merry as the day is long.

# Topic 3: Benedick
**Type:** character
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Benedick begins the play as a self-declared enemy of love and marriage, yet Shakespeare reveals through his soliloquy that he can be persuaded astonishingly quickly. The speed of his conversion exposes the gap between his public performance of independence and his private desire for connection — a gap that makes him both comic and sympathetic.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 2, Scene 3

### Extract
1  BENEDICK  [coming forward] This can be no trick.
2  The conference was sadly borne; they have the
3  truth of this from Hero; they seem to pity the
4  lady. It seems her affections have their full bent.
5  Love me? Why, it must be requited. I hear how I
6  am censured. They say I will bear myself proudly
7  if I perceive the love come from her. They say too
8  that she will rather die than give any sign of
9  affection. I did never think to marry. I must not
10  seem proud. Happy are they that hear their
11  detractions and can put them to mending. They say
12  the lady is fair — 'tis a truth, I can bear them
13  witness. And virtuous — 'tis so, I cannot reprove
14  it. And wise, but for loving me. By my troth, it is
15  no addition to her wit, nor no great argument of
16  her folly, for I will be horribly in love with her.
17  I may chance have some odd quirks and remnants
18  of wit broken on me because I have railed so long
19  against marriage; but doth not the appetite alter?
20  A man loves the meat in his youth that he cannot
21  endure in his age. Shall quips and sentences and
22  these paper bullets of the brain awe a man from
23  the career of his humour? No! The world must be
24  peopled. When I said I would die a bachelor, I did
25  not think I should live till I were married.

# Topic 4: Love and Courtship
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Shakespeare contrasts two models of courtship in Much Ado: the conventional romance of Hero and Claudio, and the combative wit of Beatrice and Benedick. This extract shows how Claudio's love operates through social formality and male negotiation — Hero herself barely speaks. Shakespeare invites the audience to question whether arranged, performative love is as valuable as the messy, honest connection Beatrice and Benedick eventually discover.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 1, Scene 1

### Extract
1  CLAUDIO  Benedick, didst thou note the daughter of
2  Signor Leonato?
3  BENEDICK  I noted her not, but I looked on her.
4  CLAUDIO  Is she not a modest young lady?
5  BENEDICK  Do you question me as an honest man
6  should do, for my simple true judgement, or would
7  you have me speak after my custom, as being a
8  professed tyrant to their sex?
9  CLAUDIO  No, I pray thee speak in sober judgement.
10  BENEDICK  Why, i'faith, methinks she's too low for a
11  high praise, too brown for a fair praise, and too
12  little for a great praise. Only this commendation I
13  can afford her, that were she other than she is she
14  were unhandsome, and being no other but as she
15  is, I do not like her.
16  CLAUDIO  Thou thinkest I am in sport. I pray thee
17  tell me truly how thou likest her.
18  BENEDICK  Would you buy her, that you enquire after
19  her?
20  CLAUDIO  Can the world buy such a jewel?
21  BENEDICK  Yea, and a case to put it into. But speak
22  you this with a sad brow, or do you play the
23  flouting jack, to tell us Cupid is a good hare-
24  finder and Vulcan a rare carpenter? Come, in what
25  key shall a man take you to go in the song?

### Question
Look at how Shakespeare presents the contrasting attitudes to love in this extract. [20]

**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4

### Question

# Topic 5: Deception and Manipulation
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Much Ado About Nothing is structured around deceptions both benign and malicious. In this extract, Don Pedro orchestrates the benevolent trick on Benedick, demonstrating that deception can serve love. Shakespeare creates a layered dramatic irony — the audience watches characters who are deceiving being watched by a character who is being deceived — forcing us to consider when manipulation is acceptable and when it becomes dangerous.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 2, Scene 3

### Extract
1  DON PEDRO  Come hither, Leonato. What was it you
2  told me of today, that your niece Beatrice was in
3  love with Signor Benedick?
4  CLAUDIO  [aside] O, ay, stalk on, stalk on; the fowl
5  sits. — I did never think that lady would have
6  loved any man.
7  LEONATO  No, nor I neither, but most wonderful that
8  she should so dote on Signor Benedick, whom she
9  hath in all outward behaviours seemed ever to
10  abhor.
11  BENEDICK  [aside] Is't possible? Sits the wind in that
12  corner?
13  LEONATO  By my troth, my lord, I cannot tell what to
14  think of it, but that she loves him with an enraged
15  affection, it is past the infinite of thought.
16  DON PEDRO  Maybe she doth but counterfeit.
17  CLAUDIO  Faith, like enough.
18  LEONATO  O God! Counterfeit? There was never
19  counterfeit of passion came so near the life of
20  passion as she discovers it.
21  DON PEDRO  Why, what effects of passion shows she?
22  CLAUDIO  [aside] Bait the hook well; this fish will bite.
23  LEONATO  What effects, my lord? She will sit you —
24  you heard my daughter tell you how.

# Topic 6: Honour and Reputation
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The church scene is the play's crisis point, where honour culture is exposed as both brutal and absurd. Claudio publicly shames Hero based on fabricated evidence, and Leonato's first instinct is to wish his daughter dead rather than face dishonour. Shakespeare forces the Elizabethan audience to confront how a patriarchal honour system destroys the innocent while protecting the accuser.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 4, Scene 1

### Extract
1  CLAUDIO  There, Leonato, take her back again.
2  Give not this rotten orange to your friend.
3  She's but the sign and semblance of her honour.
4  Behold how like a maid she blushes here!
5  O, what authority and show of truth
6  Can cunning sin cover itself withal!
7  Comes not that blood as modest evidence
8  To witness simple virtue? Would you not swear,
9  All you that see her, that she were a maid
10  By these exterior shows? But she is none.
11  She knows the heat of a luxurious bed.
12  Her blush is guiltiness, not modesty.
13  LEONATO  What do you mean, my lord?
14  CLAUDIO  Not to be married,
15  Not to knit my soul to an approved wanton.
16  LEONATO  Dear my lord, if you in your own proof
17  Have vanquished the resistance of her youth
18  And made defeat of her virginity —
19  CLAUDIO  I know what you would say: if I have known her,
20  You will say she did embrace me as a husband,
21  And so extenuate the forehand sin. No, Leonato,
22  I never tempted her with word too large,
23  But, as a brother to his sister, showed
24  Bashful sincerity and comely love.

# Topic 7: Hero
**Type:** character
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Hero is the play's most vulnerable figure — a woman whose identity and worth are entirely controlled by the men around her. In this extract, her father's reaction to the accusation reveals the terrifying fragility of female reputation in a patriarchal society. Shakespeare gives Hero very few words here, making her silence itself a commentary on women's powerlessness when male authority turns against them.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 4, Scene 1

### Extract
1  LEONATO  Hath no man's dagger here a point for me?
2  [HERO swoons]
3  BEATRICE  Why, how now, cousin, wherefore sink you
4  down?
5  DON JOHN  Come, let us go. These things come thus
6  to light smother her spirits up.
7  [Exeunt DON PEDRO, DON JOHN and CLAUDIO]
8  BENEDICK  How doth the lady?
9  BEATRICE  Dead, I think. Help, uncle! Hero, why,
10  Hero! Uncle! Signor Benedick! Friar!
11  LEONATO  O Fate, take not away thy heavy hand!
12  Death is the fairest cover for her shame
13  That may be wished for.
14  BEATRICE  How now, cousin Hero?
15  FRIAR  Have comfort, lady.
16  LEONATO  Dost thou look up?
17  FRIAR  Yea, wherefore should she not?
18  LEONATO  Wherefore? Why, doth not every earthly
19  thing cry shame upon her? Could she here deny
20  The story that is printed in her blood?
21  Do not live, Hero, do not ope thine eyes;
22  For did I think thou wouldst not quickly die,
23  Thought I thy spirits were stronger than thy shames,
24  Myself would on the rearward of reproaches

# Topic 8: Don John
**Type:** character
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Don John is the play's agent of malicious deception. As an illegitimate brother, he exists outside the bonds of legitimacy, loyalty, and social belonging that govern Messina. Shakespeare presents his villainy as rooted in resentment and exclusion — he disrupts others' happiness because he has no access to it himself. His plainness about his own nature makes him a dark mirror to the play's theme of appearance versus reality.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 1, Scene 3

### Extract
1  DON JOHN  I cannot hide what I am. I must be sad
2  when I have cause, and smile at no man's jests;
3  eat when I have stomach, and wait for no man's
4  leisure; sleep when I am drowsy, and tend on no
5  man's business; laugh when I am merry, and claw
6  no man in his humour.
7  CONRADE  Yea, but you must not make the full show
8  of this till you may do it without controlment.
9  You have of late stood out against your brother,
10  and he hath ta'en you newly into his grace, where
11  it is impossible you should take true root but by
12  the fair weather that you make yourself. It is
13  needful that you frame the season for your own
14  harvest.
15  DON JOHN  I had rather be a canker in a hedge than
16  a rose in his grace, and it better fits my blood to
17  be disdained of all than to fashion a carriage to
18  rob love from any. In this, though I cannot be said
19  to be a flattering honest man, it must not be
20  denied but I am a plain-dealing villain. I am
21  trusted with a muzzle and enfranchised with a
22  clog; therefore I have decreed not to sing in my
23  cage. If I had my mouth I would bite; if I had my
24  liberty I would do my liking. In the meantime, let
25  me be that I am, and seek not to alter me.

# Topic 9: Gender and Patriarchy
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Beatrice's explosive demand "Kill Claudio" is one of the most dramatic moments in the play. It reveals the fundamental injustice of a world where women cannot act to defend themselves or those they love. Shakespeare uses Beatrice's frustration to expose how patriarchal structures silence and disempower women, forcing them to rely on men even when those men have failed them.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 4, Scene 1

### Extract
1  BEATRICE  Is he not approved in the height a villain,
2  that hath slandered, scorned, dishonoured my
3  kinswoman? O that I were a man! What, bear her
4  in hand until they come to take hands, and then
5  with public accusation, uncovered slander,
6  unmitigated rancour — O God that I were a man!
7  I would eat his heart in the market place.
8  BENEDICK  Hear me, Beatrice.
9  BEATRICE  Talk with a man out at a window — a
10  proper saying!
11  BENEDICK  Nay but Beatrice —
12  BEATRICE  Sweet Hero, she is wronged, she is
13  slandered, she is undone.
14  BENEDICK  Beat —
15  BEATRICE  Princes and counties! Surely a princely
16  testimony, a goodly count, Count Comfect, a sweet
17  gallant, surely. O that I were a man for his sake!
18  Or that I had any friend would be a man for my
19  sake! But manhood is melted into courtesies,
20  valour into compliment, and men are only turned
21  into tongue, and trim ones too. He is now as
22  valiant as Hercules that only tells a lie and swears
23  it. I cannot be a man with wishing, therefore I will
24  die a woman with grieving.
25  BENEDICK  Tarry, good Beatrice. By this hand, I love
26  thee.
27  BEATRICE  Use it for my love some other way than
28  swearing by it.
29  BENEDICK  Think you in your soul the Count Claudio
30  hath wronged Hero?
31  BEATRICE  Yea, as sure as I have a thought or a soul.
32  BENEDICK  Enough. I am engaged. I will challenge
33  him.

# Topic 10: Appearance vs Reality
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The Watch's discovery of the deception is one of Shakespeare's most brilliantly comic scenes — the least intelligent characters in the play are the ones who uncover the truth. Borachio's confession reveals the mechanics of Don John's plot, exposing how easily appearance can be mistaken for reality. Shakespeare uses the comic incompetence of Dogberry and the Watch to suggest that truth will out, even through the most unlikely channels.

## Part A
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 3, Scene 3

### Extract
1  BORACHIO  Stand thee close then under this
2  penthouse, for it drizzles rain, and I will, like a
3  true drunkard, utter all to thee.
4  WATCHMAN  [aside] Some treason, masters; yet stand
5  close.
6  BORACHIO  Therefore know, I have earned of Don
7  John a thousand ducats.
8  CONRADE  Is it possible that any villainy should be so
9  dear?
10  BORACHIO  Thou shouldst rather ask if it were
11  possible any villainy should be so rich. For when
12  rich villains have need of poor ones, poor ones
13  may make what price they will.
14  CONRADE  I wonder at it.
15  BORACHIO  That shows thou art unconfirmed. Thou
16  knowest that the fashion of a doublet, or a hat, or
17  a cloak is nothing to a man.
18  CONRADE  Yes, it is apparel.
19  BORACHIO  I mean the fashion.
20  CONRADE  Yes, the fashion is the fashion.
21  BORACHIO  Tush, I may as well say the fool's the
22  fool. But seest thou not what a deformed thief this
23  fashion is?
24  WATCHMAN  [aside] I know that Deformed; 'a has been
25  a vile thief this seven year; 'a goes up and down
26  like a gentleman. I remember his name.
