# Topic 1: Jekyll and Hyde / Duality
**Type:** theme
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** The duality of human nature is the central concern of the novella. Stevenson uses the physical separation of Jekyll and Hyde to dramatise the Victorian idea that every person contains both good and evil impulses, and that attempts to deny or suppress one half lead to catastrophe rather than moral purity.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 10: Henry Jekyll's Full Statement of the Case

### Extract
```
 1    With every day, and from both sides of my intelligence, the
 2    moral and the intellectual, I thus drew steadily nearer to
 3    that truth, by whose partial discovery I have been doomed to
 4    such a dreadful shipwreck: that man is not truly one, but
 5    truly two. I say two, because the state of my own knowledge
 6    does not pass beyond that point. Others will follow, others
 7    will outstrip me on the same lines; and I hazard the guess
 8    that man will be ultimately known for a mere polity of
 9    multifarious, incongruous and independent denizens. I for my
10    part, from the nature of my life, advanced infallibly in one
11    direction and in one direction only. It was on the moral
12    side, and in my own person, that I learned to recognise the
13    thorough and primitive duality of man; I saw that, of the
14    two natures that contended in the field of my consciousness,
15    if I could rightly be said to be either, it was only because
16    I was radically both; and from an early date, even before
17    the course of my scientific discoveries had begun to suggest
18    the most naked possibility of such a miracle, I had learned
19    to dwell with pleasure, as a beloved daydream, on the
20    thought of the separation of these elements. If each, I told
21    myself, could but be housed in separate identities, life
22    would be relieved of all that was unbearable; the unjust
23    might go his way, delivered from the aspirations and remorse
24    of his more upright twin; and the just could walk steadfastly
25    and securely on his upward path, doing the good things in
26    which he found his pleasure, and no longer exposed to
27    disgrace and penitence by the hands of this extraneous evil.
```

# Topic 3: Hyde (Evil and Violence)
**Type:** character
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Hyde embodies pure, unrestrained evil — yet Stevenson makes him difficult to describe, suggesting that evil defies rational categorisation. His increasing violence across the novella (from trampling the child to murdering Carew) shows that evil, once released, does not remain contained but escalates beyond control.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 4: The Carew Murder Case

### Extract
```

 2    anger, stamping with his foot, brandishing the cane, and
 3    carrying on (as the maid described it) like a madman. The
 4    old gentleman took a step back, with the air of one very
 5    much surprised and a trifle hurt; and at that Mr. Hyde broke
 6    out of all bounds and clubbed him to the earth. And next
 7    moment, with ape-like fury, he was trampling his victim
 8    under foot and hailing down a storm of blows, under which
 9    the bones were audibly shattered and the body jumped upon
10    the roadway. At the horror of these sights and sounds, the

14    the police. The murderer was gone long ago; but there lay
15    his victim in the middle of the lane, incredibly mangled.
```

# Topic 4: Jekyll (Repression and Respectability)
**Type:** character
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Jekyll is not an innocent victim but a willing participant in his own destruction. His desire to appear respectable while indulging his darker impulses reflects the hypocrisy Stevenson identified at the heart of Victorian middle-class life. Students should analyse how Jekyll's language reveals both guilt and self-justification.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 10: Henry Jekyll's Full Statement of the Case

### Extract
```
 1    I was born in the year 18— to a large fortune, endowed
 2    besides with excellent parts, inclined by nature to industry,
 3    fond of the respect of the wise and good among my
 4    fellow-men, and thus, as might have been supposed, with
 5    every guarantee of an honourable and distinguished future.
 6    And indeed the worst of my faults was a certain impatient
 7    gaiety of disposition, such as has made the happiness of
 8    many, but such as I found it hard to reconcile with my
 9    imperious desire to carry my head high, and wear a more than
10    commonly grave countenance before the public. Hence it came
11    about that I concealed my pleasures; and that when I reached
12    years of reflection, and began to look round me and take
13    stock of my progress and position in the world, I stood
14    already committed to a profound duplicity of life. Many a
15    man would have even blazoned such irregularities as I was
16    guilty of; but from the high views that I had set before me,
17    I regarded and hid them with an almost morbid sense of
18    shame. It was thus rather the exacting nature of my
19    aspirations than any particular degradation in my faults,
20    that made me what I was and, with even a deeper trench than
21    in the majority of men, severed in me those provinces of
22    good and ill which divide and compound man's dual nature.
```

# Topic 5: Utterson (Rationality and the Victorian Gentleman)
**Type:** character
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Utterson is the reader's surrogate — rational, loyal, and respectable. However, his commitment to propriety means he repeatedly fails to act on his suspicions, prioritising reputation over truth. Stevenson uses Utterson to show how Victorian codes of gentlemanly conduct could enable evil by discouraging honest inquiry.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 1: Story of the Door

### Extract
```
 1    Mr. Utterson the lawyer was a man of a rugged countenance,
 2    that was never lighted by a smile; cold, scanty and
 3    embarrassed in discourse; backward in sentiment; lean, long,
 4    dusty, dreary and yet somehow lovable. At friendly meetings,
 5    and when the wine was to his taste, something eminently
 6    human beaconed from his eye; something indeed which never
 7    found its way into his talk, but which spoke not only in
 8    these silent symbols of the after-dinner face, but more
 9    often and loudly in the acts of his life. He was austere
10    with himself; drank gin when he was alone, to mortify a
11    taste for vintages; and though he enjoyed the theatre, had
12    not crossed the doors of one for twenty years. But he had
13    an approved tolerance for others; sometimes wondering,
14    almost with envy, at the high pressure of spirits involved
15    in their misdeeds; and in any extremity inclined to help
16    rather than to reprove. "I incline to Cain's heresy," he
17    used to say quaintly: "I let my brother go to the devil in
18    his own way." In this character, it was frequently his
19    fortune to be the last reputable acquaintance and the last
20    good influence in the lives of down-going men. And to such
21    as these, so long as they came to his chambers, he never
22    marked a shade of change in his demeanour.
```

**Format:** single

# Topic 7: Science and Religion
**Type:** theme
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Stevenson wrote during a period of intense conflict between scientific progress and religious faith. Jekyll's experiment transgresses both natural law and divine order, echoing contemporary anxieties about Darwin, vivisection, and the limits of human knowledge. The novella asks whether science without moral restraint is inherently dangerous.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 9: Dr Lanyon's Narrative

### Extract
```
 1    He put the glass to his lips and drank at one gulp. A cry
 2    followed; he reeled, staggered, clutched at the table and
 3    held on, staring with injected eyes, gasping with open
 4    mouth; and as I looked there came, I thought, a change — he
 5    seemed to swell — his face became suddenly black and the
 6    features seemed to melt and alter — and the next moment, I
 7    had sprung to my feet and leaped back against the wall, my
 8    arm raised to shield me from that prodigy, my mind submerged
 9    in terror.
10
11    "O God!" I screamed, and "O God!" again and again; for there
12    before my eyes — pale and shaken, and half fainting, and
13    groping before him with his hands, like a man restored from
14    death — there stood Henry Jekyll!
15
16    What he told me in the next hour, I cannot bring my mind to
17    set on paper. I saw what I saw, I heard what I heard, and my
18    soul sickened at it; and yet now when that sight has faded
```

# Topic 8: Setting (London, Fog, and Doors)
**Type:** theme
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Stevenson's London is a Gothic landscape of fog, gaslight, and hidden spaces. The contrasting facades of Jekyll's house — the handsome front on the square versus the sinister back door in the by-street — mirror the duality at the heart of the novella. Students should analyse how setting creates atmosphere and reinforces theme.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 4: The Carew Murder Case

### Extract
```
 1    It was by this time about nine in the morning, and the first
 2    fog of the season. A great chocolate-coloured pall lowered
 3    over heaven, but the wind was continually charging and
 4    routing these embattled vapours; so that as the cab crawled
 5    from street to street, Mr. Utterson beheld a marvellous
 6    number of degrees and hues of twilight; for here it would
 7    be dark like the back-end of evening; and there would be a
 8    glow of a rich, lurid brown, like the light of some strange
 9    conflagration; and here, for a moment, the fog would be
10    quite broken up, and a haggard shaft of daylight would
11    glance in between the swirling wreaths. The dismal quarter
12    of Soho seen under these changing glimpses, with its muddy
13    ways, and slatternly passengers, and its lamps, which had
14    never been extinguished or had been kindled afresh to combat
15    this mournful reinvasion of darkness, seemed, in the
16    lawyer's eyes, like a district of some city in a nightmare.
17    The thoughts of his mind, besides, were of the gloomiest
18    dye; and when he glanced at the companion of his drive, he
19    was conscious of some touch of that terror of the law and
20    the law's officers, which may at times assail the most
21    honest.
```

# Topic 9: Lanyon (Horror of Knowledge)
**Type:** character
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** Lanyon represents orthodox, respectable Victorian science. His death from the shock of witnessing Jekyll's transformation shows that some knowledge is too terrible to bear. Stevenson uses Lanyon to explore the idea that there are truths about human nature that the rational mind cannot process without being destroyed.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 6: Remarkable Incident of Doctor Lanyon

### Extract
```

 2    was visibly balder and older; and yet it was not so much
 3    these tokens of a swift physical decay that arrested the
 4    lawyer's notice, as a look in the eye and quality of manner
 5    that seemed to testify to some deep-seated terror of the
 6    mind. It was unlikely that the doctor should fear death; and
 7    yet that was what Utterson was tempted to suspect. "Yes," he
 8    thought; "he is a doctor, he must know his own state and
```

# Topic 10: Victorian Society and Hypocrisy
**Type:** theme
**Format:** single
**Teaching Point:** The novella is set in a world of wealthy, professional men who guard their reputations above all else. Stevenson critiques a society where the appearance of virtue matters more than actual morality, where gentlemen silently tolerate each other's transgressions, and where the pressure to conform creates the very conditions for hidden depravity to thrive.

**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**Extract Location:** Chapter 10: Henry Jekyll's Full Statement of the Case

### Extract
```
 1    The pleasures which I made haste to seek in my disguise
 2    were, as I have said, undignified; I would scarce use a
 3    harder term. But in the hands of Edward Hyde, they soon
 4    began to turn toward the monstrous. When I would come back
 5    from these excursions, I was often plunged into a kind of
 6    wonder at my vicarious depravity. This familiar that I
 7    called out of my own soul, and sent forth alone to do his
 8    good pleasure, was a being inherently malign and villainous;
 9    his every act and thought centered on self; drinking pleasure
10    with bestial avidity from any degree of torture to another;
11    relentless like a man of stone. Henry Jekyll stood at times
12    aghast before the acts of Edward Hyde; but the situation was
13    apart from ordinary laws, and insidiously relaxed the grasp
14    of conscience. It was Hyde, after all, and Hyde alone, that
15    was guilty. Jekyll was no worse; he woke again to his good
16    qualities seemingly unimpaired; he would even make haste,
17    where it was possible, to undo the evil done by Hyde. And
18    thus his conscience slumbered.

20    Into the details of the infamy at which I thus connived

23    warnings and the successive steps with which my chastisement
24    approached. I met with one accident which, as it brought on
25    no consequence, I shall no more than mention.
```
