# Topic 1: Shylock and Prejudice
**Type:** protagonist-theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Shylock is the play's most complex character -- simultaneously a victim of antisemitic abuse and a figure driven to cruel revenge. His "Hath not a Jew eyes?" speech forces the audience to confront the cycle of prejudice: how mistreatment breeds the very hatred it claims to oppose.

## Part A -- Extract
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 3 Scene 1, lines 53 to 86
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Shylock has just learned that his daughter Jessica has eloped with the Christian Lorenzo, taking his money and jewels. Salarino and Solanio mock him and ask what use Antonio's flesh could be.

### Extract
1  SALARINO: Why, I am sure if he forfeit thou wilt not take
2  his flesh. What's that good for?
3  SHYLOCK: To bait fish withal. If it will feed nothing else,
4  it will feed my revenge. He hath disgraced me, and
5  hindered me half a million, laughed at my losses,
6  mocked at my gains, scorned my nation, thwarted my
7  bargains, cooled my friends, heated mine enemies --
8  and what's his reason? I am a Jew. Hath not a Jew
9  eyes? Hath not a Jew hands, organs, dimensions,
10 senses, affections, passions? Fed with the same
11 food, hurt with the same weapons, subject to the
12 same diseases, healed by the same means, warmed
13 and cooled by the same winter and summer as a
14 Christian is? If you prick us, do we not bleed? If
15 you tickle us, do we not laugh? If you poison us, do
16 we not die? And if you wrong us, shall we not
17 revenge? If we are like you in the rest, we will
18 resemble you in that. If a Jew wrong a Christian,
19 what is his humility? Revenge. If a Christian wrong
20 a Jew, what should his sufferance be by Christian
21 example? Why, revenge. The villainy you teach me I
22 will execute, and it shall go hard but I will better
23 the instruction.
24 [Enter a man from Antonio]
25 SALARINO: Here comes another of the tribe. A third cannot
26 be matched, unless the devil himself turn Jew.
27 [Exeunt Salarino and Solanio]
28 SHYLOCK: How now, Tubal? What news from Genoa? Hast thou
29 found my daughter?
30 TUBAL: I often came where I did hear of her, but cannot
31 find her.
32 SHYLOCK: Why, there, there, there, there! A diamond gone
33 cost me two thousand ducats in Frankfurt! The curse
34 never fell upon our nation till now -- I never felt it
35 till now.

# Topic 3: Justice and Mercy
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The trial scene stages a direct collision between strict legal justice and the Christian ideal of mercy. Portia's famous speech argues mercy transcends earthly power, yet the court's final treatment of Shylock -- stripping his wealth, forcing conversion -- raises uncomfortable questions about whether mercy is truly shown or merely used as a weapon.

## Part A -- Extract
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 4 Scene 1, lines 182 to 214
**Extract Context:** In this extract, the trial over Antonio's bond is underway. Portia, disguised as the young lawyer Balthasar, has asked Shylock to show mercy. She now delivers her famous speech on the nature of mercy before addressing Shylock directly.

### Extract
1  PORTIA: The quality of mercy is not strained.
2  It droppeth as the gentle rain from heaven
3  Upon the place beneath. It is twice blest:
4  It blesseth him that gives and him that takes.
5  'Tis mightiest in the mightiest. It becomes
6  The throned monarch better than his crown.
7  His sceptre shows the force of temporal power,
8  The attribute to awe and majesty,
9  Wherein doth sit the dread and fear of kings;
10 But mercy is above this sceptred sway.
11 It is enthroned in the hearts of kings;
12 It is an attribute to God Himself.
13 And earthly power doth then show likest God's
14 When mercy seasons justice. Therefore, Jew,
15 Though justice be thy plea, consider this:
16 That in the course of justice none of us
17 Should see salvation. We do pray for mercy,
18 And that same prayer doth teach us all to render
19 The deeds of mercy. I have spoke thus much
20 To mitigate the justice of thy plea,
21 Which if thou follow, this strict court of Venice
22 Must needs give sentence 'gainst the merchant there.
23 SHYLOCK: My deeds upon my head! I crave the law,
24 The penalty and forfeit of my bond.
25 PORTIA: Is he not able to discharge the money?
26 BASSANIO: Yes, here I tender it for him in the court,
27 Yea, twice the sum. If that will not suffice,
28 I will be bound to pay it ten times o'er
29 On forfeit of my hands, my head, my heart.
30 If this will not suffice, it must appear
31 That malice bears down truth. And I beseech you,
32 Wrest once the law to your authority.
33 To do a great right, do a little wrong,
34 And curb this cruel devil of his will.

# Topic 4: Portia
**Type:** character
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Portia is constrained by her father's will and by the patriarchal expectations of Elizabethan society, yet she proves the most capable and decisive character in the play. Her disguise as Balthasar allows Shakespeare to explore what happens when a brilliant woman is freed from gendered restrictions and operates in a male space of power.

## Part A -- Extract
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 3 Scene 4, lines 60 to 93
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Portia has learned that Antonio's life is in danger because of the bond with Shylock. She has sent Bassanio to Venice with money, and now reveals to Nerissa her plan to disguise themselves as young men and travel to the trial.

### Extract
1  PORTIA: They shall, Nerissa, but in such a habit
2  That they shall think we are accomplished
3  With that we lack. I'll hold thee any wager,
4  When we are both accoutred like young men,
5  I'll prove the prettier fellow of the two,
6  And wear my dagger with the braver grace,
7  And speak between the change of man and boy
8  With a reed voice, and turn two mincing steps
9  Into a manly stride, and speak of frays
10 Like a fine bragging youth, and tell quaint lies
11 How honourable ladies sought my love,
12 Which I denying, they fell sick and died --
13 I could not do withal! Then I'll repent,
14 And wish, for all that, that I had not killed them.
15 And twenty of these puny lies I'll tell,
16 That men shall swear I have discontinued school
17 Above a twelvemonth. I have within my mind
18 A thousand raw tricks of these bragging jacks
19 Which I will practise.
20 NERISSA: Why, shall we turn to men?
21 PORTIA: Fie, what a question's that,
22 If thou wert near a lewd interpreter!
23 But come, I'll tell thee all my whole device
24 When I am in my coach, which stays for us
25 At the park gate; and therefore haste away,
26 For we must measure twenty miles today.
27 NERISSA: Shall we not see our husbands before we go?
28 PORTIA: We shall not be thought of, and yet I warrant
29 We shall have old swearing that they did give
30 The rings away. Come, mistress. I have in mind
31 A plan that you shall know when we are there.

# Topic 5: Love and Friendship
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Shakespeare explores multiple forms of love: the romantic love tested by the casket trial, Antonio's self-sacrificing devotion to Bassanio, and Jessica's elopement with Lorenzo. The play questions whether love is a matter of outward show or inner truth, and what sacrifices genuine love demands.

## Part A -- Extract
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 3 Scene 2, lines 1 to 34
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Bassanio has arrived at Belmont to attempt the casket test. Portia, who has fallen in love with him, urges him to delay his choice because she is afraid of losing him if he chooses the wrong casket.

### Extract
1  PORTIA: I pray you tarry. Pause a day or two
2  Before you hazard, for in choosing wrong
3  I lose your company. Therefore forbear a while.
4  There's something tells me -- but it is not love --
5  I would not lose you; and you know yourself
6  Hate counsels not in such a quality.
7  But lest you should not understand me well --
8  And yet a maiden hath no tongue but thought --
9  I would detain you here some month or two
10 Before you venture for me. I could teach you
11 How to choose right, but then I am forsworn.
12 So will I never be. So may you miss me.
13 But if you do, you'll make me wish a sin,
14 That I had been forsworn. Beshrew your eyes,
15 They have o'erlooked me and divided me.
16 One half of me is yours, the other half yours --
17 Mine own, I would say -- but if mine, then yours,
18 And so all yours. O, these naughty times
19 Put bars between the owners and their rights!
20 And so, though yours, not yours. Prove it so,
21 Let Fortune go to hell for it, not I.
22 I speak too long, but 'tis to peize the time,
23 To eke it and to draw it out in length,
24 To stay you from election.
25 BASSANIO:                   Let me choose,
26 For as I am, I live upon the rack.
27 PORTIA: Upon the rack, Bassanio? Then confess
28 What treason there is mingled with your love.
29 BASSANIO: None but that ugly treason of mistrust
30 Which makes me fear th'enjoying of my love.
31 There may as well be amity and life
32 'Tween snow and fire as treason and my love.
33 PORTIA: Ay, but I fear you speak upon the rack,
34 Where men enforced do speak anything.

# Topic 6: Wealth and Materialism
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Money drives almost every relationship and conflict in the play: Bassanio needs money to woo Portia, Antonio borrows from Shylock, and the caskets test whether suitors value gold over inner worth. Shakespeare repeatedly questions whether wealth brings happiness or corrupts the bonds between people.

## Part A -- Extract
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 1 Scene 3, lines 103 to 136
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Shylock has agreed to lend Bassanio three thousand ducats, with Antonio as guarantor. Shylock now confronts Antonio about the way Antonio has previously treated him, particularly regarding the lending of money.

### Extract
1  SHYLOCK: Signor Antonio, many a time and oft
2  In the Rialto you have rated me
3  About my moneys and my usances.
4  Still have I borne it with a patient shrug,
5  For sufferance is the badge of all our tribe.
6  You call me misbeliever, cut-throat dog,
7  And spit upon my Jewish gaberdine,
8  And all for use of that which is mine own.
9  Well then, it now appears you need my help.
10 Go to, then. You come to me and you say,
11 "Shylock, we would have moneys" -- you say so,
12 You that did void your rheum upon my beard
13 And foot me as you spurn a stranger cur
14 Over your threshold. Moneys is your suit.
15 What should I say to you? Should I not say,
16 "Hath a dog money? Is it possible
17 A cur can lend three thousand ducats?" Or
18 Shall I bend low and in a bondman's key,
19 With bated breath and whispering humbleness,
20 Say this: "Fair sir, you spat on me on Wednesday last,
21 You spurned me such a day, another time
22 You called me dog -- and for these courtesies
23 I'll lend you thus much moneys."
24 ANTONIO: I am as like to call thee so again,
25 To spit on thee again, to spurn thee too.
26 If thou wilt lend this money, lend it not
27 As to thy friends, for when did friendship take
28 A breed for barren metal of his friend?
29 But lend it rather to thine enemy,
30 Who if he break, thou mayst with better face
31 Exact the penalty.
32 SHYLOCK:            Why, look you, how you storm!
33 I would be friends with you and have your love,
34 Forget the shames that you have stained me with,
35 Supply your present wants, and take no doit
36 Of usance for my moneys -- and you'll not hear me!

# Topic 7: Appearance versus Reality
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Disguise and deception operate at every level of the play: the caskets conceal their true value behind misleading surfaces, Portia and Nerissa disguise themselves as men, and Jessica escapes disguised as a boy. Shakespeare uses the casket test as a central symbol: those who judge by outward show are deceived, while those who look beneath the surface find truth.

## Part A -- Extract
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 2 Scene 7, lines 1 to 34
**Extract Context:** In this extract, the Prince of Morocco has come to Belmont to attempt the casket test. He must choose between gold, silver, and lead caskets to win Portia's hand in marriage. He reads the inscriptions and deliberates on his choice.

### Extract
1  MOROCCO: Some god direct my judgement! Let me see.
2  I will survey th' inscriptions back again.
3  What says this leaden casket?
4  "Who chooseth me must give and hazard all he hath."
5  Must give -- for what? For lead? Hazard for lead?
6  This casket threatens. Men that hazard all
7  Do it in hope of fair advantages.
8  A golden mind stoops not to shows of dross.
9  I'll then nor give nor hazard aught for lead.
10 What says the silver with her virgin hue?
11 "Who chooseth me shall get as much as he deserves."
12 As much as he deserves -- pause there, Morocco,
13 And weigh thy value with an even hand.
14 If thou be'st rated by thy estimation,
15 Thou dost deserve enough; and yet "enough"
16 May not extend so far as to the lady.
17 And yet to be afeard of my deserving
18 Were but a weak disabling of myself.
19 As much as I deserve -- why, that's the lady!
20 I do deserve her. Am I not in birth
21 Deserve her? Am I not in fortune? In graces?
22 In qualities of breeding? Ay, and more
23 Than these, in love I do deserve.
24 What if I strayed no farther, but chose here?
25 Let's see once more this saying graved in gold:
26 "Who chooseth me shall gain what many men desire."
27 Why, that's the lady! All the world desires her.
28 From the four corners of the earth they come
29 To kiss this saint, to breathe, to live on air.
30 The Hyrcanian deserts and the vasty wilds
31 Of wide Arabia are as thoroughfares now
32 For princes to come view fair Portia.
33 The watery kingdom, whose ambitious head
34 Spits in the face of heaven, is no bar
35 To stop the foreign spirits, but they come.

# Topic 8: Antonio
**Type:** character
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Antonio opens the play with unexplained melancholy and later offers his own flesh as surety for Bassanio's loan. His willingness to die for his friend raises questions about the nature of his devotion and whether his sadness stems from an attachment he cannot fully express. Shakespeare presents him as both generous and profoundly isolated.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 4 Scene 1, lines 261 to 295
**Extract Context:** In this extract, the trial is underway and it appears that Shylock will be granted his pound of Antonio's flesh. Antonio, believing he is about to die, speaks his farewell to Bassanio and the court.

### Extract

2  Meetest for death. The weakest kind of fruit

6  DUKE: Antonio, I am sorry for thee.
7  SHYLOCK: Will you go and look upon the bond?

9  To give the judgement.
10 PORTIA: Why then, thus it is:
11 You must prepare your bosom for his knife.
12 SHYLOCK: O noble judge! O excellent young man!
13 PORTIA: For the intent and purpose of the law
14 Hath full relation to the penalty,
15 Which here appeareth due upon the bond.
16 SHYLOCK: 'Tis very true. O wise and upright judge!

# Topic 9: The Bond
**Type:** theme-plot
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Shylock's bond of a pound of flesh begins as what he calls a "merry sport" but becomes the engine of the play's central conflict. It tests Venice's legal system, exposes the hypocrisy of a city that claims equal justice while practising prejudice, and forces every character to reveal their true priorities -- money, love, loyalty, or revenge.

## Part A -- Extract
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Extract Location:** Act 1 Scene 3, lines 139 to 173
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Shylock proposes the terms of the bond to Antonio. He suggests that if the loan is not repaid on time, the forfeit will be a pound of Antonio's flesh, presenting it as a joke.

### Extract
1  SHYLOCK: This kindness will I show.
2  Go with me to a notary, seal me there
3  Your single bond, and, in a merry sport,
4  If you repay me not on such a day,
5  In such a place, such sum or sums as are
6  Expressed in the condition, let the forfeit
7  Be nominated for an equal pound
8  Of your fair flesh, to be cut off and taken
9  In what part of your body pleaseth me.
10 ANTONIO: Content, in faith! I'll seal to such a bond,
11 And say there is much kindness in the Jew.
12 BASSANIO: You shall not seal to such a bond for me.
13 I'll rather dwell in my necessity.
14 ANTONIO: Why, fear not, man, I will not forfeit it.
15 Within these two months -- that's a month before
16 This bond expires -- I do expect return
17 Of thrice three times the value of this bond.
18 SHYLOCK: O father Abram, what these Christians are,
19 Whose own hard dealings teaches them suspect
20 The thoughts of others! Pray you tell me this:
21 If he should break his day, what should I gain
22 By the exaction of the forfeiture?
23 A pound of man's flesh taken from a man
24 Is not so estimable, profitable neither,
25 As flesh of muttons, beefs, or goats. I say,
26 To buy his favour I extend this friendship.
27 If he will take it, so. If not, adieu.
28 And for my love, I pray you, wrong me not.
29 ANTONIO: Yes, Shylock, I will seal unto this bond.
30 SHYLOCK: Then meet me forthwith at the notary's.
31 Give him direction for this merry bond,
32 And I will go and purse the ducats straight,
33 See to my house -- left in the fearful guard
34 Of an unthrifty knave -- and presently
35 I'll be with you.

# Topic 10: Gender Roles
**Type:** theme
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The women in the play are bound by patriarchal rules -- Portia by her dead father's will, Jessica by Shylock's household, Nerissa by her role as waiting-woman. Yet all three find ways to assert independence through disguise, elopement, or wit. Shakespeare uses the ring plot in Act 5 to give the women the final word, reasserting their authority over the men who underestimated them.

## Part A -- Extract

**Extract Location:** Act 5 Scene 1, lines 192 to 225
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Portia and Nerissa have returned to Belmont after the trial. They confront Bassanio and Gratiano about giving away the rings that their wives made them promise never to part with. Portia pretends to be angry.

### Extract

10 You give your wife too unkind a cause of grief.
