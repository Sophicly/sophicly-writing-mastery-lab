**Question:**
How does de Waal use the character of Leon to explore identity and belonging in *My Name Is Leon*?

Write about:
- what Leon says and does in the novel
- how de Waal presents Leon.

**Teaching Points:**
Leon is a mixed-race boy navigating a care system that repeatedly fails to see him whole, and de Waal constructs his interiority through free indirect discourse that keeps readers close to his limited but acute perspective. His acts of nurture — growing allotment vegetables, tending to others' emotional needs — reveal a child forced into adult roles prematurely, which de Waal frames as both a survival strategy and a quiet indictment of the state. The novel's first-person-adjacent narration means readers experience Leon's confusion, loyalty, and loss from the inside, making his silences as eloquent as his speech. De Waal's use of the present tense for key emotional moments grounds Leon's grief in immediacy, refusing the consolation of retrospective distance.

**Topic:** Conceptual Notes
**Type:** conceptual-notes
**Marks:** 0
**AOs:** none

**Teaching Points:**
*My Name Is Leon* (2016) is set in early 1980s Birmingham during a period of social upheaval — the Handsworth riots, Thatcherite welfare cuts, and the erosion of public services form the novel's political backdrop. Kit de Waal draws on her own experience working in the care system and family law, giving the novel an insider's precision about institutional failure. The title's assertion of name — of selfhood — is itself a political act in a narrative where systems routinely strip children of agency and identity. Key contextual frames include the Toxteth and Handsworth riots (1981), transracial adoption debates, and the Children Act 1989 (which came after the novel's setting, underscoring how unprotected children were).

**Question:**
How does de Waal use the character of Jake to explore family and separation in *My Name Is Leon*?

Write about:
- what Jake says and does in the novel
- how de Waal presents Jake.

**Teaching Points:**
Jake is Leon's baby half-brother, white and therefore more adoptable within the care system, and his presence functions as a structural irony: the sibling bond is the novel's emotional centre, yet society's racial hierarchies make it the bond most likely to be severed. De Waal renders Jake primarily through Leon's protective gaze — he is held, fed, sung to — so that Jake becomes a vehicle for exploring unconditional love rather than a fully realised character in his own right. The asymmetry between Leon's fierce attachment and the bureaucratic indifference to that attachment is where de Waal locates much of the novel's moral charge. Jake's eventual adoption by a white family without Leon is never presented as triumphant rescue; de Waal's restrained prose forces the reader to feel the violence of the separation beneath the administrative language.

**Type:** modern-text
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

**Question:**
How does de Waal use the character of Carol to explore the failures of the care system in *My Name Is Leon*?

Write about:
- what Carol says and does in the novel
- how de Waal presents Carol.

**Teaching Points:**
Carol is the foster carer who takes in both boys, and de Waal presents her as a woman of genuine warmth operating within a system that structurally undermines her capacity to act on that warmth. Her alcoholism is rendered with compassion rather than condemnation, positioned as a symptom of exhaustion and grief rather than moral failing, which implicates the institutions that rely on carers like Carol without supporting them. De Waal uses Carol's domestic space — the kitchen, the smell of food, the routines of care — to suggest that home is constituted through practice and feeling rather than legal category. When Carol loses the boys, the novel refuses to assign blame tidily, distributing it instead across a system designed to manage rather than nurture.

**Question:**
How does de Waal use the character of Tufty to explore community and solidarity in *My Name Is Leon*?

Write about:
- what Tufty says and does in the novel
- how de Waal presents Tufty.

**Teaching Points:**
Tufty is the allotment keeper who offers Leon an alternative community to the care system — one organised around mutual labour, patience, and the rhythms of growing things rather than bureaucratic assessment. De Waal uses the allotment scenes to slow the novel's pacing deliberately, creating islands of contingent stability that contrast with the institutional settings' controlled chaos. Tufty's Black British identity connects Leon to a heritage the care system has not provided him with, and their relationship is one of the few in the novel where Leon is met entirely on his own terms. The political dimension of Tufty's community activism during the riots extends the novel's argument: solidarity is possible, but the state rarely facilitates it.

**Topic:** Race and Identity
**Type:** modern-text
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

**Question:**
How does de Waal explore the theme of race and identity in *My Name Is Leon*?

Write about:
- how race and identity affect Leon's experiences in the novel
- how de Waal presents ideas about race and identity.

**Teaching Points:**
De Waal makes Leon's mixed-race identity inseparable from his experiences of the care system, where race determines placement decisions and adoption eligibility in ways that are never openly discussed but structurally decisive. The novel is set against the Handsworth riots, and de Waal draws a precise connection between the state's neglect of its Black citizens and its neglect of Black and mixed-race children in care — both are managed rather than valued. Leon's confusion about his own racial identity reflects a broader cultural failure to provide him with mirrors: his white mother, absent Black father, and white care workers are all inadequate guides to who he is. De Waal's prose registers race through sensation and observation rather than polemic, trusting readers to draw the systemic conclusions the novel's specificity demands.

**Topic:** Family and Belonging
**Type:** modern-text
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

**Question:**
How does de Waal explore the theme of family and belonging in *My Name Is Leon*?

Write about:
- how ideas about family and belonging are shown through Leon's experiences
- how de Waal presents the theme of family and belonging.

**Teaching Points:**
The novel systematically dismantles the idea that family is defined by blood or legal category, rebuilding it around acts of care, loyalty, and recognition. Leon's most stable moments of belonging — with Carol in her kitchen, with Tufty at the allotment — are contingent and unofficial, precisely what the state cannot recognise or protect. De Waal structures the novel as a series of separations, each one stripping away another layer of Leon's provisional belonging and forcing the reader to understand belonging as something fragile that must be actively maintained. The recurring motif of naming — Leon insisting on his own name, Jake's name always on Leon's lips — becomes de Waal's central symbol for the human need to be known and claimed by others.

**Topic:** Social Class and the Care System
**Type:** modern-text
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

**Question:**
How does de Waal explore the theme of social class and the care system in *My Name Is Leon*?

Write about:
- how social class and the care system affect Leon's life in the novel
- how de Waal presents ideas about class and institutional care.

**Teaching Points:**
De Waal situates the novel in a working-class Birmingham already being hollowed out by Thatcherite economic policy, so that the care system's failures are inseparable from the wider social context of austerity and disinvestment. The social workers, carers, and administrators the novel depicts are not villains but overwhelmed functionaries in an underfunded system, and de Waal's refusal to individualise blame is itself a structural critique. Class manifests in the material textures of the novel — the food eaten, the houses lived in, the bureaucratic language used about children — and de Waal's precise attention to these details exposes how class shapes life chances from the earliest age. Leon's intelligence and emotional acuity are consistently unrecognised by institutions calibrated for management rather than development, a failure de Waal frames as both personal and political.

**Topic:** Hope and Resilience
**Type:** modern-text
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

**Question:**
How does de Waal explore the theme of hope and resilience in *My Name Is Leon*?

Write about:
- how hope and resilience are shown through Leon's experiences in the novel
- how de Waal presents ideas about hope and resilience.

**Teaching Points:**
De Waal refuses to write Leon as a passive victim, and his resilience is rendered through small, purposeful acts: gardening, cooking, protecting Jake, learning, making connections across difference. The novel's structure enacts resilience formally — each chapter ends with Leon still standing, still reaching toward connection — even as the cumulative losses mount. De Waal's use of sensory detail is central to her presentation of hope: the allotment's smell of earth and growth, the physical warmth of Carol's kitchen, offer a counterpoint to the institutional cold. Critically, de Waal does not resolve the novel into easy triumph; hope in *My Name Is Leon* is tenacious and earned rather than guaranteed, which gives it its moral authority.

**Topic:** The 1980s Birmingham Setting
**Type:** modern-text
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

**Question:**
How does de Waal use the 1980s Birmingham setting to explore ideas about society and inequality in *My Name Is Leon*?

Write about:
- how the setting affects Leon's experiences in the novel
- how de Waal presents the 1980s Birmingham setting.

**Teaching Points:**
Birmingham in the early 1980s is not a neutral backdrop but an active shaping force: the city's racial tensions, its working-class communities under economic pressure, and the Handsworth riots of 1981 all feed directly into Leon's story. De Waal uses topographical specificity — named streets, recognisable landmarks, local dialects — to ground the novel in a historical reality that readers can verify and contextualise, lending the fictional narrative documentary weight. The riots function in the novel as an eruption of the suppressed anger that runs beneath every page, and de Waal positions them as the collective expression of what Leon experiences privately: the rage of being unseen by the state. The period setting also allows de Waal to indict the specific policy failures of the era without releasing contemporary institutions from analogous responsibilities.
