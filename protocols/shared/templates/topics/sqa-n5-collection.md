**Topic:** Nature and Environment
**Type:** sqa-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

**Question:**
Choose a poem which explores the relationship between humanity and the natural world. By referring to appropriate techniques, show how this relationship is explored.

**Teaching Points:**
Scottish poetry has a distinctive tradition of nature writing that ranges from celebratory to elegiac, and SQA anthology poems frequently use natural imagery to explore human psychology, mortality, and belonging rather than as mere description. The key analytical move is to examine how the natural world is personified, symbolised, or set in structural contrast with human experience, and what that relationship reveals about the poem's deeper argument. Technique analysis in SQA responses must name the technique, quote precisely, and then explain the effect — a three-stage process the mark scheme rewards explicitly. Strong responses will also consider how sound devices, imagery patterns, and structural choices work together to build the poem's central treatment of nature, rather than analysing each technique in isolation.

**Topic:** Conceptual Notes
**Type:** conceptual-notes
**Marks:** 0
**AOs:** none

**Teaching Points:**
The SQA National 5 Scottish Poetry collection contains poems by a range of Scottish poets, and the assessment asks students to select an appropriate poem from the collection in response to a thematic question. The exam rewards close reading, precise quotation, and technique analysis (naming the technique, quoting, explaining effect) alongside a sustained personal response that engages with the poem's central concerns. Context knowledge in SQA National 5 focuses on the Scottish cultural, historical, and linguistic contexts relevant to the poems rather than biographical detail; students should be aware of Scots language features, the Scottish landscape as symbolic resource, and relevant historical periods (the World Wars, industrial decline, Highland clearances) as contextual frames. A well-structured SQA response introduces the poem and poet, develops two to three analytical paragraphs on key techniques, and concludes with a personal evaluative statement about the poem's effectiveness.

**Topic:** War and Conflict
**Type:** sqa-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

**Question:**
Choose a poem which explores the experience or consequences of war and conflict. By referring to appropriate techniques, show how the experience or consequences of war are explored.

**Teaching Points:**
War poetry in the Scottish tradition encompasses both the commemoration of sacrifice and a sharper critique of militarism and its costs, and students should be equipped to distinguish between poems that romanticise conflict and those that interrogate it. Technique analysis for war poetry frequently involves examining the use of contrast — between the heroic rhetoric of recruitment and the physical reality of combat, or between the living and the dead — and students should be able to show how structural and imagistic contrasts generate the poem's argument. Sound devices in war poetry carry particular weight: the rhythm of marching, the dissonance of violence, the silence of absence are all constructed through sound choices the poet has made deliberately. The most sophisticated responses will connect the poem's techniques to its ethical position, arguing that the poet's choices constitute a moral argument about conflict rather than merely depicting it.

**Topic:** Love and Loss
**Type:** sqa-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

**Question:**
Choose a poem which explores the experience of love or loss. By referring to appropriate techniques, show how love or loss is explored.

**Teaching Points:**
Love and loss are among the most technically various themes in poetry because they invite both elevated and colloquial registers, intense personal specificity and universal claim, and the full range of structural possibilities from tightly controlled forms to fractured free verse. Students should be equipped to identify what type of love or loss the poem depicts — romantic, familial, elegiac, unrequited — and to analyse how the poet's technique is calibrated to that specific emotional terrain. Scottish love poetry frequently uses landscape as an objective correlative for emotional states, and students should be alert to how natural imagery externalises internal experience. The mark scheme rewards responses that move between the particular (this word, this image, this structural choice) and the general (what the poem argues about love or loss as human experiences), demonstrating that analysis of individual techniques builds toward a coherent interpretive claim.

**Topic:** Identity and Belonging
**Type:** sqa-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

**Question:**
Choose a poem which explores questions of identity and belonging. By referring to appropriate techniques, show how identity or belonging is explored.

**Teaching Points:**
Identity in Scottish poetry is often inflected by national, linguistic, and class dimensions that are inseparable from personal selfhood, and students should be prepared to analyse how poems negotiate the relationship between individual identity and collective or cultural identity. The use of Scots language or dialect in anthology poems is itself a technique carrying meaning: it asserts a linguistic and cultural belonging, and students should be equipped to discuss what Scots vocabulary and syntax contribute to the poem's construction of identity. Belonging frequently appears as its negative — as exile, displacement, or alienation — and the structural and imagistic choices that convey belonging-as-absence are as important as those that affirm it. Strong responses will avoid treating identity poems as merely autobiographical and instead analyse how the poet uses the personal as a vehicle for broader cultural argument.

**Topic:** Memory and Time
**Type:** sqa-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

**Question:**
Choose a poem which explores the theme of memory or the passage of time. By referring to appropriate techniques, show how memory or time is explored.

**Teaching Points:**
Memory poetry frequently employs tense shifts as a primary technique — the movement between past and present tense enacts the instability of recollection and the speaker's ambivalent relationship to their own history. Students should be alert to how a poem's structure mirrors the operations of memory: the fragmentary, the associative, the intrusive return of suppressed material can all be rendered through structural choices. Scottish poetry often treats memory as both personal and communal, with individual recollection embedded in collective histories (industrial, agricultural, diaspora), and students should be equipped to identify when a poem's treatment of memory extends beyond the personal. The key analytical challenge is to show how technique choices — tense, imagery, structural organisation, sound — work together to construct memory as a dynamic and sometimes unreliable process rather than passive retrieval.

**Topic:** Social Issues
**Type:** sqa-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

**Question:**
Choose a poem which explores a social issue or injustice. By referring to appropriate techniques, show how the social issue is explored.

**Teaching Points:**
Poetry that engages with social issues faces a formal challenge: the risk of polemic, in which argument displaces complexity and the poem becomes a manifesto rather than an exploration. Students should be equipped to analyse how skilled poets manage this tension — using irony, understatement, specific human detail, and formal control to make political arguments without sacrificing poetic complexity. Scottish social poetry often draws on working-class experience, industrial history, and the consequences of economic change, and students should have contextual awareness of the relevant historical and social frames (the decline of heavy industry, housing conditions, unemployment, sectarianism). The most analytically strong responses will show how the poem's technical choices — its selection of imagery, its management of speaker and reader relationship, its structural organisation — enact as well as describe the social critique.

**Topic:** Childhood and Growth
**Type:** sqa-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

**Question:**
Choose a poem which explores the experience of childhood or the process of growing up. By referring to appropriate techniques, show how childhood or growth is explored.

**Teaching Points:**
Childhood poetry in the Scottish tradition often uses the retrospective adult speaker as a structural device, creating an ironic distance between the child's limited perspective at the time and the fuller understanding the adult now possesses — and students should be equipped to analyse how this temporal split generates the poem's central meaning. The use of sensory detail is particularly characteristic of childhood poetry, since memory is sensory before it is conceptual, and precision of imagery in this genre carries analytical weight. Growing up as a theme frequently involves a loss of innocence structured as revelation — the moment of understanding that disrupts a simpler worldview — and students should look for the structural and imagistic pivot around which this shift organises itself. Tone management is crucial in childhood poetry, where sentimentality is the primary risk; students should analyse how a poet avoids or deploys nostalgia and what that choice implies about the poem's argument.

**Topic:** Death and Mortality
**Type:** sqa-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

**Question:**
Choose a poem which explores the theme of death or mortality. By referring to appropriate techniques, show how death or mortality is explored.

**Teaching Points:**
Death and mortality are among poetry's oldest subjects, and a sophisticated response will situate the poem within this tradition — whether it draws on elegy, the memento mori, the carpe diem lyric, or a more modern, secular engagement with mortality — rather than treating the theme as purely personal. Students should analyse how the poet positions the speaker in relation to death: as observer, mourner, the dying, or the already dead, since each position generates a different set of technical possibilities and ethical implications. Scottish poetry's treatment of mortality is often unsentimentalised, using understatement, dark humour, or stark imagery to resist romantic consolation, and students should be equipped to recognise and analyse these tonal choices. The structural organisation of a mortality poem — whether it moves toward acceptance, resistance, or unresolved ambivalence — is itself an argument about how human beings should relate to death, and that argument should be traced through the technical choices the poet makes.

**Topic:** Scottish Identity
**Type:** sqa-poetry
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

**Question:**
Choose a poem which explores what it means to be Scottish or explores aspects of Scottish experience. By referring to appropriate techniques, show how Scottish identity or experience is explored.

**Teaching Points:**
Scottish identity in poetry is not a stable or homogeneous concept but a contested and evolving construction, and students should be prepared to engage with the complexity of how different poets define, celebrate, question, or problematise Scottishness. The use of Scots language is itself a political and cultural statement, asserting the legitimacy of a linguistic tradition that has been historically marginalised, and students should analyse how code-switching between Scots and standard English within a poem creates meaning. The Scottish landscape — highlands, lowlands, coasts, industrial cities — functions in poetry as a symbolic vocabulary that condenses cultural memory and national myth into specific images, and students should be equipped to identify and analyse this symbolic use of place. Strong responses will examine how the poem negotiates the tension between celebrating a distinctive Scottish identity and acknowledging the internal diversity and historical contradictions that complicate any homogeneous national narrative.
