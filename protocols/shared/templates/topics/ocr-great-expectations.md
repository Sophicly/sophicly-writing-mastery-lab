# Topic 2: Conceptual Notes
**Type:** conceptual
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Great Expectations was published in 1861 during a period of rapid social change in Victorian England. Understanding the rigid class system, the transportation of convicts to Australia, the effects of industrialisation on social mobility, and Victorian ideals of the 'gentleman' is essential for interpreting Dickens' critique of aspiration and moral worth.

# Topic 3: Miss Havisham
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Miss Havisham is one of Dickens' most gothic creations — a woman frozen in time by betrayal, who weaponises Estella as her instrument of revenge against men. **Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Dickens uses Pip's social ascent to expose the hollowness of Victorian class distinctions. Students should analyse how the language of class permeates the novel and consider Dickens' broader social argument.

Students should analyse how Dickens presents Magwitch at different points in the novel and consider what his character reveals about Victorian attitudes to crime and punishment.

# Topic 8: Joe Gargery

**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Joe represents the moral standard against which all other characters are measured. Dickens uses Joe to argue that true gentility lies in kindness and integrity rather than wealth or manners. Students should analyse how Joe's dialect and behaviour are contrasted with the speech and conduct of 'gentlemen' in the novel.

# Topic 9: Justice and the Law
**Type:** extract_and_essay
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Dickens presents the Victorian legal system as arbitrary and class-biased. Magwitch is transported for life while Compeyson, his better-spoken accomplice, receives a lighter sentence. Jaggers, the novel's most powerful lawyer, operates through manipulation and moral detachment. Students should analyse how Dickens uses the law to reinforce his argument that the justice system punishes poverty rather than crime, and consider how the theme of justice connects to the novel's broader exploration of guilt and moral responsibility.

Dickens distinguishes between superficial education (learning manners and spending money) and genuine self-improvement (developing moral awareness and humility). Pip's real education comes not from Matthew Pocket or London society but from the hard lessons of loss and shame. Students should analyse how different characters represent different attitudes to learning and growth.
