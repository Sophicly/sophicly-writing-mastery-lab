# Topic 1: Analysing Narrative Voice and Perspective
**Type:** unseen-prose
**Teaching Point:** Narrative voice is the controlling intelligence through which a story is told, shaping what the reader knows, feels, and trusts. Students should identify whether the voice is first-person (intimate, unreliable, limited) or third-person (omniscient, limited, or detached), then analyse how this choice positions the reader in relation to events and characters. Close attention to pronoun use, free indirect discourse, and moments where the narrator intrudes or withholds reveals how the writer controls emotional proximity. Effective analysis connects voice to meaning: what does this perspective allow the writer to show or conceal?
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
Read the passage below carefully. How does the writer use narrative voice and perspective to create meaning and effect?

You should consider:
- the type of narrator and what this allows the writer to reveal or conceal
- how the narrative voice shapes the reader's relationship with characters and events
- specific language choices that reflect or reinforce the narrative perspective.

# Topic 3: Analysing Descriptive Language and Imagery
**Type:** unseen-prose
**Teaching Point:** Descriptive language transforms the literal into the evocative, using imagery to make readers see, hear, and feel the world of the text. Students should identify figurative devices — metaphor, simile, personification, synecdoche — and examine the connotations of specific word choices, considering what sensory or emotional effect they create. The most sophisticated analysis looks beyond naming devices to explore how patterns of imagery accumulate meaning: a cluster of decay imagery, for instance, may foreshadow loss or signal a character's psychological state. Analysis should always connect technique to the writer's broader purpose.
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

You should consider:
- the figurative devices used and the connotations they carry
- how sensory details shape the reader's experience of the scene
- patterns of imagery and what they suggest about character, theme, or mood.

# Topic 4: Analysing Structure and Organisation
**Type:** unseen-prose
**Teaching Point:** Structure is a writer's deliberate arrangement of material across the whole text and within individual sentences and paragraphs. At the macro level, students should consider how the passage opens and closes, how tension or focus shifts across sections, and whether the writer uses non-linear sequencing, flashback, or withholding of information. At the micro level, sentence length, syntax, and paragraph breaks all create rhythm, emphasis, and pace. Students should ask what the structural choices reveal about the writer's priorities: what is foregrounded, what is delayed, and what effect does this ordering have on the reader's understanding and emotional response?
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
Read the passage below carefully. How does the writer use structure and organisation to create meaning and effect?

You should consider:
- how the passage is sequenced and what is foregrounded or delayed
- the effect of sentence structure, length, and variation on pace and emphasis
- how the opening and closing of the passage shape the reader's overall response.

# Topic 5: Analysing Tone and Atmosphere
**Type:** unseen-prose
**Teaching Point:** Tone is the writer's attitude — to characters, to events, to the reader — and it is communicated through word choice, syntax, and the accumulation of detail rather than direct statement. Atmosphere is the emotional environment created for the reader, distinct from the narrator's own stance. Students should trace how both are established and whether they shift across the passage, identifying the specific language choices that sustain or disrupt them. Sophisticated responses recognise tension between tone and content: an ironic or detached tone applied to distressing material creates a different effect from one that is openly emotional, and this gap itself is a source of meaning.
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
Read the passage below carefully. How does the writer use tone and atmosphere to create meaning and effect?

You should consider:
- how the writer establishes an emotional environment at the opening of the passage
- the specific language choices that sustain, intensify, or shift the atmosphere
- any tension between the narrator's tone and the events or subjects being described.

# Topic 6: Analysing Characterisation
**Type:** unseen-prose
**Teaching Point:** Writers reveal character through a combination of direct description, action, dialogue, thought, and the reactions of others — students should be alert to which methods the writer favours and what each technique allows them to show. Indirect characterisation is particularly rich for analysis: what a character notices, the language they use, and what they fail to say can be more revealing than explicit authorial statement. Students should consider how characterisation serves the passage's wider purpose: is the character designed to evoke sympathy, unease, admiration, or moral complexity? The most effective responses trace how characterisation develops or is complicated across the passage rather than treating it as static.
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
Read the passage below carefully. How does the writer use characterisation to create meaning and effect?

You should consider:
- the methods used to present the character and what each reveals
- how the character's speech, thought, or action shapes the reader's response
- how characterisation develops or becomes more complex across the passage.

# Topic 7: Analysing Dialogue and Speech
**Type:** unseen-prose
**Teaching Point:** Dialogue in prose is never merely transcribed conversation — it is a carefully constructed tool for revealing character, advancing narrative, creating tension, and controlling pace. Students should examine how speech is presented (direct, indirect, or free indirect speech), what each mode reveals about the relationship between narrator and character, and how the register, rhythm, and content of speech differentiate characters from one another and from their social context. Subtext is central to effective analysis: what characters avoid saying, interrupt, or contradict often carries more weight than what is stated directly. Students should also consider how dialogue interacts with surrounding narrative description to shape the reader's interpretation.
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
Read the passage below carefully. How does the writer use dialogue and speech to create meaning and effect?

You should consider:
- how speech is presented and what each mode of presentation reveals
- how dialogue distinguishes characters and reflects their relationships and social context
- what is implied or withheld in the dialogue and the effect of this on the reader.

# Topic 8: Analysing Setting and Place
**Type:** unseen-prose
**Teaching Point:** Setting in literary prose is rarely decorative — it operates as a vehicle for theme, a mirror for character psychology, and a mechanism for creating mood. Students should analyse how the writer constructs a sense of place through specific sensory detail, the selection of particular features over others, and the emotional resonance that the described environment carries. The relationship between character and setting is especially productive for analysis: does the character feel at home, alienated, threatened, or transformed by their environment? Students should also consider pathetic fallacy and symbolic landscape, asking how the external world reflects or intensifies the internal states of characters or the text's broader concerns.
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
Read the passage below carefully. How does the writer use setting and place to create meaning and effect?

You should consider:
- the specific details used to construct the setting and the sensory impression they create
- the relationship between the setting and the characters' emotional or psychological states
- any symbolic or thematic significance in the writer's choice of environment.

# Topic 9: Analysing the Writer's Purpose
**Type:** unseen-prose
**Teaching Point:** Every stylistic choice in a literary text is in service of the writer's purpose — the ideas, effects, and responses they intend to produce in the reader. Students should move beyond surface-level technique identification to consider why the writer has made these particular choices and what they are designed to achieve: to create unease, to build empathy, to critique a social reality, to explore a psychological state. Effective analysis of purpose synthesises observations about language, structure, and form, showing how these elements work together rather than in isolation. Students should also be prepared to consider multiple or competing purposes within a single passage, acknowledging that literary texts often resist single-answer readings.
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question

You should consider:
- the ideas or effects you think the writer is most concerned with creating
- how specific language and structural choices work together to achieve these effects

# Topic 10: Synthesising Multiple Techniques
**Type:** unseen-prose
**Teaching Point:** The highest-level unseen prose responses demonstrate the ability to synthesise observations across multiple techniques, showing how language, structure, voice, characterisation, and setting work together in an integrated and purposeful way. Students should practise moving between micro-level analysis (specific word or sentence choices) and macro-level argument (what the passage is doing overall), using each technique they identify as evidence for a larger interpretive claim. Synthesis also means recognising relationships between techniques: how does a shift in sentence rhythm coincide with a change in tone, or how does a particular image pattern reinforce the narrative perspective? This topic prepares students for the full demands of an unseen prose question under timed conditions.
