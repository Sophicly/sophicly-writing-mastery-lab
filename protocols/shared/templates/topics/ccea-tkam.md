# Topic 1: Atticus Finch
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Lee presents Atticus as the novel's moral compass — a figure whose quiet, principled conduct provides a counter-model to Maycomb's entrenched racism and social conformity. Through Scout's admiring but limited child's perspective, Lee ensures the reader sees Atticus's virtues as genuinely lived rather than merely stated, embodied in small daily acts as much as the courtroom. His defence of Tom Robinson is framed not as heroism but as simple professional duty, a rhetorical strategy that makes his courage all the more striking against a community that treats such duty as transgression. Lee also subtly qualifies Atticus through his paternalism and his acceptance of certain social hierarchies, inviting readers to admire rather than idealise him.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does Harper Lee present Atticus Finch in *To Kill a Mockingbird?*

You should consider:
- how Lee establishes Atticus's values and how he demonstrates them throughout the novel
- how Scout's narration shapes the reader's understanding of Atticus
- how Lee uses Atticus to explore ideas about courage, integrity, and the law.

# Topic 3: Scout
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Scout serves as both narrator and protagonist, and Lee uses the tension between her child's perspective and her adult retrospective voice to generate the novel's characteristic ironic distance. As a young girl, Scout observes racial injustice and social cruelty without fully comprehending them, a technique that both implicates the reader (who understands more than Scout) and preserves moral clarity — children see through hypocrisy that adults have learned to rationalise. Scout's gradual education in empathy — most fully expressed in Boo Radley's narrative — enacts the novel's central moral argument. Her position as a girl who resists prescribed femininity also allows Lee to explore gender expectations alongside race and class.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 4: Boo Radley
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Boo Radley functions as the novel's most complex emblem of misunderstood innocence — the neighbourhood's gothic mythology about him, sustained by fear and gossip, mirrors on a small scale the same mechanisms that destroy Tom Robinson. Lee carefully structures his revelation: Boo moves from rumour and shadow to tangible gifts in the knothole, to the unforgettable act of covering Scout with a blanket, to his climactic rescue. The climax's reversal — Boo, the feared outsider, saves the children from the actual danger — enacts the novel's central argument that true threat comes not from outsiders but from within the community. His withdrawal back into the house afterwards gives the encounter a fleeting, dreamlike quality that protects rather than diminishes its emotional force.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 5: Tom Robinson
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Tom Robinson is presented as a man of evident decency and quiet dignity whose destruction by the legal system exposes the depth of Maycomb's racial injustice. Lee is careful to individualise Tom — his one disabled arm, his evident kindness to Mayella, his reasonable courtroom testimony — so that his fate registers as the loss of a specific human being, not merely a symbolic victim. The courtroom scene allows Lee to show how evidence, logic, and truth are rendered irrelevant by racial prejudice: the jury's verdict is a structural argument, not a dramatic surprise. Tom's death while attempting to escape adds a further dimension — in a society where he has no real hope, flight becomes the only rational response.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 6: Racial Injustice
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Lee presents racial injustice not as the aberration of a few bad individuals but as the systemic product of a community's values, embedded in law, social custom, and the daily texture of segregated life. The trial of Tom Robinson is the novel's dramatic focus, but Lee surrounds it with quieter instances — the Black community seated in the gallery, Calpurnia's divided world, the language Maycomb uses casually — that show injustice as structural rather than exceptional. By filtering these observations through Scout's naïve perspective, Lee forces the reader to do the moral work of recognising what the child narrator cannot fully name. The 1930s Alabama setting carries the weight of Jim Crow law, making the injustice both historically grounded and, Lee implies, urgently contemporary.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 7: Moral Courage
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Lee distinguishes repeatedly between physical courage and moral courage, presenting the latter as the rarer and more important quality. Atticus's definition — "it's when you know you're licked before you begin but you begin anyway" — applies not only to his own defence of Tom Robinson but to Mrs Dubose's struggle against morphine addiction and, ultimately, to Boo Radley's intervention. Lee builds a pattern of quietly courageous acts performed without audience or reward, contrasting them with the performative community solidarity that enables injustice. The novel argues that moral courage requires imagination — the capacity to inhabit another's skin — and it is precisely this imaginative empathy that Maycomb's prejudice destroys.

# Topic 8: Growing Up and Innocence
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Lee structures the novel as a coming-of-age narrative in which Scout and Jem's loss of innocence is inseparable from their encounter with moral evil. The mockingbird motif — introduced in relation to both Tom Robinson and Boo Radley — frames innocence as something that must be actively protected by those with moral authority, since the world will destroy it if left unchecked. Jem's loss of faith after the trial verdict is the novel's emotional turning point: the child who believed in fairness confronts a world that systematically enforces unfairness. Lee is careful not to sentimentalise this loss — growing up in Maycomb means learning to live with injustice, a more troubling conclusion than simple disillusionment.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 9: Social Class and Prejudice
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Alongside racial injustice, Lee presents Maycomb as a community rigidly structured by class, in which the Cunninghams, Ewells, and Finches occupy distinct and largely fixed social positions. The Ewells' position at the bottom of the white social hierarchy — above only the Black community in the town's cruel calculus — is essential to understanding Bob Ewell's motivation: his accusation against Tom Robinson is an attempt to reassert status by destroying someone even more vulnerable. Lee uses Scout's puzzled encounters with class snobbery — particularly at school and through Aunt Alexandra's social codes — to expose how class prejudice operates through apparently trivial social rituals. The intersection of race and class is central: the novel shows how both hierarchies reinforce and sustain each other.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 10: Justice and the Law
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Lee presents a profound gap between justice as an ideal and law as it is actually practised in Maycomb — the legal system provides the form of a fair trial while the community's racism ensures an unjust outcome. Atticus's courtroom argument is impeccable by any rational standard, yet Lee shows that legal procedure cannot override social prejudice when the jury embodies that prejudice. The novel complicates this critique, however, through the treatment of Bob Ewell's death: Heck Tate's decision to record it as an accident is itself a circumvention of the law, justified on the grounds of protecting Boo. Lee thus suggests that justice sometimes requires working around the law, a troubling but honest conclusion that refuses simple optimism.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
