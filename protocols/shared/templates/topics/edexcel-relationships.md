# Topic 1: Sonnet 43
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Sonnet 43 by Elizabeth Barrett Browning is a passionate declaration of love written within the Petrarchan sonnet form. Students should analyse how Barrett Browning uses anaphoric repetition of "I love thee," abstract and spiritual imagery, and the structured volta to present love as an all-encompassing force that transcends physical existence. The poem's Victorian context and its place within the Sonnets from the Portuguese sequence add layers of sincerity and biographical resonance.
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO2,AO3
**Focus Poem:** Sonnet 43
**Focus Poet:** Elizabeth Barrett Browning

## Question
Compare how poets present the depth and intensity of love in 'Sonnet 43' and one other poem from the Relationships anthology.

# Topic 3: A Child to His Sick Grandfather
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Amelia Opie's poem presents a child's innocent perspective on ageing and mortality through direct address to a dying grandfather. Students should analyse how Opie uses the child's naive voice, simple diction, and the contrast between the grandfather's past vitality and present frailty to explore how familial love persists even when faced with loss. The late-eighteenth-century sentimental tradition and the poem's ballad-like simplicity are key contextual features.

# Topic 4: She Walks in Beauty
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Byron's poem presents an idealised portrait of a woman whose beauty is defined by the harmonious balance of light and dark. Students should analyse how Byron uses antithesis, sensory imagery, and the controlled rhythm of the anapaestic tetrameter to present admiration that is reverential rather than possessive. The Romantic emphasis on beauty as a reflection of inner goodness and the poem's biographical origins at a London party provide important context.

# Topic 16: Porphyria's Lover
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Robert Browning's dramatic monologue reveals a speaker who murders his lover to preserve a perfect moment of possession. Students should analyse how Browning uses the calm, conversational tone, the pathetic fallacy of the storm, and the disturbing absence of guilt to expose the psychology of obsessive love and control. The Victorian context — anxieties about female independence, the dramatic monologue tradition — and the poem's original pairing with another madness poem are key contextual elements.
