# Topic 1: Sonnet 43 — 'How Do I Love Thee?'
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Barrett Browning's sonnet from Sonnets from the Portuguese is one of the most celebrated love poems in English. Its systematic cataloguing of the ways she loves — "to the depth and breadth and height / My soul can reach" — transforms abstract emotion into something measured and tangible. Students should analyse how Barrett Browning uses the Petrarchan sonnet form, anaphora, and religious imagery to present love as infinite, spiritual, and transcending death.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Focus Poem:** Sonnet 43 — 'How Do I Love Thee?'
**Focus Poet:** Elizabeth Barrett Browning

# Topic 3: Sonnet 116 — 'Let me not to the marriage of true minds'
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Shakespeare defines love by what it is not — it does not alter, bend, or fade. The poem builds its argument through a series of negatives and nautical metaphors, presenting love as a fixed star that guides ships through storms. Students should analyse how Shakespeare uses the logical, almost legalistic structure of the sonnet and the bold final couplet to assert that true love is permanent and unchanging.

# Topic 4: La Belle Dame Sans Merci
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Keats's ballad tells of a knight destroyed by an encounter with a mysterious, beautiful woman who lures him into an enchanted sleep and abandons him. The poem's medieval setting, stark imagery of the "cold hill's side," and the haunting repetition of the opening question create a sense of love as dangerous enchantment. Students should analyse how Keats uses the ballad form, seasonal imagery, and the shift between speakers to present love as both seductive and destructive.

# Topic 5: Neutral Tones
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Hardy strips a failed relationship down to its bleakest elements — a white sun, an ash-covered pond, fallen leaves. The poem's power lies in how its drained, colourless imagery mirrors the emotional exhaustion of a love that has died, and the circular structure traps the speaker in a memory he cannot escape. Students should analyse how Hardy uses pathetic fallacy, monosyllabic diction, and the framing device to present the aftermath of love as a kind of living death.

# Topic 6: A Complaint
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Wordsworth's poem mourns the loss of a deep emotional connection — most likely with Coleridge — comparing the friend's affection to a fountain that once flowed freely but has now dried up. # Topic 7: Nettles
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Scannell uses a simple domestic incident — a child falling into nettles — to explore a father's fierce, protective love and his frustration at being unable to shield his son from pain. The military imagery ("regiment," "fierce parade," "recruits") transforms garden weeds into an enemy army, revealing how the speaker sees threats to his child everywhere. Students should analyse how Scannell uses the extended military metaphor and the final couplet's resignation to present parental love as a battle that can never be won.

# Topic 8: One Flesh
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Jennings observes her ageing parents lying apart in bed, their physical distance a quiet testament to a marriage that has settled into habit rather than passion. The poem's cool, almost clinical tone masks deep emotional disturbance as the speaker struggles to reconcile the "one flesh" of the marriage vow with two people who seem like strangers. Students should analyse how Jennings uses the detached third-person perspective, the religious allusion, and the shift to first person in the final stanza to explore love, distance, and the passage of time.

# Topic 9: Valentine
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Duffy rejects conventional tokens of romance — red roses, satin hearts — and instead offers an onion as a metaphor for love. The poem peels back layers of sentimentality to reveal love as something fierce, honest, and potentially painful: it can blind you with tears, cling to your skin, and leave a lasting mark. Students should analyse how Duffy uses the extended onion metaphor, the imperative tone, and the fragmented final lines to challenge romantic cliches and present a more truthful vision of love.

# Topic 10: Love After Love
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Walcott's poem celebrates the moment of rediscovering yourself after a relationship ends. The imperative voice — "Give back your heart / to itself" — frames self-love as an act of hospitality, welcoming back the stranger you became while loving someone else. Students should analyse how Walcott uses the extended metaphor of greeting yourself at your own door, the ceremonial imagery of feasting and wine, and the warm, reassuring tone to present the end of love not as loss but as liberation and self-renewal.

# Topic 11: Cozy Apologia
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Dove writes a love poem that is deliberately unheroic — her partner is not a knight on a charger but someone who makes her feel safe during a hurricane. The poem contrasts the melodramatic crushes of adolescence with the quiet, domestic comfort of mature love, all set against the approaching storm of Hurricane Floyd. Students should analyse how Dove uses the contrast between romantic fantasy and everyday reality, the humorous tone, and the hurricane as both literal backdrop and metaphor to celebrate love that is ordinary, steady, and resilient.

# Topic 12: Funeral Blues
**Type:** poetry_named
**Teaching Point:** Auden's elegy demands that the entire world stop to acknowledge one person's grief. The escalating hyperbole — silencing clocks, cutting off telephones, dismantling the stars — transforms private mourning into a cosmic event, and the shift from public announcements to intimate memories reveals how the dead lover was the speaker's entire universe. Students should analyse how Auden uses imperatives, hyperbole, and the contrast between the public and private to present grief as total and all-consuming.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
**Focus Poem:** Funeral Blues
**Focus Poet:** W.H. Auden
