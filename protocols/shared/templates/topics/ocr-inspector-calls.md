# Topic 1: Social Responsibility
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** This is the central theme of the play. Priestley uses the Inspector's final speech to argue that society cannot function when individuals refuse to accept responsibility for the welfare of others. The dual format allows comparison with other writers who dramatise the consequences of moral indifference.

## Part A -- Compare Extracts
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**Extract Location:** Act Three
**Extract Context:** In this extract, the Inspector delivers his final speech to the Birling family before leaving.
**Companion Text:** A Man for All Seasons by Robert Bolt
**Companion Context:** In this extract, Sir Thomas More speaks to his daughter Margaret about why he cannot sign the oath and betray his conscience, even though it may cost him his life.

### Extract
INSPECTOR: But just remember this. One Eva Smith has gone -- but there are millions and millions and millions of Eva Smiths and John Smiths still left with us, with their lives, their hopes and fears, their suffering and chance of happiness, all intertwined with our lives and what we think and say and do. We don't live alone. We are members of one body. We are responsible for each other. And I tell you that the time will soon come when, if men will not learn that lesson, then they will be taught it in fire and blood and anguish. Good night.

He walks straight out, leaving them staring, subdued and wondering. SHEILA is still quietly crying. MRS BIRLING has collapsed into a chair. ERIC is brooding desperately. BIRLING, the only active one, hears the front door slam, moves hesitatingly towards the door, stops, looks gloomily at the other three, then pours himself out a drink, which he hastily swallows.

BIRLING: (angrily to ERIC) You're the one I blame for this.

MRS BIRLING: (sharply) I'll tell you what I told him. He certainly didn't make me confess -- as you call it. I told him quite plainly that if they didn't take her back into the works, he couldn't be responsible.

SHEILA: (bitterly) So nothing has happened? So nothing has changed? You really are all so -- so --

### Companion Extract
MORE: If we lived in a State where virtue was profitable, common sense would make us good, and greed would make us saintly. And we'd live like animals or angels in the happy land that needs no heroes. But since in fact we see that avarice, anger, envy, pride, sloth, lust and stupidity commonly profit far beyond humility, chastity, fortitude, justice and thought, and have to choose, to be human at all... why then perhaps we must stand fast a little -- even at the risk of being heroes.

MARGARET: But in reason! Haven't you done as much as God can reasonably want?

MORE: Well... finally... it isn't a matter of reason; finally it's a matter of love.

MARGARET: (with pain) You're going to die. You know that, don't you?

MORE: Yes. I think that is probable.

MARGARET: Then what is the use? What is the point? Father, you are throwing your life away for nothing!

MORE: Margaret, there comes a time when a man holds himself like water in his cupped hands, and if he opens his fingers then -- he needn't hope to find himself again. I can see that. I can feel that. I cannot allow my self to be poured away.

MARGARET: But Father --

MORE: Listen, Meg. God made the angels to show Him splendour -- as He made animals for innocence and plants for their simplicity. But Man He made to serve Him wittily, in the tangle of his mind!

# Topic 3: Class and Social Hierarchy
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Class is the structural backbone of An Inspector Calls. Every character's treatment of Eva maps onto their position in the social hierarchy. Priestley argues that rigid class divisions enable cruelty and indifference, a message sharpened by comparison with other dramatists who examine how the powerful exploit their status.

## Part A -- Compare Extracts
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**Extract Location:** Act One
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Mr Birling explains to Gerald why he sacked Eva Smith from his factory after the workers went on strike for higher wages.
**Companion Text:** The Winslow Boy by Terence Rattigan
**Companion Context:** In this extract, Arthur Winslow, a middle-class father, confronts the establishment's refusal to hear his son's case. He speaks to his barrister Sir Robert Morton about why the case matters.

### Extract
BIRLING: I refused, of course.

INSPECTOR: Why?

BIRLING: (surprised) Did you say "Why?"

INSPECTOR: Yes. Why did you refuse?

BIRLING: Well, Inspector, I don't see that it's any concern of yours how I choose to run my business. Is it now?

INSPECTOR: It might be, you know.

BIRLING: Well, it's my duty to keep labour costs down, and if I'd agreed to this demand for a new rate we'd have added about twelve per cent to our labour costs. Does that satisfy you? So I refused. Said I couldn't consider it. We were paying the usual rates and if they didn't like those rates, they could go and work somewhere else. It's a free country, I told them.

INSPECTOR: And they did?

BIRLING: Yes. After a couple of weeks they tried to come back, all but one or two of them. We let them come back -- at the old rates.

INSPECTOR: All except Eva Smith?

BIRLING: (with some impatience) Yes, yes. She'd had a lot to say -- far too much -- so she had to go.

### Companion Extract
ARTHUR: Let me get this clear, Sir Robert. Are you suggesting that because my son is not the son of a duke or an earl, his case is not worth fighting?

SIR ROBERT: Not at all. I am suggesting that the Admiralty takes the position that it is not required to answer to anybody for the discipline of its cadets.

ARTHUR: And you agree with that position?

SIR ROBERT: Legally, yes. The boy was Crown property. The Crown can do as it pleases with its own.

ARTHUR: (quietly) Crown property. My son. A boy of thirteen. Crown property. Is that really what the law says?

SIR ROBERT: I'm afraid it is.

ARTHUR: Then the law must be changed. I will not accept that my son -- that any boy -- can be expelled without trial, without evidence, without even being told the charge against him. That is not justice. That is tyranny dressed in the King's uniform.

SIR ROBERT: You understand that if we proceed, it will cost you a great deal -- financially, socially, perhaps everything?

ARTHUR: I understand that perfectly, Sir Robert. But if a man cannot stand up for his own child against the state, then what exactly is he for?

# Topic 4: Generational Conflict
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The division between the older Birlings (who refuse to change) and the younger generation (Sheila and Eric, who accept guilt) is central to Priestley's message. He wrote in 1945 for an audience rebuilding after war, arguing that the young must not repeat the moral failures of their parents.

# Topic 5: Guilt
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Guilt functions differently for each character in the play. Sheila and Eric accept their guilt and are changed by it, while Mr and Mrs Birling resist it entirely. Priestley suggests that the capacity to feel guilt is the first step toward moral growth, and that those who suppress it are the most dangerous.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**Extract Location:** Act One
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Sheila has just been shown a photograph of Eva Smith by the Inspector. She has recognised the girl she had sacked from Milwards and is overcome with guilt.
**Companion Text:** Macbeth by William Shakespeare
**Companion Context:** In this extract, Lady Macbeth sleepwalks, haunted by guilt over the murders she helped to plan. A Doctor and Gentlewoman watch as she tries to wash imaginary blood from her hands.

### Extract

INSPECTOR: No one person is entirely responsible.

SHEILA: But I've just told you. I went to the manager at Milwards and I told him that if they didn't get rid of that girl, I'd never go near the place again and I'd persuade mother to close our account with them.

INSPECTOR: And why did you do that?

SHEILA: Because I was in a furious temper.

INSPECTOR: And what had this girl done to make you so angry?

SHEILA: When I was looking at myself in the mirror I caught her smiling at the assistant, and I was furious with her. I went to the manager and told him this girl had been very impertinent -- and -- and -- (She almost breaks down, but just controls herself.) How could I know what would happen afterwards? If she'd been some miserable plain little creature, I don't suppose I'd have done it. But she was very pretty and looked as if she could take care of herself. I couldn't be sorry for her.

INSPECTOR: In fact, in a kind of way, you might be said to have been jealous of her.

SHEILA: Yes, I suppose so.

# Topic 6: The Inspector's Power
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The Inspector dominates the stage through his moral authority, his control of information, and his systematic interrogation technique. His power is not physical or social but ethical -- he forces each character to confront truths they have suppressed. His ambiguous nature (real policeman, ghost, or Priestley's mouthpiece) amplifies his dramatic impact.

## Part A -- Compare Extracts
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**Extract Location:** Act One
**Extract Context:** In this extract, the Inspector has just arrived at the Birling household and begins to take control of the conversation despite Mr Birling's attempts to intimidate him.
**Companion Text:** The Crucible by Arthur Miller
**Companion Context:** In this extract, Deputy Governor Danforth presides over the Salem witch trials. He interrogates Mary Warren, who has come to the court to tell the truth, demonstrating the power of an authority figure to control others through questioning.

### Extract
INSPECTOR: (cutting through, massively) I've been round to the room she had, and she'd left a letter there and a sort of diary. Like a lot of these young women who get into various kinds of trouble, she'd used more than one name. But her original name -- her real name -- was Eva Smith.

BIRLING: (thoughtfully) Eva Smith?

INSPECTOR: Do you remember her, Mr Birling?

BIRLING: (slowly) No -- I seem to remember hearing that name -- Eva Smith -- somewhere. But it doesn't convey anything to me. And I don't see where I come into this.

INSPECTOR: She was employed in your works at one time.

BIRLING: Oh -- that's it, is it? Well, we've several hundred young women there, y'know, and they keep changing.

INSPECTOR: This young woman, Eva Smith, was a bit out of the ordinary. I found a photograph of her in her lodgings. Perhaps you'd remember her from this photograph.

He takes a photograph, about postcard size, out of his pocket and goes to BIRLING. Both GERALD and ERIC rise to have a look at the photograph, but the INSPECTOR interposes himself between them and the photograph. They are surprised and rather annoyed. BIRLING stares hard, and with recognition.

BIRLING: (after a pause) She was one of my employees and then I discharged her.

### Companion Extract
DANFORTH: Now, children, this is a court of law. The law, based upon the Bible, and the Bible, writ by Almighty God, forbid the practice of witchcraft, and describe death as the penalty thereof. Now, children, your justice would have you go quietly and confess to traffic with the Old Boy, and we will let you live.

MARY WARREN: (weeping) I cannot lie no more. I am with God, I am with God.

DANFORTH: (contained) How were you instructed in your life? Do you not know that God damns all liars?

MARY WARREN: I cannot lie no more!

DANFORTH: You have seen the Devil, you have made compact with Lucifer, have you not?

PROCTOR: God damns liars, Mr Danforth!

DANFORTH: (pounding his fist on the table) You will keep your seat!

PROCTOR: (leaping up) Mary, tell the Governor what they --

DANFORTH: (his face reddening) Sit you down! Now Mr Proctor, your old friendship would have you speak for her. But I tell you now -- I have seen marvels in this court. I have seen people choked before my eyes by spirits. I have until this moment not the slightest reason to suspect that the children may be deceiving me. Do you understand my meaning?

# Topic 9: Capitalism
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Priestley wrote An Inspector Calls in 1945, at a time when Britain was debating its post-war future. Mr Birling represents the capitalist worldview -- profit over people, individual over collective -- and Priestley systematically dismantles this ideology through the Inspector's investigation. The play is a direct argument for socialism and the welfare state.

## Part A -- Compare Extracts
**Marks:** 20
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**Extract Location:** Act One
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Mr Birling is making confident predictions about the future to Gerald and Eric before the Inspector arrives. He dismisses the idea of community and promotes a philosophy of self-interest.
**Companion Text:** The Ragged Trousered Philanthropists by Robert Tressell (adapted for stage by Stephen Lowe)
**Companion Context:** In this extract, Frank Owen, a socialist painter-decorator, tries to explain to his fellow workers why they remain poor despite working hard. He uses the "Great Money Trick" to demonstrate how capitalism exploits labour.

And look at the way the auto-mobile's making headway -- bigger and faster all the time. And then ships. Why, a friend of mine went over this new liner last week -- the Titanic -- she sails next week -- forty-six thousand eight hundred tons -- forty-six thousand eight hundred tons -- New York in five days -- and every luxury -- and unsinkable, absolutely unsinkable. # Topic 10: Truth and Lies
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The entire structure of An Inspector Calls is built on the tension between truth and concealment. Each character has a secret they are forced to reveal, and the play's final twist -- the phone call announcing that a real inspector is on his way -- suggests that truth cannot be suppressed permanently. Priestley argues that a society built on lies and denial is doomed to repeat its failures.

## Part A -- Compare Extracts
