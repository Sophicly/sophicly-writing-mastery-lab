# Topic 1: Jekyll and Hyde / Duality
**Type:** central_theme
**Teaching Point:** The dual nature of man is Stevenson's central thesis. Students should explore how Jekyll's experiment literalises the Victorian idea that every person contains both good and evil, and how Stevenson uses the Gothic to question whether these halves can ever be separated.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

## Extract
**Extract Location:** Chapter 10: Henry Jekyll's Full Statement of the Case

> 1  With every day, and from both sides of my intelligence, the moral
> 2  and the intellectual, I thus drew steadily nearer to that truth, by
> 3  whose partial discovery I have been doomed to such a dreadful
> 4  shipwreck: that man is not truly one, but truly two. I say two,
> 5  because the state of my own knowledge does not pass beyond that
> 6  point. Others will follow, others will outstrip me on the same
> 7  lines; and I hazard the guess that man will be ultimately known for
> 8  a mere polity of multifarious, incongruous, and independent
> 9  denizens. I, for my part, from the nature of my life, advanced
> 10 infallibly in one direction, and in one direction only. It was on
> 11 the moral side, and in my own person, that I learned to recognise
> 12 the thorough and primitive duality of man; I saw that, of the two
> 13 natures that contended in the field of my consciousness, if I could
> 14 rightly be said to be either, it was only because I was radically
> 15 both; and from an early date, even before the course of my
> 16 scientific discoveries had begun to suggest the most naked
> 17 possibility of such a miracle, I had learned to dwell with pleasure,
> 18 as a beloved daydream, on the thought of the separation of these
> 19 elements. If each, I told myself, could but be housed in separate
> 20 identities, life would be relieved of all that was unbearable; the
> 21 unjust might go his way, delivered from the aspirations and remorse
> 22 of his more upright twin; and the just could walk steadfastly and
> 23 securely on his upward path, doing the good things in which he
> 24 found his pleasure, and no longer exposed to disgrace and penitence
> 25 by the hands of this extraneous evil.

# Topic 4: Jekyll — Repression and Respectability
**Type:** theme_and_character
**Teaching Point:** Jekyll is a respected doctor who suppresses his desires to maintain his public reputation. Students should explore how Stevenson uses Jekyll to critique Victorian respectability — the idea that outward propriety concealed private vice — and how this repression ultimately causes his destruction.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

## Extract
**Extract Location:** Chapter 10: Henry Jekyll's Full Statement of the Case

> 1  I was born in the year 18— to a large fortune, endowed besides
> 2  with excellent parts, inclined by nature to industry, fond of the
> 3  respect of the wise and good among my fellow-men, and thus, as
> 4  might have been supposed, with every guarantee of an honourable and
> 5  distinguished future. And indeed, the worst of my faults was a
> 6  certain impatient gaiety of disposition, such as has made the
> 7  happiness of many, but such as I found it hard to reconcile with my
> 8  imperious desire to carry my head high, and wear a more than
> 9  commonly grave countenance before the public. Hence it came about
> 10 that I concealed my pleasures; and that when I reached years of
> 11 reflection, and began to look round me, and take stock of my
> 12 progress and position in the world, I stood already committed to a
> 13 profound duplicity of life. Many a man would have even blazoned
> 14 such irregularities as I was guilty of; but from the high views
> 15 that I had set before me, I regarded and hid them with an almost
> 16 morbid sense of shame. It was thus rather the exacting nature of my
> 17 aspirations, than any particular degradation in my faults, that
> 18 made me what I was and, with even a deeper trench than in the
> 19 majority of men, severed in me those provinces of good and ill
> 20 which divide and compound man's dual nature.

# Topic 5: Utterson — Rationality and the Victorian Gentleman
**Type:** supporting_character
**Teaching Point:** Utterson is the novel's rational lens — a lawyer who tries to explain the inexplicable through logic and law. Students should analyse how Stevenson uses Utterson's limited perspective to build suspense, and how his character represents the Victorian ideal of the composed, duty-bound gentleman.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

## Extract
**Extract Location:** Chapter 1: Story of the Door

> 1  Mr. Utterson the lawyer was a man of a rugged countenance, that was
> 2  never lighted by a smile; cold, scanty and embarrassed in
> 3  discourse; backward in sentiment; lean, long, dusty, dreary, and
> 4  yet somehow lovable. At friendly meetings, and when the wine was to
> 5  his taste, something eminently human beaconed from his eye;
> 6  something indeed which never found its way into his talk, but which
> 7  spoke not only in these silent symbols of the after-dinner face, but
> 8  more often and loudly in the acts of his life. He was austere with
> 9  himself; drank gin when he was alone, to mortify a taste for
> 10 vintages; and though he enjoyed the theatre, had not crossed the
> 11 doors of one for twenty years. But he had an approved tolerance for
> 12 others; sometimes wondering, almost with envy, at the high pressure
> 13 of spirits involved in their misdeeds; and in any extremity inclined
> 14 to help rather than to reprove. "I incline to Cain's heresy," he
> 15 used to say quaintly: "I let my brother go to the devil in his own
> 16 way." In this character, it was frequently his fortune to be the
> 17 last reputable acquaintance and the last good influence in the lives
> 18 of down-going men. And to such as these, so long as they came to
> 19 his chambers, he never marked a shade of change in his demeanour.

**Type:** supporting_theme
**Teaching Point:** Jekyll's experiment crosses the boundary between legitimate science and blasphemous transgression. Students should explore how Stevenson reflects late-Victorian anxieties about scientific progress — including Darwinism and vivisection — and how other characters view Jekyll's work as heresy against both professional respectability and divine order.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

## Extract
**Extract Location:** Chapter 9: Dr Lanyon's Narrative

> 1  He thanked me with a smiling nod, measured out a few minims of the
> 2  red tincture and added one of the powders. The mixture, which was
> 3  at first of a reddish hue, began, in proportion as the crystals
> 4  melted, to brighten in colour, to effervesce audibly, and to throw
> 5  off small fumes of vapour. Suddenly, and at the same moment, the
> 6  ebullition ceased, and the compound changed to a dark purple, which
> 7  faded again more slowly to a watery green. My visitor, who had
> 8  watched these metamorphoses with a keen eye, smiled, set down the

> 11
> 12 "And now," said he, "to settle what remains. Will you be wise?
> 13 will you be guided? will you suffer me to take this glass in my
> 14 hand, and to go forth from your house without further parley? or

# Topic 9: Lanyon — The Horror of Knowledge
**Type:** supporting_character
**Teaching Point:** Dr Lanyon represents orthodox science and rational certainty — until witnessing Hyde's transformation destroys him. Students should explore how Stevenson uses Lanyon's decline and death to warn that some knowledge is too terrible to bear, and to dramatise the conflict between old and new science.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

**Extract Location:** Chapter 9: Dr Lanyon's Narrative

> 2  followed; he reeled, staggered, clutched at the table and held on,
> 3  staring with injected eyes, gasping with open mouth; and as I
> 4  looked, there came, I thought, a change — he seemed to swell — his
> 5  face became suddenly black, and the features seemed to melt and
> 6  alter — and the next moment I had sprung to my feet and leaped
> 7  back against the wall, my arm raised to shield me from that
> 8  prodigy, my mind submerged in terror.
> 9
> 10 "O God!" I screamed, and "O God!" again and again; for there
> 11 before my eyes — pale and shaken, and half fainting, and groping

> 14
> 15 What he told me in the next hour I cannot bring my mind to set on
> 16 paper. I saw what I saw, I heard what I heard, and my soul sickened
> 17 at it; and yet, now when that sight has faded from my eyes, I ask

# Topic 10: Victorian Society and Hypocrisy
**Type:** supporting_theme
**Teaching Point:** The novella is set firmly in the world of professional Victorian gentlemen — lawyers, doctors, MPs — whose lives depend on maintaining appearances. Students should explore how Stevenson critiques the hypocrisy of a society that demands moral perfection in public while tacitly permitting vice in private.
**Marks:** 30
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3

## Extract
**Extract Location:** Chapter 10: Henry Jekyll's Full Statement of the Case

> 1  I found it hard to reconcile with my imperious desire to carry my
> 2  head high, and wear a more than commonly grave countenance before
> 3  the public. Hence it came about that I concealed my pleasures; and
> 4  that when I reached years of reflection, and began to look round
> 5  me, and take stock of my progress and position in the world, I
> 6  stood already committed to a profound duplicity of life.
> 7
> 8  The drug had no discriminating action; it was neither diabolical nor
> 9  divine; it but shook the doors of the prison-house of my
> 10 disposition; and, like the captives of Domodossola, that which stood
> 11 within ran forth. At that time my virtue slumbered; my evil, kept
> 12 awake by ambition, was alert and swift to seize the occasion; and
> 13 the thing that was projected was Edward Hyde. Hence, although I had
> 14 now two characters as well as two appearances, one was wholly evil,
> 15 and the other was still the old Henry Jekyll, that incongruous
> 16 compound of whose reformation and improvement I had already learned
> 17 to despair. The movement was thus wholly toward the worse.
> 18
> 19 I was the first that could thus plod in the public eye with a load
> 20 of genial respectability, and in a moment, like a schoolboy, strip
> 21 off these lendings and spring headlong into the sea of liberty. But

> 23 of it — I did not even exist! Let me but escape into my laboratory
> 24 door, give me but a second or two to mix and swallow the draught
> 25 that I had always standing ready; and, whatever he had done, Edward
> 26 Hyde would pass away like the stain of breath upon a mirror; and
> 27 there in his stead, quietly at home, trimming the midnight lamp in
> 28 his study, a man who could afford to laugh at suspicion, would be
> 29 Henry Jekyll.
