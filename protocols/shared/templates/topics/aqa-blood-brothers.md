# Topic 1: Mickey
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Mickey is the protagonist whose arc tracks the destruction caused by class inequality. Students should trace his journey from the joyful, carefree child of Act One through unemployment, depression, and imprisonment to his final desperate confrontation with Eddie, analysing how Russell uses Mickey to argue that the class system traps working-class individuals in cycles of deprivation regardless of their potential.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

## Question
How does Russell use the character of Mickey to explore ideas about class and lost innocence in *Blood Brothers*?

Write about:
- what Mickey says and does
- how Russell presents Mickey.

# Topic 5: Social Class and Inequality
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Class inequality is the structural backbone of the play. Russell uses the separated twins as a controlled experiment: same genetics, different class environments, opposite outcomes. Students should analyse how Russell employs parallel scenes, contrasting settings, and the boys' diverging language registers to build a sustained argument that British society punishes the working class and rewards the privileged from birth.

# Topic 7: Fate and Superstition
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** The Narrator's repeated warnings and Mrs Johnstone's superstitious beliefs create a sense of inevitable tragedy that pervades the play. Students should analyse how Russell uses dramatic irony, the motif of the shoes on the table, and the folk-tale framing to raise the central question: are the twins destroyed by fate or by the class system? Russell's staging suggests it is society, not superstition, that seals their doom.

# Topic 10: Nature vs Nurture
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** The twins are Russell's dramatic experiment in nature versus nurture. Genetically identical, they are raised in opposite class environments and become fundamentally different people. Students should analyse how Russell uses the parallel structure of the play to argue that nurture — meaning class, wealth, education, and opportunity — determines a person's fate far more than nature, making the play a powerful critique of social determinism.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

## Question
How does Russell use the twins Mickey and Eddie to explore the theme of nature versus nurture in *Blood Brothers*?

Write about:
- how Mickey and Eddie develop differently throughout the play
- how Russell presents ideas about nature versus nurture.
