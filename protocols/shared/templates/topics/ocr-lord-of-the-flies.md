# Topic 1: Ralph
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Ralph represents the liberal democratic ideal — leadership through consent, rule of law, and collective responsibility — and Golding tests this ideal to destruction on the island. Students should analyse how Ralph's authority is constitutionally grounded in the conch but is ultimately powerless against the appeal of Jack's visceral, immediate leadership. Ralph's gradual psychological disintegration under fear and isolation is as important as his structural role — Golding presents him as neither saint nor coward but as an ordinary boy discovering the limits of civilised values under extreme pressure. Students should consider how Golding uses Ralph's internal monologue to chart the erosion of rational thought.

# Topic 3: Jack
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Jack Merridew is Golding's most psychologically complex creation — a figure in whom the desire for dominance, violence, and worship are intertwined from the novel's earliest pages. Students should trace Jack's evolution from choirmaster to tribal chief, noting how the mask of face-paint functions not as disguise but as liberation — releasing impulses that were always present. The choir's military formation in Chapter 1 is a crucial detail: Jack's capacity for organised, hierarchical control exists from the start and is redirected rather than invented. Students should consider how Golding's prose style shifts when focussed on Jack — more visceral, sensory, and rhythmic — to embody the irrational pull of his leadership.

# Topic 4: Piggy
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Piggy is simultaneously Lord of the Flies' most intellectually capable character and its most socially vulnerable — Golding uses him to explore how class, appearance, and disability determine whose voice is heard in any community. Students should analyse the bitter irony that Piggy consistently identifies the correct course of action while being systematically ignored, his ideas appropriated by Ralph without attribution. The spectacles as symbol require close attention: they represent scientific rationalism and are progressively stolen, cracked, and destroyed in exact correspondence with the boys' retreat from reason. Piggy's death is Golding's thesis statement — the conch shatters simultaneously, confirming that rational democracy and its most faithful advocate are inseparable.

# Topic 5: Simon
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Simon is Golding's most deliberately Christ-like figure — a visionary, an epileptic, a solitary who communes with nature and whose murder by the group is the novel's most theologically charged event. Students should analyse how Golding presents Simon as the only boy who intuitively grasps that the beast is not external but internal — 'mankind's essential illness' — and trace how this insight is rendered through the novel's most experimental prose in the Lord of the Flies scene. Simon's death in the ecstatic dance is essential: Golding presents it as a ritual sacrifice that implicates even the 'good' boys, Ralph and Piggy. Students should consider how Golding uses Simon to test the Christian idea of redemptive sacrifice within a secular, pessimistic framework.

# Topic 6: Civilisation vs Savagery
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The opposition between civilisation and savagery is Lord of the Flies' structural spine, but Golding complicates any simple binary by suggesting civilisation is a learned performance rather than a natural state. Students should examine the novel's symbolic architecture — conch versus spear, signal fire versus hunting fire, shelters versus Castle Rock — as a landscape of competing values. The naval officer's arrival is Golding's most ironic stroke: he represents 'civilised' society yet arrives at the conclusion of a world war fought with weapons far more destructive than anything the boys constructed. Students should resist reading the novel as merely pessimistic and instead consider what Golding is arguing about the preconditions for civilised behaviour.

# Topic 7: Power and Leadership
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Golding presents two fundamentally different models of leadership in Ralph and Jack — rational-democratic versus charismatic-authoritarian — and traces the progressive dominance of the latter. Students should analyse the specific mechanisms by which Jack's power grows: the provision of meat satisfying immediate physical desires, the tribal ritual creating collective identity and belonging, and the manipulation of fear through the beast. Ralph's failure is not a failure of intelligence or character but of the gap between what ordered society requires and what frightened individuals in crisis actually choose. Students should consider how Golding's novel functions as a direct response to R.M. Ballantyne's optimistic Victorian adventure The Coral Island.

# Topic 8: Fear and the Beast
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The beast is Lord of the Flies' most sophisticated symbolic device — Golding presents it simultaneously as a literal fear, a social construction, a psychological projection, and ultimately a theological statement about human nature. Students should trace how the beast evolves through the novel: from the littluns' nightmares, to the dead parachutist, to the Lord of the Flies as internal voice. The crucial distinction Golding makes is between Ralph's fear, which he consciously manages to maintain rational behaviour, and Jack's fear, which is weaponised and projected outward to sustain tribal cohesion. Students should consider how the manipulation of collective fear through the beast parallels propaganda techniques in totalitarian political systems.

# Topic 9: Loss of Innocence
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Lord of the Flies is fundamentally a novel about the end of childhood — not as a gentle rite of passage but as a violent and irrecoverable rupture. Students should examine how Golding codes innocence in specific symbols and images: the platform and lagoon of Chapter 1, the mulberry birthmark of the boy who disappears in the first fire, the 'candle-bud' flowers Simon tends. The gradual contamination of these images tracks the loss of innocence precisely. Ralph's final weeping is the novel's emotional resolution — he is not crying for survival but for the 'end of innocence, the darkness of man's heart', suggesting that what has been lost is a way of understanding the world that can never be recovered.

# Topic 10: Violence and Morality
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Golding presents violence not as aberration but as a latent capacity in all human beings that civilised structures suppress rather than eliminate. Students should trace the novel's escalating violence — the first failed pig hunt, the successful kill and reenactment, Simon's murder, Piggy's death, the manhunt for Ralph — noting how each stage involves more boys and less hesitation. The moral question Golding forces is whether Ralph and Piggy's participation in Simon's death makes them culpable, and what this implies about collective guilt. Students should consider Golding's post-war context: he served in the Royal Navy during the Second World War and explicitly stated that the Holocaust destroyed his faith in natural human goodness.
