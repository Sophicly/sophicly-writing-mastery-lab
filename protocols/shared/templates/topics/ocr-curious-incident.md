# Topic 1: Christopher
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Christopher John Francis Boone is one of contemporary fiction's most original narrators — a boy whose logical, systematic worldview simultaneously illuminates and estranges the familiar world of suburban England. Students should analyse how Haddon uses Christopher's narrative voice to generate both comedy and pathos: the same literalism that produces Christopher's brilliant deductions also makes him unable to detect the emotional undercurrents that the reader can clearly see. Christopher's narration enacts neurodivergence rather than merely describing it — readers experience the world through his filters and therefore must do their own interpretive work. Students should consider how Haddon uses Christopher to challenge the reader's assumptions about what counts as intelligence, emotional competence, and a reliable narrator.

# Topic 3: Ed Boone
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Ed Boone is the novel's most complex adult figure — a man of genuine love and genuine deception, whose lie about Judy is born of desperate, protective impulse but whose failure to confess is a sustained moral failure. Students should trace the arc of Christopher's relationship with his father: the early chapters establish Ed as a devoted, painstaking carer, making the revelation of the lie all the more devastating for both Christopher and the reader. Ed's inability to cope emotionally — his grief, his loneliness, his temper — is presented with compassion rather than condemnation. Students should consider how Haddon uses Ed to explore the limits of love: that loving someone completely does not guarantee doing what is best for them.

# Topic 4: Judy Boone
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Judy Boone is the novel's most marginal major character — present mainly through her letters, absent through most of the narrative — yet her absence is itself a structural and thematic event. Students should analyse how Haddon presents Judy's abandonment sympathetically by giving her an interior voice in her letters that Ed and Christopher never receive in person: she writes honestly about her inadequacy, her panic, and her love. The revelation that she is alive and has been writing letters that Ed suppressed is the novel's central mystery and moral crisis. Students should consider how Judy's situation reflects the real impossibility of her position: she is criticised both for leaving and, implicitly, for not being the right kind of mother for Christopher's particular needs.

# Topic 5: Truth and Lies
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Truth and lies are simultaneously the novel's plot engine and its deepest thematic concern — Christopher begins the novel investigating a lie (Wellington's murder) without knowing that a far more consequential lie about his mother sits at the centre of his life. Students should examine Christopher's absolute, principled commitment to truth — he lists lying as one of the things he does not do — and consider the painful irony that this same commitment is what makes his father's lie so devastating: Christopher cannot accommodate the moral complexity of a loving person choosing to deceive. The novel implicitly questions whether absolute honesty is itself a form of ethical naivety, or whether it represents a clarity that the other characters should aspire to.

# Topic 6: Difference and Understanding
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The Curious Incident invites its readers to inhabit a consciousness that is rigorously different from the neurotypical norm, and in doing so challenges the assumption that 'understanding' others requires sameness. Students should examine how Haddon ensures that Christopher's perspective generates genuine insight — his mathematical proofs, his logic puzzles, his analysis of social conventions — while also making clear where his perspective limits his ability to function in a world designed by and for neurotypical people. The adults around Christopher (his teachers, the police, strangers on the train) range from compassionate to baffled to threatening — mapping the range of responses to neurodivergence. Students should consider whether the novel argues for acceptance of difference or for the adaptation of the world to accommodate it.

# Topic 7: Independence and Growth
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** The Curious Incident has the structural shape of a bildungsroman, but its arc of growth operates differently from most coming-of-age novels: Christopher's development is not a softening of his differences but an expansion of what he can achieve despite them and because of them. Students should trace Christopher's journey from the sheltered, controlled world of Swindon to the chaos of London and his mother's flat — each new environment forcing an expansion of capability. The A-level mathematics question that closes the novel is Christopher's statement of self: his intelligence and determination are sufficient, the world's expectations are not his ceiling. Students should consider how Haddon uses the question of what constitutes achievement to challenge readers' assumptions about what a successful life looks like.

# Topic 8: Logic vs Emotion
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Christopher's absolute reliance on logic and mathematical reasoning is presented by Haddon both as a genuine intellectual strength and as a limitation — it enables extraordinary feats of deduction while making emotional experience a foreign country. Students should examine the specific moments where Christopher's logical framework encounters experiences that cannot be processed through it: his father's confession, his mother's abandonment, Wellington's death as a trigger for grief. The novel implicitly argues that logic and emotion are not opposites but different registers of intelligence, and that Christopher's difficulties lie not in lacking feeling but in lacking the framework to process and communicate it. Students should consider how Haddon uses Christopher's appendix on mathematics (the problem about the Monty Hall paradox) to show that logical thinking can reach truths that intuitive thinking cannot.

# Topic 9: Trust and Relationships
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Trust in The Curious Incident is structurally fragile: Christopher's ability to trust is hard-won and dependent on consistency and honesty, making Ed's deception not merely a betrayal but a destabilisation of Christopher's entire world-model. Students should trace the architecture of trust in the novel — Christopher's relationship with Siobhan, his tentative trust in his mother, his broken trust with Ed, and his eventual partial reconciliation — noting how each relationship is defined by the conditions under which Christopher can engage with it. The novel raises broader questions about what trust requires: whether the good intentions of those who deceive us mitigate their betrayal, and whether rebuilt trust can ever be the same as original trust.

# Topic 10: Bravery and Fear
**Type:** B-MT
**Format:** dual
**Teaching Point:** Bravery in The Curious Incident must be understood in Christopher's terms, not conventional heroic ones: leaving the house alone is, for Christopher, as daunting as combat. Students should examine how Haddon recalibrates the reader's understanding of courage by making visible the extreme effort required for Christopher to perform actions that others take for granted — boarding a train, asking a stranger for help, walking through crowds. This reframing of bravery is the novel's most quietly radical argument: that the heroism of ordinary life is distributed unequally according to the particular shape of each person's fears. Students should also note that Christopher's bravery is inseparable from his logical determination — he does not overcome fear through emotional boldness but through systematic planning.
