# Topic 1: Jo
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Jo is the protagonist whose journey through the play exposes the realities of working-class life in 1950s Salford. Students should analyse how Delaney presents Jo as simultaneously vulnerable and fiercely independent, tracing her attempts to forge her own identity despite maternal neglect, poverty, and social prejudice, and how her teenage pregnancy forces a confrontation between her desire for independence and the inescapable cycle of her upbringing.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

## Question
How does Delaney use the character of Jo to explore ideas about youth and independence in *A Taste of Honey*?

Write about:
- what Jo says and does
- how Delaney presents Jo.

# Topic 3: Helen
**Type:** modern-text

# Topic 4: Class and Poverty
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Class and poverty form the structural backdrop of the entire play. Delaney uses the cramped, dingy flat in Salford as both a literal and symbolic space to show how poverty limits choices, traps women in cycles of dependence, and shapes every relationship in the play. Students should analyse how Delaney presents working-class life without sentimentality, using naturalistic dialogue and staging to critique a society that offers people like Jo and Helen no route to escape.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

# Topic 5: Race and Prejudice
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Jo's relationship with the Boy -- a Black sailor -- was groundbreaking for 1958 British theatre. Students should analyse how Delaney presents their relationship as tender and genuine while exposing the racial prejudice of the society around them, particularly through Helen's reaction to the pregnancy. Delaney uses the interracial relationship to challenge the audience's assumptions and to argue that love and human connection transcend racial boundaries even when society refuses to accept them.

# Topic 6: Geof
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Geof is Delaney's most compassionate character and the only person who offers Jo genuine, selfless care. Students should analyse how Delaney presents Geof as an outsider -- homosexual, gentle, and domestic in a world that expects men to be none of those things -- and how his eventual displacement by Helen's return critiques a society that rejects kindness when it comes from someone who does not conform to its expectations.

# Topic 7: Gender and Independence
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Delaney wrote A Taste of Honey at eighteen, and the play is a radical statement about women's independence in a patriarchal society. Students should analyse how Delaney presents Jo's refusal to follow her mother's path of dependence on men, how Helen's choices reveal the limited options available to working-class women, and how the play as a whole challenges 1950s assumptions about women's roles by showing female characters who are flawed, complex, and determined to survive on their own terms.

# Topic 8: Peter
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Peter represents the worst of patriarchal masculinity in the play. # Topic 9: Loneliness and Belonging
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Every character in A Taste of Honey is searching for belonging and failing to find it through conventional means. Students should analyse how Delaney presents loneliness as a universal condition that cuts across age, race, gender, and sexuality, and how the temporary households Jo forms -- first with the Boy, then with Geof -- offer fleeting moments of connection that society ultimately destroys, suggesting that true belonging requires acceptance that 1950s Britain was not prepared to offer.

# Topic 10: Motherhood and Responsibility
**Type:** modern-text
**Teaching Point:** Motherhood is the play's most complex and subversive theme. Delaney presents motherhood not as a natural instinct but as a learned behaviour shaped by circumstance -- Helen is a neglectful mother, Jo fears becoming one, and Geof proves more maternal than either woman. Students should analyse how Delaney uses these unconventional portrayals to challenge the 1950s idealisation of motherhood and to ask whether responsibility is defined by biology or by the choice to care for another person.
**Marks:** 34
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3
**AO4:** 4

## Question
How does Delaney explore the theme of motherhood and responsibility in *A Taste of Honey*?

Write about:
- how different characters experience and respond to the idea of motherhood
- how Delaney presents ideas about motherhood and responsibility.
