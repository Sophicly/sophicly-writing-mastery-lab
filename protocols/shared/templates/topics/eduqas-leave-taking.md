# Topic 1: Enid
**Type:** extract_plus_whole
**Teaching Point:** Enid is the protagonist whose deteriorating mental state drives the play's central conflict. Students should analyse how Pinnock uses Enid's confusion, her longing for Jamaica, and her increasing dependence on obeah to explore the psychological toll of displacement on Caribbean immigrants in Britain, showing how the loss of homeland, community, and cultural identity manifests as profound emotional and mental suffering.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**AO4:** 5
**Extract Location:** Act 2

## Extract
The passage in Act 2 where Enid's mental state deteriorates further and she becomes increasingly confused, mixing past and present, Jamaica and England. Her distress reveals the depth of her displacement — she is physically in Britain but psychologically tethered to the homeland she left behind. Her daughters witness her confusion with a mixture of fear and helplessness.

Students should analyse how Enid's deterioration is understood differently by different characters — as illness by her daughters, as a spiritual crisis by Mai — and how Pinnock uses obeah not as superstition but as a legitimate form of healing and cultural connection, contrasting it with the inadequacy of British institutional responses to Black mental health.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**AO4:** 5

## Extract
The passage in Act 2 where Mai performs an obeah ritual for Enid, and the contrasting reactions of the characters reveal the cultural divide at the heart of the play. For Mai and Enid, the ritual is a genuine form of healing rooted in Caribbean tradition. For Del, it is an embarrassing superstition that belongs to a world she has rejected. The scene dramatises the tension between two systems of understanding — Western medicine and Caribbean spirituality.

# Topic 9: Belonging and Displacement
**Type:** extract_plus_whole
**Teaching Point:** The tension between belonging and displacement runs through every relationship in the play. Students should analyse how Pinnock presents characters who are physically in Britain but emotionally tethered to Jamaica, and others who are culturally British but denied full belonging by racism. Pinnock uses setting, memory, and the contrasting experiences of the two generations to argue that displacement is not a single event but an ongoing condition that reshapes itself with each generation.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO4
**AO4:** 5
**Extract Location:** Act 2

## Extract
The passage in Act 2 where the theme of belonging reaches its most intense expression. Characters confront the reality that neither Jamaica nor Britain offers them a complete sense of home — Enid cannot return to the Jamaica she remembers, and her daughters cannot fully belong to the Britain that raised them. Pinnock uses this moment to argue that displacement is not resolved by choosing one culture over another but by finding a way to hold both.
