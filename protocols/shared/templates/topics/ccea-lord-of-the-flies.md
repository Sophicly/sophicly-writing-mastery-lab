# Topic 1: Ralph
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Golding presents Ralph as a figure who embodies the democratic, rule-governed civilisation whose collapse the novel traces — his physical confidence and natural authority make him the obvious leader, yet his increasing ineffectiveness reveals how thin the infrastructure of order really is when stripped of institutional support. Ralph's insistence on the fire as the priority is both practically rational (rescue) and symbolically significant: the fire represents the maintenance of connection to the adult, civilised world, and its repeated extinction by Jack's hunters maps the novel's central arc. Golding makes Ralph's tragedy his ordinariness — he is decent rather than heroic, and decency proves insufficient against the organised force of Jack's tribe. His final weeping for "the end of innocence, the darkness of man's heart" voices the novel's thesis directly, positioning him as the reader's moral representative.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

## Question
How does William Golding present Ralph in *Lord of the Flies?*

You should consider:
- how Golding establishes Ralph's leadership and what it represents
- how Ralph changes and struggles as the novel progresses
- how Ralph's experience is used to explore ideas about civilisation and its fragility.

# Topic 3: Jack
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Jack is presented as the embodiment of humanity's instinctive drive towards dominance, violence, and tribalism — a figure who finds in the island the freedom from rules that he has always craved and who uses that freedom to construct a new social order based on fear and the ecstasy of the hunt. Golding traces Jack's transition from choirmaster to painted hunter chief with careful attention to the psychology of the process: the first pig is let escape because killing requires crossing a psychological barrier; once crossed, the compulsion intensifies. The face paint is the novel's central symbol of identity dissolution — Jack becomes most himself by hiding himself, suggesting that the savage is not a discovery but an uncovering. His manipulation of the boys' fear of the Beast shows Golding's understanding that power is most efficiently sustained not by force but by the manufacture of threat.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 4: Piggy
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Piggy represents reason, science, and democratic values — the intellectual infrastructure that makes civilised society possible — and his destruction is the novel's clearest sign that the boys have crossed a point of no return. His physical vulnerability (asthma, obesity, spectacles) makes him dependent on the community's protection and therefore absolutely exposed when that community disintegrates, a structural argument about how rational culture requires social consensus to function. The spectacles — used to light the fire, progressively damaged, ultimately stolen — track the novel's loss of intellectual and moral clarity with precise symbolic logic. Piggy's death, crushed by a boulder and swallowed by the sea while asserting the right to reason, is Golding's most direct statement: the voice of rationality is not argued into silence but physically eliminated.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 5: Simon
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Simon is the novel's most enigmatic and spiritually charged figure — a mystic whose understanding of the Beast as internal rather than external constitutes the novel's deepest insight, yet whose isolation and eccentricity prevent him from communicating this understanding in time to avert catastrophe. Golding presents Simon's knowledge as visionary rather than rational: his conversation with the Lord of the Flies in the clearing, though hallucinated, articulates the novel's thesis ("Fancy thinking the Beast was something you could hunt and kill!") more clearly than any rational argument. His death — mistaken for the Beast by the frenzied hunters — is the novel's supreme irony: the boy who understood the truth about the Beast is sacrificed to it. Golding surrounds Simon's death and body with imagery of transcendence (phosphorescence, the sea) that suggests a quasi-Christ-like martyrdom.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 6: Civilisation vs Savagery
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The central opposition in *Lord of the Flies* is not between civilisation and savagery as separate human possibilities but between civilisation as an achieved, maintained state and the instinctive drives that exist beneath its surface in every individual. Golding's boys bring civilisation with them — the conch, the choir's structure, the assumption of rescue — but it is imposed order rather than internalised value, and it depends on adults who are not present. The progressive deterioration of the boys' behaviour maps the unravelling of that imposed order as the fear of consequences recedes. Golding resists the idea that any particular group is more savage than another — Ralph's tribe and Jack's are capable of the same violence under the right conditions — making his argument about human nature rather than culture or class.

# Topic 7: Power and Leadership
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Golding presents two contrasting models of leadership in Ralph and Jack — democratic consultation versus charismatic authority — and shows that in a context of fear and moral deterioration, the latter is more effective and more dangerous. Ralph's leadership depends on the assembly, the conch's authority, and the boys' rational acceptance of collective rules; Jack's depends on the ecstasy of the hunt, the satisfaction of immediate desires, and the manufactured threat of the Beast. The conch's progressive loss of authority — ignored, then shattered — charts the decline of the democratic model. Golding is not optimistic: the naval officer's arrival at the end restores adult authority, but his bemusement at the boys' violence suggests that the adult world from which this order will be reimposed is itself engaged in the same tribal warfare on a larger scale.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 8: Fear and the Beast
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** The Beast is the novel's central symbol — a projection of the boys' own capacity for violence that they externalise in order to avoid confronting it within themselves. Golding traces the Beast's development from the littluns' nighttime terrors through the misidentified parachutist to the fully institutionalised mythology of Jack's tribe, showing how fear is manufactured and sustained by those who benefit from it. The Lord of the Flies — the pig's head on a stick — gives the Beast a physical form and a voice, and Simon's vision of it as internal truth is the moment at which the novel's psychological argument is most explicit. Golding's insight — that the Beast is real precisely because it exists inside the boys — is more frightening than any external monster, and deliberately so: it means the problem cannot be solved by hunting.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 9: Loss of Innocence
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** *Lord of the Flies* can be read as a dark inversion of the adventure story tradition, in which children's liberation from adult supervision reveals not freedom and self-discovery but the frightening speed at which innocence is lost when social constraints are removed. Golding uses the physical deterioration of the boys' appearance — filth, matted hair, painted faces — to externalise an internal moral process, so that the loss of innocence is literally visible. The littluns, eating fruit that gives them diarrhoea and crying in the night for their mothers, are the novel's emblem of innocence unable to protect itself — they need the very adult order that the island lacks. Ralph's weeping at the end — not for rescue but for "the end of innocence, the darkness of man's heart" — reframes the boys' experience as an irrecoverable moral awakening.

**AOs:** AO1,AO2

# Topic 10: Violence and Morality
**Type:** single
**Teaching Point:** Golding presents violence not as an aberration but as a persistent human possibility that civilised society suppresses rather than eliminates — and the island strips away those suppressions one by one. The first kill's ritual ("Kill the pig. Cut her throat. Spill her blood.") shows how violence is aestheticised and collectivised into a performance that diffuses individual moral responsibility, a process that reaches its logical conclusion in Simon and Piggy's deaths. Golding is particularly disturbing in his treatment of moral responsibility: Roger's gradual escalation from avoiding the littluns to rolling boulders shows how each violent act lowers the threshold for the next, while the mob psychology of the hunting dance gives individuals a moral alibi through collective action. The naval officer's arrival neither restores morality nor refutes Golding's argument — the adult world is engaged in its own version of the same violence.
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2
