# Topic 1: Benedick and Beatrice
**Type:** shakespeare_extract
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Benedick and Beatrice's relationship is built on a "merry war" of wit that masks deeper feelings. Shakespeare uses their verbal sparring to explore how pride and vulnerability coexist, and how genuine love can emerge from apparent antagonism.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4
**Extract Location:** Act 1 Scene 1
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Beatrice and Benedick meet for the first time in the play as Don Pedro's company arrives in Messina. Their exchange reveals the competitive wit and mutual preoccupation that defines their relationship.

### Extract
BEATRICE
I wonder that you will still be talking, Signior
Benedick: nobody marks you.

BENEDICK
What, my dear Lady Disdain! Are you yet living?

BEATRICE
Is it possible disdain should die while she hath
such meet food to feed it as Signior Benedick?
Courtesy itself must convert to disdain, if you come
in her presence.

BENEDICK
Then is courtesy a turncoat. But it is certain I
am loved of all ladies, only you excepted: and I
would I could find in my heart that I had not a hard
heart; for, truly, I love none.

BEATRICE
A dear happiness to women: they would else have
been troubled with a pernicious suitor. I thank God
and my cold blood, I am of your humour for that: I
had rather hear my dog bark at a crow than a man
swear he loves me.

BENEDICK
God keep your ladyship still in that mind! So some
gentleman or other shall 'scape a predestinate
scratched face.

BEATRICE
Scratching could not make it worse, an 'twere such
a face as yours were.

### Question
Explore how Shakespeare presents the relationship between Benedick and Beatrice in the play. Refer to this extract from Act 1 Scene 1 and elsewhere in the play.

## Or

### Question
'Benedick and Beatrice are more alike than either would care to admit.' To what extent do you agree with this view of the play?

# Topic 2: Conceptual Notes
**Type:** shakespeare_extract
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Much Ado About Nothing is a comedy that interrogates serious themes beneath its witty surface. Shakespeare structures the play around parallel plots — the Beatrice-Benedick courtship and the Claudio-Hero romance — to contrast different kinds of love, deception, and social performance in Messina's patriarchal society.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4
**Extract Location:** Act 2 Scene 3
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Benedick delivers a soliloquy reflecting on love and marriage before he is tricked by Don Pedro, Leonato, and Claudio into believing Beatrice loves him.

### Extract
BENEDICK
I do much wonder that one man, seeing how much
another man is a fool when he dedicates his
behaviours to love, will, after he hath laughed at
such shallow follies in others, become the argument
of his own scorn by falling in love: and such a man
is Claudio. I have known when there was no music
with him but the drum and the fife; and now had he
rather hear the tabor and the pipe. I have known
when he would have walked ten mile a-foot to see a
good armour; and now will he lie ten nights awake,
carving the fashion of a new doublet. He was wont to
speak plain and to the purpose, like an honest man
and a soldier; and now is he turned orthography; his
words are a very fantastical banquet, just so many
strange dishes. May I be so converted and see with
these eyes? I cannot tell; I think not. I will not
be sworn but love may transform me to an oyster; but
I'll take my oath on it, till he have made an oyster
of me, he shall never make me such a fool.

### Question
Explore how Shakespeare presents attitudes towards love and marriage in the play. Refer to this extract from Act 2 Scene 3 and elsewhere in the play.

## Or

### Question
'Much Ado About Nothing is as much about self-deception as it is about the deception of others.' How far do you agree with this view of the play?

# Topic 3: Deception
**Type:** shakespeare_extract
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Deception operates on multiple levels in the play — benevolent tricks (the gulling scenes) sit alongside malicious schemes (Don John's plot). Shakespeare uses this duality to explore how easily perception can be manipulated and how trust within a community can be both built and destroyed through staged performances.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4
**Extract Location:** Act 3 Scene 3
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Borachio drunkenly confesses to Conrade how he carried out Don John's scheme to deceive Claudio into believing Hero was unfaithful, while the Watch listen unseen.

### Extract
BORACHIO
Thou shouldst rather ask if it were possible any
villany should be so rich; for when rich villains
have need of poor ones, poor ones may make what
price they will.

CONRADE
I wonder at it.

BORACHIO
That shows thou art unconfirmed. Thou knowest that
the fashion of a doublet, or a hat, or a cloak, is
nothing to a man.

CONRADE
Yes, it is apparel.

BORACHIO
I mean, the fashion.

CONRADE
Yes, the fashion is the fashion.

BORACHIO
Tush! I may as well say the fool's the fool. But
seest thou not what a deformed thief this fashion
is? — But know that I have tonight wooed Margaret,
the Lady Hero's gentlewoman, by the name of Hero:
she leans me out at her mistress' chamber-window,
bids me a thousand times good night — I tell this
tale vilely: — I should first tell thee how the
prince, Claudio and my master, planted and placed
and possessed by my master Don John, saw afar off in
the orchard this amiable encounter.

### Question
Explore how Shakespeare presents the theme of deception in the play. Refer to this extract from Act 3 Scene 3 and elsewhere in the play.

## Or

### Question
'In Much Ado About Nothing, the characters who deceive others are never as clever as they believe themselves to be.' How far do you agree with this view of the play?

# Topic 4: Honour and Reputation
**Type:** shakespeare_extract
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Honour and reputation in the play are shown to be fragile social constructs, particularly for women. Shakespeare dramatises how a woman's honour is treated as male property — Leonato's reaction to Hero's shaming reveals that his concern for family reputation outweighs his concern for his daughter's wellbeing.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4
**Extract Location:** Act 4 Scene 1
**Extract Context:** In this extract, after Claudio has publicly shamed Hero at the wedding and she has fainted, Leonato responds with fury — not at Claudio, but at his own daughter, believing the accusation to be true.

### Extract
LEONATO
Hath no man's dagger here a point for me?

*HERO swoons*

BEATRICE
Why, how now, cousin! wherefore sink you down?

DON JOHN
Come, let us go. These things, come thus to light,
Smother her spirits up.

*Exeunt DON PEDRO, DON JOHN, and CLAUDIO*

BENEDICK
How doth the lady?

BEATRICE
Dead, I think. Help, uncle!
Hero! why, Hero! Uncle! Signior Benedick! Friar!

LEONATO
O Fate! take not away thy heavy hand.
Death is the fairest cover for her shame
That may be wish'd for.

BEATRICE
How now, cousin Hero!

FRIAR FRANCIS
Have comfort, lady.

LEONATO
Dost thou look up?

FRIAR FRANCIS
Yea, wherefore should she not?

LEONATO
Wherefore! Why, doth not every earthly thing
Cry shame upon her? Could she here deny
The story that is printed in her blood?
Do not live, Hero; do not ope thine eyes.

### Question
Explore how Shakespeare presents ideas about honour and reputation in the play. Refer to this extract from Act 4 Scene 1 and elsewhere in the play.

## Or

### Question
'In Much Ado About Nothing, men care more about their own honour than about the women they claim to love.' How far do you agree with this view of the play?

# Topic 5: Claudio
**Type:** shakespeare_extract
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Claudio is presented as a young, conventional lover whose feelings are bound up with social propriety and male honour. Shakespeare uses his rapid shifts — from adoration to violent rejection — to expose the shallowness of a love based on appearance and reputation rather than genuine knowledge of another person.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4
**Extract Location:** Act 4 Scene 1
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Claudio publicly rejects Hero at the altar, accusing her of infidelity based on the false evidence arranged by Don John. He returns Hero to her father in front of the assembled wedding guests.

### Extract
CLAUDIO
There, Leonato, take her back again:
Give not this rotten orange to your friend;
She's but the sign and semblance of her honour.
Behold how like a maid she blushes here!
O, what authority and show of truth
Can cunning sin cover itself withal!
Comes not that blood as modest evidence
To witness simple virtue? Would you not swear,
All you that see her, that she were a maid,
By these exterior shows? But she is none:
She knows the heat of a luxurious bed;
Her blush is guiltiness, not modesty.

LEONATO
What do you mean, my lord?

CLAUDIO
Not to be married,
Not to knit my soul to an approved wanton.

LEONATO
Dear my lord, if you, in your own proof,
Have vanquish'd the resistance of her youth,
And made defeat of her virginity —

CLAUDIO
I know what you would say: if I have known her,
You will say she did embrace me as a husband,
And so extenuate the 'forehand sin:
No, Leonato,
I never tempted her with word too large;
But, as a brother to his sister, show'd
Bashful sincerity and comely love.

### Question
Explore how Shakespeare presents the character of Claudio in the play. Refer to this extract from Act 4 Scene 1 and elsewhere in the play.

## Or

### Question
'Claudio does not deserve Hero.' To what extent do you agree with this view of the play?

# Topic 6: Gender Roles
**Type:** shakespeare_extract
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Shakespeare uses Beatrice's frustration at her powerlessness to expose the constraints placed on women in a patriarchal society. Her famous command "Kill Claudio" is not mere vengeance — it reveals that women must rely on men to act on their behalf, even when they see injustice more clearly than the men around them.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4
**Extract Location:** Act 4 Scene 1
**Extract Context:** In this extract, after the failed wedding, Benedick and Beatrice are left alone. Benedick declares his love, and Beatrice, overwhelmed by Hero's treatment, makes a shocking demand.

### Extract
BENEDICK
Come, bid me do anything for thee.

BEATRICE
Kill Claudio.

BENEDICK
Ha! not for the wide world.

BEATRICE
You kill me to deny it. Farewell.

BENEDICK
Tarry, sweet Beatrice.

BEATRICE
I am gone, though I am here: there is no love in
you: nay, I pray you, let me go.

BENEDICK
Beatrice —

BEATRICE
In faith, I will go.

BENEDICK
We'll be friends first.

BEATRICE
You dare easier be friends with me than fight with
mine enemy.

BENEDICK
Is Claudio thine enemy?

BEATRICE
Is he not approved in the height a villain, that
hath slandered, scorned, dishonoured my kinswoman? O
that I were a man! What, bear her in hand until they
come to take hands; and then, with public
accusation, uncovered slander, unmitigated rancour —
O God, that I were a man! I would eat his heart in
the market-place.

# Topic 7: Don John
**Type:** shakespeare_extract
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Don John is presented as a self-consciously villainous figure whose motivations stem from resentment and social exclusion. As an illegitimate brother, he exists outside the bonds of loyalty and community that hold Messina together. Shakespeare uses him to show how a society built on surfaces and performance is vulnerable to those who exploit its weaknesses.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4
**Extract Location:** Act 1 Scene 3
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Don John reveals his resentful nature to his follower Conrade, who urges him to conceal his discontent. Don John refuses to perform a false role and declares his commitment to villainy.

### Extract
DON JOHN
I cannot hide what I am: I must be sad when I have
cause and smile at no man's jests, eat when I have
stomach and wait for no man's leisure, sleep when I
am drowsy and tend on no man's business, laugh when
I am merry and claw no man in his humour.

CONRADE
Yes, but you must not make the full show of this
till you may do it without controlment. You have of
late stood out against your brother, and he hath
ta'en you newly into his grace; where it is
impossible you should take true root but by the fair
weather that you make yourself: it is needful that
you frame the season for your own harvest.

DON JOHN
I had rather be a canker in a hedge than a rose in
his grace, and it better fits my blood to be
disdained of all than to fashion a carriage to rob
love from any: in this, though I cannot be said to
be a flattering honest man, it must not be denied
but I am a plain-dealing villain. I am trusted with
a muzzle and enfranchised with a clog; therefore I
have decreed not to sing in my cage. If I had my
mouth, I would bite; if I had my liberty, I would do
my liking: in the meantime let me be that I am and
seek not to alter me.

# Topic 8: Love and Marriage
**Type:** shakespeare_extract
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** Shakespeare contrasts two models of love in the play: the conventional, appearance-based courtship of Claudio and Hero, and the combative, intellectually equal partnership of Benedick and Beatrice. The play suggests that love built on genuine knowledge of another person — flaws and all — is more resilient than love based on idealized surfaces.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4
**Extract Location:** Act 5 Scene 4
**Extract Context:** In this extract, at the play's resolution, Benedick and Beatrice are confronted with evidence of their love for each other — sonnets each has written — and finally acknowledge their feelings publicly, though characteristically refusing to surrender their wit entirely.

### Extract
BENEDICK
A miracle! Here's our own hands against our hearts.
Come, I will have thee; but, by this light, I take
thee for pity.

BEATRICE
I would not deny you; but, by this good day, I yield
upon great persuasion; and partly to save your life,
for I was told you were in a consumption.

BENEDICK
Peace! I will stop your mouth.

*Kissing her*

DON PEDRO
How dost thou, Benedick, the married man?

BENEDICK
I'll tell thee what, prince; a college of
wit-crackers cannot flout me out of my humour. Dost
thou think I care for a satire or an epigram? No:
if a man will be beaten with brains, a' shall wear
nothing handsome about him. In brief, since I do
purpose to marry, I will think nothing to any
purpose that the world can say against it; and
therefore never flout at me for what I have said
against it; for man is a giddy thing, and this is my
conclusion.

# Topic 9: The Watch
**Type:** shakespeare_extract
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The Watch are comic figures whose incompetence paradoxically leads to the truth being uncovered. Shakespeare uses Dogberry and his men to satirise authority and social pretension, while also making a structural point: in Messina, the least sophisticated characters are the ones who ultimately restore justice, while the educated nobles are easily deceived.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4
**Extract Location:** Act 3 Scene 3
**Extract Context:** In this extract, Dogberry gives his instructions to the Watch before their night patrol. His comically confused orders reveal both his incompetence and his inflated sense of his own importance.

### Extract
DOGBERRY
You are thought here to be the most senseless and
fit man for the constable of the watch; therefore
bear you the lantern. This is your charge: you shall
comprehend all vagrom men; you are to bid any man
stand, in the prince's name.

SECOND WATCHMAN
How if a' will not stand?

DOGBERRY
Why, then, take no note of him, but let him go; and
presently call the rest of the watch together and
thank God you are rid of a knave.

VERGES
If he will not stand when he is bidden, he is none
of the prince's subjects.

DOGBERRY
True, and they are to meddle with none but the
prince's subjects. You shall also make no noise in
the streets; for, for the watch to babble and to
talk is most tolerable and not to be endured.

SECOND WATCHMAN
We will rather sleep than talk: we know what belongs
to a watch.

DOGBERRY
Why, you speak like an ancient and most quiet
watchman; for I cannot see how sleeping should
offend: only, have a care that your bills be not
stolen.

# Topic 10: Appearance vs Reality
**Type:** shakespeare_extract
**Format:** either_or
**Teaching Point:** The gap between appearance and reality runs through every level of the play — from the gulling scenes to Hero's shaming to the title itself ("nothing" was pronounced "noting," meaning observation or gossip). Shakespeare shows that in a society obsessed with surfaces, seeing is never simply believing, and the truth depends on who is doing the looking and why.

## Either
**Marks:** 40
**AOs:** AO1,AO2,AO3,AO4
**Extract Location:** Act 2 Scene 1
**Extract Context:** In this extract, during the masked ball at Leonato's house, characters speak behind masks and visors. Don Pedro woos Hero on Claudio's behalf, while Don John exploits the confusion to plant doubt in Claudio's mind.

### Extract
DON JOHN
Are not you Signior Benedick?

CLAUDIO
You know me well; I am he.

DON JOHN
Signior, you are very near my brother in his love:
he is enamoured on Hero; I pray you, dissuade him
from her: she is no equal for his birth: you may do
the part of an honest man in it.

CLAUDIO
How know you he loves her?

DON JOHN
I heard him swear his affection.

BORACHIO
So did I too; and he swore he would marry her
tonight.

DON JOHN
Come, let us to the banquet.

*Exeunt DON JOHN and BORACHIO*

CLAUDIO
Thus answer I in name of Benedick,
But hear these ill news with the ears of Claudio.
'Tis certain so; the prince woos for himself.
Friendship is constant in all other things
Save in the office and affairs of love:
Therefore, all hearts in love use their own tongues;
Let every eye negotiate for itself
And trust no agent; for beauty is a witch
Against whose charms faith melteth into blood.

Explore how Shakespeare presents the theme of appearance versus reality in the play. Refer to this extract from Act 2 Scene 1 and elsewhere in the play.

## Or

### Question
'In Much Ado About Nothing, characters are too willing to believe what they see and hear.' How far do you agree with this view of the play?
