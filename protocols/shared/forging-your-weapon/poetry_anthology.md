### **OPTION A: OZYMANDIAS (The Crumbling Monument)**

The Task: "Explore how Shelley presents the idea of power."  
Focus: The description of the ruined statue.

PARAGRAPH 1:  
"Shelley shows that Ozymandias was a very powerful king. The sculptor carved a 'sneer of cold command' on his face, which shows he was cruel and bossy. Then Shelley says the statue is broken with 'Two vast and trunkless legs of stone,' which shows that his power didn't last. The words on the pedestal say 'Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!' which is ironic because there is nothing left. This links to the theme of power because it shows that even powerful people are forgotten eventually. Shelley uses the desert setting to show that nature is more powerful than humans."

PARAGRAPH 2:  
"Shelley constructs Ozymandias not as a cautionary tale about one tyrant, but as a universal indictment of autocratic hubris. The 'sneer of cold command' survives not because of the king's legacy, but because of the sculptor's perceptive art—suggesting that the artist's truth outlasts the tyrant's propaganda. The sonic violence of 'boundless and bare' and 'lone and level sands stretch far away' uses alliteration and sibilance to create a vast, hissing emptiness that aurally enacts the erasure of power. The imperative 'Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!' operates on a devastating double irony: the original command was meant to intimidate rivals, yet now it inadvertently mocks its own speaker, as the 'Works' have been reduced to nothing. This creates a profound unease in the reader, as they are forced to confront the impermanence of their own achievements. Shelley, writing in the aftermath of the Napoleonic Wars, weaponizes the sonnet form to warn that all empires—no matter how 'colossal'—are destined for the same obliterating silence."

### **OPTION B: LONDON (The Manacles of the Mind)**

The Task: "Explore how Blake presents suffering in the city."  
Focus: The speaker's journey through London's streets.

PARAGRAPH 1:  
"Blake shows that London is a very miserable place. He says he sees 'Marks of weakness, marks of woe' on every face, which shows people are suffering. The word 'marks' is repeated to show how common the suffering is. He also says the people have 'mind-forg'd manacles,' which is a metaphor for being trapped. This shows they feel like prisoners even though they are free. He hears the 'Chimney-sweeper's cry,' which shows child labour was bad in those times. This links to the theme of power because the rich controlled the poor."

PARAGRAPH 2:  
"Blake transforms the pastoral 'wander' into a forensic autopsy of a diseased society. The relentless anaphora of 'every' in 'every face... every Man... every Infant's cry' creates a suffocating universality, denying the reader any escape from complicity. The oxymoronic 'charter'd' Thames—a river that should symbolize natural freedom—reveals how capitalism has commodified even nature itself, caging the organic within the bureaucratic. The most devastating weapon in Blake's arsenal is the compound metaphor 'mind-forg'd manacles,' which transfers the blame for oppression from the institutions to the oppressed themselves, suggesting that the greatest chains are psychological—people have internalized their own subjugation. This shocks the reader into recognizing their own passive acceptance of injustice. It compels the audience to confront how systems of power maintain control not through brute force alone, but through the colonization of thought. Blake, a radical Romantic visionary, indicts not merely the government but an entire civilization that has chosen commerce over compassion, branding even its children's tears as industrial byproducts."

### **OPTION C: MY LAST DUCHESS (The Gilded Cage)**

The Task: "Explore how Browning presents the Duke's desire for control."  
Focus: The Duke's monologue about his former wife.

PARAGRAPH 1:  
"Browning shows the Duke is very controlling. He says 'I gave commands; Then all smiles stopped together,' which suggests he had her killed because she smiled too much at other people. He didn't like that 'She ranked / My gift of a nine-hundred-years-old name / With anybody's gift.' This shows he was jealous because she treated everyone equally. The painting of her is hidden behind a curtain that 'none puts by... but I,' showing he wants to control her even after death. Browning uses the dramatic monologue form so we only hear the Duke's side. This links to the theme of power."

PARAGRAPH 2:  
"Browning engineers the dramatic monologue as a trap—the Duke believes he is performing authority, yet every word is a confession of pathological insecurity. The chillingly understated 'I gave commands; Then all smiles stopped together' is devastating precisely because of what it conceals: the caesura enacts a pause that mirrors the Duke's cold deliberation, while the euphemism allows him to sanitize murder as mere administration. His fury at the Duchess's egalitarian joy—that she 'ranked / My gift of a nine-hundred-years-old name / With anybody's gift'—exposes a man whose entire identity depends on hierarchy. The enjambment across 'ranked / My gift' physically ruptures the verse, mirroring the Duke's fractured ego. The curtain concealing her portrait transforms her from a person into a possession, an object he can exhibit or withhold at will—granting her in death the obedience she denied him in life. This creates a creeping horror in the reader, who realizes they have been listening to a murderer negotiate his next marriage. Browning forces his Victorian audience to confront the uncomfortable truth that the aristocratic performance of refinement and culture can mask the most brutal forms of domestic tyranny."

### **OPTION D: EXPOSURE (The Frozen Siege)**

The Task: "Explore how Owen presents the suffering of soldiers."  
Focus: The soldiers' experience of cold and waiting.

PARAGRAPH 1:  
"Owen shows that the soldiers are suffering because of the cold weather. He says 'Our brains ache, in the merciless iced east winds that knive us,' which uses personification because the wind is described as 'kniving' them like a weapon. The word 'merciless' means the wind has no mercy and won't stop. He also says 'But nothing happens,' which is repeated to show that they are bored and waiting. This creates sympathy because we feel sorry for them. Owen was a war poet who fought in World War One, so he knew what it was like. This links to the theme of the reality of conflict."

PARAGRAPH 2:  
"Owen constructs the battlefield not as a site of heroic action, but as a theatre of atmospheric annihilation where nature itself becomes the true enemy. The sibilant assault in 'merciless iced east winds that knive us' transforms the weather into a sentient aggressor, the verb 'knive' weaponizing the natural world with brutal precision—note that Owen chooses 'knive' rather than 'knife,' the unfamiliar form destabilizing the reader's expectations just as the war destabilizes the soldiers' reality. The devastating refrain 'But nothing happens' operates as both structural anchor and existential verdict, its repetition at each stanza's close creating a cyclical purgatory that denies the soldiers the narrative arc of traditional war poetry—there is no charge, no glory, no resolution. The half-rhyme scheme (e.g., 'knive us'/'nervous') refuses the reader the comfort of sonic resolution, aurally replicating the soldiers' fractured psychological state. This forces the reader to endure a fraction of the soldiers' torment—the agony of anticipation without climax. Owen, writing from the trenches, dismantles the Romantic ideal of noble sacrifice to expose the War's true obscenity: not the violence of battle, but the slow, invisible erosion of the human spirit by cold, fear, and futility."

### **OPTION E: PORPHYRIA'S LOVER (The Strangling Calm)**

The Task: "Explore how Browning presents the speaker's disturbed mind."  
Focus: The speaker's account of the murder of Porphyria.

PARAGRAPH 1:  
"Browning shows the speaker is mentally unstable. He says 'I found / A thing to do, and all her hair / In one long yellow string I wound / Three times her little throat around, / And strangled her.' This shows he killed Porphyria by strangling her with her own hair. The fact that he describes it so calmly is disturbing. He then says 'And yet God has not said a word!' which shows he thinks God approves of what he did. The pathetic fallacy at the start with the storm reflects his anger. Browning uses an unreliable narrator to show the reader that the speaker is mad. This links to the theme of obsessive love."

PARAGRAPH 2:  
"Browning constructs the speaker's psychosis not through dramatic revelation, but through the horrifying normalcy of his narration. The metrical regularity of 'In one long yellow string I wound / Three times her little throat around' is deeply unsettling—the sing-song iambic tetrameter and the childlike precision of 'three times' transform murder into a methodical, almost ritualistic act, as if the speaker is threading a needle rather than extinguishing a life. The possessive transformation is complete: Porphyria, who entered the poem as the active agent ('She shut the cold out... made the cheerless grate / Blaze up'), is reduced to a passive object whose head rests on his shoulder like 'a shut bud that holds a bee.' The simile is exquisite in its horror—the 'bee' of her vitality is now permanently trapped within the closed flower of death. The final declarative 'And yet God has not said a word!' is not triumphant defiance but delusional self-justification, revealing a mind that has constructed its own moral framework to accommodate the unthinkable. This compels the reader to confront their own uncomfortable proximity to the speaker's logic—the dramatic monologue has forced them to inhabit his perspective. Browning, writing during an era of rigid Victorian morality, exposes the terrifying potential for violence that lurks beneath the surface of possessive love and patriarchal entitlement."

### **OPTION F: THE CHARGE OF THE LIGHT BRIGADE (The Glorious Slaughter)**

The Task: "Explore how Tennyson presents attitudes towards conflict."  
Focus: The cavalry charge and its aftermath.

PARAGRAPH 1:  
"Tennyson shows that the soldiers were brave even though they knew they would die. He says 'Into the valley of Death / Rode the six hundred,' which creates a dramatic image of them riding to their deaths. The repetition of 'six hundred' reminds us how many soldiers there were. He says 'Theirs not to reason why, / Theirs but to do and die,' which shows they had to follow orders even though the order was a mistake. The phrase 'do and die' rhymes, making it sound like a motto. Tennyson uses lots of repetition to make the poem feel fast and exciting. He wrote the poem to honour the soldiers. This links to the theme of duty."

PARAGRAPH 2:  
"Tennyson constructs the charge as simultaneously glorious and catastrophic, creating a poem that both celebrates and interrogates obedience. The dactylic rhythm of 'Half a league, half a league, / Half a league onward' mimics the thundering cadence of hooves, propelling the reader forward with the same unstoppable momentum as the cavalry itself—the form becomes the content, denying both soldier and reader the opportunity to pause and question. The biblical allusion 'valley of Death' elevates the military blunder into a quasi-religious martyrdom, sanctifying the soldiers' obedience as a form of faith. Yet the devastating couplet 'Theirs not to reason why, / Theirs but to do and die' contains a buried subversion: the neat rhyme and epigrammatic certainty package unquestioning obedience as a virtue, but the starkness of 'do and die' strips away any pretence of strategic purpose—the only certainty is death. The anaphoric 'Cannon to right of them, / Cannon to left of them, / Cannon in front of them' creates a claustrophobic tricolon of encirclement, surrounding the reader in the same inescapable kill-zone. This forces the reader to experience both the exhilaration and the horror of the charge simultaneously. Tennyson, the Poet Laureate writing for public consumption, performs the difficult act of honouring the soldiers' sacrifice whilst embedding a subtle indictment of the aristocratic command that sent them to their deaths."

## **6. THE ANATOMY OF THE WEAPON**

### **PHASE 4: THE ANATOMY**

**"Why is Paragraph 2 a Weapon? (The TTECEA Structure)"**

Break down the winning paragraph to show the student the hidden structure:

1. **T (Topic Sentence):** Sets up a **Concept/Argument** about the poet's purpose — not a plot summary.
2. **T (Technique):** Identifies the method (Metaphor, Alliteration, Enjambment, Form, etc.).
3. **E (Evidence):** Embeds the anchor quote precisely within the argument.
4. **C (Close Analysis):** Zooms in on key words — explores connotations, sound, and alternative meanings.
5. **E (Effect):** Explains the impact on the audience/reader (2 Sentences: "This creates..." / "It forces the reader to...").
6. **A (Author's Purpose/Context):** Explains *why* the poet did this (Big Idea / Historical Context / Societal Comment).

**Poetry-Specific Additions to Highlight:**

When breaking down the winning paragraph, draw attention to these poetry-specific analytical moves that distinguish top-level responses:

* **Form & Structure:** Does the response discuss how the poem's form (sonnet, dramatic monologue, free verse, ballad) contributes to meaning?
* **Sound & Rhythm:** Does the response analyse sonic effects (sibilance, plosives, caesura, enjambment, metre) and connect them to the poem's themes?
* **The Volta / Shift:** Does the response identify turning points, tonal shifts, or structural pivots?
* **Voice & Perspective:** Does the response consider who is speaking, to whom, and how that shapes meaning?
