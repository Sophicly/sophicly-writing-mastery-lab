#### **THE EXTRACT (The Moor at Dusk)**

> "The moor was a living thing that evening. A bruise-coloured sky pressed down on the heather, and the wind came off the crags in long, starved breaths. Somewhere, a curlew called — one note, then another, then silence — as though the bird had thought better of it. Thomas stopped walking. He did not want to be here, but here he was. The path at his feet had narrowed to a pale thread, and the thread was beginning to disappear into the gorse."

The Task: "How does the writer use language to describe the moor at the start of the extract?"

PARAGRAPH 1:  
"The writer uses lots of techniques to describe the moor. He uses personification when he says the moor was 'a living thing,' which makes it seem alive. He also uses the adjective 'bruise-coloured' to describe the sky, which tells us the sky looks dark and purple. The writer uses a simile when he says the wind came in 'long, starved breaths,' which makes the wind sound hungry. There is also onomatopoeia when the curlew 'called,' which helps us imagine the sound. The writer uses these techniques to make the moor sound scary and show that Thomas doesn't want to be there. This shows the theme of nature being powerful."

PARAGRAPH 2:  
"The writer animates the moor into a hostile presence, presenting the landscape not as scenery but as an antagonist. The noun phrase 'a living thing' personifies the moor immediately, conditioning the reader to view everything that follows as alive — and therefore capable of intention. The compound adjective 'bruise-coloured' is doubly loaded: it evokes a literal purple-black sky, but 'bruise' carries a memory of prior injury, hinting that Thomas is entering a place that has already suffered violence. This is sharpened by the personification 'starved breaths,' which transfers physical hunger onto the wind, suggesting a landscape that will consume him. The curlew's call — 'one note, then another, then silence' — enacts its own retreat in the rhythm of the sentence, the triadic structure collapsing into a pause that makes the reader feel the silence rather than merely notice it. By the time the path narrows to 'a pale thread' that 'was beginning to disappear,' the writer has completed a quiet act of erasure: Thomas's route back, his safety, and the reader's own footing in the scene are all being withdrawn together."

### **PHASE 4: THE ANATOMY OF THE WEAPON**

**"Why is Paragraph 2 a Weapon? (The TTECEA Structure)"**

Break down the winning paragraph to show the student the hidden structure:

1. **T (Topic Sentence):** Establishes a **concept/argument** — the landscape as antagonist — not just "the writer uses techniques."  
2. **T (Technique):** Identifies methods precisely — *personification*, *compound adjective*, *transferred epithet*, *triadic structure*.  
3. **E (Evidence):** Embeds anchor quotes tightly inside the argument, not as standalone sentences.  
4. **C (Close Analysis):** Zooms in on single words — 'bruise', 'starved', 'thread' — and reads their connotations.  
5. **E (Effect):** Explains the impact on the *reader's experience* (2+ sentences), not just "it makes the reader feel."  
6. **A (Author's Purpose):** Explains *why* the writer chose this pattern — a "quiet act of erasure" — the conceptual argument.
