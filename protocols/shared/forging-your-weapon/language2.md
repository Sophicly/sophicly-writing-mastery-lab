#### **THE EXTRACT (A 21st-Century Opinion Column — "The Case Against Fast Fashion")**

> "I used to love a bargain. A £4 T-shirt, a £9 pair of jeans — for a brief, guilty hour in a changing-room mirror, I was the person I wanted to be, and I'd only paid the price of a sandwich to become her. But I know, now, what that sandwich really cost. It cost a river somewhere in Bangladesh, running blue and green with dye. It cost a woman my own age working a fourteen-hour shift so that I could feel, for an afternoon, slightly more like myself. The T-shirt is in a landfill now. The woman, I hope, is not."

The Task: "How does the writer use language to express their point of view about fast fashion?"

PARAGRAPH 1:  
"The writer uses lots of techniques to show she doesn't like fast fashion any more. She uses anecdote when she says 'I used to love a bargain,' which shows she is talking about herself. She uses lists when she writes '£4 T-shirt, a £9 pair of jeans' to show how cheap the clothes are. She also uses imagery about the 'river running blue and green with dye,' which shows pollution. The writer uses first person, which makes it more personal. She uses emotive language like 'guilty' to make the reader feel bad. This is to make the reader agree that fast fashion is bad."

PARAGRAPH 2:  
"The writer constructs a rhetoric of complicity, drawing the reader into her own former guilt before steering them toward shared moral reckoning. The confessional opening 'I used to love a bargain' disarms the reader — this is not a lecture, it is an admission — and the price pairing '£4 T-shirt, a £9 pair of jeans' lulls the reader into agreeing that the figures are harmless before the pivot undoes them. The extended metaphor of the 'sandwich' reframes the T-shirt as a trivial consumption; the writer then weaponises that triviality through the anaphoric 'It cost,' each repetition widening the scale of harm from a dyed river to a named human life. The colour imagery 'blue and green' — which on another page would evoke natural beauty — here signals poison, an inversion that forces the reader to reread the phrase and feel the damage. The passage closes with a bleakly restrained contrast: 'The T-shirt is in a landfill now. The woman, I hope, is not.' The parallel syntactic structure makes the reader confront the grotesque equivalence the industry has already made between the two, and the tonal understatement of 'I hope' does more moral work than any raised voice could."

### **PHASE 4: THE ANATOMY OF THE WEAPON**

**"Why is Paragraph 2 a Weapon? (The TTECEA Structure)"**

Break down the winning paragraph to show the student the hidden structure:

1. **T (Topic Sentence):** Establishes a **concept/argument** — a "rhetoric of complicity" — not just "the writer uses techniques."  
2. **T (Technique):** Identifies methods precisely — *confessional opening*, *pivot*, *extended metaphor*, *anaphora*, *inverted imagery*, *parallelism*, *tonal understatement*.  
3. **E (Evidence):** Embeds anchor quotes tightly inside the argument, not as standalone sentences.  
4. **C (Close Analysis):** Zooms in on single words and phrases — 'sandwich', 'It cost', 'I hope' — and reads their connotations in context.  
5. **E (Effect):** Explains the *reader's journey* (disarmed → lulled → pivoted → confronted), not just "it makes the reader feel."  
6. **A (Author's Purpose):** Explains *why* the writer chose restraint over volume — understatement doing "more moral work than any raised voice could."
