#### **OPTION A: AN INSPECTOR CALLS (Sheila's Change)**

The Task: "Explore how Priestley presents the younger generation's response to the Inspector."  
Focus: Sheila returning the ring / accepting blame.

PARAGRAPH 1:  
"Priestley shows that Sheila is sorry for what she did to Eva Smith. She says, 'I know I'm to blame – and I'm desperately sorry.' This shows she accepts responsibility unlike her parents. She hands back the engagement ring to Gerald, which is a dramatic symbol that she is changing. She tells her father he is wrong to care only about business. This links to the theme of social responsibility because Priestley wants the younger generation to learn from their mistakes and stop being selfish capitalists."  
PARAGRAPH 2:  
"Priestley constructs Sheila as a proxy for the post-war audience, using her transformation to allegorize the death of capitalist individualism. Her declarative admission, 'I know I'm to blame,' signals not just personal remorse but a rejection of her father's dogmatic worldview. By forcefully returning the ring—a symbol of commodified love and patriarchal ownership—Sheila physically detaches herself from the capitalist hierarchy. Priestley uses her as a vehicle for his socialist message, warning that unless the younger generation rejects the 'fire and blood and anguish' of the past, society is doomed to repeat the catastrophe of the World Wars."

#### **OPTION B: BLOOD BROTHERS (Mickey's Downfall)**

The Task: "Explore how Russell presents the influence of social class on Mickey."  
Focus: The ending / "I could have been him."

PARAGRAPH 1:  
"Russell shows that Mickey's life is ruined because he is poor. At the end, he screams, 'I could have been him\!' to Edward. This shows he is jealous that Edward got the good life and he got the bad one. He is addicted to antidepressants which make him depressed. The gun in his hand shows he is violent now. Russell uses the theme of class to show that it isn't fair that some people have money and others don't."  
PARAGRAPH 2:  
"Russell presents Mickey not merely as a victim of poverty, but as the tragic product of a deterministic class system that grinds the working class into submission. His final, agonized exclamative, 'I could have been him\!', creates a moment of anagnorisis (realisation) where the 'game' of childhood is revealed as a rigged trap. The weapon in his hand is a physical manifestation of his powerlessness; he uses violence because he has been denied a voice by the establishment. Russell challenges the Thatcherite myth of meritocracy, arguing that class, not character, is the ultimate architect of destiny."

#### **OPTION C: A VIEW FROM THE BRIDGE (Eddie's Obsession)**

The Task: "Explore how Miller presents Eddie's growing obsession."  
Focus: The kissing scene / The telephone booth.

PARAGRAPH 1:  
"Miller shows that Eddie is obsessed with Catherine and jealous of Rodolpho. When he kisses Rodolpho, it is a very shocking moment. He tries to prove Rodolpho is gay to make Catherine stop liking him. Eddie also calls the Immigration Bureau, which is a betrayal of his community. This breaks the code of honour in Red Hook. The phone booth lighting up shows he has done something wrong."  
PARAGRAPH 2:  
"Miller presents Eddie’s transgressive desire as a tragic force that dismantles the moral structure of the Red Hook community. The shocking act of kissing Rodolpho is a desperate, physical attempt to reassert a toxic masculinity that he feels is slipping away; by attempting to feminize his rival, he inadvertently exposes his own unnatural fixation. The visual symbolism of the glowing phone booth serves as a spotlight on his betrayal, marking the moment he trades his tribal loyalty for personal vengeance. Miller positions Eddie as a modern Greek tragic hero, driven by a fatal flaw (hamartia) that is inextricably linked to the unspoken codes of his environment."

#### **OPTION D: THE CURIOUS INCIDENT (Christopher's World)**

The Task: "Explore how the play presents Christopher's unique perspective."  
Focus: The train station scene / Sensory overload.

PARAGRAPH 1:  
"The play shows that Christopher finds the world scary and loud. In the train station, the stage directions say there is a lot of noise and flashing lights. This makes Christopher groan and curl up in a ball. He likes maths because it is safe. The audience feels sorry for him because he is overwhelmed. It shows that people with autism see the world differently to us."  
PARAGRAPH 2:  
"Stephens utilizes immersive stagecraft to force the audience to inhabit Christopher’s sensory experience, rather than merely observing it. The cacophony of 'electronic noise' and the fragmented lighting design do not just show Christopher's distress; they physically assault the audience, bridging the gap of neurodivergence through shared visceral experience. By retreating into the logic of mathematics, Christopher seeks order in a chaotic world. Stephens challenges the label of 'disability,' reframing Christopher’s perspective not as broken, but as a hyper-lucid lens that exposes the illogical and overwhelming nature of the 'normal' world."

#### **OPTION E: SQA TEXTS (Bold Girls / Tally's Blood)**

The Task: "Explore how the writer presents the theme of conflict/identity."  
Focus: Key conflict scene (e.g., Marie vs Cassie / Rosinella's prejudice).

PARAGRAPH 1:  
"The writer shows that the characters are fighting about their past. In Bold Girls, Cassie is angry at Marie for pretending everything is fine. She smashes the picture to show she is done with lies. In Tally's Blood, Rosinella doesn't want Massimo to help his family because she is selfish. This shows the conflict between the characters and how the Troubles or the War affects them."  
PARAGRAPH 2:  
"The writer utilizes the domestic setting to microcosmically explore the wider political trauma of the era. In Bold Girls, Cassie’s destruction of the picture is a symbolic shattering of the 'saintly' mythology Marie has constructed to survive the brutal reality of Belfast. It is an act of iconoclasm that forces the truth to surface. Similarly, in Tally's Blood, Rosinella’s prejudice is presented not just as personal flaw, but as a defensive mechanism forged by the displacement of war. The writer suggests that the external political conflict has invaded the private sphere, fracturing relationships and warping individual identity."

### **PHASE 4: THE ANATOMY OF THE WEAPON**

**"Why is Paragraph 2 a Weapon? (The TTECEA Structure)"**

Break down the winning paragraph to show the student the hidden structure:

1. **T (Topic Sentence):** Sets up a **Concept/Argument** (e.g., "Sheila as a proxy," "Mickey as a product of class").  
2. **T (Technique):** Identifies the method (Symbolism, Declarative, Stagecraft).  
3. **E (Evidence):** Embeds the quote or reference precisely.  
4. **C (Close Analysis):** Zooms in on implications (e.g., "rejection of dogmatic worldview").  
5. **E (Effect):** Explains the impact on the audience/reader (2 Sentences).  
6. **A (Author's Purpose/Context):** Explains *why* the writer did this (The Social Message).
