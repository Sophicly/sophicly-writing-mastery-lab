# Effects Knowledge Bank (Shared — All Subjects)

**Version:** 1.0.0
**Scope:** Universal — Language Papers AND Literature (all boards)
**Source:** Adapted from OCR Poetry Protocol §1.E — expanded for universal use

---

## Purpose

Teach students to write precise, level-appropriate effects analysis. This is the single biggest differentiator between mid-band and top-band responses across ALL exam boards.

## The Focus → Emotions → Thoughts → Actions Hierarchy

Use this hierarchy to scaffold students from basic to sophisticated effects analysis:

| Level | Statement Type | Example |
|-------|---------------|---------|
| **Level 3 — Focus** | "This draws attention to..." | "This draws attention to the speaker's isolation." |
| **Level 4 — Emotions** | "This creates [feeling] in the reader..." | "This creates a sense of unease in the reader." |
| **Level 5 — Thoughts** | "This causes the reader to question / reconsider..." | "This causes the reader to question whether the speaker's grief is really for themselves rather than the other person." |
| **Level 6 — Actions** | "This challenges / implicates / transforms the reader..." | "This implicates the reader in the speaker's complicity — we are invited to recognise our own capacity for the same wilful self-deception." |

**[AI_INTERNAL]:** When a student writes a Level 3 effect, use Socratic questioning to push toward Level 5–6: "That's a good observation — but what does the reader now THINK or QUESTION because of this? How might it change their assumptions?"

## Emotional Effects Vocabulary

Replace vague "it makes the reader feel sad" with precise vocabulary:

| Category | Precise Vocabulary |
|----------|--------------------|
| Comfort / Peace | consolation, serenity, solace, harmony, resolution |
| Tension / Unease | disquiet, apprehension, foreboding, dread, trepidation |
| Empathy / Care | compassion, tenderness, solidarity, protectiveness |
| Outrage / Injustice | indignation, moral outrage, righteous anger |
| Awe / Wonder | reverence, astonishment, transcendence |
| Melancholy / Loss | elegiac sadness, wistfulness, yearning, desolation, bereft |
| Joy / Celebration | exhilaration, elation, warmth, delight, buoyancy |
| Alienation | exclusion, disconnection, invisibility, marginalisation |
| Irony / Critique | exposure, subversion, challenge to assumptions |
| Surprise / Disruption | disconcerted, startled, destabilised, jolted |

## Weak vs Strong Examples

**Weak (Level 3):** "The word 'feast' shows the speaker is happy."

**Strong (Level 6):** "The imperative 'Feast on your life' transforms self-acceptance from passive contentment to active, sensuous celebration — the reader is invited to experience self-love not as quiet satisfaction but as a banquet of full engagement with existence, challenging the cultural tendency to reserve feasting exclusively for relationships with others."

## Scaffolding Questions (Use During Planning and Polishing)

- "What feeling does this create in you specifically?"
- "What does the reader now understand or believe because of this technique?"
- "How does this change or challenge assumptions the reader might bring?"
- "What is the emotional or intellectual journey the reader takes?"
- "Could you upgrade 'sad' to something more precise — wistful? Elegiac? Bereft?"

## Ideas vs Concepts — Quick Reference

| Idea (Mid-band) | Concept (Top-band) |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| "The poem is about conflict" | "The poet uses conflict to interrogate political injustice — where X presents it as systemic and external, Y internalises it as psychological fracture" |
| "The writer uses imagery" | "The writer deploys a semantic field of [X] to construct a sustained parallel between [Y] and [Z], suggesting [abstract claim]" |
| "The character is angry" | "The writer presents anger not as emotional reaction but as political consciousness — the character's rage is the text's argument for systemic change" |

## Technique Interrelationship (Level 5→6 Leap)

Writers layer techniques across form, structure, and language to compound effects. When planning or assessing any paragraph, always consider: "Is there another technique working alongside this one that AMPLIFIES its effect?"

**Example:** A dramatic monologue form works with controlled iambic pentameter (Structure) and matter-of-fact diction (Language) to create a chilling effect — the speaker sounds reasonable while describing something horrific. NEITHER the form alone NOR the language alone creates this effect; it is the compound.

**[AI_INTERNAL]:** This question should appear in EVERY body paragraph planning session. It is the single most effective way to push students from Level 5 to Level 6.
