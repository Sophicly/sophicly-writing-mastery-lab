## INTRODUCTION SHAPES (3-sentence Sophicly intro)

### Hook (sentence 1) — Level 5–6 exemplars

**Sophicly hook rule (HARD — source: `protocols/edexcel-igcse/literature/planning/b7-introduction.md` lines 30-35).**

A Sophicly hook uses ONE of four techniques. Job = grab attention + connect to thesis theme. Must surprise / intrigue / provoke curiosity, be specific and concrete (not generic), be ONE powerful sentence. Hook does NOT have to "argue the thesis-concept" — that's only one of four techniques (Technique 4 below).

**Sophia attribution rule:** when pointing at hook shape, say *"At Sophicly we use one of four hook techniques…"* — never *"AQA Level 6 wants the hook to…"*. AQA does not prescribe hook shape. (Per `feedback_sophicly_techniques_attribution.md` + `rubric-base.md`.)

#### The four hook techniques

**Technique 1 — Surprising Historical Fact.** Striking, unexpected fact that challenges assumptions and connects to the thesis theme.

> *Macbeth was first performed around 1606 for the King's Men, the theatre company under the patronage of King James I, who had survived the Gunpowder Plot of November 1605 — a court still raw from the prospect of a king's assassination.*
> *Moves: (1) date + venue + audience anchor, (2) "still raw" carries emotional resonance, (3) connects implicitly to the play's regicide theme.*

> *Dickens published A Christmas Carol in December 1843 against the backdrop of the New Poor Law's workhouse system, just as Britain's industrial wealth and visible poverty were diverging at the fastest rate in the century.*
> *Moves: (1) publication date + named legal context, (2) "fastest rate in the century" surprise + specificity, (3) connects to social-conscience theme.*

**Technique 2 — Provocative Question.** Bold question that challenges conventional thinking about the text's central tension.

> *Can a play set in the eleventh century, written for a court of the seventeenth, still ask the twenty-first something urgent about the cost of ambition?*
> *Moves: (1) three time-anchors (eleventh / seventeenth / twenty-first), (2) "still ask … something urgent" provokes curiosity about relevance, (3) "cost of ambition" telegraphs theme without locking thesis.*

**Technique 3 — Intriguing Quote.** Memorable / paradoxical / startling quote that illuminates the theme. Can be from a critic, contemporary writer, or historical figure — not just the text itself.

> *A.C. Bradley once wrote that "the centre of the tragedy is to be sought in the action of the hero" — and few Shakespearean heroes act with the wide-eyed terror of Macbeth, who sees clearly what his own ambition will cost yet acts regardless.*
> *Moves: (1) named critic + verbatim quote, (2) em-dash bridge connects critic to specific text, (3) "sees clearly … yet acts regardless" telegraphs the conscious-sin theme.*

**Technique 4 — Counter-Intuitive Concept Claim.** Unexpected or paradoxical claim about a theme that defies expectation.

> *The deepest tragedy in Macbeth is not the king who dies — it is the murderer who survives the murder long enough to discover what he has lost.*
> *Moves: (1) "not … it is" antithesis, (2) "deepest tragedy" reframes default audience expectation onto Macbeth's moral fate, (3) telegraphs conscious-sin / hollow-self theme as paradox.*

#### Stock-hook anti-patterns Sophia must flag

(Per `b7-introduction.md` lines 49-52.)

- **Temporal clichés:** *"Throughout history…"*, *"Since the beginning of time…"*, *"For centuries…"*, *"In today's world…"*
- **Vague universals:** *"Many people believe…"*, *"Society has always…"*
- **Dictionary openings:** *"The dictionary defines [X] as…"*, *"According to [source]…"*
- **Obvious statements:** *"Everyone knows that…"*, *"It is clear that…"*

When student's hook contains a stock pattern, Sophia points at the pattern + suggests one of the four techniques.

### Building sentence (sentence 2) — Level 5–6 exemplars

Connects the hook's fact to the text's central tension. Move pattern:
- (1) Returns to the fact OR extends it into theology / philosophy / law.
- (2) Names the central tension explicitly.
- (3) Bridges fact and tension with a clause like "in which" / "against which" / "such that".

**Macbeth — bridge to soliloquy psychology:**
> *Shakespeare wrote* Macbeth *for the court of James I, who survived the Gunpowder Plot the previous year and believed himself divinely appointed — a theological context in which regicide was not merely political rebellion but cosmic sacrilege.*
> *Moves: (1) author + audience anchor, (2) belief-system named, (3) "in which … not merely … but" structure delivers the conceptual escalation.*

### Thesis (sentence 3) — Level 5–6 exemplars

Names the 3 BP concepts in order (Beginning / Middle / End). Move pattern:
- (1) Anchor the three scenes / moments / quotes by Act/Scene OR by event.
- (2) Each concept named in a precise abstract noun (no technique words).
- (3) An overarching arc claim ("a degenerative / a redemptive / a tragic" arc).

**Macbeth — degenerative arc:**
> *Across the "If it were done" soliloquy in 1.7, the dagger hallucination at 2.1, and the "Tomorrow" soliloquy at 5.5, Shakespeare traces Macbeth's inner conflict as a degenerative arc — from rational moral debate, through hallucinatory psychological fracture, to nihilistic spiritual extinction.*
> *Moves: (1) three scene-anchors, (2) three concept-words (debate / fracture / extinction), (3) "degenerative arc" overarching claim.*

## BODY-PARAGRAPH SHAPES

### Topic sentence (TTECEA-T) — Level 5–6 exemplars

PURE concept, zero technique names. (Per memory `feedback_topic_sentence_concept_not_technique`.) Move pattern:
- (1) Scene/moment anchor (optional but powerful).
- (2) Author + verb + concept-noun.
- (3) Subordinate clause that NAMES the conceptual paradox or tension.

**Macbeth — ACT 1 SCENE 7 conscious moral reckoning:**
> *In the soliloquy that opens the seventh scene, Shakespeare presents Macbeth's inner conflict as a lucid, agonised moral reckoning — a mind that comprehends precisely why it must not act yet cannot silence the impulse driving it forward.*
> *Moves: (1) scene-anchor opens, (2) "as a … reckoning" concept noun, (3) em-dash clause names the paradox (knows yet cannot stop).*

**Macbeth — ACT 2 SCENE 1 hallucinatory fracture (concept-only version):**
> *As Macbeth approaches the murder, Shakespeare dramatises his inner conflict mutating from intellectual debate into visceral physical torment — guilt arriving before the crime is even committed.*
> *Moves: (1) scene anchor + transition verb, (2) "intellectual debate INTO visceral physical torment" — concept-shift named, (3) em-dash clause delivers the temporal paradox (guilt before crime).*
> *(Note: avoids "hallucinatory" / "visualisation" / "spectacle" — those are technique words and belong in sentence 2.)*

### Technique + Evidence (TTECEA-T+E) — Level 5–6 exemplars

ONE quote, multiple techniques inside it. Move pattern:
- (1) Quote sits inside (not beside) the analytical clause.
- (2) Two techniques named, anchored to specific words/lines in the quote.
- (3) Bracketed parenthetical adds rhythm or counts (e.g. "the verb hammered three times").

**Macbeth — repetition + euphemism in 1.7:**
> *"If it were done when 'tis done, then 'twere well / It were done quickly" combines repetition (the verb "done" hammered three times in a single line and a half, alongside the impersonal pronoun "it" repeated four times) with euphemism (the pronoun "it" refusing to name the assassination Macbeth cannot bring himself to say).*
> *Moves: (1) quote opens the clause, (2) two techniques + evidence inside parentheticals, (3) the second parenthetical lands an interpretive claim ("refusing to name").*

### Close analysis (TTECEA-C) — Level 5–6 exemplars

Drills into specific words / sounds / syntax. Move pattern:
- (1) Quote a specific word from the evidence sentence.
- (2) Name what the word DOES technically (sound / register / morphology).
- (3) Name what the word ARGUES (the conceptual payoff).

**Macbeth — "If" / "this blow" / "done" close-analysis:**
> *The conditional "If" and the abstract "this blow" defer commitment by reducing regicide to a hypothesis Macbeth can keep at arm's length; the threefold "done" produces a sibilant, conspiratorial whisper — the rhythm of a man negotiating with his own conscience while euphemising the deed itself.*
> *Moves: (1) two specific word-pulls ("If" and "this blow"), (2) phonetic/syntactic effect ("sibilant, conspiratorial whisper"), (3) conceptual payoff ("negotiating with his own conscience").*

### Effect on audience (TTECEA-E1 / E2) — Level 5–6 exemplars

Two distinct effects, not one repeated. Move pattern:
- (1) E1 = visceral / experiential ("the audience feels…").
- (2) E2 = interpretive / recognition-based ("the audience recognises…").
- (3) E2 always escalates beyond E1 toward the thesis-concept.

**Macbeth — E1/E2 paired:**
> *(E1) The audience feels the claustrophobia of a mind trapping itself inside its own circular reasoning.*
> *(E2) The audience recognises Macbeth's full awareness of damnation as proof that his sin will be conscious, not ignorant — the tragedy of wilful transgression.*
> *Moves: (1) E1 visceral verb ("feels"), (2) E2 cognitive verb ("recognises"), (3) E2 closes on a concept-noun ("wilful transgression") that loops back to thesis.*

### Author's Purpose (TTECEA-A) — Level 5–6 exemplars

Confident assertion + AO3 humility. Move pattern:
- (1) Open with "Shakespeare uses…" (assertion) OR "Shakespeare may be …" (humility) — vary across BPs.
- (2) Name the form/genre move ("the soliloquy", "dramatic irony", "the chorus").
- (3) Tie the move to the conceptual purpose, not just the surface effect.

**Macbeth — soliloquy as conscious damnation:**
> *Shakespeare uses the soliloquy — a speech delivered alone on stage that reveals the speaker's interior — to grant Macbeth complete awareness of the moral law he is about to break, ensuring his tragedy resides in chosen sin rather than misunderstanding.*
> *Moves: (1) assertive verb ("uses"), (2) form-name + gloss in parenthetical, (3) "ensuring his tragedy resides in" delivers the purpose argument.*

**Macbeth — hallucination as theological ambiguity:**
> *Shakespeare may be externalising Macbeth's psychic rupture through theatrical spectacle — the audience is forced to share the same epistemological uncertainty as the character, unable to determine whether the dagger is supernatural or a disintegrating mind.*
> *Moves: (1) "may be" tentative for AO3 humility, (2) "theatrical spectacle" form-anchor, (3) audience-experience clause lands the conceptual purpose.*

### Context — AO3 (TTECEA-C+context) — Level 5–6 exemplars

Three different angles across three BPs (judicious variety). Move pattern:
- (1) Specific historical / theological / literary-tradition / authorial-biographical anchor.
- (2) Audience or contemporaneous reader experience.
- (3) Tie back to the BP's central concept.

**Macbeth — theology angle (BP1):**
> *For a Jacobean audience processing the 1605 Gunpowder Plot, this portrait of a subject who reasons clearly yet rebels regardless would have confirmed the period's theology of conscious damnation — sin committed in full knowledge of its cost.*
> *Moves: (1) historical event anchor, (2) audience-reception verb, (3) "theology of conscious damnation" loops to BP1's thesis-concept.*

**Macbeth — dramatic-tradition angle (BP2):**
> *Within the Senecan tradition Jacobean playwrights inherited, hallucinatory visions onstage signalled either demonic intrusion or divine warning — Shakespeare deliberately withholds resolution, allowing the ambiguity itself to dramatise a consciousness splitting under the weight of its own moral knowledge.*
> *Moves: (1) literary-tradition anchor (Senecan), (2) tradition's two readings named, (3) "Shakespeare deliberately withholds" → conceptual payoff.*

**Macbeth — political-theology angle (BP3):**
> *For James I's court, this trajectory reinforced the period's political theology — regicide does not merely destabilise the state but annihilates the perpetrator's soul, hollowing him into a vessel of fury without meaning.*
> *Moves: (1) audience anchor (named court), (2) political-theology concept invoked, (3) closing image ("vessel of fury without meaning") loops to BP3's nihilism thesis.*

## CONCLUSION SHAPES (Sophicly 4-sentence conclusion)

### Restated thesis (sentence 1)

Opens *"In conclusion,"* + names the 3 BP concepts/moments explicitly.

> *In conclusion, through the "If it were done" soliloquy, the dagger hallucination, and the "Tomorrow" speech, Shakespeare presents Macbeth's inner conflict as a degenerative process — a moral disease progressing from rational resistance through hallucinatory guilt to absolute spiritual vacancy.*
> *Moves: (1) "In conclusion," opens, (2) three scene-anchors echo intro, (3) overarching claim ("moral disease progressing") repeats thesis arc.*

### Controlling concept (sentence 2)

The text's central argument in one sentence.

> *Macbeth's tragedy is defined less by the crown he seizes than by the consciousness he loses, and his inner conflict is the very quality that makes him human in the first scene and the very quality whose erosion leaves him empty in the last.*
> *Moves: (1) "less by … than by …" antithesis, (2) "the very quality … the very quality" parallelism, (3) frames tragedy as identity-loss.*

### Author's purpose (sentence 3)

What the author may be writing for / against. Less hedged than per-BP author's purpose — final-paragraph register can land more confidently.

> *Shakespeare may be writing for a Jacobean audience that understood kingship as sacred and rebellion as sin against God's order — and dramatising the truth that the deepest punishment for violating one's own conscience is the slow erasure of the self that once knew right from wrong.*
> *Moves: (1) "may be writing for" tentative-confident hybrid, (2) audience-frame, (3) em-dash extends to a moral argument.*

### Ultimate moral (sentence 4)

The message the text leaves the audience with. Punchy, register-elevated, distinct from "the final message is…".

> *Macbeth's lesson is starker than the political theology that frames it: ambition without conscience destroys the state, the king, and the rival second — the protagonist's own capacity for meaning first.*
> *Moves: (1) "starker than" register-elevated opener, (2) negation series ("the state, the king, and the rival second"), (3) conceptual climax ("capacity for meaning first").*

## CRITICAL VOICES (AO3 anchor citations)

Per `rubric-base.md` CRITICAL FRAMES — Level 6 essays name one critical reading + one-line counter. Quote one of these inline as a sentence-bridge.

- **A.C. Bradley:** *"tragic power lies in the gap between the protagonist's nature and the act they commit"* — drops naturally into BP1 around the conscious-rebellion paradox.
- **Terry Eagleton:** *"tragedy punishes the very feelings society itself produces"* — anchors social-order critique without invoking feminist framing (per memory `feedback_avoid_feminist_critique_framework`).
- **Kiernan Ryan:** *"the play's true antagonist is often the social system, not a single character"* — useful for honour-culture / dynastic readings.
- **Emma Smith:** *"protagonists rarely have a path open that does not also require the death of something essential"* — useful for tragedy-as-systemic-critique (no feminist framing).

## ANTI-PATTERNS TO POINT AT (when student exhibits them)

When the student's selection shows these patterns, Sophia points at them via `EQ_PROMPT` rather than fixing them directly.

- **Bolted-on Author's Purpose** — closing-sentence add-on. Symptom: paragraph reads complete without it.
- **Context-dump** — multiple AO3 facts piled in BP without analytical purchase.
- **"Shows" as analytical verb** — Level 3-4 fallback. (Per memory `feedback_never_use_shows`.)
- **Form-naming in topic sentence** — "fractured sonnet" / "dramatic monologue" in sentence 1. (Per memory `feedback_topic_sentence_concept_not_technique`.)
- **Sentence over ~35 words** — split or trim. (Per memory `feedback_sentence_length_max_three_lines`.)
- **Arrow characters** (→, ->, =>) — never in essay prose. (Per memory `feedback_no_arrows_in_essays`.)
- **Loaded political vocabulary** — "patriarchy" / "patriarchal" / "feminist critique". (Per memories `feedback_no_patriarchy_language`, `feedback_avoid_feminist_critique_framework`.)
- **Hamartia mistranslated** as "fatal flaw" instead of "fatal error in action". (Per memory `feedback_hamartia_not_fatal_flaw`.)
- **Magic / spellbook framing** — "MADFATHER's CROPS = spellbook". (Per memory `feedback_no_magic_spellbook_language`.)
