# Rubric — AQA GCSE Literature, Paper 2 Section B (Anthology Poetry — Comparison)

**Loads on top of:** `rubric-base.md`. Universal Sophicly rules apply unless overridden here.

**Used by:** `inline-coaching-engine-1.md` for tasks `task='exam_crib'` (Power & Conflict / Love & Relationships / Worlds and Lives anthologies) + `task='polishing'` (AQA Lit P2 Section B redraft).

**Paper structure:** Single 45-minute comparison question — TWO named anthology poems. **30 marks** (AO1+AO2+AO3 only — NO AO4 SPaG marks).

---

## ASSESSMENT OBJECTIVES + WEIGHTING (PAPER 2 SECTION B)

| AO | Marks | Description |
|---|---:|---|
| AO1 | ~12 | Read, understand, respond + textual references — to BOTH poems. |
| AO2 | ~12 | Analyse language / form / structure for meaning + effect — including comparison of methods. |
| AO3 | ~6 | Context — relationships between BOTH poems and contexts. |
| AO4 | — | NOT assessed in this section. |

**Pointer phrase:** *Anthology poetry comparison rewards comparative method analysis (AO2) — not parallel single-poem essays bolted together.*

---

## QUESTION SHAPE — COMPARISON

> Compare how poets present [X] in '[Poem 1]' and one other poem from [Anthology].

One poem named, second poem chosen by student.

**Critical:** the Q is a comparison Q. Sequential treatment (Poem 1 in BP1, Poem 2 in BP2) caps at Level 3. Comparative treatment per BP (BOTH poems compared on a single concept) earns Level 5+.

**Pointer phrase:** *Comparison Qs reward comparative analysis WITHIN each BP — not sequential parallel treatments.*

---

## LEVEL DESCRIPTORS

Same Level 3-6 ladder as Shakespeare. Per-paper Q-shape differs but mark-scheme language matches.

**Comparison-specific addition:** Level 5+ requires *exploration of similarities AND differences* in methods, not just themes.

---

## TTECEA SHAPE — ADAPTED FOR COMPARISON

For comparison Qs, TTECEA expands to compare both poems within each BP:

1. T (concept-only — applies to BOTH poems).
2. T+e (technique + quote from POEM 1).
3. T+e (technique + quote from POEM 2 — compared).
4. C (close analysis — words inside BOTH quotes, comparing).
5. E (effect — single combined effect across both poems, OR distinct E1 / E2 per poem).
6. A (Author's purpose — comparing what the two poets are arguing).
7. C (Context — both poems' contexts compared / contrasted).

**Pointer phrase:** *Comparative TTECEA folds two poems into one BP — concept first, then both poems' techniques + quotes, then comparison of effects + purposes.*

## HOOK RATIO — FACT-HEAVY (PER MEMORY)

For anthology comparison essays, hook ratio per memory `feedback_hook_ratio_lean_fewer_questions`:

- ~1 question hook per 6 poems.
- ~2-3 fact hooks per 6 poems.
- ~2-3 quote hooks per 6 poems.

Too many Q-hooks feel pretentious. Lead with fact + quote.

**Pointer phrase:** *Anthology essay hooks lean fact-heavy — too many rhetorical questions feel pretentious.*

---

## ANTHOLOGY-SPECIFIC ANTI-PATTERNS

(Stack on top of base anti-patterns.)

- **Sequential single-poem treatment.** Each BP discusses only one poem in turn. Caps at Level 3.
- **Theme-only comparison.** Comparing only what the poems argue, not how (their methods). AO2 demands method-comparison.
- **No second-poem choice.** Student picks Poem 2 but never names it explicitly. Must be named in intro.
- **Form-naming in topic sentence.** "This sonnet shows..." → form belongs in sentence 2 (per `rubric-base.md`).
- **Excessive Q-hooks** at intro / between BPs (per hook ratio above).
- **Free indirect / dramatic monologue / dramatic verse named in topic.** Forms appear in sentence 2.
- **Generic "the poet uses imagery."** Too vague — name the specific image and what it does.

## CONTROLLING-CONCEPT EXEMPLARS — POEM-LEVEL

For anthology, controlling concepts are per-poem and combine in comparative thesis:

- **Ozymandias:** *"Power inscribed in stone is the most fragile kind — Time outlasts every empire's pretension."*

- **My Last Duchess:** *"Aristocratic possession destroys the very person it tries to keep."*
- **Charge of the Light Brigade:** *"Honour and obedience can be the most beautiful — and the most criminal — of military virtues."*
