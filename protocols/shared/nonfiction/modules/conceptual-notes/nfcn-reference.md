## Appendix A: Text Type Quick Reference

### Text Type Categories by Primary Function

| Primary Function | Text Type Family | Key Conventions | Meaning Function |
|-----------------|-----------------|-----------------|------------------|
| Persuade / Inspire | Speech, Opinion Piece, Personal Essay | Direct address, rhetorical devices, call to action, personal anecdotes, inclusive pronouns | Changes beliefs; creates solidarity; inspires action |
| Inform / Report | Newspaper Article, Reportage | Factual reporting, quoted sources, editorial angle, inverted pyramid, third-person distance | Shapes public understanding; creates informed citizens |
| Describe / Immerse | Travel Writing, Literary Memoir | Vivid description, sensory detail, cultural observation, artful prose, personal response | Creates vicarious experience; broadens perspective |
| Reflect / Explore | Memoir, Autobiography | First-person reflection, selective memory, hindsight, identity construction, emotional honesty | Builds empathy; illuminates human experience |
| Argue / Expose | Personal Essay, Investigative Reportage | First-person argument, evidence, conversational tone, clear thesis | Challenges assumptions; reveals hidden truths |

Most sophisticated texts blend functions. Establish PRIMARY function first, identify SECONDARY functions, explain WHY the writer combines them, show how the blend creates MEANING.

## Appendix B: Writer's Voice Categories — Quick Reference

| Category | Definition | Typical Texts | Key Questions |
|----------|-----------|---------------|---------------|
| **A: Writer as Central Voice** | The writer IS the subject. They describe their own experiences, share their own views, or argue their own position. | Adichie (speech), Ralston (autobiography), Zephaniah (personal essay), Yen Mah (autobiography) | What does the writer reveal about themselves? How does their personal experience become universal? |
| **B: Writer as Observer/Reporter** | The writer describes others' experiences or events. They have a perspective but aren't the 'main character.' | Morris (journalist), Levine (travel writer) | What does the writer choose to focus on? How does their position as outsider/observer shape the account? |
| **C: Writer as Reflective Narrator** | A hybrid — the writer reflects on events they witnessed or participated in. Focus shifts between inner experience and external subjects. | Alagiah (reflective journalist), Herbert (reflective observer), Zeppa (reflective traveller), Macdonald (literary memoirist) | How does the writer's personal response interact with the external events? Where does the text shift between inner and outer? |

## Appendix C: "Did You Know" Strategic Deployment Guide

### Tracking & Limits
- Maximum 3 deployments per complete 8-section session
- Mental tracking: Note each use as you deploy
- Distribution: Ideally spread across sections, not clustered

### High-Impact Deployment Moments

**Section 1 (Writer's Voice) - Best Moments:**
- Step 6B: When identifying the writer's conceptual perspective
- Step 9: When connecting perspective development to meaning

Example: "Did you know that Adichie deliberately opens her speech by calling herself a 'storyteller' rather than an 'author' or 'academic'? This choice of identity positions her argument as being about EVERYONE's stories — not just literary ones. How might this identity choice shape her perspective?"

**Section 2 (Context) - Best Moments:**
- Step 2: Connecting context of subject to real-world events
- Step 6: How context creates deeper meaning

Example: "Did you know that Alagiah's reporting in Somalia coincided with the 'CNN effect' — the theory that 24-hour news coverage could pressure governments into humanitarian intervention? His text doesn't just describe suffering; it grapples with whether his OWN reporting was part of the solution or part of the problem."

**Section 3 (Structure) - Best Moments:**
- Step 2: Structure deep dive
- Step 5: How structure creates meaning

Example: "Did you know that Herbert deliberately places factual information about the Inughuit people's survival BETWEEN two emotional descriptions of the narwhal hunt? This forces the reader through the same mental journey she experienced — from sentiment to pragmatism. The structure doesn't just organise content; it PERSUADES."

**Section 4 (Text Type) - Best Moments:**
- Step 2: Effect Chain analysis
- Step 5: How text type creates meaning

Example: "Did you know that text types are cultural technologies — not just ways of arranging words? A TED Talk is designed for maximum virality: one clear idea, personal narrative, and a memorable phrase. When Adichie distils post-colonial theory into 'the danger of a single story,' she's using the FORM to spread her argument far beyond academia."

**Section 5 (Techniques) - Best Moments:**
- Step 2: Rhetorical technique identification
- Step 4: How techniques work together

Example: "Did you know that Morris's article in The Guardian uses almost every technique of objective journalism — quoted sources, factual detail, attribution — but deploys them in a way that is deeply subjective and critical? The 'neutral' journalistic techniques become weapons of mockery. How does Morris turn 'objective' reporting into criticism?"

**Section 6 (Themes) - Best Moments:**
- Step 2: Deepening theme-text connection
- Step 4: How theme creates meaning

Example: "Did you know that many of these anthology writers grapple with the same fundamental tension — the ethics of turning other people's lives into stories? Alagiah, Herbert, and Levine all face moments where the act of observing becomes morally complicated. In your text, how does [author] navigate this tension?"

**Section 7 (Purpose) - Best Moments:**
- Step 2: Evidence for purpose

Example: "Did you know that Zephaniah's personal essay was published in The Guardian — a newspaper read primarily by educated, liberal adults? His audience is NOT dyslexic children — it's the parents, teachers, and policymakers who shape how dyslexia is understood. How does knowing the audience change your understanding of his purpose?"

**Section 8 (Overall Message) - Best Moments:**
- Step 2: Message articulation
- Step 4: Contemporary application

Example: "Did you know that Adichie's concept of the 'single story' has been cited in over 3,000 academic papers and adopted in fields from international development to mental health? Her TED Talk hasn't just informed — it's created a new vocabulary for talking about the power of narrative. What does this lasting impact tell us about her overall message?"

### Quality Control Checklist

Before deploying any "Did You Know":
- ✓ Has student struggled despite scaffolding?
- ✓ Will this insight lead to conceptual breakthrough?
- ✓ Is it directly relevant to their current work?
- ✓ Have you used fewer than 3 already?
- ✓ Will it enhance rather than replace their thinking?

## Appendix D: Quick Reference — Question Bank

### Conceptual Trigger Questions

When students need to move from description to concept:

- "What aspect of humanity does this represent?"
- "What social issue is being explored here?"
- "What universal tension or struggle does this embody?"
- "If this writer's perspective represents an attitude or worldview, what is it?"
- "What would the original audience have understood that we might miss?"
- "How does [specific technique] serve [the writer]'s purpose?"
- "What shift or development is being traced here?"

### Meaning Trigger Questions

When students need to articulate meaning:

- "So what? Why does this matter?"
- "What does [the writer] want us to understand from this?"
- "What would be lost if this element was removed?"
- "Why did [the writer] make this choice instead of another?"
- "What does this tell us about human nature/society/morality?"
- "How does this connect to [the writer]'s purpose?"
- "What are we meant to FEEL, and why?"

### Non-Fiction-Specific Trigger Questions

When students need to engage with the text's real-world dimension:

- "Why does this real person's perspective matter?"
- "How does knowing the context change what we understand?"
- "What would a different writer have seen differently in this situation?"
- "How does the form of this text shape the message?"
- "What ethical questions does this text raise about writing about real people/events?"
- "How does this text speak to our world today?"
