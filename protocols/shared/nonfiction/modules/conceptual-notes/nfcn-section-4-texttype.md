### Why Text Type Matters (Conceptual Foundation)

**Guiding Principle for This Section:**
Text type in non-fiction functions like genre in fiction — it tells us what conventions to expect, how the reader is positioned, and what kind of meaning the form creates. A speech positions us as an audience being directly addressed; a memoir invites us into private memory; a newspaper article presents us with curated information. Understanding text type helps us see HOW the writer uses the conventions of their chosen form to create specific effects.

**The Effect Chain:**
TEXT TYPE → CONVENTIONS → READER POSITION → RESPONSE → ACTION

Every text type carries this chain. When students identify the text type, they should trace the complete chain to understand how form shapes meaning.

**If Speech (including TED Talk, political speech, public address):**

"Texts in the SPEECH form address one of humanity's oldest needs: to persuade, unite, or inspire through spoken word. The speech is perhaps the most ancient form of non-fiction — from Greek oratory to modern TED Talks.

**Why This Form Matters:**
A speech creates an IMMEDIATE relationship between speaker and audience. Unlike a book that you read alone, a speech assumes a listener who is present and who can be directly addressed, challenged, and moved. The speaker must earn the audience's attention in real time — they can't rely on the reader turning the page.

**How the Form Works:**
- The speaker establishes **ethos** (credibility) early — why should we listen to THIS person?
- **Direct address** ('you,' 'we,' 'us') creates solidarity and shared experience
- **Personal anecdotes** make abstract arguments feel concrete and human
- The argument builds through **emotional escalation** — each example more powerful than the last
- A **clear, memorable core message** is distilled — a phrase the audience takes away
- The **call to action** at the end tells the audience what to DO with what they've heard

**Key Convention: The Power of the Platform**
The PLACE where a speech is delivered matters as much as its content. A TED Talk reaches a global online audience. A parliamentary speech reaches legislators. A classroom speech reaches students. The platform determines who hears the message and what they can do about it. When analysing a speech, always ask: WHO is in the room, and WHAT POWER do they have to act?

How does [text_title] use these speech conventions to persuade or inspire? How does [author] position themselves as a speaker worthy of our attention?"

---

**If Memoir:**

"Texts in the MEMOIR form invite the reader into the writer's private experience. Unlike autobiography (which tells a whole life story), memoir zooms in on a specific period, event, or theme — using personal memory as a lens for understanding something larger.

**Why This Form Matters:**
Memoir creates INTIMACY. When a writer shares their memories — selectively, honestly, with the benefit of hindsight — they're offering us something private. This creates a powerful reader position: we become the confidant, the trusted listener, the person deemed worthy of hearing this story. That intimacy generates empathy in a way that objective reporting cannot.

**How the Form Works:**
- **Selective memory** — the writer doesn't tell us everything, only what matters. What they choose to include IS the argument
- **Retrospective insight** — the writer knows how the story ends, which gives their narration a layer of wisdom or irony
- **Sensory detail** — vivid descriptions of what they saw, heard, felt, creating the sense that we WERE THERE
- **Emotional honesty** — the writer reveals vulnerability, confusion, or failure alongside triumph
- **The gap between then and now** — the difference between the person who experienced events and the person writing about them creates meaning

**Key Convention: The Unreliable Narrator Problem**
All memoir is filtered through memory, which is selective, subjective, and sometimes self-serving. This isn't a weakness — it's the form's greatest strength. The way a writer REMEMBERS reveals as much as what they remember. When analysing memoir, always ask: What does the writer's WAY OF REMEMBERING tell us about what this experience meant to them?

How does [text_title] use memoir conventions to create intimacy? What does [author]'s selective use of memory reveal about what this experience meant to them?"

---

**If Travel Writing:**

"Texts in the TRAVEL WRITING form describe encounters with unfamiliar places, cultures, and people. The writer is always an outsider looking in — and this outsider position is both the form's strength and its central ethical tension.

**Why This Form Matters:**
Travel writing satisfies our curiosity about worlds beyond our own. It creates VICARIOUS EXPERIENCE — we travel without leaving our chair. But it also raises profound questions about who has the right to describe another culture, whose perspective is centered, and whether the 'exotic' gaze distorts what it describes.

**How the Form Works:**
- **Vivid description** creates sensory immersion — sights, sounds, smells, tastes, textures of the unfamiliar
- **Personal response** — the writer doesn't just describe; they reveal how the experience AFFECTS them
- **Comparison with home** — the unfamiliar is often understood through the lens of the familiar
- **Cultural observation** — the writer interprets customs, behaviours, and values
- **The outsider position** — the writer is always, to some degree, a visitor, a foreigner, a spectator

**Key Convention: The Outsider's Gaze**
Travel writing is always filtered through the writer's own cultural assumptions. What seems 'exotic,' 'strange,' or 'beautiful' reveals as much about the writer's home culture as about the place being described. The most sophisticated travel writing is AWARE of this — it questions its own assumptions even as it describes. When analysing travel writing, always ask: What does the writer's REACTION reveal about their own cultural position?

How does [text_title] use travel writing conventions to immerse the reader? How does [author]'s outsider position shape what they see and how they describe it?"

---

**If Newspaper Article:**

"Texts in the NEWSPAPER ARTICLE form present information to a general readership with an apparently objective stance. But 'objectivity' in journalism is a constructed performance — every choice of angle, headline, source, and detail shapes how the reader understands the story.

**Why This Form Matters:**
Newspaper articles shape public opinion. They determine which events matter, which people are heroes or villains, and which perspectives get heard. The form's apparent neutrality gives it enormous persuasive power — readers trust 'the news' in a way they might not trust an opinion piece, even when the article is just as carefully constructed to guide their response.

**How the Form Works:**
- **Headline framing** — the headline tells the reader HOW to interpret the story before they've read it
- **Inverted pyramid** — the most important information comes first (in case readers stop reading)
- **Quoted sources** — direct quotes from witnesses, experts, or officials create the appearance of balance
- **Third-person distance** — the journalist typically writes in third person, creating apparent objectivity
- **Editorial angle** — despite apparent neutrality, the selection and arrangement of facts IS an argument

**Key Convention: The Constructed Objectivity**
No news article is truly objective. The journalist decides what to include, what to omit, who to quote, and in what order. The HEADLINE alone is an interpretation, not a neutral statement. When analysing a newspaper article, always ask: What is the journalist's ANGLE? How do they want us to feel about the people and events described, and what techniques create that feeling while maintaining the appearance of neutrality?

How does [text_title] use journalistic conventions? Where does the apparent objectivity conceal an editorial angle or attitude?"

---

**If Autobiography:**

"Texts in the AUTOBIOGRAPHY form tell the story of the writer's own life. The writer is simultaneously the subject, the narrator, and the constructor of their own identity through narrative.

**Why This Form Matters:**
Autobiography is an act of IDENTITY CONSTRUCTION. By choosing which events to include, which to emphasise, and which to interpret, the writer creates a version of themselves — a narrative identity that makes sense of lived experience. We read autobiography not just for the facts of someone's life, but for how they MAKE SENSE of those facts.

**How the Form Works:**
- **First-person narration** — 'I' is both the character living the events and the narrator reflecting on them
- **Chronological or thematic ordering** — life events are arranged to create a coherent narrative arc
- **Formative experiences** — the writer selects moments that explain who they became
- **Identity construction** — the narrative answers the question 'How did I become who I am?'
- **Hindsight** — the adult narrator understands what the younger self could not

**Key Convention: The Two Selves**
Every autobiography contains at least two selves: the person who LIVED the experience and the person who WRITES about it. These two selves often have different levels of understanding. The child who was rejected doesn't understand why; the adult who writes about that rejection does. This gap between experiencing self and narrating self is where much of the meaning lies. When analysing autobiography, always ask: What does the NARRATING SELF understand that the EXPERIENCING SELF didn't?

How does [text_title] use autobiographical conventions? Where do you see the gap between the experiencing self and the narrating self creating meaning?"

---

**If Personal Essay / Opinion Piece:**

"Texts in the PERSONAL ESSAY form use the writer's own experience as the foundation for an argument. Unlike journalism, which claims objectivity, the personal essay is openly subjective — it says 'THIS is what I think, and HERE is why, and you should think about it too.'

**Why This Form Matters:**
The personal essay is uniquely persuasive because it combines the authority of lived experience with the clarity of argument. When a writer says 'I know this is true because I LIVED it,' they bypass abstract debate and ground their argument in flesh and blood. The form says: theory is one thing, but here is what it actually FEELS LIKE.

**How the Form Works:**
- **First-person argument** — the writer's voice IS the evidence
- **Personal experience as proof** — anecdotes serve as case studies for broader claims
- **Conversational tone** — the writer addresses the reader directly, as equals
- **Clear thesis** — there is always a central argument, even if it's arrived at gradually
- **Reframing** — the essay takes something the reader thinks they understand and shows them a different way to see it

**Key Convention: Experience as Authority**
In the personal essay, LIVED EXPERIENCE is the primary evidence. This gives the form enormous emotional power — but it also means the argument is only as strong as the reader's willingness to accept one person's experience as meaningful. The best personal essays anticipate this by connecting individual experience to universal truths. When analysing a personal essay, always ask: How does the writer move from 'THIS happened to ME' to 'this matters to ALL of us'?

How does [text_title] use personal essay conventions? How does [author] use their own experience to make an argument that extends beyond themselves?"

---

**If Literary Memoir:**

"Texts in the LITERARY MEMOIR form apply the techniques of literary fiction to real-life experience. The writer doesn't just report what happened — they CRAFT it, using metaphor, symbolism, imagery, and structural artistry to transform lived experience into art.

**Why This Form Matters:**
Literary memoir occupies a unique space between journalism and literature. It has the authenticity of real experience but the aesthetic power of fiction. This means the reader engages on TWO levels simultaneously: as someone learning about a real life, AND as someone experiencing carefully crafted prose. The beauty of the writing becomes part of the meaning — HOW something is said matters as much as WHAT is said.

**How the Form Works:**
- **Artful prose** — the writing itself is a source of pleasure and meaning, not just a vehicle for information
- **Metaphor and symbolism** — real objects, events, or beings become symbols for abstract ideas
- **Emotional depth** — the writer goes beyond describing feelings to CREATING them through craft
- **Thematic complexity** — the text operates on multiple levels simultaneously (literal, symbolic, philosophical)
- **Literary techniques applied to real life** — pathetic fallacy, imagery, motif, and structure serve the same purposes as in fiction

**Key Convention: The Objective Correlative**
In literary memoir, external objects often carry internal meaning. A hawk becomes grief. A landscape becomes a state of mind. A journey becomes self-discovery. This technique — using the concrete and physical to express the abstract and emotional — is the literary memoir's defining feature. When analysing literary memoir, always ask: What does the central OBJECT, CREATURE, or EXPERIENCE symbolise? How does the writer use it as a vehicle for exploring something internal?

How does [text_title] use literary memoir conventions? What does [author] use as a symbolic vehicle for their internal experience?"

---

**If Reportage:**

"Texts in the REPORTAGE form combine the factual rigour of journalism with the personal engagement of memoir. The writer is a professional witness — someone paid to be where we cannot go, to see what we cannot see, and to bring it back in a way that makes us CARE.

**Why This Form Matters:**
Reportage creates moral proximity. It takes distant events — wars, famines, crises — and brings them close enough to affect us. The journalist-narrator serves as our surrogate: they go INTO the situation, experience its impact, and share both the facts AND their emotional response. This double layer — objective information plus subjective reaction — is what makes reportage more powerful than either pure news or pure memoir.

**How the Form Works:**
- **Eyewitness authority** — 'I was there' creates immediate credibility
- **Descriptive immediacy** — vivid, present-tense-like description creates the sensation of being at the scene
- **Blend of fact and feeling** — statistical/factual information sits alongside personal emotional response
- **Ethical tension** — the journalist grapples with their role: observer or participant? Helper or documenter?
- **The distant audience** — the text bridges the gap between those who suffer and those who read about it from comfort

**Key Convention: The Journalist's Dilemma**
Reportage always carries an ethical tension: the journalist is simultaneously there to WITNESS and to REPORT. Sometimes these roles conflict. Documenting suffering can feel exploitative. Creating powerful images of crisis can reduce real people to symbols. The best reportage is AWARE of this tension and makes it part of the text's meaning. When analysing reportage, always ask: How does the writer navigate the tension between professional duty and human empathy? Does their text acknowledge the ethical complexity of turning real suffering into a piece of writing?

How does [text_title] use reportage conventions? Where do you see [author] navigating the tension between observing professionally and responding humanly?"
