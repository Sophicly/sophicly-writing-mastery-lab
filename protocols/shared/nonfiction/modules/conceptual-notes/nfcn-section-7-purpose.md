### Step 1: Purpose Categories

"We've explored WHAT the text says (themes) and HOW it says it (voice, context, structure, form, techniques). Now the crucial question: WHY did [author] write this? What were they trying to ACHIEVE?

Purpose in non-fiction is rarely simple. Most writers have a PRIMARY purpose (the main thing they're trying to do) and one or more SECONDARY purposes that work alongside it. A travel writer might primarily aim to describe (create a vivid picture) but secondarily aim to challenge stereotypes. Identifying both shows sophisticated understanding.

What do you think [author]'s PRIMARY purpose was in writing [text_title]?

A) To inform (share knowledge or facts with the reader)
B) To persuade (change the reader's beliefs or behaviour)
C) To describe (create a vivid picture of an experience or place)
D) To entertain (engage the reader emotionally or narratively)
E) To argue (make a case for a specific position)
F) To advise (guide the reader toward a course of action)
G) To reflect (explore and make sense of personal experience)
H) To expose (reveal something hidden, uncomfortable, or important)

Remember the [context] and [author]'s perspective that we explored earlier."

### Step 3: Target Audience

"Purpose and audience are inseparable. A writer's choices — their language, their tone, how much they explain, what they assume — all depend on WHO they're writing for. Identifying the audience helps us understand WHY the writer made the specific choices they did.

Who was [author]'s intended audience?
- What kind of reader was this text written for?
- What assumptions does the text make about its reader's knowledge, values, or beliefs?
- How does knowing the audience help us understand [author]'s purpose?

**Provide a quote that reveals something about the intended audience (their assumed values, concerns, or knowledge).**"
