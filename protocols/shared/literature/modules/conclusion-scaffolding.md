# Conclusion Planning — Scaffolded 4-Step Structure
**Version:** 1.0.0 • **Scope:** All literature planning (B.8 Conclusion)

This module provides the universal scaffolded conclusion planning workflow. Load in the conclusion planning step for any literature protocol. The 4-step structure adapts automatically based on whether context is assessed.

---

## Overview

Level 5 conclusions SYNTHESISE rather than summarise. They add a layer of insight that couldn't exist without the body paragraph analysis. Every conclusion needs four elements, each with Socratic scaffolding for struggling students.

---

## Step 1 — Restated Thesis

"First, restate your thesis from the introduction — but **don't just repeat it**. Restate it with the added insight gained from your three body paragraphs.

Your original thesis was: '[WORKING_THESIS]'

Now, having analysed the beginning, middle, and end of [text], how would you express this argument differently? What have your body paragraphs revealed that adds depth to your original claim?"

Wait. Store restated thesis.

**[AI_INTERNAL — Anti-Verbatim Scaffold:]**
If student repeats thesis verbatim: "That's your original thesis word-for-word. Top-level conclusions show SYNTHESIS — restate the core claim but with added complexity. What did your three body paragraphs REVEAL that you didn't fully see at the start?"

If still struggling: "Try completing this: 'Through [text], [author/poet] ultimately reveals that [concept] is not simply [original claim] but rather [deeper insight from body analysis]...'"

---

## Step 2 — Controlling Concept

"Now the most important part of your conclusion — the **controlling concept**. This is the 'so what?' that connects ALL THREE body paragraphs into one overarching argument.

Think about it this way: your three body paragraphs explored [concept from B1], [concept from B2], and [concept from B3]. What's the SINGLE controlling idea that unifies all three?"

Wait.

**[AI_INTERNAL — 3-Level Scaffolding:]**

**If student lists rather than synthesises:**
"You've described what each paragraph does, but you haven't yet synthesised them. Try this: 'Across the whole text, [author/poet] argues that [ONE overarching idea that encompasses all three body paragraph concepts]...' What would fill that gap?"

**If student gives two ideas instead of one:**
"You've identified two important strands. But a controlling concept should UNIFY them. How are [idea A] and [idea B] connected? What's the single thread running through both?"

**If student gives a strong controlling concept:**
"Excellent — '[their controlling concept]' connects all three of your body paragraphs. Now, does this concept feel genuinely different from your thesis, or are they saying the same thing in different words? A controlling concept should DEEPEN the thesis, not duplicate it."

Wait. Validate. Store controlling concept.

---

## Step 3 — Author's / Poet's Purpose

**[AI_INTERNAL]** This step adapts based on whether context is assessed:

### Path A — Context IS assessed (AO3/AO4 protocols)

"Your conclusion must show how [text] remains significant beyond its immediate setting.

Think about this: [author] wrote [text] in [historical context]. Why does [text]'s message about [controlling concept] still matter today? What was [author]'s broader purpose — not just for readers in [original period], but for ALL readers?

Use causal language: 'BECAUSE [context], [author] NEEDED to show that [purpose]...'"

**[AI_INTERNAL — Causal Context Scaffold:]**
- Level 1: "What was happening in [author]'s world that made this text URGENT?"
- Level 2: "You've identified the context. Now: how did that context make [author] COMPELLED to explore [concept]?"
- Level 3: "Complete this: 'Writing in [period], [author] witnessed [specific context]. This reality DROVE them to explore [concept] because...'"

### Path B — Context NOT separately assessed (unseen poetry, EDUQAS shakespeare, etc.)

"What do you believe was the [author/poet]'s primary intention or reason for crafting this [text/poem]? Connect this to their methods — what were they ultimately trying to achieve, communicate, or challenge?

Think about:
- What MESSAGE did they want readers to take away?
- What ASSUMPTIONS were they challenging?
- What EMOTIONS were they trying to provoke — and why?"

**[AI_INTERNAL — Purpose Scaffold:]**
If student gives vague purpose ("to make people think"): "That's too broad. WHAT specifically did [author/poet] want readers to think ABOUT? What truth were they exposing or what assumption were they challenging?"

If student focuses only on technique: "You're describing HOW they did it, but I'm asking WHY. What was the purpose behind those technique choices?"

Wait. Store author's/poet's purpose.

---

## Step 4 — Universal Message

"Finally, the **universal message** — what does [text] teach us about human nature, society, or the human condition that transcends [author/poet]'s specific time and place?

This is where your conclusion moves from literary analysis to genuine insight. Think beyond [text] specifically:
- What truth about HUMAN NATURE does this text reveal?
- What does it teach us about SOCIETY — how communities, institutions, or individuals operate?
- What MORAL or PHILOSOPHICAL question does it leave the reader with?

Draft one sentence that captures this universal claim."

Wait.

**[AI_INTERNAL — Anti-Cliché Scaffold:]**

**If student's response is too text-specific** (still talking about characters/plot):
"You're still inside the text. Step outside it — what does [text] teach us about PEOPLE IN GENERAL, not just [specific characters]?"

**If student gives a cliché** ('people should be kind' / 'war is bad' / 'prejudice is wrong'):
"That's a valid human truth, but it's quite general. Can you make it more SPECIFIC to the particular kind of insight [text] offers? What does [text] reveal about [theme] that's more surprising or nuanced?"

**If strong:** "That's a genuinely perceptive universal insight. It connects your essay's specific analysis to a broader truth that gives the whole piece resonance."

Wait. Store universal message.

---

## Conclusion Review

Present the complete four-part conclusion plan:

"Here's your planned conclusion:
- **Restated Thesis:** [their synthesis]
- **Controlling Concept:** [their unifying idea]
- **[Author's/Poet's] Purpose:** [their purpose statement]
- **Universal Message:** [their transcendent claim]

**A** — Happy with this conclusion plan
**B** — I want to revise an element"

If B: "Which element would you like to revise?" → Socratic refinement → re-present.
If A: Proceed to next step.
