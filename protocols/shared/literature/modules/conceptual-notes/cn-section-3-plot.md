### Step 1: Plot Pattern Recognition

"Based on [protagonist's name]'s transformation in a world dealing with [historical context], which plot type best describes [text title]? Choose the one that most closely fits:

**1) Hero's Journey**
- The protagonist leaves their ordinary world, faces trials, and returns transformed
- Symbolism: transformation, self-discovery, the cyclical nature of life
- Explores courage, growth, personal development, and facing fears
- Creates feelings of excitement, fear, anticipation, triumph

**2) Tragedy**
- A high-status protagonist makes a critical error (hamartia) leading to catastrophic downfall
- Requires a specific sequence: rise of high-status protagonist → hamartia (critical mistake) → chain reaction of suffering → anagnorisis (recognition) → catharsis
- Functions as BOTH plot structure (requiring these events in sequence) AND form (framing emotional/philosophical impact)
- Explores consequences of choices, societal critique, and human limitation
- Creates feelings of pity, fear, and catharsis (emotional purging through witnessing)

**3) Rags to Riches**
- The protagonist rises from humble beginnings to success
- Explores transformation, social mobility, and reward for virtue
- Creates feelings of hope, satisfaction, and joy

**4) Redemption**
- The protagonist atones for past wrongs and transforms morally
- Explores guilt, forgiveness, and second chances
- Creates feelings of hope, relief, and empathy

**5) The Quest**
- The protagonist journeys to achieve a specific goal
- Explores adventure, perseverance, and self-discovery
- Creates feelings of excitement, anticipation, and triumph

**6) Overcoming the Monster**
- The protagonist confronts and defeats a great evil
- Explores good vs. evil, courage, and sacrifice
- Creates feelings of tension, fear, then triumph

**7) Voyage and Return**
- The protagonist travels to an unfamiliar world and returns changed
- Explores discovery, growth, and the familiar vs. unfamiliar
- Creates feelings of curiosity, excitement, and satisfaction

**8) Coming of Age**
- The protagonist transitions from innocence to experience
- Explores personal development, maturity, and growing up
- Creates feelings of empathy, nostalgia, and hope

**9) Comedy** (in the classical sense)
- Disorder is resolved into harmony and unity
- Explores misunderstandings, social order, and reconciliation
- Creates feelings of amusement, relief, and joy

Consider: [protagonist's name] goes from [their starting state] to [their ending state]. Which pattern does this follow?"

### Step 2: Understanding the Plot Type's Deeper Meaning

"You selected [their chosen plot type]. Let's explore what this structure means conceptually.

[Based on their selection, provide the relevant deep dive:]

**If Tragedy:**
"In tragedy, the protagonist's downfall isn't just personal—it's cosmic, and it serves as societal critique. Tragedy functions as BOTH a plot structure AND a form:

**As Plot Structure:** Tragedy requires a specific sequence of events:
1. **Rise of a high-status protagonist** (so the fall is significant)
2. **Hamartia** (critical mistake/error in action)—this is NOT 'fatal flaw' (a common mistranslation). The tragedy comes from a DECISION or MISCALCULATION, not necessarily a character defect
3. **Chain reaction of suffering** triggered by this error
4. **Anagnorisis** (recognition)—the moment they realise the truth of what they've done
5. **Catharsis**—emotional purging for the audience through witnessing

**As Form:** Tragedy frames the play's emotional and philosophical impact, positioning it as societal critique. As critic Terry Eagleton notes, tragedy operates with a 'double role'—both validating and questioning societal institutions. The protagonist's downfall often exposes problems with the values their society prizes.

**The Broader Implications:**
- Tragedy's scope extends beyond individual fate to highlight societal forces
- It critiques the cultural, ethical, or political norms of the protagonist's world
- By combining personal downfall with societal reflection, tragedy explores human limitations and the ripple effects of actions on the wider community

**Proto-Gothic Connection:**
Renaissance tragedy, including Macbeth, prefigures the Gothic genre—blending psychological depth with supernatural and moral themes. This crossover shows how tragedy influences later genres.

**Catharsis and Emotional Purging:**
Aristotle's concept of catharsis describes how tragedy affects the audience emotionally—allowing them to feel strong emotions like pity and fear, then release those emotions in a safe way. The audience feels pity for the protagonist's wasted potential and fear as they witness the devastating consequences.

What critical mistake (hamartia) does [protagonist's name] make? What is their moment of recognition (anagnorisis) when they realise the consequences? How does their downfall critique society?"

**Additional Note on Tragedy:**
"At Sophicly, we classify tragedy primarily as a plot structure due to its reliance on a specific sequence of events. However, its designation as a form underscores its broader impact, serving as a vehicle for exploring human ambition, morality, and the consequences of transgression. This duality ensures tragedy transcends its narrative, offering both a meticulously crafted story and a profound exploration of societal and personal conflicts."

**If Rags to Riches:**
"The Rags to Riches structure isn't just about material success—it's about human potential. The structure explores:
- **Symbolism:** Transformation, fulfillment of potential, reward for virtue
- **Core Themes:** Ambition, perseverance, social mobility, transformation
- **Emotional Journey:** The audience feels hope, empathy, and satisfaction
- **Moral Lesson:** Hard work and virtue lead to success; adversity can be overcome

How does [protagonist's name]'s rise embody these elements? What virtues enable their transformation?"

**If Redemption:**
"Redemption plots show that moral transformation is always possible. The structure explores:
- **Symbolism:** Forgiveness, transformation, the power of second chances
- **Core Themes:** Guilt, atonement, forgiveness, moral awakening
- **Emotional Journey:** The audience moves from judgment to empathy to joy
- **Moral Lesson:** It's never too late to change; forgiveness transforms

How does [protagonist's name]'s redemption work? What catalyzes their moral transformation?"

**If The Quest:**
"Quest narratives are about more than reaching a goal—they're about self-discovery. The structure explores:
- **Symbolism:** The search for meaning, self-discovery, life's journey
- **Core Themes:** Adventure, perseverance, the search for truth
- **Emotional Journey:** Excitement, anticipation, fulfillment
- **Moral Lesson:** The journey matters more than the destination

What is [protagonist's name] really searching for? How do they change through the quest?"

**If Overcoming the Monster:**
"This structure pits good against evil in its starkest form. The structure explores:
- **Symbolism:** The triumph of good, the power of courage
- **Core Themes:** Good vs. evil, heroism, sacrifice, moral courage
- **Emotional Journey:** Tension, fear, triumph, satisfaction
- **Moral Lesson:** Courage conquers evil; we must stand against threats

What 'monster' does [protagonist's name] face? Is it external, internal, or both?"

**If Voyage and Return:**
"This structure uses the unfamiliar to illuminate the familiar. The structure explores:
- **Symbolism:** Exploration and return, growth through displacement
- **Core Themes:** Discovery, the familiar vs. unfamiliar, perspective
- **Emotional Journey:** Curiosity, disorientation, relief, new understanding
- **Moral Lesson:** Growth comes from venturing beyond comfort zones

Where does [protagonist's name] 'voyage' to? How are they changed by the return?"

**If Coming of Age:**
"This structure traces the universal journey from innocence to experience. The structure explores:
- **Symbolism:** Growth, maturity, loss of innocence
- **Core Themes:** Personal development, identity formation, growing up
- **Emotional Journey:** Empathy, nostalgia, recognition, hope
- **Moral Lesson:** Maturity comes through facing hard truths

What innocence does [protagonist's name] lose? What wisdom do they gain?"

**If Comedy:**
"Comedy restores social harmony after chaos. The structure explores:
- **Symbolism:** Social order, reconciliation, renewal
- **Core Themes:** Misunderstanding, social bonds, harmony restored
- **Emotional Journey:** Confusion, recognition, relief, celebration
- **Moral Lesson:** Communication and forgiveness heal divisions

What disorder needs resolving? How is harmony achieved?"
