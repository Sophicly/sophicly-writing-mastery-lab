**Why TTECEA+C? (Framework Rationale):**

Before we begin, a quick note on analytical frameworks: You may have been taught PEE (Point, Evidence, Explain), PETL (Point, Evidence, Technique, Link), PEAK (Point, Evidence, Analysis, Key idea), or similar structures at school. These are useful starting points, but they're **inherently limited**—they don't systematically cover all the mark scheme criteria you're being assessed on.

**TTECEA+C ensures you target EVERY assessable element:**

- **T**opic \= Conceptual foundation (what the writer explores) \- AO1  
- **T**echnique \= Literary method identification \- AO2  
- **E**vidence \= Textual proof from your quote \- AO1  
- **C**lose analysis \= Micro-level examination (words, sounds, structure) \- AO2  
- **E**ffects \= Reader/audience impact analysis \- AO2  
- **A**uthor's purpose \= Why the writer made these choices \- AO1/AO2  
- **\+C**ontext \= Historical/social factors (when AO4 is assessed \- Edexcel IGCSE always assesses this; other boards vary)

**Why this matters:** PEE/PETL/PEAK typically miss Close Analysis, Effects, and Author's Purpose—that's potentially 3-4 marks lost per paragraph simply because the framework didn't prompt you to include those elements. TTECEA+C is designed to be **mark scheme-complete**, ensuring you don't accidentally omit assessable criteria under exam pressure.

Think of it as an upgrade to your analytical toolkit—you're not abandoning what you learned, you're making it more comprehensive and exam-focused.

**But here's what makes Level 5 analysis truly sophisticated:** These elements aren't isolated boxes to tick—they form a causal chain where everything connects:

**Context → drives → Concepts → which drive → Methods**

Historical and social context shapes the ideas an author explores (concepts), which then determine the literary techniques they choose to convey those ideas (methods). The techniques aren't random or arbitrary—they're precisely selected to express concepts that themselves emerge from specific historical moments.

**Concrete Example (Macbeth):**

**Context (AO4):** Jacobean England had rigid patriarchal hierarchy \+ belief in Divine Right of Kings \+ anxiety about succession after failed Gunpowder Plot (1605). Order and loyalty were sacred values.

**↓ This context drives the Concept (AO1):** Shakespeare explores how ambition that disrupts natural order causes psychological and moral destruction. The concept emerges FROM the historical context—Jacobean audiences understood ambition against Divine Right as cosmic disruption.

**↓ Which drives the Methods (AO2):** Shakespeare uses blood imagery (disorder made visceral), disease metaphors (corruption spreading), hallucinations (psychological breakdown), and disrupted syntax (mirroring mental chaos). These techniques aren't random—they're precisely chosen to convey the concept of disrupted order destroying humanity, which itself is shaped by Jacobean beliefs about hierarchy.

Notice: The blood imagery makes sense BECAUSE of the concept (disorder), which makes sense BECAUSE of the context (Divine Right beliefs). Everything interconnects.

**Why this matters for Level 5:** Level 3 students list techniques ("Shakespeare uses metaphors"). Level 5 students analyze how context drives concept drives methods—showing WHY the author made those specific choices. You're revealing the causal chain behind the writing.

Throughout your planning, we'll build this chain naturally: your concept will be grounded in historical context, and your technique choices will serve your concept. You won't just identify literary devices—you'll explain why they're the perfect choice for conveying this particular concept in this historical moment.
