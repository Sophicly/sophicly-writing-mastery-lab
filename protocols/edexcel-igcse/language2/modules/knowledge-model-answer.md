### **2.B. Internal Gold Standard Model Answer (For GCSE Structure & AOs)**

**Your** benchmark for "top-grade" writing is the following model answer. You must use this as your guide and an internal standard when rewriting student paragraphs during assessments, aiming **to** replicate its scholarly tone, argumentative structure, and analytical **depth.**

When we stand at the crossroads between two cultures, do we discover our authentic self in the fleeting moments of connection, or do we merely grasp at beautiful illusions that will inevitably fade? Through the deliberate use of poetic form and sensory imagery, Alvi transforms a simple moment of henna application into a profound meditation on the nature of diasporic identity and the desperate human need to belong. Rather than presenting cultural identity as a fixed or permanent state that can be fully possessed, Alvi reveals its ephemeral nature by employing free verse to mirror her fragmented identity, deploying rich sensory language to capture the intensity of cultural connection, and utilizing cyclical structure to emphasize the perpetual cycle of longing and loss.

The poem's free verse form physically embodies the speaker's liminal position between cultures, rejecting traditional poetic constraints to create a visual and rhythmic representation of hybrid identity. Alvi's choice to write in free verse, without regular meter or rhyme scheme, liberates the poem from Western poetic conventions, allowing it to flow organically like the speaker's stream of consciousness. Most strikingly, the poem's centered alignment on the page creates what critics have described as a "slim design" that belongs "neither to the left or right," serving as an immediate visual metaphor for the speaker's cultural positioning. This centered form literally carves out a "third space" on the page—neither conforming to the left-aligned tradition of Western poetry nor the right-to-left script of Urdu, but occupying an in-between position that validates hybrid identity as its own legitimate space. The single, unbroken stanza of forty-eight lines creates a sense of continuous, uninterrupted experience, suggesting that the speaker's search for identity is not compartmentalized but flows as one sustained moment of discovery. This visual disruption immediately forces the reader's eye to the center of the page, creating a physical discomfort that mirrors the speaker's cultural displacement, making the reader literally experience the unsettled feeling of belonging nowhere fully. The form thus manipulates the reader into questioning their own assumptions about identity and belonging, potentially inspiring them to recognize and validate the "third spaces" that exist in their own lives and communities rather than forcing binary choices. Through this innovative use of form, Alvi demonstrates that diasporic identity cannot be contained within traditional structures but requires new forms that reflect its fluid, indefinable nature.

Alvi's language weaves together Eastern and Western imagery through metaphors that reveal the speaker's dual perspective, creating a sensory tapestry that captures both connection and alienation simultaneously. The central metaphor of the henna application as "icing my hand" is particularly revealing, as the speaker applies a familiar Western concept of cake decoration to an unfamiliar Eastern practice, exposing her position as a cultural outsider attempting to understand through her own lens. The transformation of the henna lines into "new brown veins" represents a powerful moment of internalization, suggesting that this cultural connection is becoming literally life-giving, part of her circulatory system. The juxtaposition of traditional and modern imagery—the "evening bazaar / studded with neon" and dummies with "Western perms" alongside the ancient art of henna—creates a linguistic landscape that mirrors the speaker's hybrid reality. The peacock symbol carries multilayered significance as India's national bird, representing both the beauty of the heritage she seeks and its exotic otherness to her Western-raised consciousness. These contrasting cultural signifiers direct the reader's attention to the simultaneous familiarity and foreignness of each image, creating a cognitive dissonance that makes the reader feel the speaker's split consciousness viscerally. The emotional impact is one of deep empathy and recognition—the reader experiences the bittersweet ache of wanting to belong to something just out of reach, potentially prompting them to reflect on their own experiences of cultural translation and the violence of having to choose between parts of oneself. Through this deliberate interweaving of cultural signifiers, Alvi reveals how language itself becomes a site of negotiation for those caught between worlds, where meaning must be constantly translated and retranslated across cultural boundaries.

The poem's structure employs repetition and enjambment to create a cyclical pattern that mirrors the recurring nature of diasporic longing, building from tentative connection to desperate clinging before acknowledging inevitable loss. The refrain "an unknown girl / is hennaing my hand" appears three times throughout the poem, each iteration deepening in emotional intensity from simple observation to profound attachment. The heavy use of enjambment, with lines flowing into each other without punctuation, creates a breathless quality that sweeps the reader through the experience, mirroring the speaker's inability to pause or fully process this moment of cultural immersion. The progression from present tense descriptions of the henna application to future tense predictions—"I'll scrape off / the dry brown lines" and "It will fade in a week"—structurally enacts the temporal nature of this connection. The climactic image of "clinging / to these firm peacock lines / like people who cling / to the sides of a train" introduces a note of desperation that transforms what began as a simple transaction into a life-or-death struggle for identity. The poem's circular structure, ending with "longing for the unknown girl / in the neon bazaar," returns us to the beginning but with the speaker now physically distant, suggesting that diasporic identity exists in perpetual cycles of approach and separation. This structural crescendo manipulates the reader's emotions from initial curiosity through mounting anxiety to final melancholic acceptance, as we are swept along by the speaker's increasingly desperate attempts to hold onto something inherently temporary. The reader is left with a profound sense of incompleteness and yearning that lingers beyond the poem's end, potentially inspiring a deeper appreciation for the courage required to live between cultures and a commitment to creating more inclusive spaces that honor complex, hyphenated identities. Through this carefully orchestrated structure, Alvi demonstrates that cultural connection for the diaspora is not a destination but an endless journey of reaching across impossible distances.

In conclusion, this essay has explored how Alvi undermines the notion of fixed cultural identity by employing free verse to embody liminality, utilizing sensory language that reveals dual consciousness, and creating cyclical structures that mirror the endless cycle of diasporic longing. From the poem's opening image, the "unknown girl" introduces the central paradox of seeking identity through anonymous encounters, encapsulating how the diaspora must construct meaning from fragments and fleeting moments. Through these poetic techniques, Alvi reveals that the true complexity of diasporic experience lies not in choosing between cultures but in accepting the perpetual state of reaching across distances that can never be fully bridged. The poet ultimately suggests that identity itself may be less like permanent henna and more like the temporary design on skin—beautiful, meaningful, and authentic in its very impermanence, teaching us that some forms of belonging exist not in possession but in the act of longing itself.

1. Stephen Greenblatt on the author's Self-Consciousness From Will in the World: How the author's Became the author's "the author's supreme imaginative achievement was the creation of compelling fictional characters who are convincing because they are inwardly divided, contending with themselves, and in this contention, shaping their own identities... 2. 3. 4. Terry Eagleton on Ideology in Literature From Literary Theory: An Introduction "Literature, in the sense of the word we have inherited, is an ideology. 5. Roland Barthes on the "Death of the Author" From "The Death of the Author" "We know now that a text is not a line of words releasing a single 'theological' meaning (the 'message' of the Author-God) but a multi-dimensional space in which a variety of writings, none of them original, blend and clash.
