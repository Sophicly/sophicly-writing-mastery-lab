### **Imagery**

| Type | Definition | Example |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Sensory imagery** | Appeals to sight, sound, touch, smell, taste | Owen's contrast: past (colour, warmth) vs. present (grey, cold) |
| **Symbolism** | Objects/images carrying meaning beyond literal | Necklace \= wealth/illusion; window \= freedom; henna \= temporary connection |
| **Pathetic fallacy** | Environment reflecting emotional/psychological state | Storm in Hill's extract; spring imagery in Chopin |

### **Figurative Language**

| Type | Definition | Example |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Simile** | Explicit comparison using "like" or "as" | "clinging... like people who cling to the sides of a train" (Alvi) |
| **Metaphor** | Implicit comparison; one thing IS another | "I'm a black ocean, leaping and wide" (Angelou) |
| **Personification** | Giving human qualities to non-human things | Buzz saw "snarled," "leaped out" (Frost); wind "howls," "rages" (Hill) |
| **Oxymoron** | Contradictory terms combined | "joy that kills" (Chopin) |

### **Sound Devices (Primarily Poetry)**

| Type | Definition | Effect |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Onomatopoeia** | Words that sound like their meaning | "snarled," "rattled," "buzz" (Frost) |
| **Alliteration** | Repeated consonant sounds at start of words | Creates musicality, emphasis |
| **Assonance** | Repeated vowel sounds | Creates musicality, mood |
| **Sibilance** | Repeated 's' sounds | Hissing, sinister, or soothing depending on context |
| **Plosives** | Hard consonants (b, p, d, t, g, k) | Aggression, impact, violence |

### **Diction and Tone**

| Element | Description |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Word choice** | Concrete vs. abstract nouns; dynamic vs. static verbs; register (formal/informal) |
| **Semantic field** | Groups of related words creating pattern (e.g., words of colour, words of death) |
| **Tone** | Speaker's attitude toward subject (defiant, clinical, bitter, longing, detached) |
| **Register** | Level of formality; shifts in register signal shifts in relationship/mood |

### **Irony**

| Type | Definition | Example |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Situational irony** | What happens vs. what's expected | Ten years of suffering to replace worthless fake (The Necklace) |
| **Dramatic irony** | Reader knows what characters don't | We know Louise dies of crushed hope, not "joy" |
| **Verbal irony** | Saying opposite of meaning | "joy that kills" |

### **Narrative Voice (Prose)**

| Type | Definition | Effect |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **First person** | "I" narrator | Intimate but limited; subjective |
| **First person retrospective** | Older self looking back | Traumatized narrator; foreshadowing; dual consciousness |
| **Third person limited** | Close to one character's consciousness | Intimacy AND ironic distance; we see through their eyes |
| **Third person omniscient** | Access to all characters | Objective, clinical; god-like perspective |
| **Free indirect discourse** | Narrative voice merges with character's consciousness | Character's idiom in third person; blurred boundary |

### **Scaffolding Questions for Language Analysis**

- "What SPECIFIC WORD stands out most to you? Why that word rather than a synonym?"  
- "What IMAGERY dominates? What senses are engaged—and what senses are absent?"  
- "What is the TONE? How do you know? What specific words create that tone?"  
- "Where is there IRONY? What gap exists between appearance and reality?"  
- "How does sentence LENGTH change? What does that shift create?"  
- "What SOUNDS does the language create? What effect do those sounds have on you as a reader?"  
- "Is there a pattern of words—a semantic field? What does that pattern suggest?"
