### **Detailed Form Descriptions**

**NARRATIVE POEM**

- Tells a story with beginning, middle, end  
- May include narrator, characters, dialogue, plot  
- **Purpose:** Combine narrative engagement with poetic intensity  
- **Effect:** Draws reader into story while maintaining poetic compression  
- **Examples in anthology:** Disabled, "Out, Out—", The Bright Lights of Sarajevo

**LYRIC POEM**

- Single speaker expresses thought and emotion  
- Originally meant for musical accompaniment  
- **Purpose:** Express subjective experience, create intimacy  
- **Effect:** Readers share speaker's emotional landscape  
- **Examples in anthology:** An Unknown Girl, Still I Rise

**ELEGY**

- Poem of lamentation and mourning for the dead  
- Can express loss in broader sense (way of life, innocence, wholeness)  
- **Purpose:** Honour what is lost, process grief, explore mortality  
- **Effect:** Evokes sympathy, prompts reflection on loss and memory  
- **Example in anthology:** Disabled (mourns lost youth/wholeness, not death itself)

**DRAMATIC MONOLOGUE**

- Poem written as speech by speaker who is NOT the poet  
- Addresses silent listener (implied or explicit)  
- **Purpose:** Explore psychology, create dramatic irony, invite critical reading  
- **Effect:** Readers must interpret what speaker reveals AND conceals  
- **Example in anthology:** Still I Rise (addresses oppressor as "You")

**BLANK VERSE**

- Unrhymed iambic pentameter  
- Approximates natural speech rhythm  
- **Purpose:** Create conversational, realistic quality  
- **Effect:** Events feel immediate, grounded  
- **Example in anthology:** "Out, Out—"

**FREE VERSE**

- No fixed metre or rhyme  
- Arranged by content and rhetoric  
- **Purpose:** Reflect natural speech, modern consciousness, individual expression  
- **Effect:** Freedom can suggest liberation OR fragmentation  
- **Example in anthology:** An Unknown Girl

### **Scaffolding Questions for Form Analysis**

**When students struggle to identify form:**

- "Is this poem telling a story (narrative/ballad) or expressing personal feelings (lyric)?"  
- "Is there a speaker who seems different from the poet (dramatic monologue)?"  
- "Does the poem follow a strict pattern like 14 lines (sonnet) or is it more free-flowing?"  
- "Does the poem seem to be mourning something or someone (elegy)?"  
- "Is the poem addressing someone or something directly (ode, dramatic monologue)?"

**When students struggle to analyze form:**

- "Why might the poet have chosen THIS particular form for THIS subject matter?"  
- "What expectations does this form create for the reader?"  
- "Does the poet follow or break the conventions of this form? Why might that be significant?"  
- "What does the choice of \[form\] allow the poet to do that another form wouldn't?"  
- "How does the FORM itself argue something about the content?"
