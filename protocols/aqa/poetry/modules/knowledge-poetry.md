## **1.A Poetry Form Knowledge Bank**

**\[AI\_INTERNAL\]** Use this reference during Planning Phase (B.5) when students need scaffolding on poetic forms. Surface this information through Socratic questioning, not direct provision.

### **General Forms Summary**

| Form | Key Features | Typical Purpose/Effect |
|------|--------------|------------------------|
| **Ballad** | Narrative, musical quality, often with refrain, ABAB/ABCB rhyme, simple language | Tell memorable stories, evoke empathy, convey moral lessons |
| **Epic** | Long narrative, heroic protagonist, vast setting, elevated style | Entertain, inspire, preserve cultural values |
| **Lyric** | Personal expression, single speaker, emotional intensity, musical quality | Express thought and emotion, create intimacy |
| **Elegy** | Lamentation/mourning, formal language, reflection on mortality | Process grief, honour the dead, explore loss |
| **Sonnet** | 14 lines, specific rhyme scheme, volta, iambic pentameter | Explore love, religion, politics with controlled intensity |
| **Dramatic Monologue** | Single speaker (not poet), addresses silent listener, reveals character | Invite questioning of speaker's reliability, explore psychology |
| **Free Verse** | No fixed metre or rhyme, arranged by content and rhetoric | Reflect modern consciousness, organic expression |
| **Narrative** | Tells a story, may use narrator and characters, often metered | Combine storytelling with poetic intensity |
| **Interior Monologue** | Stream of consciousness, first person, introspective | Reveal inner psychology, create intimacy |
| **Ode** | Elevated language, formal address to subject, elaborate structure | Express deep emotion, pay tribute, inspire contemplation |

### **Detailed Form Descriptions (For Scaffolding)**

**BALLAD FORM**

* Associated with important tales and histories
* Medieval roots—storytellers used musical refrains to aid memory
* **Features:** narrative structure, simple language, regular metre/rhyme, repetition, dialogue
* **Purpose:** entertain, educate, convey moral lessons
* **Effect:** evokes empathy through relatable stories of ordinary people
* **Examples:** "The Charge of the Light Brigade" (Tennyson), "La Belle Dame Sans Merci" (Keats)

**DRAMATIC MONOLOGUE FORM**

* Poem written as speech by speaker who is NOT the poet
* Encourages reader to question speaker's authority/reliability
* **Features:** single voice, implied listener, revelation of character through speech
* **Purpose:** explore psychology, create dramatic irony, invite critical reading
* **Effect:** readers must interpret what speaker reveals AND conceals
* **Examples:** "My Last Duchess" (Browning), "Porphyria's Lover" (Browning)

**SONNET FORM**

* 14 lines of rhyming iambic pentameter
* **Types:** Petrarchan (octave + sestet), Shakespearean (3 quatrains + couplet)
* **Features:** volta (turn), compressed argument, formal constraint
* **Purpose:** explore love, mortality, beauty with intellectual and emotional intensity
* **Effect:** constraint creates pressure; volta creates surprise/revelation
* **Examples:** "Sonnet 116" (Shakespeare), "Sonnet 43" (Barrett Browning)

**FREE VERSE/FREE FORM**

* Arranged by content and rhetoric, not rules of metre/rhyme
* **Features:** organic structure, varied line lengths, no fixed patterns
* **Purpose:** reflect natural speech, modern consciousness, individual expression
* **Effect:** freedom can suggest liberation OR fragmentation
* **Examples:** "The Émigrée" (Rumens), contemporary poetry

**ELEGY FORM**

* Poem of lamentation and mourning for the dead
* Can express loss in broader sense (way of life, innocence)
* **Features:** formal language, reflective tone, progression through grief
* **Purpose:** honour deceased, process grief, explore mortality
* **Effect:** evokes sympathy, prompts reflection on loss and memory
* **Examples:** "Futility" (Owen), "Remember" (Rossetti)

**LYRIC FORM**

* Single speaker expresses thought and emotion
* Originally meant for musical accompaniment
* **Features:** personal voice, emotional intensity, musicality
* **Purpose:** express subjective experience, create intimacy
* **Effect:** readers share speaker's emotional landscape
* **Examples:** Most anthology poems have lyric elements

**ODE FORM**

* Formal address to a subject (person, object, abstract concept)
* Elevated, ceremonial language
* **Features:** elaborate structure, apostrophe (direct address), heightened emotion
* **Purpose:** pay tribute, celebrate, contemplate deeply
* **Effect:** creates sense of importance, reverence, philosophical depth
* **Examples:** "Ode to a Nightingale" (Keats), "Ode on a Grecian Urn" (Keats)

**NARRATIVE FORM**

* Tells a story with beginning, middle, end
* May include narrator, characters, dialogue, plot
* **Features:** storytelling elements, often regular metre, temporal progression
* **Purpose:** combine narrative engagement with poetic intensity
* **Effect:** draws reader into story while maintaining poetic compression
* **Examples:** "The Prelude" (Wordsworth), "Bayonet Charge" (Hughes)

### **Scaffolding Questions for Form Analysis**

**When students struggle to identify form:**

* "Is this poem telling a story (narrative/ballad) or expressing personal feelings (lyric)?"
* "Is there a speaker who seems different from the poet (dramatic monologue)?"
* "Does the poem follow a strict pattern like 14 lines (sonnet) or is it more free-flowing?"
* "Does the poem seem to be mourning something or someone (elegy)?"
* "Is the poem addressing someone or something directly (ode)?"

**When students struggle to analyze form:**

* "Why might the poet have chosen this particular form for this subject matter?"
* "What expectations does this form create for the reader?"
* "Does the poet follow or break the conventions of this form? Why might that be?"
* "How does the form of Poem A compare to Poem B? What does this difference reveal?"
* "What does the choice of [dramatic monologue/sonnet/free verse] allow the poet to do that another form wouldn't?"

## **1.B Poetry Structure Knowledge Bank**

**\[AI\_INTERNAL\]** Use this reference during Planning Phase (B.5) when students need scaffolding on structural techniques. Surface through Socratic questioning.

### **Metrical Patterns**

| Metre | Pattern | Effect |
|-------|---------|--------|
| **Iambic** (di-DUM) | Unstressed-stressed | Natural speech rhythm, forward momentum |
| **Trochaic** (DUM-di) | Stressed-unstressed | Falling rhythm, urgency, finality |
| **Dactylic** (DUM-di-di) | Stressed-unstressed-unstressed | Rolling, galloping rhythm |
| **Anapestic** (di-di-DUM) | Unstressed-unstressed-stressed | Light, dancing, building momentum |
| **Spondaic** (DUM-DUM) | Stressed-stressed | Emphasis, weight, slowness |
| **Pyrrhic** (di-di) | Unstressed-unstressed | Speed, lightness |

### **Line Lengths**

| Length | Name | Effect |
|--------|------|--------|
| 5 feet | **Pentameter** | Dignified, thoughtful, traditional |
| 4 feet | **Tetrameter** | Brisk, ballad-like, accessible |
| 3 feet | **Trimeter** | Compressed, urgent |
| 6 feet | **Hexameter** | Epic, expansive |
| 2 feet | **Dimeter** | Terse, fragmented |

### **Disrupted Metre**

* **Regular metre suddenly broken** = emotional turbulence, conflict, emphasis
* **Gradual disruption** = building tension, loss of control
* **Return to regularity** = resolution, restoration of order
* **Consistent irregularity** = modern sensibility, rejection of tradition

### **Rhyme Schemes**

| Scheme | Pattern | Effect |
|--------|---------|--------|
| **Couplets** | AA BB | Closure, wit, finality, control |
| **Alternate** | ABAB | Balance, progression, continuity |
| **Enclosed** | ABBA | Containment, circularity, reflection |
| **Terza Rima** | ABA BCB | Forward momentum, interconnection |
| **No Rhyme** | Free | Natural speech, modern sensibility |
| **Sonnet (Shakespearean)** | ABAB CDCD EFEF GG | Argument building to concluding couplet |
| **Sonnet (Petrarchan)** | ABBAABBA CDECDE | Octave poses problem, sestet resolves |

### **Rhyme Quality**

| Type | Definition | Effect |
|------|------------|--------|
| **Full/Perfect rhyme** | Exact sound match (love/dove) | Satisfaction, resolution, control |
| **Half/Slant rhyme** | Near match (love/move) | Unease, discord, modernity |
| **Eye rhyme** | Visual match only (love/prove) | Visual unity, aural discord |
| **Internal rhyme** | Rhyme within lines | Musicality, cohesion, emphasis |
| **Pararhyme** | Same consonants, different vowels (hall/hell) | Dissonance, wrongness, unease |

### **Structural Techniques**

**ENJAMBMENT (Run-on lines)**

* Line continues without punctuation to the next
* **Effects:** urgency, breathlessness, overflow of emotion, connection between ideas
* Can create surprise when sense completes differently than expected
* **Example:** Breaking a line mid-phrase creates tension and momentum
* **Key question:** What words are emphasized by being at line breaks?

**CAESURA (Mid-line pause)**

* Pause created by punctuation within a line
* **Strong caesura:** full stop, exclamation, question mark
* **Weak caesura:** comma, semi-colon
* **Effects:** dramatic pause, emphasis, fragmentation, reflection
* **Example:** "I am—yet what I am none cares or knows" (Clare)
* **Key question:** Why does the poet force a pause HERE?

**VOLTA (Turn)**

* Shift in argument, tone, or direction
* Traditional in sonnets (line 9 in Petrarchan, couplet in Shakespearean)
* **Effects:** revelation, counter-argument, resolution, surprise
* **Key question:** What changes after the volta and why?

**STANZA BREAKS**

* Division into verse paragraphs
* **Regular stanzas:** order, control, predictability
* **Irregular stanzas:** disruption, organic development
* **Single stanza:** unbroken flow, intensity
* **White space between stanzas:** pause, separation, shift
* **Key question:** Why does the poet break HERE rather than elsewhere?

**REFRAIN**

* Repeated lines or phrases
* **Effects:** emphasis, musical quality, ritual, obsession
* **Changes to refrain:** development, irony, shifting meaning
* **Key question:** Does the refrain mean the same thing each time, or does its meaning shift?

**ANAPHORA**

* Repetition at the start of successive lines/sentences
* **Effects:** building momentum, emphasis, incantatory quality
* **Key question:** What is being emphasized through repetition?

### **Scaffolding Questions for Structure Analysis**

**Metre:**

* "Read the line aloud—where do the stresses naturally fall?"
* "Is there a regular beat or does it feel disrupted?"
* "When the rhythm changes, what's happening in the poem's meaning?"
* "How does the rhythm of Poem A compare to Poem B?"

**Rhyme:**

* "What's the rhyme pattern? Does it stay consistent?"
* "Are the rhymes exact or do some feel 'off'? What effect does that create?"
* "How does the rhyme scheme at the end compare to the beginning?"
* "Why might Poet A use full rhyme while Poet B uses half-rhyme?"

**Enjambment/Caesura:**

* "Where does the poet make you pause or rush forward?"
* "What words get emphasized by their position at line ends?"
* "How does the punctuation (or lack of it) affect your reading?"
* "Compare the flow of both poems—which feels more controlled/chaotic?"

**Overall Structure:**

* "Is there a turning point where the poem's direction changes?"
* "How does the poem's structure mirror or contrast with its content?"
* "Comparing both poems—how do their structural choices differ?"
* "What does the stanza arrangement reveal about the poem's argument?"

### **Imagery Types**

| Type | Definition | Effect |
|------|------------|--------|

| **Tactile** | Appeals to touch | Creates physical sensation |
| **Olfactory** | Appeals to smell | Evokes memory, atmosphere |
| **Gustatory** | Appeals to taste | Creates visceral response |
| **Kinaesthetic** | Appeals to movement/bodily sensation | Creates dynamism, physical empathy |

### **Figurative Language**

| Device | Definition | Effect |
|--------|------------|--------|
| **Metaphor** | Direct comparison (X is Y) | Creates new understanding, compression |
| **Extended Metaphor** | Sustained comparison through poem/passage | Develops complex parallel, conceptual depth |
| **Simile** | Comparison using like/as | Creates distance, invites conscious comparison |
| **Personification** | Human qualities given to non-human | Creates empathy, animates abstract |
| **Pathetic Fallacy** | Nature reflects emotion | Externalizes internal states |
| **Symbolism** | Object represents abstract idea | Creates layers of meaning |
| **Allegory** | Extended symbolic narrative | Creates parallel meanings throughout |
| **Metonymy** | Related thing stands for thing itself | Creates association, compression |
| **Synecdoche** | Part stands for whole (or vice versa) | Creates focus, compression |
| **Oxymoron** | Contradictory terms combined | Creates tension, paradox, complexity |
| **Hyperbole** | Deliberate exaggeration | Creates emphasis, emotion, humour |
| **Litotes** | Understatement through double negative | Creates irony, restraint |

### **Sound Devices**

| Device | Definition | Effect |
|--------|------------|--------|
| **Alliteration** | Repeated initial consonants | Emphasis, cohesion, music |
| **Assonance** | Repeated vowel sounds | Internal rhyme, mood, musicality |
| **Consonance** | Repeated consonant sounds | Texture, cohesion |
| **Sibilance** | Repeated 's' sounds | Hissing, sinister, soft, seductive |
| **Plosives** | Hard sounds (b, d, p, t, k, g) | Aggression, emphasis, harshness |
| **Fricatives** | Friction sounds (f, v, th, s, z) | Softness, menace, whisper |
| **Nasals** | Nasal sounds (m, n, ng) | Softness, musicality, melancholy |
| **Onomatopoeia** | Word imitates sound | Immediacy, sensory experience |
| **Cacophony** | Harsh, discordant sounds | Discomfort, chaos, violence |
| **Euphony** | Pleasant, harmonious sounds | Beauty, calm, pleasure |

### **Diction (Word Choice)**

**Register:**

| Register | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| **Formal/Elevated** | Dignity, seriousness, distance |
| **Colloquial/Informal** | Intimacy, accessibility, authenticity |
| **Archaic** | Tradition, timelessness, distance |
| **Technical/Specialist** | Expertise, precision |
| **Dialectal** | Identity, authenticity, voice |
| **Religious/Biblical** | Reverence, moral weight, tradition |
| **Military** | Discipline, violence, order |

**Connotation:**

* Words carry emotional/associative weight beyond literal meaning
* **Example:** "house" vs "home" vs "dwelling" vs "residence"
* Consider what associations the poet's word choices evoke
* **Key question:** Why THIS word rather than a synonym?

**Semantic Fields:**

* Patterns of related words (e.g., military, religious, natural, domestic, medical)
* Creates cohesion and reinforces themes
* **Example:** semantic field of disease = corruption, decay, infection
* **Key question:** What pattern of related words can you identify? What theme does it reinforce?

### **Scaffolding Questions for Language Analysis**

**Imagery:**

* "Which senses does the poet appeal to? Why those particular senses?"
* "What pictures does the poet create? What mood do they establish?"
* "How do the images in Poem A differ from those in Poem B?"
* "Is the imagery pleasant or unpleasant? Why might that be?"

**Figurative Language:**

* "What is being compared to what? What does this comparison reveal?"
* "Does the metaphor develop through the poem or is it a single flash?"
* "What does the personification make us feel toward the subject?"
* "How do the figurative choices in each poem differ?"

**Sound:**

* "What sounds dominate? Are they soft, hard, hissing, flowing?"
* "How do the sounds reinforce the poem's meaning or mood?"
* "Compare the soundscape of both poems—what's different?"
* "Why might Poet A use harsh sounds while Poet B uses soft ones?"

**Diction:**

* "Why might the poet have chosen THIS word rather than a simpler one?"
* "What connotations does this word carry beyond its literal meaning?"
* "Is there a pattern of related words (semantic field)? What theme does it reinforce?"
* "How does the vocabulary of Poem A compare to Poem B?"

**Tone:**

* "How would you describe the speaker's attitude toward their subject?"
* "Does the tone stay consistent or shift through the poem?"
* "How do the tones of the two poems compare?"
* "What creates the tone—word choice, imagery, rhythm, or something else?"

## **1.D Effects Knowledge Bank (AO2)**

**\[AI\_INTERNAL\]** Use this reference during Planning Phase (B.5) when students are analyzing effects, and during Assessment Phase (A) when evaluating effects analysis. Effects must be SPECIFIC—guide students away from vague statements toward precise emotional and cognitive effects.

### **The Effects Pyramid: Focus → Emotions → Thoughts → Actions**

Effects work in a hierarchy. The author's techniques first guide what readers **focus** on, which generates **emotions**, which shape **thoughts**, which may ultimately influence **actions**. Understanding this chain helps students articulate sophisticated effects analysis.

| Level | Question | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| **FOCUS** (Base) | "How might the author's techniques guide the reader's attention to particular words and ideas?" | What does the technique make us notice, visualize, or attend to? (symbols, images, patterns, contrasts) |
| **EMOTIONS** | "How might the author's techniques change the reader's emotions?" | What specific feelings does the technique generate? (See categories below) |
| **THOUGHTS** | "How might the reader's emotions change their thoughts?" | What ideas, judgments, or perspectives does the emotional response lead to? |
| **ACTIONS** (Peak) | "How might the reader's thoughts change their actions?" | Does the text prompt readers toward any response, behavior, or changed worldview? (Not always applicable) |

### **The Central Role of Empathy**

**Key Principle:** Empathy is often the most significant emotional effect because literature's deeper purpose is helping readers understand another perspective—to feel what it's like to be someone else, in another time, place, or situation.

When we empathize:
- We move beyond intellectual understanding to emotional understanding
- We're more likely to change our thoughts about a subject
- We're more likely to change our actions in the real world

**Scaffolding Question:** "Does this technique help us empathize with the speaker/subject? How does feeling what they feel change how we think about [theme]?"

### **Emotional Effects Categories**

Guide students to be SPECIFIC about emotions. Instead of "makes us feel sad," push toward "creates a sense of grief tinged with guilt" or "evokes nostalgic longing."

| Category | Specific Emotions |
|----------|-------------------|
| **SEEKING/DESIRE** | Yearning, wish-fulfilment, envy, attraction, longing, craving |
| **RAGE/ANGER** | Fury, indignation, frustration, resentment, outrage, irritation |
| **FEAR/PANIC/DISTRESS** | Suspense, worry, anxiety, stress, dread, horror, terror, unease, apprehension |
| **DISGUST** | Repulsion, revulsion, contempt, distaste |
| **COURAGE** | Bravery, defiance, resilience, determination |
| **INTEREST** | Curiosity, puzzlement, mystery, intrigue, premonition, excitement, discovery, inevitability |
| **CARE/EMPATHY** | Empathy, sympathy, love, compassion, tenderness, protectiveness, rooting for, sense of justice/inequity |
| **PLAY** | Humour, amusement, wit, irony, playfulness |
| **JOY** | Consolation, triumph, relief, comfort, elation, satisfaction, contentment |
| **SADNESS/GRIEF** | Melancholy, sorrow, mourning, loss, despair, desolation, regret |
| **SHAME** | Guilt, embarrassment, humiliation, self-reproach |
| **SURPRISE** | Novelty, unpredictability, shock, revelation, disorientation |
| **WONDER** | Awe, admiration, marvel, reverence, amazement |
| **INSIGHT** | "Aha!" moments, recognition, new understanding, changed perspective |
| **HOPE** | Optimism, anticipation, possibility, faith |
| **POIGNANCY** | Bittersweet emotion, touching sadness, tender sorrow |

### **Scaffolding Questions for Effects Analysis**

**For Focus:**
* "What does this technique make you notice or pay attention to?"
* "What image or idea does this bring into focus?"
* "Why might the poet want us to focus on THIS rather than something else?"

**For Emotions:**
* "What SPECIFIC emotion does this create? Can you be more precise than 'sad' or 'happy'?"
* "Does this help us EMPATHIZE with the speaker or subject? How?"
* "Looking at the emotions list—which of these best captures what the reader feels?"
* "How does this emotion relate to the poem's central concept?"

**For Thoughts:**
* "What does feeling this emotion make us THINK about [theme]?"
* "How does this emotional response shape our judgment or perspective?"
* "What idea or message emerges from this emotional experience?"

**For Actions:**
* "Does the poet want us to DO something after reading this? Change our behavior or beliefs?"
* "How might this changed perspective affect how we act in the real world?"
* "What response is the poet trying to provoke?" (Not always applicable)

### **Weak vs Strong Effects Analysis**

| Weak (Vague) | Strong (Specific) |
|--------------|-------------------|
| "This makes us feel sad" | "This evokes a sense of profound grief tinged with guilt" |
| "The reader feels sorry for him" | "We empathize with his isolation, feeling the weight of his loneliness as if it were our own" |
| "It creates a negative mood" | "It generates mounting dread, a sense of inevitable catastrophe" |
| "This makes us think about war" | "This forces us to confront the dehumanizing reality of combat, challenging any romanticized notions" |
| "The reader is interested" | "This creates a sense of mystery and premonition, making us lean in to discover what's hidden" |

## **1.F Ideas vs Concepts: The Journey to Level 5-6**

**\[AI\_INTERNAL\]** This framework is critical for helping students progress from Level 3-4 to Level 5-6 responses.

### **The Progression**

| Level | Type | Characteristics | Example |
|-------|------|-----------------|---------|
| Level 2-3 | **Observation** | Surface-level, descriptive | "Owen describes cold weather" |
| Level 3-4 | **Idea** | Simple theme identification | "Owen shows that war is harsh" |
| Level 4-5 | **Contextual Understanding** | Links to context | "Owen's trench experience shaped his portrayal" |
| Level 5-6 | **Concept** | Abstract, synthesized, perceptive | "Owen subverts pastoral imagery to expose the lie of patriotic rhetoric" |
| Level 6 | **Comparative Concept** | What comparison reveals | "While Owen's nature-as-enemy imagery indicts pastoral promises, Hughes's synaesthetic fragmentation suggests trauma's assault on perception—together revealing how war poetry must reinvent form to represent the unrepresentable" |

### **Why This Matters for Comparison**

* AQA Level 5-6 descriptors require "thoughtful," "perceptive," "critical," and "exploratory" analysis
* Comparative concepts show what emerges from examining BOTH poems together
* Moving from ideas to comparative concepts demonstrates sophisticated synthesis
* The comparison itself should generate new insight

### **Prompting for Comparative Conceptual Thinking**

When students provide idea-level topic sentences:

* "That identifies what each poet does, but what's the deeper COMPARATIVE CONCEPT this reveals?"
* "What does seeing these two approaches TOGETHER tell us that neither alone would show?"
* "How do the poets' different contexts explain their different technique choices?"
* "If you had to describe what the COMPARISON reveals in one abstract phrase, what would it be?"
