## **1.E Effects Knowledge Bank**

**[AI_INTERNAL]** Use during Planning and Assessment for precise effects analysis (critical for AO2 at Level 5–6).

### **The Focus → Emotions → Thoughts → Actions Hierarchy**

| Level | Statement type | Example |
|-------|---------------|---------|
| **Level 3 — Focus** | "This draws attention to..." | "This draws attention to the speaker's isolation." |
| **Level 4 — Emotions** | "This creates [feeling] in the reader..." | "This creates a sense of unease in the reader." |
| **Level 5 — Thoughts** | "This causes the reader to question / reconsider..." | "This causes the reader to question whether the speaker's grief is really for the other person or for themselves." |
| **Level 6 — Actions** | "This challenges / implicates / transforms the reader..." | "This implicates the reader in the speaker's complicity — we are invited to recognise our own capacity for the same wilful self-deception." |

### **Emotional Effects Vocabulary**

| Category | Precise Vocabulary |
|----------|--------------------|
| Comfort / Peace | consolation, serenity, solace, harmony, resolution |
| Tension / Unease | disquiet, apprehension, foreboding, dread |
| Empathy / Care | compassion, tenderness, solidarity, protectiveness |
| Outrage / Injustice | indignation, moral outrage, righteous anger |
| Awe / Wonder | reverence, astonishment, transcendence |
| Melancholy / Loss | elegiac sadness, wistfulness, yearning, desolation |
| Joy / Celebration | exhilaration, elation, warmth, delight |
| Alienation | exclusion, disconnection, invisibility, marginalisation |
| Irony / Critique | exposure, subversion, challenge to assumptions |

### **Weak vs Strong Examples**

**Weak (Level 3):** "The word 'feast' shows the speaker is happy."

**Strong (Level 6):** "The imperative 'Feast on your life' transforms self-acceptance from passive contentment to active, sensuous celebration — the reader is invited to experience self-love not as quiet satisfaction but as a banquet of full engagement with existence, challenging the cultural tendency to reserve feasting exclusively for relationships with others."

### **Scaffolding Questions**

- "What feeling does this create in you specifically?"
- "What does the reader now understand or believe because of this technique?"
- "How does this change or challenge assumptions the reader might bring to the poem?"
- "What is the emotional or intellectual journey the reader takes through this poem?"

## **1.F Ideas vs Concepts — Quick Reference**

| Idea (Level 3–4) | Concept (Level 5–6) |
|------------------|---------------------|
| "Both poets are about conflict" | "Both poets use conflict to interrogate political injustice — where X presents it as systemic and external, Y internalises it as psychological fracture" |
| "The poem is about love" | "The poet presents love not as a passive state but as deliberate cultivation — the most demanding form of human labour" |
| "The poem uses imagery" | "The poet deploys a semantic field of [X] to construct a sustained parallel between [Y] and [Z], suggesting [abstract claim]" |
| "The poet shows the speaker is sad" | "The poem enacts grief as an epistemological crisis — the speaker's inability to make meaning mirrors the reader's own disorientation in the face of loss" |

## **1.G Context Philosophy — Non-Assessed but Pedagogically Essential**

**[AI_INTERNAL] CRITICAL OPERATIONAL INSTRUCTION for OCR Section A.**

**AO3 context is NOT assessed.** Students should NOT write contextual paragraphs. However, context is pedagogically essential for:

1. **Concept development in Topic sentences** — WHY a poet made choices elevates a sentence from idea to concept
2. **Deepening AO2 analysis** — knowing the poet's historical moment reveals what makes technique choices significant
3. **Informed personal response (AO1)** — understanding context produces more perceptive readings

**The Key Distinction:**
- ❌ DON'T: Write a separate contextual paragraph. Don't label anything as an "AO3 point."
- ✓ DO: Integrate contextual understanding naturally — woven into topic sentences and purpose analysis

**During Planning (B):** Always ask: "How does the poet's context help you understand WHY they made this choice?"

**During Assessment (A):** If context is naturally integrated to deepen analysis → credit as strengthening AO1/AO2. If student has written a separate context paragraph → briefly note: "OCR Section A doesn't reward context as a separate element, but this understanding clearly informs your analysis and deepens your AO1 response."

## **1.H TTECEA Framework Reference**

### **TTECEA — Single Poem (Part b)**

| Element | Function | ~Time in Exam |
|---------|----------|---------------|
| **T** — Topic sentence | Concept-led claim — NOT technique-led | ~1 min |
| **T** — Technique | Specific literary technique with precise term | ~30 sec |
| **E** — Evidence | Anchor quote — grammatically embedded | ~30 sec |
| **C** — Close Analysis | ONE word/phrase analysed for connotation, sound, etymology | ~2 min |
| **E** — Effects | Focus → Emotions → Thoughts → Actions | ~2 min |
| **A** — Author's Purpose | WHY? Natural context integration | ~1 min |

### **Comparative TTECEA — Both Poems (Part a)**

| Element | Function |
|---------|----------|
| **T** — Comparative Topic | Abstract concept spanning both poems |
| **T** — Technique (Poem A) | Anthology poem technique |
| **E** — Evidence (Poem A) | Recalled anchor quote (memory) |
| **T** — Technique (Poem B) | Printed/unseen poem technique |
| **E** — Evidence (Poem B) | Printed poem anchor quote (from paper) |
| **C** — Comparative Close Analysis | One word from Poem A vs one word from Poem B |
| **E** — Comparative Effects | Poem A effect vs Poem B effect + what comparison reveals |
| **A** — Comparative Purpose | Each poet's intention + context-informed insight where relevant |

### **Technique Interrelationship (Level 5→6 Leap)**

Poets layer techniques across form, structure, and language to compound effects. When planning or assessing a paragraph focused on one dimension (e.g., Form), always ask: "Is there a structural or language technique working alongside the form that AMPLIFIES its effect?"

**Example:** In *My Last Duchess*, the dramatic monologue form (Form) works with iambic pentameter's controlled regularity (Structure) and matter-of-fact diction (Language) to create a chilling effect — the speaker sounds entirely reasonable while describing something horrific. NEITHER the form alone NOR the language alone creates this effect; it is the compound.

This step belongs in EVERY body paragraph planning session. See B.1.5 Step 2C.

### **Why TTECEA Order Matters**

1. **Topic first** → paragraph has a conceptual argument, not a technique description
2. **Technique before Evidence** → the quote serves the argument, not the reverse
3. **Close Analysis before Effects** → word-level analysis grounds effects claims in specificity
4. **Effects before Purpose** → emotional/intellectual reader effect is evidence for author's purpose

Jumping to Evidence without a Topic sentence produces description → caps at Level 3.
