## **2.A Gold Standard Model Answer — Part (a) Comparison**

**[AI_INTERNAL]** Internal benchmark for Level 6 Part (a). Reference when constructing Gold Standard rewrites in Assessment Protocol A Step 4.

**Question:** Compare the ways in which these poems present how a person feels about themselves.
**Poem A (Anthology):** *Love After Love* — Derek Walcott
**Poem B (Printed/Unseen):** *Bride* — Maggie Smith
*(June 2024 OCR paper — used as worked example. In assessment, rewrite to match student's actual poems.)*

---

**[INTRODUCTION]**

Both Walcott and Smith explore the transformative power of self-acceptance, presenting it as an act of intimate devotion that demands the same emotional investment typically reserved for another person. Yet while Walcott constructs this epiphany as a future gift waiting to be claimed — a homecoming still to come — Smith inhabits it as an already-unfolding present-tense reality, positioning self-love not as an arrival but as a continuous daily practice of becoming.

---

**[BODY 1 — FORM]**

Both poets employ free verse as a vehicle for authentic, unmediated self-revelation, the absence of inherited formal constraint itself embodying the liberation each poem celebrates — yet the specific texture of their free verse reveals fundamentally different philosophical stances toward self-knowledge. Walcott shapes *Love After Love* through loosely irregular stanzas that fracture and expand organically, the variable line lengths resisting any fixed external discipline; the second-person address — "you will greet yourself arriving / at your own door" — simultaneously addresses the reader, the self, and all humanity, collapsing the distance between the personal and the universal. Smith, by contrast, organises *Bride* into compressed two- and three-line stanzas that function almost like vows or private affirmations, each unit self-contained and settled. The compressed form mirrors the poem's controlling conceit: the stanza as a kind of ring, an unbroken circle of self-enclosure. Where Walcott's "photographs, the desperate notes" sprawl across a line to convey the scattered debris of a life lived outwardly, Smith's phrase "Married less / to the man than to the woman" achieves its meaning through strategic enjambment — the line break after "less" momentarily holding the negative in suspension before revealing the counter-claim. The word "silvering" — "silvering with the mirror" — is particularly striking: simultaneously the mirror's surface reflecting light AND the speaker herself ageing into self-knowledge, fusing the observer and the observed into a single image. Walcott's expansive form creates a quality of gradual release in the reader — an unclenching, a slow letting-go; Smith's tighter, more compressed structure generates settled conviction that feels almost startling. Walcott, shaped by a Caribbean post-colonial tradition in which the 'stranger' self has been diminished by colonial encounter, charges self-recognition with urgency beyond the merely psychological; Smith, writing from a contemporary feminist tradition, uses the compact form to propose the self as a sufficient and complete unit — a claim carrying the quiet radicalism of a philosophical position, not merely a personal feeling. These different forms compound with the poems' respective language registers: Walcott's imperative "Feast" (Language) and Smith's legal vocabulary "wed" (Language) both work WITH their free verse forms to enact the very acts they describe — one a communal ceremony, the other a private covenant.

---

**[BODY 2 — LANGUAGE]**

Both poets deploy extended metaphors drawn from the language of ceremony and devotion to articulate self-acceptance as an act of profound commitment — yet the specific ceremonies each poet invokes reveal distinctly different registers and cultural inflections of self-love. Walcott's extended feast metaphor — "Give wine. Give bread. Give back your heart" — draws on the ritual language of Eucharistic communion, constructing self-love as simultaneously nourishing, sacred, and communal; the tripling of the imperative "Give" has a liturgical cadence, investing self-acceptance with spiritual generosity. Smith's controlling metaphor is that of marriage — "wed to myself," "I am my own bride, / lifting the veil to see / my face" — transforming the institution of union into a private covenant with the self. The verb "ignored" in Walcott — "the stranger who was your self... whom you ignored / for another" — carries a reproachful tenderness: not hatred of the self, but careless, absentminded neglect that invites gentle recognition rather than punishment. Smith's "I know the kind of wife / I need and I become her" operates differently: the present-tense shift from "knowing" to "becoming" suggests that self-love is not a discovered truth but a continuously performed one — an ongoing act of will. Walcott's feast metaphor generates warmth, nourishment, and welcome in the reader — we may feel drawn toward the imagery as toward a meal laid for us, invited to take our place at the table; Smith's marriage metaphor, with its undertones of legal and spiritual covenant, generates a sense of self-possession and autonomy that may feel both quietly radical and deeply consoling to a reader accustomed to defining themselves through their relationships with others. Walcott uses the feast — a communal, generous act — to suggest that self-acceptance can be shared and transmitted; Smith's more intimate marriage ceremony frames self-love as a fundamental right and obligation — as necessary and serious as any formal human covenant.

---

**[BODY 3 — STRUCTURE]**

Both poems are structured as journeys from separation or uncertainty to integration and self-knowledge — but the direction of each journey is strikingly reversed, revealing different assumptions about where in human experience this self-knowledge awaits. Walcott's poem opens in future tense — "The time will come / when... you will greet yourself" — sustaining the promise as not-yet-achieved but inevitable; Smith structures her poem around accumulating rhetorical questions — "How long have I been wed / to myself?... How long have I been / alone in this house but not / alone?" — that build towards triumphant declaration: "I am my own bride." The enjambment in Walcott's final lines — "Give back your heart / to itself, to the stranger who has loved you / all your life" — stretches the act of giving across three lines, slowing time and investing it with ceremony; the paratactic arrest at "Sit. Feast on your life." then shatters this flow with a jolt of arrival. Smith's enjambment throughout enacts the speaker's excited momentum toward self-discovery, each stanza spilling forward into the next. The closing "I have waited for you all my life" echoes Walcott's "all your life" but inverts its direction: where Walcott's stranger has been patiently waiting to be noticed, Smith's speaker has been the one who was waiting. Walcott's forward-moving temporal structure creates hope and anticipation in the reader — we are drawn toward the poem's end as toward a promised destination; Smith's retrospective questioning followed by present-tense declaration generates satisfaction, the poem enacting in its own structure the self-recognition it describes.

---

**[CONCLUSION]**

Both Walcott and Smith present self-love not as narcissism but as the most demanding and necessary relationship available to us — a covenant of devoted attention to a self too long neglected. Walcott's poem offers this as a universal promise, his future tense suggesting an epiphany latent in all human lives; Smith's present-tense declaration insists that self-knowledge is already within reach — requiring only that we lift the veil and look. Together, the poems present self-acceptance as both a destination we travel toward and a home we already inhabit, waiting only to be recognised.

## **2.B Gold Standard Model Answer — Part (b) Single Poem**

**[AI_INTERNAL]** Internal benchmark for Level 6 Part (b).

**Question:** Explore in detail one other poem from your anthology which presents a satisfying relationship.
**Poem:** *How Do I Love Thee? (Sonnet 43)* — Elizabeth Barrett Browning

---

**[INTRODUCTION]**

Barrett Browning's *Sonnet 43* presents love not as a passive emotional state but as an act of absolute, unceasing measurement — the speaker is compelled to account for every dimension of love because it has infiltrated every dimension of her being, leaving no aspect of selfhood untouched. In this poem, a satisfying relationship is one that constitutes and completes the self rather than supplementing it.

---

**[BODY 1 — LANGUAGE]**

Barrett Browning presents love as spatially and spiritually boundless by constructing an extended metaphor of measurement — the act of measuring love enacting precisely the impossibility of containing it. This metaphor operates through an extended enumeration — "I love thee to the depth and breadth and height / My soul can reach" — establishing love as something three-dimensional, occupying every possible physical direction. The tricolon "depth and breadth and height" maps love across all three spatial axes simultaneously, suggesting it fills the entirety of existence rather than a single direction. The word "reach" is particularly resonant: simultaneously a measurement (the furthest point the soul can extend) and an aspiration (the act of reaching toward something greater than oneself). The soul's participation — "My soul can reach" — elevates love beyond the physical or emotional into a spiritual act. This language generates in the reader a sense of overwhelming fullness — love experienced not as a feeling but as an atmosphere that surrounds and permeates, that the reader may find themselves reconsidering the familiar emotional limits of their own experience. Barrett Browning, writing as a Victorian woman in an era when female desire was carefully policed, uses the language of philosophy and measurement to claim emotional territory with intellectual authority — the precision of "depth and breadth and height" borrowing from scientific discourse to quietly insist that a woman's love is subject to the same rigorous accounting as any measurable reality.

---

**[BODY 2 — FORM]**

By choosing the Petrarchan sonnet form, Barrett Browning deliberately places her declaration within a tradition historically associated with the male admiring gaze — and then systematically fills that form with a distinctly female voice, claiming the tradition for a different kind of love. The Petrarchan structure (octave ABBAABBA + sestet CDECDE) encloses the poem in a tightly controlled formal architecture, while the volta at line 9 marks the moment the poem's tone shifts from enumeration to spiritual intensity: "I love thee freely, as men strive for Right; / I love thee purely, as they turn from Praise." The word "freely" at the volta is structurally significant — it appears at the exact pivot of the poem, suggesting that freedom is its fulcrum: this love is not obligatory or performed but genuinely chosen. The comparison "as men strive for Right" is striking in its gender specificity — Barrett Browning does not say "as people strive" but as "men strive," aligning her love with the moral seriousness historically reserved for male public endeavour. The sonnet form creates a paradox the reader feels: the tightest possible formal constraint is used to describe the most unlimited of emotional states, making the reader experience the pressure of feeling too large for its own expression. Barrett Browning uses the sonnet — the quintessential love poem form — to assert that her love is as serious and as worthy of formal elaboration as any love celebrated in the male tradition.

---

**[BODY 3 — STRUCTURE]**

Barrett Browning structures the poem as an escalating catalogue of love's qualities, each accumulation leading inevitably to the poem's final transformation — love not only as the largest thing in the speaker's life but as something that will outlast life itself. The anaphoric repetition of "I love thee" across the poem creates an incantatory, liturgical structure, each new clause adding a further dimension to the love being described. The poem's final movement — "and, if God choose, / I shall but love thee better after death" — introduces the poem's only moment of uncertainty: the conditional "if God choose" is a single wavering before vast confidence. Yet "I shall but love thee better after death" transforms that uncertainty into a statement of such conviction that the conditional is effectively annihilated; the word "but" here means "only" or "merely," as if loving better after death is the smallest, most natural consequence imaginable. The anaphoric escalation generates in the reader a sensation of building — each repetition of "I love thee" like a further room opening in a vast house of feeling, until the final clause reveals that the house extends beyond the visible world entirely. The extension beyond death suggests that love, for Barrett Browning, is the one human experience that refuses to be bounded by mortality — and the reader is implicated in this vision, invited to consider whether their own experience of love has this dimension, or whether they have settled for something smaller.

---

**[CONCLUSION]**

Barrett Browning presents a satisfying relationship as one that achieves completeness — it fills every spatial dimension, aligns with every moral aspiration, and outlasts every physical limit. Most powerfully, the poem argues that love of this quality does not require the beloved to be physically present — it is an internal state, a mode of being, that ultimately transcends the relationship that occasioned it. The sonnet form, the accumulated anaphora, and the final volta into death together argue that to love fully is to discover what the soul is actually made of.
