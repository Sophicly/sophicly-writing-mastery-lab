## **B.1.2 — Form vs Structure Teaching Moment**

📌 Planning > Part (a) > Framework > Step 2 of 10

SAY: "Before we plan, let's make sure the Form/Structure/Language framework is clear — this is where many students lose marks by confusing Form and Structure.

**FORM = WHAT TYPE of poem it is (the genre)**
Examples: sonnet, dramatic monologue, free verse, elegy, ballad, lyric, ode

**STRUCTURE = HOW the poem is built internally (the patterns)**
Examples: iambic pentameter, ABAB rhyme scheme, enjambment, caesura, volta, stanza arrangement

**LANGUAGE = WORD-LEVEL choices**
Examples: metaphor, imagery, semantic fields, sound devices, diction

**Common mistake:** 'The form is iambic pentameter' — WRONG.
'The poem is written in sonnet form; its structure uses iambic pentameter.' — CORRECT.

Think of it like a building: Form = type of building (cathedral, cottage). Structure = how it's built (arched ceilings, stone walls). Language = the decorative detail (carvings, stained glass).

Does that distinction make sense?

**A** — Yes, I understand — let's move on
**B** — Can you give me a poetry example to make it clearer?"

**[AI_INTERNAL]:**
- If A: Proceed to B.1.3.
- If B: SAY "Let's use *Ozymandias* by Shelley: **Form** = sonnet (a 14-line poem with volta, traditionally about love — Shelley subverts this to write about power and decay). **Structure** = irregular rhyme scheme (ABABACDCEDEFEF), disrupted iambic pentameter at 'Two vast and trunkless legs of stone,' strategic enjambment. These are different. The form tells us what TYPE of poem; the structure tells us HOW it's built. Does that example help?
A) Yes
B) I'd like another example"
