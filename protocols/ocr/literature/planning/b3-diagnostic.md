**Pedagogical Note: Why We Plan Body Paragraphs First**

Say: "Before we gather quotes, a quick note about our planning sequence: **We'll plan your three body paragraphs first, then your introduction, then your conclusion.** This might seem backwards, but here's why it works: your ideas will evolve as you plan—they *should* evolve. If you plan your introduction first, you lock yourself into ideas before you've fully developed them. Planning body paragraphs first gives you flexibility to discover your strongest arguments, then craft an introduction that accurately reflects your *developed* thinking. This creates a cohesive whole rather than forcing your essay to match early ideas that might not be your best."

But if you plan your introduction *after* your body paragraphs, you can introduce the actual argument you've developed—not a guess about what you might say. **CHUNK 2 \- Text Structure and Meaning:**

"Here's why this matters beyond just showing you know the plot:

Authors deliberately structure their texts so that meaning **builds and develops** from beginning to end. When you analyze quotes from all three positions, you're not just summarizing plot—you're showing how the author constructs meaning across the text's architecture. OCR examiners call this 'understanding of how the text develops' and it's essential for Level 5-6."

**CHUNK 3 \- Applying B/M/E Strategy:**

"**How to Apply This to Your Essay:**

Even if your focus is a **theme** (like redemption, ambition, duality) or a **secondary character** (like Banquo, Fred, Utterson), the same principle applies—everything in the text relates back to the protagonist's journey. Themes develop through the protagonist's experiences, and secondary characters exist to illuminate aspects of the protagonist.

**Flexibility Note:** If your focus is a character who doesn't appear throughout the entire text (example: Banquo mainly appears in Macbeth Acts 1-3), adapt B/M/E to cover where that character IS significant. The goal is showing development across their appearance, not forcing artificial coverage of absent material.

**Extract Integration:** If your question includes an extract, ensure at least ONE of your B/M/E quotes comes from that extract. This shows you can integrate close reading of the passage with whole-text understanding."
