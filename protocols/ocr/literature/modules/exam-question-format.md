# BOARD FORMAT MODULE — OCR GCSE English Literature

**Board:** OCR GCSE English Literature (J352)
**Papers:** Component 1 (Modern + 19th Century) | Component 2 (Shakespeare + Poetry + Unseen)
**Format:** PAIRED-EXTRACT for 19th Century, single extract for Shakespeare and Modern

---

## QUESTION FORMAT RULES — NON-NEGOTIABLE

### 19th Century Novel (Component 1, Section B) — PAIRED EXTRACT FORMAT

This is OCR's unique feature. Students receive TWO extracts from the same novel.

1. **Phrasing:** *"Explore how [author] presents [theme/character] in these extracts and elsewhere in the novel."*
2. **TWO extracts required:** Extract A (~15 lines) and Extract B (~15 lines) from DIFFERENT parts of the novel
3. **Single question:** ONE question covering both extracts
4. **Mark allocation:** 40 marks
5. **The two extracts should show contrast or development** — e.g. Scrooge in Stave 1 vs Stave 5, or Jekyll's composure vs Hyde's violence

### Shakespeare (Component 2, Section A) — SINGLE EXTRACT

1. **Phrasing:** *"Explore how Shakespeare presents [theme/character] in this extract and elsewhere in the play."*
2. **Extract:** ~20 lines of real text with speaker labels
3. **Single question:** ONE question
4. **Mark allocation:** 40 marks

### Modern Prose/Drama (Component 1, Section A) — SINGLE EXTRACT

1. **Phrasing:** *"Explore how [author] presents [theme/character] in this extract and elsewhere in the [novel/play]."*
2. **Extract:** ~20 lines
3. **Single question:** ONE question
4. **Mark allocation:** 40 marks

---

## KEY OCR DIFFERENCE — PAIRED EXTRACTS

For 19th Century texts, OCR gives students MORE textual evidence than any other board (two passages instead of one). This demands:
- Close reading of BOTH passages
- Comparison/contrast between the two extracts
- Plus discussion of the wider novel

When generating paired extracts, choose passages that:
- Come from DIFFERENT parts of the novel (not the same chapter)
- Show a **contrast, development, or connection** in the theme/character
- Together, tell a story about how the theme/character evolves

## COMPLETE QUESTION STRUCTURE — 19th Century (Paired Extract)

1. **Extract header** — *"Read the two extracts below from [title] and then answer the question."*
2. **Extract A header** — *"Extract A — from [Chapter/Stave reference]"*
3. **Context line A** — italicised, brief context
4. **Extract A text** — ~15 lines
5. **Extract B header** — *"Extract B — from [Chapter/Stave reference]"*
6. **Context line B** — italicised, brief context
7. **Extract B text** — ~15 lines
8. **The question** — *"Explore how [author] presents [theme/character] in these extracts and elsewhere in the novel."*
9. **Mark allocation** — (40 marks)

## COMPLETE QUESTION STRUCTURE — Shakespeare / Modern (Single Extract)

1. **Extract header** — *"Read the extract below from [title] and then answer the question."*
2. **Context line** — italicised
3. **The actual extract** — ~20 lines
4. **The question** — *"Explore how [author] presents [theme/character] in this extract and elsewhere in the [play/novel]."*
5. **Mark allocation** — (40 marks)

## EXAMPLE OUTPUT — 19th Century Paired Extract (A Christmas Carol)

**Read the two extracts below from A Christmas Carol and then answer the question.**

**Extract A — from Stave 1**

*In this extract, we are introduced to Scrooge.*

> Oh! But he was a tight-fisted hand at the grindstone, Scrooge! a squeezing, wrenching, grasping, scraping, clutching, covetous, old sinner! Hard and sharp as flint, from which no steel had ever struck out generous fire; secret, and self-contained, and solitary as an oyster. The cold within him froze his old features, nipped his pointed nose, shrivelled his cheek, stiffened his gait; made his eyes red, his thin lips blue; and spoke out shrewdly in his grating voice. He carried his own low temperature always about with him; he iced his office in the dog-days; and didn't thaw it one degree at Christmas.

**Extract B — from Stave 5**

*In this extract, Scrooge has woken on Christmas morning after the visits of the three spirits.*

> "I don't know what to do!" cried Scrooge, laughing and crying in the same breath. "I am as light as a feather, I am as happy as an angel, I am as merry as a schoolboy. I am as giddy as a drunken man. A merry Christmas to everybody! A happy New Year to all the world! Hallo here! Whoop! Hallo!"
>
> He had been sobbing violently in his conflict with the Spirit, and his face was wet with tears. "I will live in the Past, the Present, and the Future!" Scrooge repeated, as he scrambled out of bed. "The Spirits of all Three shall strive within me. Oh Jacob Marley! Heaven and the Christmas Time be praised for this! I say it on my knees, old Jacob; on my knees!"

**Explore how Dickens presents Scrooge's transformation in these extracts and elsewhere in the novel.**

(40 marks)

## EXAMPLE OUTPUT — Shakespeare (Macbeth)

**Read the extract below from Act 1 Scene 7 of Macbeth and then answer the question.**

*In this extract, Macbeth considers the reasons not to proceed with the plan.*

> **MACBETH:**
> He's here in double trust:
> First, as I am his kinsman and his subject,
> Strong both against the deed; then, as his host,
> Who should against his murderer shut the door,
> Not bear the knife myself. Besides, this Duncan
> Hath borne his faculties so meek, hath been
> So clear in his great office, that his virtues
> Will plead like angels, trumpet-tongued, against
> The deep damnation of his taking-off;
> And pity, like a naked new-born babe,
> Striding the blast, or heaven's cherubin horsed
> Upon the sightless couriers of the air,
> Shall blow the horrid deed in every eye,
> That tears shall drown the wind. I have no spur
> To prick the sides of my intent, but only
> Vaulting ambition, which o'erleaps itself
> And falls on the other.

**Explore how Shakespeare presents Macbeth's ambition in this extract and elsewhere in the play.**

(40 marks)
