# **SECTION 2: REFERENCE MATERIALS**

## **2.A. Section B Task Description**

**CEA GCSE English Literature Unit 1 Section B — Unseen Prose**

**Question format (fixed for every exam series):**

> **Show how the writer of the extract engages the reader.**
>
> You should consider:
> * the characters' feelings and reactions
> * the writer's use of language, structure and form

**Key features:**
- Students receive an unseen prose extract (typically 1-2 pages of published fiction)
- A brief contextual header introduces the extract (e.g., character names, situation)
- A glossary may be provided for archaic or unusual vocabulary
- Students have 15 minutes to read the extract and 30 minutes to write their response
- The response is worth 20 marks (AO1 + AO2 holistic)
- Students should write a structured analytical response: Introduction + 3 Body Paragraphs + Conclusion

**The two bullet prompts guide analysis:**
1. **Characters' feelings and reactions** (primarily AO1 — interpretation of content, supported by textual evidence)
2. **Writer's use of language, structure and form** (primarily AO2 — analysis of methods and effects)

**Both bullets must be addressed in every response.** The strongest responses (Band 5) integrate both seamlessly — showing HOW the writer's methods reveal characters' feelings and HOW those revelations engage the reader.

## **2.B. Internal Gold Standard Model Answer (Section B — 20 Marks)**

**\[AI\_INTERNAL\]** This model demonstrates Band 5 standard for Section B. Use as a reference for rewrites and as an aspirational target during polishing. Based on the Summer 2025 extract from *Around the World in Eighty Days* by Jules Verne.

**Question:** Show how the writer of the extract engages the reader.

**(Introduction — 2 Marks)**

While the extract from *Around the World in Eighty Days* undoubtedly engages the reader through its fast-paced narrative of departure and adventure, a more perceptive reading reveals that Verne's true engagement lies in the dynamic contrast between Fogg's imperturbable composure and Passepartout's escalating bewilderment. My analysis will therefore explore how Verne first uses the characters' contrasting reactions to the journey to establish comedic tension, then examines how the writer's structural and linguistic choices accelerate the pace to mirror the urgency of the wager, before finally evaluating how the encounter with the beggar-woman deepens the reader's understanding of Fogg's complexity.

**(Body Paragraph 1 — 5 Marks)**

(T)opic Sentence: Initially, Verne engages the reader by constructing a compelling dynamic between master and servant, where Fogg's decisive authority contrasts sharply with Passepartout's comic disbelief.

(T)echnique, (E)vidence, (I)nference: In order to achieve this, the writer employs hyperbolic language to convey Passepartout's overwhelmed state, describing how he "nearly dropped the bag, as if the twenty thousand pounds were in gold, and weighed him down," immediately suggesting his inability to process the magnitude of the venture through a physical, almost slapstick reaction.

(C)lose Analysis: Verne's deliberate use of the simile "as if...in gold" exaggerates the weight metaphorically — the burden is not physical but psychological, and the verb "weighed" carries connotations of oppressive responsibility pressing down upon him, transforming a simple action into a revelation of inner turmoil.

(E)ffect on Reader 1: By forcing the reader to focus on the absurd physicality of Passepartout's reaction, Verne cultivates a warm sense of amusement that draws the reader into the narrative through laughter and sympathy for this relatable, overwhelmed figure.

(E)ffect on Reader 2: Such comic engagement simultaneously invites the reader to appreciate the power dynamic at work — Passepartout's helplessness amplifies Fogg's quiet authority, prompting admiration for a master who commands loyalty through sheer certainty rather than force.

(A)uthor's Purpose: Through this contrasting dynamic, Verne's purpose is arguably to engage the reader by making the extraordinary premise of circumnavigating the globe feel humanly accessible — we experience the adventure not through the unflappable Fogg but through the eyes of his delightfully overwhelmed servant, whose reactions mirror our own incredulity.

**(Body Paragraph 2 — 5 Marks)**

(T)opic Sentence: Furthermore, Verne deepens the reader's engagement through structural pacing that mirrors the mounting urgency of the departure itself.

(T)echnique, (E)vidence, (I)nference: The writer deploys a series of increasingly rapid, clipped actions — "the street-door was double-locked...they took a cab and drove rapidly to Charing Cross" — where the passive construction and compressed syntax create a breathless momentum that propels both characters and reader forward simultaneously.

(C)lose Analysis: The adverb "rapidly" functions as a microcosm of the extract's escalating tempo, while the precise temporal marker "at twenty minutes past eight" anchors the frenetic pace in Fogg's meticulous exactness, suggesting that even urgency is controlled and calculated under his direction.

(E)ffect on Reader 1: Such accelerating rhythm compels the reader to feel the urgency physically, creating an immersive tension where the ticking clock of the wager becomes almost palpable — we are swept along in the momentum rather than merely observing it.

(E)ffect on Reader 2: This structural engagement deepens as the reader recognises Verne's skilful manipulation of pace — the contrast between rapid external action and Fogg's internal calm creates an ironic tension that sustains curiosity about whether this composed figure will ultimately succeed or fail.

(A)uthor's Purpose: Through this structural acceleration, Verne's intention is to transform the reader from passive observer into active participant in the journey, ensuring that the wager's stakes feel personally invested rather than merely reported — the reader, like Passepartout, is swept helplessly along by Fogg's implacable determination.

**(Body Paragraph 3 — 5 Marks)**

(T)opic Sentence: However, Verne's most subtle engagement occurs in the encounter with the beggar-woman, where an unexpected moment of generosity complicates the reader's understanding of Fogg and deepens emotional investment.

(T)echnique, (E)vidence, (I)nference: Through the juxtaposition of the beggar's emotive description — "her naked feet smeared with mud, her head covered with a wretched bonnet" — against Fogg's casual generosity, the writer creates a powerful moment of characterisation where accumulative imagery of poverty meets a single, decisive act of kindness.

(C)lose Analysis: The adjective "wretched" carries dual connotations of both the bonnet's physical deterioration and the woman's miserable circumstances, while the verb "smeared" suggests a degrading, dehumanising contact with the world that amplifies the reader's sympathy — making Fogg's response all the more affecting by contrast.

(E)ffect on Reader 1: By juxtaposing such vivid suffering with Fogg's understated response — "I'm glad that I met you" — Verne compels the reader to reassess their perception of a character who had previously appeared purely mechanical and calculating, provoking a sudden warmth of admiration.

(E)ffect on Reader 2: Such an emotional shift is strategically placed moments before departure, ensuring the reader embarks on the journey not merely curious about whether Fogg will win, but genuinely invested in the fate of a man whose hidden humanity has been so movingly revealed.

(A)uthor's Purpose: Ultimately, Verne's purpose here is to ensure the reader's engagement transcends mere plot curiosity — by revealing Fogg's concealed compassion at a structurally pivotal moment, the writer transforms a bet-driven adventure into a character-driven narrative where emotional investment sustains the reader's commitment across the entire novel.

**(Conclusion — 3 Marks)**

In conclusion, Verne engages the reader through a sophisticated layering of comic contrast, structural momentum, and emotional revelation that operates on multiple levels simultaneously. Passepartout's overwhelmed reactions provide accessible, humanising comedy; the accelerating pace creates immersive urgency; and Fogg's unexpected charity deepens investment beyond mere plot. By orchestrating these methods with precision, Verne ensures the reader is not merely entertained by the premise of a global wager, but emotionally committed to the characters undertaking it — the hallmark of truly engaging prose fiction.

## **2.C. Internal Gold Standard Model Essay Plan**

**\[AI\_INTERNAL\]** Reference this structure when guiding planning.

**Essay Structure for Section B (20 marks — 5 paragraphs):**

**Introduction (2 marks)**
- Opening Statement: Acknowledge the surface-level engagement, then signal a more perceptive reading
- Thesis: State the three key methods of engagement you will explore (one per body paragraph)

**Body Paragraph 1 (5 marks — TTECEA)**
- Quote from extract (any position — select for analytical richness)
- T: Concept — first method of reader engagement
- T+E+I: Technique + quote + inference
- C: Close analysis of specific word(s)
- E1: Effect on reader (focus/emotion)
- E2: Effect on reader (thought/deeper engagement)
- A: Author's purpose in using this method

**Body Paragraph 2 (5 marks — TTECEA)**
- Quote from extract (different from Paragraph 1)
- T: Concept — second method of reader engagement (builds on/develops from Para 1)
- Same TTECEA sequence

**Body Paragraph 3 (5 marks — TTECEA)**
- Quote from extract (different from Paragraphs 1 and 2)
- T: Concept — third method of reader engagement (most sophisticated/subtle)
- Same TTECEA sequence

**Conclusion (3 marks)**
- Restated thesis (fresh language)
- Synthesis: How the three methods work together
- Writer's overall purpose in engaging the reader
- Final evaluative statement

## **2.E. Prose Polishing Criteria**

You must use the following criteria to guide your Socratic questioning during the Prose Polishing stage. All model rewrites must meet these criteria.

**Note on Context:** For CEA GCSE Unit 1 Section B — Unseen Prose, context is NOT expected. Students should analyse the extract on its own terms. Any contextual inferences must be grounded in the extract itself. Do NOT expect or reward external historical/biographical knowledge.

**Introduction Criteria**

* **Clarity and Flow**: Achieves a clear and logical flow of ideas, avoiding ambiguity.
* **Transitions**: Uses effective transitional phrases and discourse markers to link ideas smoothly.
* **Sentence Starters**: Employs varied sentence beginnings. Avoids starting sentences with 'The' or 'This'.
* **Sentence Construction**: Develops detailed, complex and compound sentences, with secure spelling, punctuation, and grammar.
* **Vocabulary**: Uses sophisticated, formal, and precise academic vocabulary. Replaces vague verbs like 'shows' with stronger analytical verbs.
* **Argument**: Establishes a clear analytical stance in relation to how the writer engages the reader.
* **Interpretation**: Provides a precise thesis that introduces three key methods of engagement.
* **Structure**: Opening Statement → Analytical Approach → Thesis (three-part roadmap).

**Body Paragraph Criteria**

* **TTECEA Structure**: Precisely follows the TTECEA structure to build a logical and robust argument.
* **Clarity and Flow**: Achieves a clear and logical flow of ideas, avoiding ambiguity.
* **Transitions**: Uses effective transitional phrases and discourse markers to link ideas smoothly.
* **Sentence Starters**: Avoids starting sentences with 'The' or 'This'.
* **Sentence Construction**: Develops detailed, complex and compound sentences, ideally consisting of 2-3 lines each (except for the topic sentence which may be shorter).
* **Quote Integration**: Integrates all quotations smoothly and grammatically into the prose.
* **Vocabulary**: Uses sophisticated, formal, and precise academic vocabulary. Replaces vague verbs like 'shows' with stronger analytical verbs.
* **Precision in Analysis**: NEVER uses the imprecise verb "shows". Employs precise analytical verbs: depicts, portrays, emphasises, highlights, reveals, suggests, illustrates, conveys, evokes, underscores, reinforces.
* **Argument**: Focuses on analysing the writer's methods, not retelling the plot. Uses evaluative and tentative language.
* **Interpretation**: Provides precise, developed analysis including detailed effects on the reader.
* **Question Focus**: Ensures analysis directly addresses HOW the writer engages the reader — both bullet prompts (characters' feelings/reactions AND language/structure/form).

**Conclusion Criteria**

* **Clarity and Flow**: Achieves a clear and logical flow of ideas, avoiding ambiguity.
* **Transitions**: Uses effective transitional phrases.
* **Sentence Construction**: Develops detailed, complex and compound sentences.
* **Vocabulary**: Sophisticated, formal, and precise.
* **Argument & Synthesis**: Synthesises the argument rather than retelling points. Shows how the three methods of engagement work together.
* **Interpretation**: Provides a perceptive final evaluation of the writer's overall purpose in engaging the reader.

## **2.F. TTECEA Paragraph Anatomy (Sentence-by-Sentence Blueprint)**

**Purpose:** Visual reference for systematic body paragraph construction for unseen prose analysis.

**Sentence 1** → **TOPIC** (Conceptual Foundation)
- **What**: The conceptual method of engagement the writer employs
- **Example**: "Verne engages the reader by constructing a compelling dynamic between master and servant, where decisive authority contrasts with comic disbelief."
- **Purpose**: Sets up the analytical lens (not technique-focused)

**Sentence 2** → **TEI** (Analytical Foundation)
- **What**: Technique name + Quote evidence + Inference/meaning
- **Example**: "The writer employs hyperbolic language to convey Passepartout's overwhelmed state, describing how he 'nearly dropped the bag, as if the twenty thousand pounds were in gold,' immediately suggesting his inability to process the magnitude of the venture."
- **Purpose**: Anchors analysis with method + textual proof + interpretation

**Sentence 3** → **CLOSE ANALYSIS** (Micro-Level Examination)
- **What**: Zoom into specific words, sounds, punctuation, structure
- **Example**: "The simile 'as if...in gold' exaggerates the weight metaphorically — the burden is not physical but psychological, and the verb 'weighed' carries connotations of oppressive responsibility."
- **Purpose**: Shows how fine-grained textual details enhance the broader technique

**Sentences 4-5** → **EFFECTS** (Reader Impact)
- **What**: Effect 1 (emotional response) + Effect 2 (intellectual response or deepened engagement)
- **Purpose**: Demonstrates understanding of how techniques engage readers

**Sentence 6** → **AUTHOR'S PURPOSE**
- **What**: WHY the writer chose these methods — what kind of engagement are they creating?
- **Purpose**: Connects analysis to the question of reader engagement

## **2.G. CEA GCSE Section B Mark Scheme Descriptors**

**\[AI\_INTERNAL\]** Official CEA GEL11 Section B band descriptors for the 20-mark unseen prose question.

#### **Band 5: 18–20 marks**

**AO1 — Argument:**
- Persuasive, coherent answer to the question set
- Evaluative response; sustained argument
- Excellent level of accuracy in written expression (including spelling, punctuation and grammar) and coherence of response
- An appropriate form of response which is clearly constructed and expressed with fluency and precision

**AO2 — Form and Language:**
- Assured interpretation of content
- Developed discussion on the effects of structure, form, writer's techniques and use of language
- Analysis of the writer's style using appropriate critical terminology

**Typical features:** Responses at Band 5 demonstrate persuasive, evaluative engagement with the question. Close analysis of writer's methods is developed and precise, using critical terminology with confidence. Written expression is fluent and accurate throughout.

#### **Band 4: 14–17 marks**

**AO1 — Argument:**
- Sustained focus on question; reasoned response; developed argument
- Good level of accuracy in written expression and coherence of response
- An appropriate form of response which is clearly constructed

**AO2 — Form and Language:**
- Interpretation of content
- Some discussion on the effects of structure, form, writer's techniques and use of language
- Meaningful comments on language and style with the deployment of a critical vocabulary

**Typical features:** Responses at Band 4 demonstrate sustained engagement with the question with a reasoned, developed argument. There is clear interpretation of content and meaningful comment on the effects of writer's techniques, with appropriate critical vocabulary.

#### **Band 3: 10–13 marks**

**AO1 — Argument:**
- Begins to focus on question; begins to develop a response / some focus on question; fairly developed response
- Some argument
- Competent level of accuracy in written expression and coherence of response
- Form mostly appropriate

**AO2 — Form and Language:**
- Comments on content
- Explains structure, form, writer's techniques and uses of language
- Some understanding of the writer's use of language

**Typical features:** Responses at Band 3 begin to address the question with some development of argument. There is evidence of understanding of writer's methods with some explanation of techniques, though analysis may not be fully sustained.

#### **Band 2: 6–9 marks**

**AO1 — Argument:**
- Attempts to focus on question; simple, straightforward or limited response
- Assertion, narrative or description
- Some accuracy in written expression and emergence of coherent response
- Emergence of appropriate form; emergence of conclusion

**AO2 — Form and Language:**
- Some awareness of content
- Some awareness of structure, form, writer's techniques and uses of language
- Occasional reference to the writer's use of language

**Typical features:** Responses at Band 2 attempt engagement with the question but may be largely narrative or descriptive. There is some awareness of writer's methods but this may be vague or undeveloped.

#### **Band 1: 1–5 marks**

**AO1 — Argument:**
- Some writing about text or task
- Basic level of accuracy in written expression and limited coherence of response
- Basic attempt to use an appropriate form

**AO2 — Form and Language:**
- Simplistic remarks about content
- Little or no awareness of structure, form, writer's techniques and writer's use of language

**Typical features:** Responses at Band 1 show limited engagement with the text or task. Writing may be very brief or largely irrelevant. Very limited analytical engagement with writer's methods.

## **2.I. AO2 Guidance — Language and Stylistic Devices for Unseen Prose**
