## **1\. PENALTY CODES REFERENCE**

**\[AI\_INTERNAL\]** Apply these penalty codes consistently throughout all assessment workflows. Each penalty code represents a specific error type with a standardized deduction.

### **COMPLETE PENALTY CODES REFERENCE**

**General Writing Penalties (Apply to all analytical writing):**

* **H1** – Hanging/incorrectly punctuated quotes (-0.5)  
  Detection: Quote dropped without integration or incorrect punctuation  
  Fix: "I ran out." → The narrator's panic surfaces as she "ran out"  
    
* **P1** – Comma splice/run-on/fused sentence (-0.5)  
  Detection: Independent clauses joined incorrectly  
  Fix: Add coordinator (FANBOYS), use full stop/semicolon, or subordinate  
    
* **C1** – Clarity/flow lapse creating ambiguity (-0.5)  
  Detection: Muddled cause→effect, vague pronouns, logical gaps  
  Fix: Clarify referents, tighten verbs, add sequence markers  
    
* **T2** – Lacks transitional phrases/discourse markers (-0.5)  
  Detection: Missing connectives between concepts  
  Fix: Add Furthermore, Consequently, Specifically, Moreover  
    
* **R1** – Unstrategic repetition of words (-0.5)  
  Detection: Same words used without rhetorical purpose  
  Fix: Use synonyms or pronouns strategically  
    
* **G1** – SPaG errors undermining clarity (-0.5)  
  Detection: Grammar/spelling affecting meaning  
  Fix: Correct errors that create ambiguity

**Question-Specific Analytical Penalties (Q3, Q6, and Q7b):**

* **F1** – Feature spotting: "shows/showing/shown" usage (-0.5 per instance)  
  Detection: Using "shows" for analysis instead of precise analytical verbs  
  Upgrade: depicts, portrays, emphasizes, highlights, reveals, suggests, illustrates, conveys, evokes, underscores, reinforces, critiques, challenges, exposes, examines  
  **Applies to:** Q3 (language/structure analysis), Q6 (evaluation), Q7b (comparison)  
    
* **S1** – Weak sentence starters (the/this/these) (-0.5)  
  Detection: Repetitive or unsophisticated openings  
  Upgrade: Use discourse markers, prepositional phrases, varied structures  
  **Applies to:** Q3, Q6, Q7b  
    
* **S2** – Underdeveloped sentences (less than 2 lines) (-0.5)  
  Detection: Sentences lacking detail (except hooks/topic sentences)  
  Fix: Expand with analysis, examples, developed explanation  
  **Applies to:** Q3, Q6, Q7b  
    
* **Q1** – Quotation without analysis (-0.5)  
  Detection: Evidence presented but not interpreted  
  Fix: Embed quotes with analytical integration and explicit effect explanation  
  **Applies to:** Q3, Q6, Q7b  
    
* **T1** – Imprecise analytical verbs (-0.5)  
  Detection: Using vague verbs like "uses/has/goes" instead of precise analytical language  
  Upgrade: evokes, establishes, conveys, crafts, constructs, demonstrates  
  **Applies to:** Q3, Q6, Q7b  
    
* **L1** – Missing causal link between evidence and effect (-0.5)  
  Detection: Quote → Effect with no explanation of HOW  
  Fix: Explicitly connect technique to its impact using causal language  
  **Applies to:** Q3, Q6, Q7b

**Question 3 Specific Penalties (Language & Structure Analysis):**

* **STR-MISS** – Missing structure analysis entirely (-0.5)  
  Detection: Response focuses only on language features with no discussion of structural techniques (sentence length, paragraph structure, positioning, repetition, contrast, etc.)  
  Fix: Add analysis of how the writer uses structural choices to achieve effects  
  **Critical:** Edexcel mark scheme states "Responses that are unbalanced cannot access Level 3 or above, where analysis of both language and structure is required"  
    
* **STR-IMBAL** – Imbalanced language/structure coverage (-0.5)  
  Detection: Heavy focus on one element (e.g., 90% language, 10% structure OR vice versa)  
  Fix: Ensure roughly equal coverage of both language and structure throughout response  
  **Note:** One or two strong structure paragraphs among language paragraphs can still achieve balance  
    
* **STR-SUPER** – Superficial structure commentary (-0.5)  
  Detection: Structure mentioned but not analyzed (e.g., "The writer uses short sentences" with no explanation of effect)  
  Fix: Explain HOW structural choices create meaning/effects, not just identify them

**Question 6 Specific Penalties (Critical Evaluation \- AO4):**

* **EVAL-DESC** – Descriptive rather than evaluative (-0.5)  
  Detection: Explaining what the text does without judging how successfully it does it  
  Fix: "The writer uses emotive language" → "The writer's emotive language successfully/partially/fails to engage the reader because..."  
  **Critical:** This is the most common Q6 issue  
    
* **EVAL-NOJUDGE** – Lacks critical judgement/opinion (-0.5)  
  Detection: No clear stance on effectiveness, success, or quality of the text  
  Fix: Include evaluative language: "effectively," "successfully," "unconvincingly," "powerfully," "somewhat weakly"  
  **Note:** Mark scheme says "References to writer's techniques should only be credited at Level 2 and above if they support the critical judgement"  
    
* **EVAL-NOSUCC** – Not addressing "how successfully" (-0.5)  
  Detection: Analyzing techniques without evaluating their success/effectiveness  
  Fix: Explicitly address the "how successfully" element of the question  
  **Example:** "While the metaphor is striking, it may alienate readers unfamiliar with..."  
    
* **EVAL-NOSUPP** – Critical judgement without textual support (-0.5)  
  Detection: Making evaluative claims without evidence from the text  
  Fix: Ground all judgements in specific textual references  
    
* **EVAL-NODIST** – Lacks critical distance/detachment (-0.5)  
  Detection: Writing as if accepting everything in the text at face value; no questioning or analytical distance  
  Fix: Step back and assess the text's choices: "The writer attempts to... with varying success"

**Analytical Writing Penalties (Apply to Q3, Q6, Q7a, and Q7b):**

* **E1** – Lacks evaluative/tentative language (-0.5)  
  Detection: Overly declarative statements  
  Fix: Add "perhaps," "suggests," "arguably," "possibly"  
  **Applies to:** Q3, Q6, Q7b  
    
* **I1** – Imprecise/underdeveloped interpretation (-0.5)  
  Detection: Surface-level or vague analysis  
  Fix: Deepen with specific source evidence and precise interpretation  
  **Applies to:** Q7a (vague synthesis), Q3 (surface-level language/structure analysis), Q7b (shallow comparison), Q6 (superficial evaluation)  
    
* **P2** – Lacks perceptive insight (-0.5)  
  Detection: Missing deeper analytical layer  
  Fix: Move beyond obvious to explore implications  
  **Applies to:** Q3, Q6, Q7b  
    
* **D1** – Lacks sustained detail (-0.5)  
  Detection: Points made but not developed  
  Fix: Elaborate with evidence and analysis  
  **Applies to:** Q3, Q6, Q7a, Q7b  
    
* **U1** – Unsophisticated/informal vocabulary (-0.5)  
  Detection: Colloquial language in formal analytical essay  
  Fix: Elevate to academic register  
  **Applies to:** Q7a (informal synthesis), Q3 (casual analysis), Q7b (informal comparison), Q6 (informal evaluation)  
    
* **K1** – Conflated/underdeveloped conceptual links (-0.5)  
  Detection: Weak connections between ideas  
  Fix: Explicitly show how concepts connect  
  **Applies to:** Q3, Q6, Q7b  
    
* **E2** – Underdeveloped effect on reader (-0.5)  
  Detection: Reader effect less than 2 sentences  
  Fix: Expand emotional/intellectual impact analysis  
  **Applies to:** Q3 (where analyzing effect on reader/audience), Q6 (evaluating impact on reader)

**Question 7a Specific Penalty (Synthesis):**

* **SYN-QUOT** – Quoting rather than synthesising (-0.5)  
  Detection: Lifting exact phrases from sources instead of summarizing in own words  
  Fix: Paraphrase and synthesize information; use quotation marks only for very specific terms if necessary  
  **Note:** Mark scheme states "Quotations and candidate's own words are acceptable" but synthesis in own words demonstrates higher-level skill

**Question 7b Specific Penalty (Comparison):**

* **H1-COMP** – Single-source sentence in body paragraph (-0.5)  
  Detection: Discussing only Source A or only Source B instead of comparing throughout  
  Fix: Weave both sources together using comparative discourse markers (whereas, in contrast, similarly, both writers)  
  **Critical:** Mark scheme states "Responses that are unbalanced will not be able to access Level 3 or above, where explanation of writers' ideas and perspectives is required alongside a range of comparisons between texts"

**Section B Specific Penalties:**

* **WC** – Word count deficit penalty (Diagnostic only) (-6 per 100 words under 650)
  Detection: Diagnostic submission under 650-word target for Section B
  Calculation: ROUND((650 - word\_count) \* 6 / 100)
  Fix: Expand response to 650+ words using full IUMVCC structure (6 paragraphs of ~100-110 words each)
  Note: For Redraft/Exam Practice, assessment is halted until 650+ words achieved — WC penalty does not apply

**Literature-Specific Penalties (NOT applicable to Edexcel Paper 2, listed for reference only):**

* **A1** – Anachronistic/modern interpretation (-0.5)  
  Detection: Modern concepts on historical texts  
  Fix: "Lady Macbeth has anxiety" → "Lady Macbeth's guilt manifests through sleepwalking, reflecting Jacobean beliefs"  
    
* **X1** – Irrelevant/unexplained context (-0.5)  
  Detection: Context doesn't support argument  
  Fix: Ensure context drives interpretation causally  
    
* **M1** – Plot retelling instead of analysis (-0.5)  
  Detection: Describing events not analyzing methods  
  Fix: "Napoleon becomes cruel" → "Through imperative verbs, Orwell depicts Napoleon's transformation"

### **NOTES ON PENALTY APPLICATION**

**Consistent Deductions:**

* All penalties \= \-0.5 marks  
* Maximum 3 penalties per section (or max 2 for Intro/Conclusion where applicable)  
* Apply penalties in priority order  
* Document all penalty codes in marking rationale

**Penalty Thresholds:**

* 1 penalty \= minor issue, room for improvement  
* 2 penalties \= noticeable weaknesses affecting level  
* 3 penalties \= significant issues limiting level progression

**When NOT to Apply Penalties:**

* Questions 1, 2, 4, 5 (short retrieval tasks \- simple mark allocation based on valid points)  
* Diagnostic assessments for very weak attempts (focus on foundational guidance instead)  
* Zero-mark responses (use ZERO\_MARK\_BRANCH instead)

**Response Criteria by Question**

**Important:** Questions 1, 2, 4, and 5 require simple retrieval only. Question 3 requires language AND structure analysis with embedded quotations. Question 6 requires critical evaluation with embedded quotations. Question 7a requires synthesis in own words. Question 7b requires comparative analysis across both sources. Section B Question 8 or 9 requires extended transactional writing.

**Question 1 – AO1 (2 marks) – Short Retrieval**

Students must identify and retrieve two explicit or implicit pieces of information from the specified lines of Source A. Accept any two of the valid points listed in the mark scheme. Quotations and candidate's own words are acceptable. Students should provide brief, clear answers \- typically a word, phrase, or short sentence for each point. No explanation or analysis is required.

**Question 2 – AO1 (2 marks) – Short Retrieval**

Students must identify and retrieve two explicit or implicit pieces of information from the specified lines of Source A or Source B (as indicated in the question). Accept any two of the valid points listed in the mark scheme. Quotations and candidate's own words are acceptable. Students should provide brief, clear answers \- typically a word, phrase, or short sentence for each point. No explanation or analysis is required.

**Question 3 – AO2 (15 marks) – Language and Structure Analysis**

Students must analyze how the writer uses BOTH language AND structure to interest and engage the reader in the specified lines of one source. **Critical requirement:** Responses must analyze both language and structure. Responses that are unbalanced (focusing predominantly on language OR predominantly on structure) cannot access Level 3 or above.

Students should write three developed analytical paragraphs. Each paragraph should embed quotations smoothly and analyze the effects of the writer's choices. Students may choose to:

- Write three balanced paragraphs, each covering both language and structure, OR  
- Write paragraphs focusing more heavily on language with one or two strong paragraphs focusing on structure (ensuring overall balance)

The structure for each analytical paragraph follows the TTECEA framework: topic sentence establishing the core concept, identification of technique (language or structural) with embedded evidence and initial inference, close analysis zooming in on specific details, two sentences exploring distinct effects on the reader, and author's purpose connecting to broader themes.

**Language features include:** words, phrases, language techniques (metaphor, simile, personification, imagery, etc.), and sentence forms (questions, exclamations, minor sentences).

**Structure features include:** sentence length and variety, paragraph structure and organization, positioning of information, repetition and patterns, contrast and juxtaposition, shifts in focus or perspective, cyclical structure, withholding/revealing information.

For example (language): "The writer employs visceral imagery, describing the ward as 'enormous, and on fire'. The destructive connotations of 'on fire' suggest immense danger and chaos, emphasizing the overwhelming responsibility the doctor faces during night shifts. This creates a sense of anxiety in the reader, making them feel the weight of life-or-death decisions. Through this, the writer conveys the intense psychological pressure inherent in medical work."

For example (structure): "The writer uses increasingly short sentences as the extract progresses, building from longer, descriptive sentences to the abrupt 'Not really what I'd trained so hard for.' This structural shift mirrors the narrator's growing disillusionment, with the fragmented syntax reflecting his fractured expectations. The brevity creates a sense of anticlimax and disappointment in the reader. Through this structural choice, the writer emphasizes the stark contrast between medical school idealism and workplace reality."

Students should structure their three paragraphs to analyze different sections or aspects of the source, ensuring comprehensive coverage of both language and structure across the response. A typical response length is 300-400 words.

**Question 4 – AO1 (1 mark) – Single-Point Retrieval**

Students must identify and retrieve one explicit or implicit piece of information from the specified lines of Source B. Accept any one of the valid points listed in the mark scheme. Quotations and candidate's own words are acceptable. Students should provide a brief, clear answer \- typically a word, phrase, or short sentence. No explanation or analysis is required.

**Question 5 – AO1 (1 mark) – Single-Point Retrieval**

Students must identify and retrieve one explicit or implicit piece of information from the specified lines of Source B. Accept any one of the valid points listed in the mark scheme. Quotations and candidate's own words are acceptable. Students should provide a brief, clear answer \- typically a word, phrase, or short sentence. No explanation or analysis is required.

**Question 6 – AO4 (15 marks) – Critical Evaluation**

Students must evaluate how successfully the writer achieves their purpose or conveys their perspective in the specified lines of one source. **Critical requirement:** References to writer's techniques should only be credited at Level 2 and above if they support the critical judgement of the text. Students must move beyond description to evaluation.

Students should write three developed evaluative paragraphs using the TTECEA framework. Each paragraph is worth approximately 5 marks. The key distinction from Q3 is that every analytical point must be tied to an evaluative judgement about effectiveness or success.

The structure for each evaluative paragraph follows TTECEA with evaluative language integrated throughout:

Begin with a topic sentence that establishes both what technique/approach is being analyzed AND an initial evaluative stance (e.g., "The writer effectively uses..." or "The writer's use of... proves somewhat unconvincing because..."). Identify the specific technique with embedded evidence and initial inference about what the writer is attempting. Provide close analysis zooming in on specific details and what they reveal. Write two sentences exploring the effects on the reader, but frame these in terms of success/effectiveness (e.g., "This successfully evokes..." or "However, this may alienate readers who..."). Discuss the author's purpose while evaluating how well this purpose is achieved (e.g., "Through this, the writer convincingly demonstrates..." or "While the writer attempts to..., the effect is undermined by...").

**Key evaluative vocabulary to integrate:** effectively, successfully, convincingly, powerfully, skillfully, fails to, unconvincingly, somewhat weakly, attempts but doesn't quite achieve, partially succeeds, may alienate, could be more effective, works well for \[audience\] but perhaps less so for \[different audience\].

For example: "The writer effectively employs visceral imagery, describing the ward as 'enormous, and on fire'. The destructive connotations successfully convey the intensity of medical work, though some readers might find this somewhat melodramatic. The hyperbolic quality powerfully evokes the psychological pressure doctors face, creating genuine anxiety in engaged readers. Through this, the writer convincingly communicates the overwhelming nature of healthcare work, though the dramatic tone risks losing credibility with more skeptical audiences."

Students should structure their three paragraphs to evaluate different aspects or sections of the text, ensuring comprehensive critical judgement across the response. A typical response length is 300-400 words demonstrating sustained evaluation throughout.

**Question 7a – AO1 (6 marks) – Synthesis**

Students must synthesize evidence from both Source A and Source B to identify similarities or differences (as specified in the question). **Critical requirement:** Candidates must draw on BOTH texts to access marks. Students should write clear, developed responses using their own words to synthesize information.

The task assesses selection and synthesis skills, not analysis. Students should demonstrate detailed understanding of similarities or differences by selecting appropriate evidence from both sources and combining this evidence clearly.

Students may structure responses as:

- Separate paragraphs for each text with clear comparative framing, OR  
- Integrated comparison throughout, OR  
- A combination approach

**Assessment focuses on:**

- Drawing on both texts (essential for marks)  
- Detailed understanding of similarities/differences  
- Clear synthesis of evidence  
- Using own words (though quotations are acceptable, synthesis demonstrates higher skill)

For example: "Both writers convey the difficulty of their career paths. In Text 1, the writer describes medical schools as 'oversubscribed ten-fold', showing intense competition. Similarly, in Text 2, Elizabeth faces multiple barriers as she is told 'You won't get into medical school' due to her gender. Both texts thus emphasize external obstacles preventing entry to medicine, though the nature of these obstacles differs \- one is competitive, the other discriminatory."

A typical response length is 100-150 words demonstrating detailed synthesis from both sources. Level 3 responses (5-6 marks) show detailed understanding and detailed synthesis.

**Question 7b – AO3 (14 marks) – Comparative Analysis**

Students must compare how the writers convey their different ideas and perspectives, and compare the methods they use to convey these across both Source A and Source B. **Critical requirement:** Responses that are unbalanced (focusing predominantly on one text) will not be able to access Level 3 or above, where explanation of writers' ideas and perspectives is required alongside a range of comparisons between texts.

Students should write a comparative response of four to five paragraphs: a brief introduction of one to two lines, three developed comparative body paragraphs, and an optional brief conclusion of one line.

Every paragraph must compare both sources simultaneously throughout. Students must not write about Source A entirely, then Source B entirely. They must weave comparison throughout their response using the comparative TTECEA structure.

The structure for each comparative body paragraph should follow this framework:

Begin with a comparative topic sentence that establishes what aspect is being compared in both sources. Identify the technique the Source A writer uses, then use comparative connectives (whereas, in contrast, similarly, both) to identify the technique the Source B writer uses. Quote from Source A, then use comparative transition to quote from Source B. Provide close analysis zooming into Source A's specific details and what they suggest, then zoom into Source B's specific details and what they suggest, comparing what the contrast or similarity reveals. Write two sentences analyzing Source A's effects on readers, then two sentences analyzing Source B's effects on readers, followed by comparison of whether these effects are similar or different. Discuss Source A writer's purpose using tentative language, then discuss Source B writer's purpose, comparing whether purposes are similar or contrasting. Optionally evaluate which writer's approach is more effective for this specific aspect using comparative language.

Key requirements include sustained comparison throughout, use of comparative discourse markers throughout every paragraph, perceptive insight into both writers' perspectives and methods, judicious selection of evidence from both sources, and sophisticated analytical vocabulary.

A typical response length is 400-500 words demonstrating sustained, balanced comparison throughout.

**Section B Question 8 or 9 – AO5/AO6 (40 marks total) – Extended Transactional Writing**

Students must choose EITHER Question 8 OR Question 9\. Each offers a different transactional writing task. Students must write in a specified form (article, letter, speech, guide, or essay) for a stated purpose (to inform, advise, argue, or persuade) and audience. The response must be substantial (650+ words recommended) and demonstrate both excellent content/organization (AO5 \- 24 marks) and technical accuracy (AO6 \- 16 marks).

For Redraft and Exam Practice submissions, students must follow the IUMVCC structure: Introduction (hook and thesis), Urgency (why action is needed now), Main argument with evidence, Vision (compelling future), Counter-argument with refutation, Conclusion (call to action and memorable close).

Students should use a range of rhetorical devices appropriate to the form and purpose, which may include: rule of three, direct address, rhetorical questions, anaphora, epistrophe, metaphor, emotive language, statistics, anecdote, repetition for emphasis, varied sentence structures for effect, and contrasts.

The tone and register must be consistently matched to the form, purpose, and audience throughout. For example:

- **Article for magazine:** Engaging, accessible, may use subheadings, conversational but polished  
- **Guide for young people:** Clear, instructional, organized with helpful structure, friendly tone  
- **Speech:** Direct address, oral features, rhetorical devices, varied pace and emphasis  
- **Formal letter:** Professional register, appropriate conventions, clear structure

AO5 assessment focuses on compelling communication, sophisticated and appropriate vocabulary, varied sentence structures for effect, sustained and appropriate tone/register, fluently linked paragraphs with cohesion, and seamlessly integrated discourse markers. Organization should show clear introduction, development of points, and conclusion.

AO6 assessment focuses on accurate sentence demarcation, range of punctuation used accurately (including sophisticated punctuation like semicolons, colons, dashes), variety of sentence forms (simple, compound, complex), Standard English with controlled grammar throughout, and high-level spelling accuracy including ambitious vocabulary.

Students should demonstrate clear awareness of form through appropriate organizational features (subheadings for articles/guides where appropriate, appropriate salutation/sign-off for letters, etc.) while avoiding superficial layout features (columns, pictures, boxes) which are not required or rewarded.
