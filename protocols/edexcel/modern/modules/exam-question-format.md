# BOARD FORMAT MODULE — Edexcel GCSE Literature

**Board:** Edexcel (Pearson) GCSE English Literature (1ET0)
**Papers:** Paper 1 (Shakespeare + Post-1914 Lit) + Paper 2 (19th Century + Poetry + Unseen)
**Format:** Extract-based, single question, "Explore how" phrasing

---

## QUESTION FORMAT RULES — NON-NEGOTIABLE

1. **Phrasing:** Every question MUST use *"Explore how [author] presents [theme/character] in this extract and in the [play/novel] as a whole."* — NEVER use *"Starting with this extract, how does..."* (that's AQA), *"Discuss"*, or *"Analyse"*.

2. **Extract:** Every question MUST include a real extract of **~20 lines** from the text. For Shakespeare, include speaker labels. For prose, include narrative text.

3. **Single question:** Generate ONE question (not paired Either/Or). Edexcel literature uses single-question format.

4. **Mark allocation:**
   - Shakespeare (P1 Section A): 40 marks (includes AO4 within the 40)
   - Post-1914 Lit (P1 Section B): 40 marks
   - 19th Century (P2 Section A): 40 marks

5. **Two-part response structure:** The question explicitly asks about "this extract AND the [play/novel] as a whole."

---

## COMPLETE QUESTION STRUCTURE

1. **Extract header** — e.g. *"Read the extract below from Act 3 Scene 1 of Romeo and Juliet. Answer the question that follows."*
2. **Context line** (italicised) — Brief context for the extract
3. **The actual extract** — ~20 lines of real text
4. **The question** — *"Explore how [author] presents [theme/character] in this extract and in the [play/novel] as a whole."*
5. **Mark allocation** — (40 marks)

## EXAMPLE OUTPUT — Macbeth (Shakespeare)

**Read the extract below from Act 1 Scene 5 of Macbeth. Answer the question that follows.**

*In this extract, Lady Macbeth has just received her husband's letter about the witches' prophecy.*

> **LADY MACBETH:**
> Glamis thou art, and Cawdor; and shalt be
> What thou art promised. Yet do I fear thy nature;
> It is too full o' the milk of human kindness
> To catch the nearest way. Thou wouldst be great,
> Art not without ambition, but without
> The illness should attend it. What thou wouldst highly,
> That wouldst thou holily; wouldst not play false,
> And yet wouldst wrongly win. Thou'dst have, great Glamis,
> That which cries, "Thus thou must do," if thou have it,
> And that which rather thou dost fear to do,
> Than wishest should be undone. Hie thee hither,
> That I may pour my spirits in thine ear
> And chastise with the valour of my tongue
> All that impedes thee from the golden round,
> Which fate and metaphysical aid doth seem
> To have thee crowned withal.

Explore how Shakespeare presents Lady Macbeth's ambition in this extract and in the play as a whole.

(40 marks)

## EXAMPLE OUTPUT — An Inspector Calls (Post-1914)

**Read the extract below from Act 1 of An Inspector Calls. Answer the question that follows.**

*In this extract, the Inspector has just arrived at the Birling household.*

> INSPECTOR: (*taking a seat*) Thank you, sir. (*He sits down, and then goes on*) I'd like some information, if you don't mind, Mr Birling. Two hours ago a young woman died in the Infirmary. She'd been taken there this afternoon because she'd swallowed a lot of strong disinfectant. Burnt her inside out, of course.
>
> ERIC: (*involuntarily*) My God!
>
> INSPECTOR: Yes, she was in great agony. They did everything they could for her at the Infirmary, but she died. Suicide, of course.
>
> BIRLING: (*rather impatiently*) Yes, yes. Horrid business. But I don't understand why you should come here, Inspector—
>
> INSPECTOR: (*cutting through, massively*) I've been round to the room she had, and she'd left a letter there and a sort of diary. Like a lot of these young women who get into various kinds of trouble, she'd used more than one name.

Explore how Priestley presents the Inspector as a powerful character in this extract and in the play as a whole.

(40 marks)
