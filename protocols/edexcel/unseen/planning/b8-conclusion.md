### B.8 Conclusion (MANDATORY)

📌 Planning > Part B.8: Conclusion > Step 1 of 4

"Let's plan your conclusion. Top-level conclusions SYNTHESISE rather than summarise — they add a layer of insight that couldn't exist without the analysis you've done in your body paragraphs.

Your conclusion needs four elements, and we'll work through each one."

---

**Step 1 — Restated Thesis:**

"First, restate your thesis from the introduction — but **don't just repeat it**. Restate it with the added insight gained from your three body paragraphs.

Your original thesis was: '[WORKING_THESIS]'

Now, having analysed form/beginning, language, and ending, how would you express this argument differently? What have your body paragraphs revealed that adds depth to your original claim?"

If still struggling: "Try completing this: 'Through this poem, the poet ultimately reveals that [concept] is not simply [original claim] but rather [deeper insight from body analysis]...'"

---

**Step 2 — Controlling Concept:**

📌 Planning > Part B.8: Conclusion > Step 2 of 4

"Now the most important part — the **controlling concept**. This is the 'so what?' that connects ALL THREE body paragraphs into one overarching argument.

Your three paragraphs explored [concept from B1], [concept from B2], and [concept from B3]. What's the SINGLE controlling idea that unifies all three?"

Wait.

**[AI_INTERNAL — Scaffolding:]**
- If student lists rather than synthesises: "You've described what each paragraph does, but you haven't SYNTHESISED. Try: 'Across the poem, the poet argues that [ONE overarching idea]...' What fills that gap?"
- If student gives two ideas: "You've identified two strands. A controlling concept should UNIFY them. How are they connected? What's the single thread?"
- If strong: "Excellent — does this feel genuinely different from your thesis? A controlling concept should DEEPEN it, not duplicate it."

**Step 3 — Poet's Purpose:**

📌 Planning > Part B.8: Conclusion > Step 3 of 4

"What do you believe was the poet's primary intention or reason for crafting this poem? Connect this to their methods — what were they ultimately trying to achieve, communicate, or challenge?

Think about:
- What MESSAGE did they want readers to take away?
- What ASSUMPTIONS were they challenging?
- What EMOTIONS were they trying to provoke — and why?"

Wait.

**[AI_INTERNAL — Scaffolding:]**
If vague ("to make people think"): "That's too broad. WHAT specifically did the poet want readers to think ABOUT? What truth were they exposing or assumption were they challenging?"
If technique-focused only: "You're describing HOW they did it, but I'm asking WHY. What was the purpose behind those technique choices?"

Wait. Store.

---

**Step 4 — Universal Moral Message:**

📌 Planning > Part B.8: Conclusion > Step 4 of 4

"Finally, the **universal message** — what does this poem teach us about human nature, society, or the human condition that transcends this specific poem?

Think beyond the poem specifically:
- What truth about HUMAN NATURE does it reveal?
- What does it teach us about how people experience [theme]?
- What MORAL or PHILOSOPHICAL question does it leave the reader with?

Draft one sentence that captures this universal claim."

Wait.

**[AI_INTERNAL — Scaffolding:]**
If too text-specific (still about the poem's speaker/situation): "Step outside the poem — what does it teach us about PEOPLE IN GENERAL?"
If cliché ('life is hard' / 'nature is beautiful'): "Can you make it more SPECIFIC to the particular insight this poem offers? What does it reveal about [theme] that's more surprising or nuanced?"
