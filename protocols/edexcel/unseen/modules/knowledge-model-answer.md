## **2.F. Internal Gold Standard Model Answer (For GCSE Structure & AOs)**

**Your** benchmark for "top-grade" writing is the following model answer. You must use this as your guide and an internal standard when rewriting student paragraphs during assessments, aiming **to** replicate its scholarly tone, argumentative structure, and analytical **depth.**

**Note:** This model is based on Kaplinski's 'The washing never gets done' from the Edexcel June 2024 paper. For any other unseen poem, you must generate equivalent-quality models on the fly using the same structural principles, TTECEA anatomy, and analytical depth demonstrated here.

**[INTRODUCTION — Hook + Theme + Thesis]**

Embedded within the quiet rhythm of domestic chaos, Kaplinski's 'The washing never gets done' confronts the reader with a deceptively simple paradox: that the very incompleteness of human life — its relentless, unfinishable tasks — is precisely what creates the conditions for transcendent wonder. Through a deliberate structural pivot from claustrophobic domestic cataloguing to expansive natural imagery, the poet argues that beauty does not exist despite life's futility, but because of it. This essay will explore how Kaplinski uses the poem's opening anaphoric repetition and domestic imagery to establish the relentless burden of daily existence, before examining how the poet's language transforms ordinary dew and birdsong into symbols of infinite continuity, and finally analysing how the poem's concluding movement into "the dusk" suggests that wonder is found not in escaping mundanity, but in the capacity to notice within it.

**[BODY PARAGRAPH 1 — Beginning: TTECEA Structure]**

**[TOPIC — Conceptual]** From its very first line, Kaplinski's poem establishes a tone of weary resignation, cataloguing the failures of domestic life to convey the poet's central argument that human existence is defined by incompleteness. **[TECHNIQUE + EVIDENCE]** The poet employs anaphoric repetition — "The washing never gets done. / The furnace never gets heated. / Books never get read" — creating a rhythmic accumulation of unfinished tasks that mirrors the relentless, cyclical nature of daily obligation. **[CLOSE ANALYSIS]** The emphatic adverb "never" recurs with insistent force, its absolute negativity denying the reader any hope of resolution or completion; each short, declarative sentence lands with the finality of a door closing. **[EFFECT 1]** This relentless repetition creates in the reader a claustrophobic sense of entrapment, as though the poem itself is caught in an endless loop of domestic frustration, the syntax physically enacting the monotony it describes. **[EFFECT 2]** Moreover, the progression from "washing" to "furnace" to "books" to "life" reveals a careful escalation — from the mundane to the existential — suggesting that the incompleteness of household chores is merely a symptom of life's larger, philosophical incompleteness. **[AUTHOR'S PURPOSE]** The poet's purpose in opening with this catalogue of domestic defeat is to establish the very conditions from which wonder will later emerge — without first feeling the weight of life's futility, the reader cannot fully appreciate the transcendence that follows.

**[BODY PARAGRAPH 2 — Language: TTECEA Structure]**

**[TOPIC — Conceptual]** Perhaps the most striking transformation in Kaplinski's poem occurs through its language, as mundane domestic imagery gives way to luminous natural description, conveying the poet's belief that wonder persists alongside — and perhaps because of — life's incompleteness. **[TECHNIQUE + EVIDENCE]** The poet employs a sustained extended metaphor of nature as boundless continuity, captured in the image of "the spring which is so full of everything / continuing in all directions." **[CLOSE ANALYSIS]** The modifier "full" suggests an almost overwhelming abundance that contrasts sharply with the incompleteness of human tasks — where washing is "never" done and fences "collapse," spring is replete, teeming, inexhaustible. Furthermore, the poet layers sensory imagery across multiple senses: auditory ("the redwing's song"), visual ("every blade of grass"), and tactile ("every drop of dew"), creating an immersive, panoramic experience that envelops the reader in nature's richness. **[EFFECT 1]** The cumulative effect of the repeated "into" — "into the evening clouds, / into the redwing's song and into every / drop of dew" — generates an almost incantatory rhythm, as though the act of noticing itself becomes boundless, spreading outward in all directions. **[EFFECT 2]** This rhythmic expansion creates in the reader a sense of being swept along, as if carried by the very momentum of the poet's attention — each "into" opening a new dimension of perception. **[AUTHOR'S PURPOSE]** Crucially, Kaplinski positions this natural abundance as something that exists "beside all this" — the domestic chaos — rather than in opposition to it. The poet's purpose, perhaps, is to argue that transcendence is not an escape from the ordinary, but an awareness that coexists with it: one can notice the spring precisely because one is immersed in the unfinishable business of living.

**[BODY PARAGRAPH 3 — Ending: TTECEA Structure]**

**[TOPIC — Conceptual]** In its final lines, Kaplinski's poem achieves a remarkable tonal transformation, dissolving the claustrophobic domestic world into the boundless expanse of the natural one, conveying the poet's ultimate argument that wonder is discovered not by escaping ordinary life, but by looking up from it. **[TECHNIQUE + EVIDENCE]** The poet deploys the striking image of dew "on every blade of grass in the meadow, / as far as the eye can see, into the dusk," where the phrase "as far as the eye can see" evokes an almost infinite visual horizon, stretching the reader's perception outward from the confined domestic space of leaking roofs and torn trousers into limitless nature. **[CLOSE ANALYSIS]** The final word — "dusk" — is particularly resonant: dusk occupies the threshold between day and night, between light and darkness, and its positioning as the poem's concluding image suggests that this moment of transcendent perception exists in a liminal, fleeting space — available only to those who pause long enough to notice. **[EFFECT 1]** This creates in the reader a bittersweet emotional response — a sense of quiet awe tempered by the awareness that such moments are fragile and impermanent, existing at the very edge of darkness. **[EFFECT 2]** Furthermore, the enjambment across the poem's final three lines — each phrase flowing without pause into the next — enacts the boundlessness the poet describes, the form itself refusing the closure that the poem's content denies. **[AUTHOR'S PURPOSE]** The poet's purpose in ending with "dusk" rather than "dawn" is arguably to acknowledge that wonder is not triumphant or celebratory, but quiet, transient, and discovered in the fading light — a deeply human, deeply modest form of transcendence.

**[CONCLUSION — Four-Part Quartet]**

**[RESTATED THESIS]** Ultimately, Kaplinski's 'The washing never gets done' argues that wonder and mundanity are not opposites, but necessary companions — that the capacity to perceive beauty exists precisely because life is relentlessly, exhaustingly incomplete. **[CONTROLLING CONCEPT]** The poem's controlling concept — the paradox that transcendence emerges from futility, not despite it — challenges the reader to reconsider their own relationship with the ordinary. **[AUTHOR'S PURPOSE]** Through the deliberate structural pivot from claustrophobic domestic cataloguing to expansive natural imagery, the poet's purpose is to demonstrate that the act of noticing — of pausing within the chaos to register spring, birdsong, and dew — is itself a form of defiance against the incompleteness that defines human existence. **[UNIVERSAL MORAL MESSAGE]** Perhaps the poem's universal message is that meaning is not found by completing the tasks of living, but by developing the capacity to look up from them — to see, in the midst of broken fences and torn clothes, the spring continuing "in all directions."

### **2.F.3 Q12 Gold Standard Model — Comparison Paragraphs**

**[PARAGRAPH 1 — Focus on Poem 1, compare to Poem 2]**

Both Kaplinski and Thomas employ imperative or instructive language to suggest that connecting with nature requires a conscious, deliberate act of attention amidst life's demands. Kaplinski's observation that "The wonder is / that beside all this one can notice" frames the ability to perceive beauty as remarkable and unexpected, the abstract noun "wonder" elevating the simple act of noticing into something approaching the miraculous. Similarly, Thomas's direct instruction to "pause a minute, / Let the mind take its photograph" uses an imperative mood to urge the reader to actively halt their daily routine and consciously preserve the natural scene. However, while Kaplinski's tone is one of quiet astonishment — the wonder arises spontaneously despite chaos — Thomas's approach is more urgent and prescriptive, the imperative "Let" suggesting that without deliberate effort, the beauty will be lost to "the long cold." This difference in tone reveals contrasting perspectives on the relationship between daily life and nature: for Kaplinski, wonder survives effortlessly alongside incompleteness, whereas for Thomas, beauty must be actively seized before it vanishes.

**[PARAGRAPH 2 — Focus on Poem 2, compare to Poem 1]**

Thomas's use of personification and metaphor in 'A Day in Autumn' creates a vivid portrait of nature as both generous and fleeting, contrasting with Kaplinski's presentation of nature as an inexhaustible, ever-present force. Thomas personifies the autumn trees as figures adorned for a special occasion, with leaves "adding their decoration / To the trees' shoulders, braiding the cuffs / Of the boughs with gold," the verbs "adding" and "braiding" suggesting a deliberate, almost ceremonial act of beautification. The metaphor of the mind taking "its photograph" presents memory as a conscious preservation technique — nature must be captured because it will not last. In contrast, Kaplinski's nature does not need to be captured; it simply continues "in all directions," its permanence emphasised by the expansive spatial language. This fundamental difference in how the poets present nature's temporality shapes the reader's emotional response: Thomas creates a gentle urgency — savour this moment, for it will pass — while Kaplinski creates quiet reassurance — the spring is always there, if only you look up from your tasks.
