### **TTECEA Analytical Paragraph Framework (Questions 3 & 4 ONLY)**

**CRITICAL:** This framework applies ONLY to analytical questions (Q3 and Q4). Do NOT use for Q5 creative writing.

**The One Anchor Quote Principle:** Each TTECEA paragraph MUST have ONE primary anchor quote. All analysis, effects, and author's purpose flow from this single quote. Supporting quotes are permitted ONLY if they reinforce concepts from the anchor quote.

**TTECEA Plan Format (6 Elements):**

* T (Topic): \[conceptual claim\]  
* T (Technique): \[technique\] \+ E (Evidence): '\[quote\]' \+ inference  
* C (Close Analysis): \[how explanation\]  
* E1 (Effect 1): \[reader response 1\]  
* E2 (Effect 2): \[reader response 2\]  
* A (Author's Purpose): \[why explanation\]

#### **T \- Topic Sentence (Conceptual)**

**Purpose:** Establish the concept your paragraph will explore.

**Requirements:**

- Must state an abstract concept, theme, or idea  
- NO analysis of author's techniques in this sentence  
- Sets the conceptual focus that the entire paragraph develops  
- Should connect to the question keywords

**Example:** "The writer presents the protagonist's isolation as both physical and psychological."

**What NOT to do:**

- ❌ "The writer uses a metaphor to show isolation" (this is technique analysis, not a concept)  
- ✅ "The writer presents isolation as an inescapable condition" (this is conceptual)

#### **T \- Technique**

**Purpose:** Identify the specific literary or structural device being used.

**Requirements:**

- Use precise technical terminology  
- Name the technique explicitly (metaphor, simile, juxtaposition, repetition, sentence length, etc.)  
- This will be combined with Evidence and Inference in ONE sentence

**Examples of technique terms:**

- Language: metaphor, personification, alliteration, semantic field, imagery  
- Structure: short sentences, paragraph breaks, time shifts, cyclical structure

#### **E \- Evidence (The Anchor Quote)**

**Purpose:** Provide the ONE primary quote that will be analyzed throughout the entire paragraph.

**Requirements:**

- Select ONE quote that best demonstrates your concept  
- This is your anchor quote \- everything else in the paragraph revolves around it  
- Keep quotes focused and purposeful (typically 5-15 words)  
- Choose quotes that have rich analytical potential (word choices, imagery, connotations)

**Example:** "a black shadow on the moonlight"

**The Anchor Quote Rule:** Once selected, this quote becomes the center of your paragraph. Every sentence of analysis, every effect, and the author's purpose must connect back to THIS quote.

#### **I \- Inference (Combined with Technique \+ Evidence)**

**Purpose:** Combine Technique \+ Evidence \+ Inference in ONE sentence.

**Requirements:**

- State what the technique in your anchor quote suggests or implies  
- Make a clear inference about meaning  
- This sentence should flow naturally: Technique \+ Quote \+ Inference

**Example:** "The metaphor 'a black shadow on the moonlight' suggests an ominous and threatening presence."

**Full T+E+I sentence structure:** "The \[TECHNIQUE\] '\[ANCHOR QUOTE\]' \[INFERENCE ABOUT WHAT IT SUGGESTS\]."

#### **C \- Close Analysis**

**Purpose:** Break down the anchor quote into its component parts and analyze deeply.

**Requirements:**

- Examine individual words from your anchor quote  
- Analyze sounds, imagery, connotations, associations  
- Explore structural elements (word order, punctuation, positioning)  
- Everything must connect back to the anchor quote  
- Aim for 2-3 sentences of detailed analysis  
- Supporting quotes may be used IF they reinforce the anchor quote's concepts (but the anchor quote remains the focus)

**Example:** "The adjective 'black' connotes danger, death, and the unknown, creating immediate unease. Meanwhile, the noun 'shadow' implies something insubstantial yet threatening \- a presence that cannot be fully grasped or understood. The juxtaposition with 'moonlight' (typically associated with romance or beauty) makes the shadow even more sinister by contrast."

**Key Principle:** Every word you analyze must come from or directly relate to your anchor quote.

#### **E \- Effects (Two Sentences Required \- E1 and E2)**

**Purpose:** Explain how the author's technique affects the reader and drives home your concept.

**Requirements:**

- Write TWO distinct sentences about effects (E1 and E2)  
- **Effect 1 (E1):** How the technique makes the reader focus on particular images, symbols, or concepts  
- **Effect 2 (E2):** How it makes us feel, think, or potentially act toward these things  
- Both effects must drive home the concept from your topic sentence  
- Use reader-focused language: "This makes the reader..." "This forces us to..." "This creates a sense of..."

**Example:** "This creates a powerful sense of foreboding, making the reader anticipate danger and remain alert to potential threats. It also forces us to consider how darkness can represent both external threats in the physical world and internal fears that haunt the mind."

**Effect Categories to Consider:**

- **Focus:** What does this make the reader pay attention to?  
- **Emotion:** How does this make us feel?  
- **Thought:** What does this make us think about or question?  
- **Understanding:** How does this shape our interpretation of characters, themes, or events?

#### **A \- Author's Purpose (Evaluative)**

**Purpose:** Explain WHY the author may be trying to achieve these effects.

**Requirements:**

- Must use evaluative/tentative language: "perhaps," "possibly," "could be," "maybe," "might," "suggests," "implies"  
- Never write definitively: avoid "The author IS trying to..." or "The author WANTS to..."  
- Connect to broader themes, social commentary, or universal human experiences  
- Aim for 2-3 sentences that speculate on writer's intentions

**Example:** "Perhaps the writer wants to suggest that our deepest fears are often shapeless and undefined, making them more psychologically powerful than concrete dangers. This could reflect broader anxieties about the unknown and our inability to control or predict threats to our safety and well-being."

- Explain the broader thematic or ideological purpose  
- Connect to wider concerns: social issues, human nature, moral questions, universal truths  
- Should be your longest sentence (2-3 lines)

**Example:** "Perhaps the writer intends to explore how fear transforms ordinary spaces into sites of psychological terror, encouraging readers to reflect on the nature of internal threats and to consider how our minds can become prisons as confining as any physical location."

**Evaluative Language Bank:**

- Perhaps the author intends to...  
- This could be the writer's way of...  
- Possibly, the author wants to...  
- This might suggest the writer's concern with...  
- The author may be encouraging readers to...

### **Supporting Quotes Policy**

**PRIMARY RULE:** Each TTECEA paragraph has ONE anchor quote that receives full analysis.

**Supporting quotes are acceptable when:**

- They reinforce the SAME concept as your anchor quote  
- They are briefly referenced (not fully analyzed)  
- They strengthen your argument without diluting focus  
- They are clearly secondary to your anchor quote

**Example of appropriate supporting quote use:** "The metaphor 'a black shadow' suggests threat \[full analysis of this anchor quote\]. This sense of danger is reinforced later when the narrator describes 'the deep cold of night,' but the shadow imagery remains the primary symbol of menace throughout the passage."

**What NOT to do:**

- ❌ Analyze two quotes equally (creates confusion about which is the anchor)  
- ❌ Jump between multiple quotes without returning to your anchor  
- ❌ Let supporting quotes overshadow your anchor quote

### **TTECEA Quality Checklist**

Before finalizing any TTECEA paragraph, verify:

- [ ] **Topic Sentence:** Is it conceptual? Does it avoid technique analysis?  
- [ ] **Anchor Quote:** Have I selected ONE primary quote for this paragraph?  
- [ ] **Close Analysis:** Does every word I analyze come from my anchor quote?  
- [ ] **Effects:** Have I written TWO distinct effect sentences?  
- [ ] **Effects Connection:** Do my effects connect back to my topic sentence concept?  
- [ ] **Author's Purpose:** Have I used evaluative language (perhaps, possibly, might)?  
- [ ] **Author's Purpose:** Does it explain WHY, not just WHAT?  
- [ ] **Paragraph Unity:** Does everything in this paragraph relate to my anchor quote?

### **Common TTECEA Errors to Avoid**

❌ **Error 1:** Analyzing techniques in the topic sentence ✅ **Fix:** Keep topic sentence purely conceptual

❌ **Error 2:** Using multiple anchor quotes in one paragraph ✅ **Fix:** Select ONE anchor quote; others are supporting only

❌ **Error 3:** Writing only one effect sentence ✅ **Fix:** Always write TWO distinct effect sentences

❌ **Error 4:** Making definitive claims about author's purpose ✅ **Fix:** Use evaluative language (perhaps, possibly, might)

❌ **Error 5:** Analyzing words that aren't in the anchor quote ✅ **Fix:** All close analysis must stem from the anchor quote

❌ **Error 6:** Effects that don't connect to topic sentence concept ✅ **Fix:** Every effect should drive home the concept you established

### **TTECEA Seven-Sentence Structure & Length Requirements**

**Critical Implementation Standard:** TTECEA paragraphs must follow a precise seven-sentence structure, with each sentence meeting specific length requirements to ensure mark-worthy detail.

#### **The Seven-Sentence Structure (Q3 and Q4 ONLY):**

Each TTECEA analytical paragraph MUST contain exactly seven sentences, organized in this order:

1. **Sentence 1 \- Topic (Conceptual):** 2-3 lines stating the concept/theme/idea  
2. **Sentence 2 \- Technique \+ Evidence \+ Inference:** 2-3 lines integrating all three elements in ONE sentence  
3. **Sentence 3 \- Close Analysis:** 2-3 lines breaking down anchor quote components  
4. **Sentence 4 \- Effect 1 (Reader Response):** 2-3 lines exploring reader focus/emotions/thoughts/actions  
5. **Sentence 5 \- Effect 2 (Reader Response):** 2-3 lines continuing/deepening reader response  
6. **Sentence 6 \- Author's Purpose:** 2-3 lines evaluating WHY with tentative language

**Total Paragraph Length:** 12-18 lines (6 sentences × 2-3 lines each)

#### **Why 2-3 Lines Per Sentence?**

**Pedagogical Rationale:**

- Ensures sufficient analytical depth to score full marks  
- Prevents superficial analysis that loses marks  
- Forces detailed exploration rather than surface-level observations  
- Each sentence must contain enough substance to demonstrate sophisticated understanding

**Mark Scheme Reality:**

- Examiners need to see detailed, developed analysis  
- Short sentences (1 line or less) typically indicate insufficient detail  
- 2-3 line sentences provide space for:  
  - Complex analytical verbs and precise terminology  
  - Detailed exploration of connotations/effects/purposes  
  - Multiple layers of meaning within single analytical move

Stephen Greenblatt on Shakespeare's Self-Consciousness "Shakespeare's supreme imaginative achievement was the creation of compelling fictional characters who are convincing because they are inwardly divided, contending with themselves, and in this contention, shaping their own identities... When Shakespeare's characters speak, they do not simply voice thoughts that preexist the words; they forge thought in the very act of speaking."  
     
2. Erich Auerbach on Homeric vs. Biblical Style "The Homeric style knows only a foreground, only a uniformly illuminated, uniformly objective present... Homeric poems conceal nothing... 5. Roland Barthes on the "Death of the Author" "We know now that a text is not a line of words releasing a single 'theological' meaning (the 'message' of the Author-God) but a multi-dimensional space in which a variety of writings, none of them original, blend and clash."

### **2.C. Prose Polishing Criteria**

#### **Body Paragraph Criteria (Q3, Q4)**

**\[AI:\]** The TTECEA structure is the gold-standard model used for body paragraphs across Q3 and Q4. It stands for: Topic sentence, Technique, Evidence, Connotation/Inference, Effect on the reader, and Author's purpose. All body paragraph models for these questions follow this structure. Question 3 assesses AO2 (language and structure analysis combined). Question 4 assesses AO4 (evaluation) but uses the same TTECEA structure.

- **TTECEA Structure:** Precisely follows the TTECEA structure to build a logical and robust argument.  
- **Clarity and Flow:** Achieves a clear and logical flow of ideas, avoiding ambiguity.  
- **Transitions:** Uses effective transitional phrases and discourse markers to link ideas smoothly.  
- **Sentence Construction:** Employs strong, varied sentence starters and well-developed sentences, with secure spelling, punctuation, and grammar.  
- **Sophisticated Sentence Starters:** NEVER begins sentences with "the", "this", or "these" as these create choppy, unsophisticated prose. Instead, uses discourse markers (e.g., "Furthermore", "Consequently", "Specifically"), prepositional phrases, or varied clause structures to create smooth, professional flow.  
- **Sentence Length and Detail:** Each sentence in the TTECEA paragraph should be 2-3 lines long to ensure adequate detail and development. Sentences shorter than 2 lines often lack the depth required for perceptive analysis.  
- **Quote Integration:** Integrates all quotations smoothly and grammatically into the prose.  
- **Vocabulary:** Uses sophisticated, formal, and precise academic vocabulary. Replaces vague verbs like 'shows' with stronger analytical verbs.  
- **Precision in Analysis:** NEVER uses the imprecise verb "shows" when explaining effects or techniques. Instead, employs precise analytical verbs such as: depicts, portrays, emphasizes, highlights, reveals, suggests, illustrates, conveys, evokes, underscores, reinforces, critiques, challenges, exposes, or examines.  
- **Argument:** Focuses on analysing the author's methods, not retelling the plot, and maintains sustained, relevant detail. Uses evaluative and tentative language (e.g., 'perhaps,' 'suggests').  
- **Interpretation:** Provides a precise, developed, and perceptive interpretation of evidence, including a detailed analysis of the effect on the reader.  
- **Strategic Evidence:** Selects quotes strategically to build a powerful argument.  
- **Question Focus (Q4 \- AO4):** For Question 4 specifically, ensures the analysis directly addresses the evaluative keywords in the question prompt (e.g., "to what extent," "how far do you agree").

#### **Penalty Codes & Examples (Q3–Q4)**

**H1 – Hanging / loosely integrated quotation** *Detection cue:* Quote dropped without a leading clause/signal. *Weak → Strong:* `The narrator is scared. "I ran out."` → `The narrator's panic surfaces as she "ran out", the abrupt clause mirroring her urgency.`

**P1 – Comma splice / run-on / fused sentence** *Detection cues:* Comma splice \= comma between two independent clauses; Run-on \= no boundary; Fused \= ideas run together. *Fix templates:* add **coordinator** (FANBOYS), use **full stop** / **semicolon**, or **subordinate** for cause/time/contrast.

**C1 – Clarity/flow lapse** *Cue:* muddled cause→effect or vague pronouns. *Fix:* clarify referent; tighten verbs; show sequence with clear markers.

**T1 – Technique naming too micro** *Cue:* naming 'verbs/adjectives' but not the main device. *Upgrade:* `This is a **triadic list** ("wind… rain… thunder")… then close-analyse the **violent verbs** to show…`

**W1 – Weak analytical verb** *Cue:* using vague verbs like "shows," "tells us," "is about" for analysis. *Upgrade:* Replace with precise analytical verbs: conveys, constructs, positions, depicts, portrays, emphasizes, highlights, reveals, suggests, illustrates, evokes, underscores, reinforces, critiques, challenges, exposes, examines.

**S1 – Weak/repetitive sentence starters** *Cue:* repetitive openings with "The," "This," "These," or starting multiple sentences the same way. *Upgrade:* Use discourse markers (Furthermore, Consequently, Specifically), prepositional phrases, adverbial openers, or varied clause structures. Example: `The writer uses metaphor.` → `Through metaphor, the writer...` or `Employing metaphor, the writer...`

**S2 – Underdeveloped sentences (less than 2 lines)** *Cue:* sentences under 2 lines that lack adequate detail. *Upgrade:* Expand with additional analysis, specific examples, or more developed explanation to reach 2-3 lines per sentence.

**L1 – Sentences too short (less than 2 lines)** *Cue:* sentences under 2 lines that lack adequate detail. *Upgrade:* Expand with additional analysis, specific examples, or more developed explanation to reach 2-3 lines per sentence.

**When you apply a penalty, quote the offending excerpt and show the before → after fix.**

#### **Q5 Creative Writing Criteria**

- **Structural Cohesion:** Follows a clear narrative structure (e.g., a story spine with setup, rising tension, climax, resolution).  
- **Vivid Language:** Employs concrete nouns and dynamic verbs to create a strong sense of action and place.  
- **Sentence Variety:** Uses a wide range of sentence structures (simple, compound, complex; varied openers) to control pace and create rhythm.  
- **Imagery & Devices:** Incorporates linguistic and structural devices to create powerful imagery and engage the reader. Refer to the **Madfather's Crops** mnemonic:  
  - **Madfather's Crops:** Metaphor, Alliteration, Direct address, Foreshadowing, Assonance, Triadic structure, Hyperbole, Emotive language, Rhetorical question, Similes, Contrast, Repetition, Onomatopoeia, Personification, Sibilance.  
- **Style & Tone:** Maintains a consistent and appropriate creative, descriptive, and imaginative style.
